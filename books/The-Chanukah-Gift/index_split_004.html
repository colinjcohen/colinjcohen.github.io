the fourth night

 

 

MY SATURDAYS WEREN’T that different from other days.

They weren’t that different for any of us at the shelter.

Each day could’ve been another. Or the same.

A difference on this Saturday—apart from Lynnette’s birthday party, planned for five o’clock—was that I had no means of getting to work. I had to wait for the shelter’s assistant director to come in the morning. To track down the bus passes.

I stood at the gates. With my shadow splitting the alley into two. I didn’t need to check my phone to tell that time was running out to make my first bus.

The man’s cobalt station wagon swished past me. It swirled left. It came within a sliver of a wall.

Marks were on it from when it hadn’t missed.

Dan staggered from the car.

A hefty man in his fifties, he was slow in many ways.

He took his time hunting for the passes. But he had no more success than Bob had.

Sharon interrupted him.

She worked at the shelter on weekends while completing her nursing degree. “Some women have expressed an interest in learning first aid. I’m thinking that I could teach a course on it.”

Dan frowned at her. “You should teach them a more practical skill. Like sewing. Or cooking. Or taking care of babies.”

Sharon was taken aback. Her head fell askance. “What?”

He flipped his frown onto me. “Do you really need a pass now?”

“I have to be at work in Hesperia. I’ll buy a pass if that’s okay.”

“It’s not okay. We’d throw you out if you did that.”

So he gave me change from a jar in his desk. But only three dollars’ worth.

“A day pass costs $3.50,” I said.

“I paid $3.00 for the last one I bought.”

“It’s not $3.00 now.”

This didn’t convince him.

I had to yank out my phone and show him the website for the Victor Valley Transit Authority before he would drop the last pair of quarters onto my palm.

 

 

RUNNING FOR THE bus didn’t help.

I missed it. I got to the motel an hour late.

This meant that I would have to move faster than normal if I wanted to make it back for the party.

Amoun would often have me help the cleaning women. I’d get them through their rooms faster. By cleaning the uncommon messes. Like the blood in Room 122 earlier in the week.

Uncommon messes would be in plenty after a Friday night.

In Room 206, I had to mop the walls. A mix of red wine and a substance more solid was hanging from them.

It was humbling work. For someone who had needed humbling.

Sania pushed her cart into the room.

I fumbled for words. “Hey.”

She became flushed. She scurried into the bathroom. “I’m sorry about yesterday.”

“Nothing to be sorry about.”

“It’s . . . I read a disturbing article on the Internet yesterday.”

“Yeah?”

“They broke up a trafficking ring in Adelanto the other day.”

She pushed her head through the doorway. “It’s horrible what these women go through.”

From the horrible in her eyes, she wasn’t speaking about hypothetical women.

I fumbled for more words.

But I couldn’t come up with any. Neither of us spoke.

Her flush returned. She scampered back into the bathroom.

I pretended to work. “Do you have no one you can talk to?”

“About what?” she asked with false innocence.

I didn’t answer. I lowered my mop into the bucket. I dragged it to the front door by the bathroom.

Sania was cleaning the sink. She was glued to the running water. She was fighting to hold herself together. “Don’t look at me like I’m a victim. I hate that.”

I shrank from her. “I’m sorry.”

“Don’t say that either.”

“I . . .”

“You might be wondering how I wound up in this dump.”

“It had crossed my mind. But I’ve wondered it about many people. I’ve wondered it about myself.”

She was hesitant to say more.

“You don’t have to tell me.”

“I wanted to be a doctor. I got good marks in school. I was set to go to the university in Leeds. But my parents had a different ‘set.’ They wanted me to marry this guy twice my age. ‘To keep me out of trouble,’ they said. Actually, to say ‘they wanted’ isn’t factual. I didn’t have a choice. Until I came upon an ad for au pairs on Craigslist.”

“I can guess the rest.”

“No, you can’t.”

I quieted. I must’ve had the dumbest of faces.

She fretted. “I had this behind me. I’m starting classes at Cal State next month.”

“That’s terrific.”

“But I don’t have it behind me.”

“You should try—”

“—They don’t help. These people, they’re so far from getting me. From getting what I went through.”

“I have a sister . . .”

“What is she, a counselor?”

“Not exactly. But she helps a lot of people. It’s an obsession for her. Fixing the world. Or her piece of it. You should see her house and the people she’s taken in over the years. It’s because of what she’s been through. She’s not so far from you. She’d get you.”

“I don’t . . .”

“I could call her.”

I dug into my pants pocket. For my phone.

Sania gulped. “You mean now?”

I didn’t answer. I swiped at the screen.

“I don’t have time. I have to finish my rooms before check-in.”

“Talk to her for a few minutes.”

Rudi picked up on the first ring. “What’s up?”

“You must be busy. But I have someone who I would like you to speak with. She could use some help.”

“Put her on.”

I offered the phone to Sania.

She wouldn’t take it.

I held it out. “Please.”

She grumbled.

But she snared the device. She spoke with Rudi.

I hurried from the room with my mop and bucket. I shut the door behind me.

Sania opened it. She popped her head into the hallway. “What about your phone?”

“I’ll get it from you later.”

 

 

I LEFT THE motel after my shift. I crossed Cataba Road to the bus stop.

Sania yelled my name.

She raced to me. With my phone in her hand and the happy back on her face.

I felt happy with her. Beyond what I would’ve expected that I could have felt.

We hugged. For a while we did.

“Your sister is amazing,” she said.

“Believe me, I know.”

We broke our embrace. She handed me the phone. “It was like talking to my older sister. Better. Rudi and I are doing a Skype call tomorrow morning with someone she knows. Nadia. She, she’s someone like me. She runs a support group in LA. Rudi says that I can participate remotely.”

“It’s gonna work out for you.”

“How can I thank you?”

“Get better. It could inspire me to do the same.”

“One day I could be the one giving help.”

“Life is a series of ever-renewing cycles.”

“That sounds kind of Sufi.”

“Kind of Sufi. Kind of Kabbalah. It’s much the same. We’re much the same.”

I went off.

“She’s worried about you,” Sania called out. “Your sister.”

“She’s not the only one.”

“I told her that I’d keep an eye on you. Or two.”

“I appreciate that.”

 

 

I GOT CURIOUS about the trafficking ring Sania had mentioned. I searched for the story on the bus. I discovered a couple on local news sites.

The police had arrested two men in Old Adelanto, a miserable place the next town over that made Victorville Paradise in comparison.

They believed that the men had an accomplice. But the only other detail that stuck out was that both men had served in the army together.

 

 

THE DAY WAS slipping past the alley.

I came through the shelter gates. Upon the birthday decorations that Nichelle had gotten her mittens on. I came upon Nichelle too.

She rambled to me. Flashes of panic and frustration were on her face. “Have you seen Josh?”

I began to answer.

But Mateo called my name. He pulled me from her.

He tipped toward the office. “You’ve got a letter.”

Nichelle flung herself at me, her panic and frustration higher. “Have you seen Josh?”

“No.”

“There’s no Josh. So there’s no cake. And there’s no birthday party without a cake. Lynnette and her mom will be here in an hour.”

“We have time to buy one.”

Mateo crossed his arms. He dipped his head. “I wouldn’t do that.”

Nichelle pointed down the road. “Stater Brothers has a sheet cake for twenty bucks. That includes decorating it.”

“We can ask people to chip in,” I said.

Mateo dipped his head farther. “I wouldn’t do that.”

“They can’t throw everyone out.”

“They can’t?”

Nichelle and I ignored him.

We went through the courtyard. We collected money in super stealth mode.

I was surprised that most gave. Some gave all the change in their pockets. All many had. For a girl most couldn’t have described.

Sharon also gave, having figured out what we were up to. She gave, disregarding how it could’ve gotten her fired.

Mateo gave as well. In spite of himself.

I threw in my five dollars. I hoped that it would make me less paranoid about losing it.

We raised in excess of what we needed. Enough for some trimmings.

Héctor sped me to the supermarket in his car.

We got the cake and had it decorated. We returned with it. Along with a box of candles. Snacks and soda. A teddy bear.

Nichelle grabbed the cake from me. She lobbed ten candles onto it.

“They’re coming!” someone shouted from the gates.

Nichelle lit the candles. Another someone turned off the lights.

They tossed them on as Lynnette and her mother entered. Doing so to a chorus of “Happy Birthday to You.”

None of us would forget the girl’s pear-shaped eyes. Or a face that was lighting up the night.

Her mother was in tears. So was Nichelle.

Lynnette blew out the candles.

The fervor was leaping from her. She would’ve smashed through a ceiling if one had been over her.

On that night, she was a normal ten-year-old. A normal girl celebrating a birthday, made to feel that she was special and mattered and loved.

We, too, were normal people at a birthday party. The party was as much for us as for Lynnette. That could’ve been why everyone gave.

We needed it as much as she did. If not more.

 

 

JOSH SHOWED UP. Without a cake.

But he was surprised to see a cake. Lynnette and her mother hadn’t been so surprised.

Nichelle stormed toward him, her fists raised. “Where were you?”

“I got stuck on the 395. A police action.”

This caught my notice. The 395 went through Old Adelanto.

But what caught my notice more was recalling that Josh had been in the army. Like the two men the cops had arrested.

Could this have been other than a coincidence?

 

 

FOR THE SECOND night in a row, I got ahead of Gerry in the check-in line. Well ahead of him.

Josh handed me the letter that had been waiting for me on the desk.

But he wouldn’t let it go. “How did you guys get the cake?”

I stammered. I sought an answer while hanging over the words on the desk’s nameplate. JOSHUA MAXWELL. “I, I had some money on an old EBT card. We can use that, right?”

He said nothing. But he did release the envelope.

I started from the office.

“I’m gonna be watching you,” he called out.

“Quis custodiet ipsos custodes?” I shot back. In my Jersey-accented Latin.

“What’s that?”

“It’s from a poem by Juvenal.”

What I didn’t tell him was what it meant.

Who watches the watchers?

Only one person could.

 

 

I STRUTTED INTO the courtyard with Yon’s letter.

The party was tailing off. Lynnette and other kids were getting their last licks in on the banana split sundaes that Rich had made for them. From ice cream that he had conjured.

I took in the beauty of this as I brought Yon’s letter to the corner booth. I called Rudi.

Unlike that morning and the previous nights, it rang several times.

These entries would include difficult ones.

On the fifth ring, she picked up.

We didn’t make small talk.

I opened the envelope. I continued the story.

 

 

YON STRUGGLED TO accept what was happening. That he was against a window in his mother’s bedroom. With Rudi wrapped around him and his mother glaring at them both.

The falling sun was projecting onto the woman. It drenched her in light and shade. It made her glare sharper.

Yon’s reality bull-rushed him.

Releasing his arms from Rudi, he swung himself toward the woman.

Rudi crashed to the floor. A thump came with it.

“Sorry about this, Mom,” he mumbled while Rudi jumped to her feet.

Mrs. Levy stepped up her glare. “It’s fortunate for the three of us that I didn’t arrive about ten minutes later.”

“It’s not like that, Mom. Really.”

Yon and Rudi trudged toward his mother. They could’ve been passing through a swamp.

Closer the two got to the woman and her disgust. Of Rudi and her hair and her clothes and her makeup. She didn’t bother to hide it.

The pair stopped in front of her.

She sneered at them both. She checked them out. From all angles.

Her glare got stronger. “Did you come from the shower?”

“It’s not like that, Mom,” Yon screeched below his breath. “The rain caught us. Sorta.”

The woman clenched her teeth. “Aren’t you going to introduce me?”

“This is Rudi, my . . .”

“I’m his friend,” Rudi blurted out. “Sorta.”

Mrs. Levy focused her ire on Yon. “Will your friend be staying for dinner?”

“Yes,” he said at the same time Rudi said, “No.”

The glare shifted to her.

Mrs. Levy’s jaw returned to its clenched position. The disgust returned to her face. “Without question, stay for dinner, Rudi. I’m dying to learn more about you.”

 

 

RUDI AND YON were sprawling out on the living room couch. Rudi was extra overanxious. “She hates me. And if I were her, I’d hate me too.”

“It’s that you remind her of someone. Someone close.”

“Wait till she learns about my record. And the drugs I’ve done. Which is just about every one, by the way.”

“I don’t care about the past. I . . . I don’t care about the future either. What I care about is now.”

“Some of us can’t separate our past from our now. Or our future.”

“Listen—”

“—And she’ll be overjoyed when she learns about the kid who got killed.”

“I don’t care.”

“Look, we had . . . whatever the hell that was.”

Yon grinned. “It was pretty awesome.”

Rudi grinned back. She didn’t want to. But she did.

She shook it off. “Take me home.”

“After dinner.”

“Mariana said that she lives near you.”

“Down the block.”

Rudi jumped to her feet. “Point me in her direction.”

“Stay. Please stay.”

She whined. But she fell back onto the couch.

Yon lifted a remote off the coffee table. “TV?”

Rudi crossed her arms. She screwed up her face. “I hate TV.”

Yon switched it on. He flipped through the channels.

Pausing at football highlights, he gave Rudi a questioning glance.

She crinkled her nose. “I hate football. And any pursuit that involves large groups of people who act alike. Which I define as ‘any group larger than one.’”

Yon went back to the television. He went back to his flipping.

Coming to MTV and Van Halen’s “Pretty Woman” video, he gave Rudi another glance.

She didn’t give it any due.

Yon returned to his flipping.

He landed on a UHF station.

A peculiar man in a checkered suit and fedora was telling a more peculiar puppet that Squeeze would be playing later in the show.

Rudi uncrossed her arms. Her face became agog. “I hate all TV but this.”

Yon didn’t budge from the screen. “What’s that?”

“You’re telling me that you watch Uncle Floyd?”

She didn’t wait for a response. She put her eyes on the irreverent comedy show, whose existence few were aware of. I’m not sure Floyd was. Or the puppet.

The two laughed. They laughed from out of nowhere. At the same dumb joke.

Their widened eyes flew toward each other.

Rudi shook this off as well. “It means nothing.”

“Dinner’s ready!” Mrs. Levy called out.

 

 

RUDI AND YON took seats across from Mrs. Levy. By a table setting that Rudi slanted her head at. “Why do I have four plates?”

Yon glowered at his mother. “We usually don’t eat so formally.”

The woman glowed back at him. “It’s a nice change.”

Yon’s glower endured. It had clicked how his mother was wanting Rudi to feel out of place, and how this was working.

He arched toward Rudi. “It’s real simple. The first plate is the charger—”

“—The what?”

“It’s for show.”

“Uh-huh.”

“Then come the dinner plate, the salad plate, and the soup bowl. The plate off to the side is for bread.”

Vexation washed over Rudi. “Why can’t I use the same plate? Is it some kind of crime?”

Mrs. Levy laughed. It dripped with condescension. “You eat any way you want, my dear.”

Rudi bristled.

The kitchen doors opened.

Elizabeth swept into the room. She served a tray of piping-hot parsley soup.

This serving made Rudi uncomfortable. As uncomfortable as she had been outside the house.

She hid it under another quip. “Now I know where they got the idea for Upstairs, Downstairs.”

Elizabeth and Yon smiled. But it wasn’t so funny to Mrs. Levy. She scowled. At no one in particular. “Thank you, Elizabeth.”

The smile flew from Elizabeth. She shuffled off with her silver tray.

Rudi’s eyes fell upon her soup. They stayed there.

Mrs. Levy enjoyed the girl’s fog. “I should warn you, Rudi. My son has been on a fad of late. That I’ve regrettably indulged.”

Rudi lifted herself from the bowl. “What do you mean?”

“He doesn’t eat meat.”

Yon picked up his spoon. “Rudi is a vegetarian too.”

“How convenient,” the woman yelped.

Rudi did her best to hide her snarl. But it showed itself. “I’ve been a vegetarian since I was fourteen.”

“I’m sure you have, dear.”

Rudi stewed more.

But with Yon sipping his soup, she calmed. She took her own sip.

She jumped in her seat. “This is good. It sure beats stale fries.”

The comment and the inferences from it unsettled Yon. He nudged himself toward Rudi.

She took her next sip. This larger. She slurped it too. She drew the scrutiny of Mrs. Levy.

The woman forced her focus onto her son. She strained to keep herself calm. “Tell me, Yon, wherever did you two meet?”

“Rudi is in my math class.”

The woman gaped at him. “Aren’t you taking AP Calculus?”

“Rudi is the smartest one there. She might be the smartest person in school.”

Rudi snorted. “That’s not true.”

“What did you get on your SAT?”

“I forget.”

“I bet. I bet it was perfect.”

“You’d lose that bet. They cheated me out of a few points.”

Mrs. Levy’s face let on how much she didn’t want this to win her over. But it let her down.

Yon couldn’t hold back his excitement. “You should’ve been there when she solved this partial fraction decomposition integration. She solved it like addition. Even Mrs. Elkind was impressed. And that was before Rudi wrote ‘Q.E.D.’ beside it.”

Mrs. Levy’s face lit up. “Quite easily done.”

“Actually,” Rudi countered, “it means—”

“—Quod erat demonstrandum.”

Yon sat back in his chair. “My mom was Phi Beta Kappa at Radcliffe. The other night, she mentioned that she saw E. E. Cummings at school.”

Rudi raised her brows. “Really?”

Mrs. Levy shook her head. “That was ages ago, Yon.”

Yon’s eyes fell upon his mother. “He was giving what he called a ‘nonlecture’ at Harvard. As he walked to the lectern, she and some other girls hopped up and recited . . . what was the poem of his you recited?”

The woman sighed. “‘Buffalo Bill ’s.’”

“And what did he do?”

She sighed for a second time. “He stopped in stride as we finished. Then he took out his pocket square and waved it at us. I thought he would cry. The world for decades had forgotten him. He had to borrow money from his mother to publish his poetry. And here were these silly coeds treating him like a rock star.”

“Wow” was the bounds of what Rudi could say.

Yon couldn’t hold back his excitement any more than before. “Rudi wrote a poem in his style for one of her classes.”

Rudi spiraled toward Yon. She hoisted her fist. “Don’t you dare recite it.”

Mrs. Levy put down her spoon. She clasped her hands. “Well, Rudi, you certainly are a Renaissance woman. Calculus and modernist poetry. If only the school offered a beauty class.”

Rudi had no retort. Her sole reaction was to withdraw. Into herself.

“Mom,” Yon snarled.

The woman brushed this aside. Him too. She continued with Rudi. “Do you actually pay someone to do that to your hair?”

Yon got flustered. “Didn’t you tell me that you were a beatnik?”

“That was completely different.”

“How? Are you saying that people didn’t prejudge you? That they didn’t look down on you because of how you dressed and acted?”

Mrs. Levy rose in her chair. Along with her wrists. “We had beliefs and ideals! We weren’t painting our faces and running around like idiots!”

Rudi slammed her forearms onto the table. “That’s not what we’re doing! We’re showing who we are. How we feel. And either you can’t get this or can’t handle it. And probably both.”

The woman had no comeback.

She changed course. “I didn’t catch your last name, Rudi.”

“Weiss,” Rudi snapped.

Mrs. Levy gaped at her. She gaped harder and longer than she had at her son. “You’re Jewish?”

“Wwwhat?” Rudi stammered, her gape deeper than the woman’s. “‘Weiss’ is German, isn’t it? It means ‘white.’”

“It’s also a common Jewish name.”

“I have heard you say ‘oy vey,’” Yon interjected. “More than once.”

Rudi whipped herself to him. “That’s Jewish?”

Mrs. Levy was quick to correct her. “It’s Yiddish.”

“You think I could be Jewish?” Rudi asked Yon, her voice breaking and shock in her eyes.

His mother twisted her lips. “Are you ashamed of it?”

Rudi withdrew from them both. Her horror increased. “Not in the way you believe.”

Silence followed. A good amount of it. It went on with them going back to their soups.

Mrs. Levy shattered it. She stretched her head in her son’s way. “You’ll never guess who I ran into this afternoon at the Short Hills Mall . . . Darlene. So beautiful. So . . .”

The woman assayed Rudi. From each side and view. “So not like any other.”

Rudi lowered her eyes and her spoon. She crossed her arms and legs.

Yon would’ve never imagined that Rudi would’ve been self-conscious of her looks. Or insecure about them. But his mother’s current line of attack was hurting her more than the others.

Her confidence was sinking. It was dropping her defenses.

He dropped his head at his mother. “That’s enough.”

“I’m telling it as it is. I bet Rudi can tell too.”

“Like I told you before, I’m sorry about what happened this afternoon. But that doesn’t give you the right to treat Rudi like this. I won’t have it.”

“Darlene was telling me that she got into Penn. Swarthmore too. Of course, she’s waiting for you to decide.”

“She shouldn’t.”

“And why is that?”

“Darlene’s gorgeous. No doubt about that. She’s smart too. She can be funny at times. But I can barely stand the sound of her voice. I cringe when she touches me.”

“Well, you weren’t cringing this afternoon.”

Yon was about to reply. But Rudi shot up her head. She did so while scrambling to keep her emotions from flying out with it. “Listen, Mrs. Levy, despite what you saw earlier, and despite what Yon thinks he feels, nothing serious is going on between us.”

Yon leaned over the table. “Nothing but Glenn Miller.”

“Glenn Miller?” Mrs. Levy gasped.

“But that’s nothing serious. It’s not as if we feel and want the same. Or that we dream the same. Or that we laugh at the same dumb jokes. Or that any incredible happens when I take her hand.”

To disprove this, to both his mother and Rudi, he placed his hand on hers.

She trembled. As she had in the bedroom.

Fighting this, she yanked her hand away. She passed her no-return point. “Next week, Yon, next week you’ll move on to someone new.”

“That’s not true.”

“I’m some exotic flavor you’ve never tasted.”

It was Yon’s turn to slam his arms onto the table. “That’s not true!”

“Look at me,” she babbled, far from him and getting farther. “Really look. Not at my hair. Or at my clothes. But at who I am. I’m not gonna end up with some preppy football player Republican. Nor am I gonna end up at Penn. Or Swarthmore. We both know that I’m not gonna end up anywhere!”

Rudi jumped to her feet.

Holding back her tears, she and her muddle swarmed from the room.

Yon jumped to his own feet.

“Let her go,” his mother whispered.

“Shut up!” he barked. He sped after Rudi.

“She’s not for you!”

“Her eyelids’ flutter tells me you’re wrong!”

Yon got to the foyer.

Rudi was slinging her seabag over her shoulder. Her tears were edging closer to an escape.

She threw open the front door. She dashed off.

He went with her. He flew outside at the same moment she stomped onto the street. “Rudi!”

“Leave me alone!” she shrieked, her voice past broken and her stomps slowing. “I was doing just fine until I met you. And now . . .”

“Rudi?”

She wrenched herself toward him. “What can you possibly . . .”

“It’s the other way around.”

“What?”

“Yeah, I’m good-looking . . . and rich. I made All-State last year. I could go to any college I want. But all I see is how slight I am. Slighter than paper.”

He inched forward. “If you see more, more could be.”

Rudi lingered. With a twinge of hope in her eyes.

Neither lasted.

She spirited off into the night.

Yon couldn’t move. Or could say the words that he wanted to say.

The same ones that he had wanted to say to her by the waterfall.

 

 

I DIDN’T HAVE to ask Rudi what happened after she left Yon’s house.

From the pause on the line after I finished the entry, she must’ve expected the question. She began telling me.

 

 

RUDI WANDERED THROUGH Newstead.

She was getting nowhere but upset. At herself.

If sex had been clouding her mind, she could’ve written off what she was feeling.

But it wasn’t. It wasn’t Yon either. It was his hand and how she wanted to hold it.

No reasoning could account for this. No means could rationalize it.

Rudi retreated to where she fled when troubled. Into her music.

However, the one negative aspect of punk rock is that it offers no love songs. None. Few of its songs have broached the subject.

But she had one with her. “Love Und Romance” by the Slits, the most cynical song ever recorded about the feelings swirling around her.

This was what she wanted to hear. Or so she told herself.

She snatched the tape.

Throwing it into her Walkman, she got to the song. She cranked the volume to its highest.

Over it she screamed with Ari Up. She screamed with her for what could’ve been hours, rewinding the song each time it had ended. She rewound it so many times that she could press the rewind button to the song’s beginning in a single try.

But no matter how she screamed, no matter how many times she played the song, she couldn’t regain her old self.

This was when it crystallized that she had gotten herself cooked. That she was “head over heels in love”—regardless of how stupid it sounded—and all she could do about it was cry.

Which hadn’t happened in so many years that she couldn’t remember when it had last.

What was crazier was that she couldn’t figure out why she was crying. Or whether she was crying because she was happy or sad.

No longer seeing straight, she halted in the road. She wiped her eyes.

A rickety woman was lugging herself and a rickety dog across the intersection ahead.

Rudi staggered toward them.

The woman took a full step back. For the obvious reason.

But Rudi’s tears must’ve ushered in another instinct. A much less malevolent one. “Are you okay, sweetheart?”

“I’m lost,” Rudi said, suspecting the words had infinite meanings.

“Where do you want to go?”

“A friend of mine lives nearby. Her name is Mariana Gonzales.”

“Oh, I know the Gonzaleses. What a nice family. They live on Overhill Road over there. You can’t miss it. It’s a yellow house with a lovely Nativity scene in the yard.”

“Thank you!” Rudi blubbered. She lunged to hug the woman.

But she stopped herself and sprinted off, I bet with the woman seeking sense from the enigma that had been in front of her.

An enigma wrapped inside a paradox.

 

 

RUDI RANG THE doorbell for a third time.

Lights were on in the house. But was anyone home?

The door cracked open.

Mariana stood in the threshold.

Her jaw was slack. But nothing was exiting it.

Rudi begged. “Can I come in?”

Mariana twirled around.

A faint voice was coming from inside the kitchen.

Returning to Rudi, she took her arm. “You’re lucky my dad’s on the phone. Quick.”

This was how they snuck up the stairs. Into Mariana’s bedroom.

Mariana locked the door.

Grabbing a flashlight from a bureau drawer, she switched it on. In the same motion, she switched off the room’s light. She collapsed onto the floor with Rudi, who told her what had happened since her happening with Yon outside the school. She only left out the sordid parts.

Mariana didn’t restrain her glee. “He’s in love with you. You should be psyched. Pretty much every girl in school is crazy about him.”

Rudi shook her body. She wormed away. “Not me.”

“‘The lady doth protest too much, methinks.’”

Rudi slipped a peek at Mariana. “Nobody gets that quote right. You must love Shakespeare.”

“I actually wrote a modern teenage version of All’s Well That Ends Well for the Drama Club last spring.”

“All’s Well That Ends Well? Isn’t that the one where the woman tricks her husband into sleeping with her by pretending to be someone else?”

“I kinda glossed over that part when I described it to my dad.”

“And you wrote a modern version of it? That’s awesome.”

“The teacher said that I should become a playwright. But it’s silly.”

“Why?”

“How many women playwrights have you read?”

“Lillian Hellman. Lorraine Hansberry. Sophie Treadwell. They are among the many.”

“I’ve never heard of those people.”

“Whose fault is that?”

Mariana slapped her thigh. “Don’t change the subject.”

“There is no subject.”

“There is from where I’m sitting.”

“You need glasses.”

“It’s you who needs them.”

Rudi tossed up her hands. “How could I be in love with him? He’s a Republican!”

Mariana didn’t restrain her glee any more than she had before. “I’m no expert, but I don’t suppose you get to choose.”

“You say that you’re no expert, but you sure sound as if you’re speaking from experience.”

Mariana blushed.

“I get that your dad is strict. But you must be hot for someone.”

Mariana blushed more. But she wouldn’t add to it.

Rudi’s resolve rose. “Come on, spill it.”

Mariana kept up her reluctance.

Rudi kept up her prodding.

Mariana relented. “There is this someone.”

“Yeah?”

“We notice each other and stuff.”

“What’s his name?”

Mariana’s reluctance returned.

So did Rudi’s prodding.

Mariana gave in. “Terry.”

“Terry? Is that the guy . . .”

“Terry . . . Louise.”

Quiet ensued. A weighty load of it.

Mariana shut off the flashlight.

“Why did you turn off the light?” Rudi asked.

“You must hate me.”

“Why would I hate you?”

“Because I’m a . . .”

Rudi seized the flashlight. She shined it onto her face. “I think it’s cool.”

“You do?”

“It takes courage. Not to be different. But to express it.”

“That’s my problem.”

“What is?”

“I don’t have courage.”

“Have you talked to this, this Terry Louise?”

“God, no.”

“Why not?”

“My dad would kill me. He’d kill me if I talked to boys.”

“Are you gonna live your life for your dad? Are you gonna marry some guy and have babies just to make him happy?”

Mariana said nothing.

Rudi thrashed about. Both her arms and her torso. “‘To thine own self be true!’”

Mariana got frantic. “Sshhh! My dad would freak out if he caught you here.”

“Tomorrow, you’re gonna march up to Terry. Whether it’s in the hallway or wherever it is you two do your noticing. And you’re gonna say hello.”

“No way.”

“Either you talk to her or I won’t talk to you. I won’t be friends with a coward.”

 

 

A GLINT OF glimmer slunk through the looming squall. In what was passing for the morning outside Yon’s windows.

He was sitting on his bed. As he had been for hours. His head was on his knees. His arms were around his legs.

Leave me alone!

For the jillionth time, Rudi’s words flew at him. The ones that had given him another out. One better than before.

To avoid his fate—to fulfill the replay of his life—he only had to do what she wanted.

Stay away from her.

But he had less success at persuading himself to do this than he had when he tried it last. Or on any other occasion.

He got off his bed. Without showering, he tossed on a white Gap tee and some 501s. He slipped into a pair of Docksides and his football jacket.

Leaving his house, he sped on his bike in the spitting rain. To Rudi’s.

He didn’t bother with the old burgundy pickup truck. He parked by her house. He waited.

Nobody left. Nor were signs that anyone was inside. The blue sedan wasn’t anywhere.

More troubling, Yon recalled the Porsche that had been outside his house the day before. As well as a pair of words more troubling than that.

Caveat Amor.

Had the fine print on the replay card meant Beware Love? Or had it meant Let Love Beware?

Yon checked his watch. School was about to begin.

He zipped to Columbia in the increasing rain.

At the red light at the corner of Valley and Parker, he pivoted toward the school.

Rudi and Mariana were starting through the parking lot. Rudi wasn’t wearing her coat. She had on the same clothes that she had on the day before.

Deke’s Porsche shot into the school’s lot from Parker. It circled around. It ground to a stop beside the two girls.

They stopped with it.

Its window rolled down.

Rudi blanched and reeled. So did Mariana. Deke couldn’t have knocked them back farther if he had run into them.

The light turned green.

But Yon didn’t drive down Parker. He made a U-turn in front of a bus.

It came to a jolting stop.

Yon jumped the curb. He rode over the grass. To the lot.

Yet he idled well behind the Porsche.

His wanting to be brave was not enough. He couldn’t make himself be it.

Rudi’s head fell. Like a boulder into a chasm.

She crept alongside the car. To the passenger door. She slithered inside it. Despite the pleas of Mariana and the writhing of her arms.

The Porsche bolted off. From the lot and down Parker.

Yon didn’t follow it. He was more afraid than ever.

Mariana caught sight of him. Her frenzy jacked up. “Aren’t you going after her?”

He said nothing.

Her face reddened. “You stupid idiot! She only went with him because he was threatening you!”

Yon strove for these words to have meaning. To get him to move.

He clenched his eyes shut. He willed himself onward.

But he couldn’t go anywhere.

You get one chance to fall in love for the first time. Don’t let it get by you.

The words of his father couldn’t make him stir either.

Footsteps opened his eyes.

Owen was rushing at him from the bus stop.

He was panting for breath. He had a furor that said if she were my girl, I would’ve already saved her. He grabbed Yon’s handlebar. “If you won’t go after her, I will.”

This got Yon going. He gunned his bike. He rode off.

Following a path similar to the one that he had followed Deke on before, he tailed the Porsche. To Springfield Avenue.

From there, Deke went not toward Maplewood. He went the opposite way. He reached the Garden State Parkway.

He took it south to Route 22. A highway that bisected the state’s width.

On the border of Newark, Deke cornered onto the lot of a seedy motel. He parked by a room in the pouring rain.

Yon pulled up nearby.

Deke leapt from the Porsche. He strutted to the other side.

Rudi was getting out with less urgency. With a face that was fighting to project apathy and failing at it.

Snickering, Deke grabbed her arm. He tugged her out. He shut the door. He dragged her away.

Yon got off his bike.

But he didn’t speak. It was his last chance not to do so. He was sure of it.

The two made it to the room. Deke shoved the key into the lock.

Yon shook everywhere. “Rudi!”

She and Deke spun toward him.

“What are you doing here?” she hollered, her face tinted with equal amounts of anger and fear.

“What are you?” he hollered back.

Deke pounced toward Yon.

But Rudi stopped him with her arm. She pleaded with him beneath her breath, “You promised.”

Yon whined. “What’s going on?”

Rudi raged at him, her fright higher than before. “How dumb can you be? I’m walking into a motel room with another guy. Can’t you see that I’m trash?”

Yon wanted to believe this. But he couldn’t make himself do so. “That’s not ‘the what i see’!”

“Go home!”

He wouldn’t.

Deke opened his jacket. He showed off the gun in his waistband. “Listen to her, man. Unless you wanna end up like that other kike.”

Panicked, Rudi collared Deke. She hauled him off. “Let’s just do this.”

“Sure thing, babe.”

The two made it back to the room. Deke unlocked and opened the door. They entered.

Yon stepped ahead. “I want you to be my girl!”

Deke dallied. A few paces into the room.

But Rudi puttered on as if she didn’t hear. Or care.

Yon wouldn’t give up.

Throwing out his chest, he rose to his toes. He shouted with all his might, “I want you to be my girl!”

Deke came back to the door. He cranked his head down the highway. “Take off while you can.”

He didn’t wait for a reply. He slammed the door. He locked it.

Yon was frozen. He was simmering in his scare.

Take off while you can.

The words sounded familiar to Yon. He supposed that Deke had said them.

But it had been someone other.

His head churned.

Behind him to his right was the blue sedan.

Inside it was the FBI agent. He was sneering at Yon through the open window.

A sound sent Yon back toward the room. A lamp had crashed to the floor.

“You’re not getting off that easy!” Deke yelled. “You can’t believe that I’d let you testify!”

“I’m not!” Rudi yelled back.

“That’s right, you’re not!”

The two fought. Someone hit the floor.

“You must’ve forgotten who taught you!” Deke roared. “I taught you it all! I made you a man—better than a man! And what do I get? You only wanna screw Jews!”

Yon leapt at the door. But he didn’t leap farther. “You can’t stop him. He’d kill you.”

The fighting resumed.

Rudi’s toughness left her.

In its place was a frightened child. The one who had been hiding under her toughness, desperate for a way out.

Yon returned to the sedan. “Aren’t you gonna help her?”

The FBI man added to his sneer. His contempt had runneth over. “In good time, young man. In good time.”

“What are you waiting for?”

“Backup. You can’t expect me to confront a dangerous armed suspect without backup, can you?”

It became clear to Yon. As clear as the makeup on Rudi’s face. It was the FBI who had told Deke where Rudi was and what she would do.

They had no intent of letting her testify.

She was an addict with a record. She was unreliable.

But the man in the car, he would be the perfect witness to murder. The perfect means toward a conviction.

That was all that mattered.

Rudi called out for help.

Yon drifted toward her. As a truth drifted toward him.

He didn’t have a choice in what he did and didn’t and never had. Free will was nothing but a joke. If given a jillion replays of his life, he would make the same choice each time.

He’d choose her.

This sent him hurling at the door.

He was seconds from smashing into it.

 

 

SILENCE WAS EVERYWHERE.

It was on the phone line. In the courtyard. It was in the alley and on Amargosa Road past it.

Sound had cut off.

A moth glided at me. It floated below the flickering light above me.

I couldn’t come up with words to say to Rudi. I could tell that she was crying. I didn’t need to hear it.

“Call me tomorrow,” she slurred, her voice less than a whisper.

“Tomorrow’s Sunday. No mail.”

“Call me anyway.”

She was ready to hang up. But I couldn’t let her go. “I never told you how I came into this story.”

“You never did.”

 

 

I WOKE TO another me. A me who was both different and the same.

It happened before dawn. With the sole source of light coming from the streetlamp over my room.

My grandpa had screamed.

This wasn’t unusual.

He would scream at this hour. From the hell that had followed him from the pits of Verdun and from a worse hell in the war that came after it.

But this scream hadn’t come from my grandparents’ bedroom. The two were arguing in the kitchen below my room.

I listened to them, not aware of how this would unhinge my life. I grasped that they were yelling in German.

This wasn’t usual.

They spoke English most often while often breaking into Czech, a language associated with wonderful memories for them both. German, though, was associated only with terror, despite it being their native tongue.

It was a terror that hung over our family. It waited for the chance to swoop down and take a piece of it.

The exception the two had for speaking the language—apart from greetings and the like—was when they didn’t want me to get what they were saying.

That didn’t stop me from picking up a few words that Grandma had said. “She, she’s a Nazi. Like her . . .”

Grandpa didn’t speak. Stillness prevailed.

It faded. The kitchen door opened. The screen door beyond it squeaked forward.

Grandpa’s faltering steps shuffled to the house’s side.

He raised and lowered the garbage can’s metal lid.

The echo of it emptied into the skies. Joined by his shuffling back into the house.

I was drowning in curiosity.

So I threw on some clothes. I stumbled from my room. I snuck down the stairs and out the front door.

As the sun skulked across our neighborhood, spreading a shimmer across the lawns and the road, I made my way to the garbage can.

I lifted the lid.

A crumpled page from a newspaper was on top.

I pulled it out. I uncrumpled it.

It was from the Star-Ledger. The headline read: “Drug Dealer Shot Dead in Newark Motel Room.”

Under the headline were the mugshots of two people. The first was the dead man. The other was a punked-out teenage girl who otherwise was the same as me.

I was face-to-face with a funhouse mirror. I wasn’t a normal teenager in the early 1980s. Not with my wild, curly brown hair and my scruffy face.

But I was the cover of Tiger Beat compared to her.

Coldness came over me. I focused on the story.

Not able to make out the text, I went back into the house. I climbed the stairs to my bed. To an article that hypnotized me.

What grabbed me right off was that the police hadn’t hammered out the details of what had happened. What they had learned was that an unnamed minor had broken into the motel room where Deke Cox and Gertrud Weiss had been fighting. That Deke had attacked the boy, who lost so much blood that the doctors couldn’t say if he would make it.

But what grabbed me harder and around the throat was that Deke and Gertrud were skinheads.

Both had criminal records and ties to neo-Nazis. Both were involved in the killing of a Jewish Rutgers student that fall.

This couldn’t sink in. That I could have a twin living within driving distance of me was incredible enough. But that she was a skinhead and a murderer of Jews was an infinity past this.

I had spent years forgetting a near-identical evil. My instinct was to rip the paper apart. To forget further.

Though my curiosity continued. So did my drowning in it.

They were sufficient to get me to reread the article. I reread it and reread it.

I made out that what it hadn’t said could’ve been as important as what it had.

No mention was in it of who had killed Deke. Or what had happened to the girl who had to have been my sister. The lone clue that they gave was that she and the boy went to a high school in the area.

This wouldn’t prove to be much of a clue.

Some suburban families build pools. Others build gardens. Or game rooms. Mine built a library.

It was mammoth. It had interconnected rooms and more books than in my high school library. It had some rare books as well. Such as a French edition of Gogol’s Dead Souls that a young Marc Chagall had illustrated, whose imagery would haunt many of my childhood nights. My adult nights too.

The library included a bookcase of New Jersey telephone directories. I pulled out the one for Essex County and those for the adjoining ones.

Browsing through them, I discovered that there were dozens of high schools within an hour’s drive from the motel, spread everywhere. Locating my sister appeared an impossible task.

Until I recollected that I was the grandson of a man who had made quests like these trivial.

I left the library. I pushed myself through the kitchen doors.

My grandparents were sitting beside a white porcelain table that had been there as far back as my memories went. The two weren’t making an effort to eat their breakfast and were somewhere. But not there.

I stood before them. “Hey.”

Neither spoke. Neither reacted.

I tipped my head at Grandpa. “Remember how I was telling you that I’m taking a film class at school?”

“Cože?” he mumbled, stuck in his stupor.

He followed this by gulping down an entire cup of piping hot coffee.

For years, my grandpa had been suffering from a form of dementia. A form difficult to diagnose. Let alone treat.

It fluctuated.

Through stretches, he would be lucid. During others, he’d be in another place and time. His past and present were dueling it out in his head.

More frustrating were periods like this. You couldn’t say which was winning.

I hoped for the best. “For my class project, I’m gonna make a documentary on New Jersey. I’m gonna need your car in the afternoons for a while.”

He slid toward me in his seat. “Jistě. Surely. Just remember to bring it home on Fridays before service.”

“Yes, Grandpa.”

“It would not hurt if you joined us.”

“Yes, Grandpa.”

“The rabbi is forever asking about you. The cantor too. It is time.”

I slithered off, feeling guilty about lying to him.

But I justified it by convincing myself that he would’ve done the same.

 

 

EACH WEEKDAY AFTERNOON, I cut last class.

I would drive to multiple high schools. I’d show my sister’s picture from the newspaper to students and teachers. To anyone I’d pass.

None recognized her. They didn’t recognize her from TV. Despite the shooting having been a story for days on the local news.

I couldn’t count the number of times that I gave up. But it was sizable.

It wasn’t only the search’s futility weighing on me. But my doubts as to what I would say to my sister if I did locate her.

Greater than that, I feared how it would dredge up the nightmare that had derailed me. That could derail me worse than before.

Was I doing my best not to find her? So I would have an excuse for not facing her and the past?

I can’t answer this. But I kept getting into Grandpa’s well-rusted Matador. I kept searching.

 

 

I REACHED A dead end. I had run the gauntlet of high schools in the area and of some outside it. I was no closer to my sister than when I’d begun. I had no choice but to give up for good.

“How is that documentary of yours coming?” Grandpa asked me one night during dinner.

“Everywhere is the same.”

“What do you mean?”

“Everywhere in New Jersey is the same.”

“You are not looking well enough. Gems are everywhere in the state. Gems are in Newark. Especially in Newark.”

Grandma raised her fork. “Hillside too. Some of my favorite homes are in Hillside. Of course, they cannot compare to my old villa in Vinohrady. But nothing can.”

Grandpa agreed. “Hillside is a perfect example. Who would expect those lovely homes on the border of Newark and Elizabeth?”

“Hillside?” I said. “Isn’t that where Pingry is?”

“Exactly my point,” he pointed out while pointing at me. “You would never believe that the most renowned private school in the state would be in Hillside. But there it is.”

At that half-instant, that I hadn’t checked private schools smacked me upside my head.

I must’ve assumed that a girl like my sister must’ve gone to a public one.

 

 

THE NEXT AFTERNOON, I drove to Hillside and the leafy campus of Pingry. A place rich kids from across northern New Jersey went.

Years earlier, a chunk of my sixth-grade class went there rather than to the local junior high.

This, I believe, had more to do with the school’s racial makeup than its academics. When I parked my car, I made out a guy that I had been friends with.

He jumped into an Alfa across the lot and drove off. Both would’ve gotten lost in the snow.

As I had at the other places that I’d been, I asked everyone I passed about the girl in the picture. I was feeling less hopeful than normal.

The shaking of their heads wasn’t unexpected.

I drew out a breath. I started to leave. I swore to forget about my sister for a final time.

But nearing Grandpa’s car, I came upon three boys. I showed them the photo.

A scrawny kid, with short, frizzy sandy hair and light blue eyes and a wardrobe out of GQ, studied the image. “Isn’t she the girl who shot that drug dealer?”

“How do you know that she shot him?” I asked, having never discovered who fired the gun.

“There are rumors.”

“I guess she doesn’t go here.”

“She goes to Columbia. A friend of mine was telling me about her. He goes there.”

“I went to Columbia weeks ago. Nobody knew her.”

“It doesn’t surprise me.”

“What do you mean?”

“There are rumors.”

He and the others walked off.

My frustration flew out. “What rumors?”

“I’m not saying,” he said with smugness that made me ill.

 

 

I GOT HOME from school the following day. I swept Grandpa’s car keys off the dining room cabinet. I headed out.

This time I had a reason to believe that I was heading somewhere. Or I could hope I was.

But was it to a good place?

I dithered toward our house’s front door. I passed Grandpa.

He was asleep on the living room couch. By a rerun of Columbo on the TV. Much as he had been doing in the afternoons for years.

He had been retired since before I went to live with him and Grandma when I was a toddler. So he didn’t have a lot to do during the day when Grandma worked. She was a decade younger than him and the director at the local YM-YWHA.

Along with being alone a lot, Grandpa was running low on steam.

He didn’t have any place to use what he had.

Could this have been the cause of his stupors?

I observed him that day as I went by. I wished that he could’ve been with me. I wished that I could’ve given him purpose. I could’ve also used his help.

With an inexact anxiety, I left the house. I drove to Maplewood.

Like I had when I was there last, I parked on Valley Road by a diner called Ralph’s.

Kids streamed from the school. They meandered through the parking lot.

I flashed the photograph to all those who went by.

They shook their heads. They kept them away from the newspaper clipping. From me too.

They were avoiding both. They had been doing this when I had been there before. But only now did I pick up on it.

Not far from the school doors, I approached two girls.

The first was mousy and conservative looking. The other was lanky and redheaded.

The mousy girl slowed. Her eyes widened.

She stopped steps from me. “Are you her brother?”

“Yeah,” I said.

She took the remaining steps.

Wrapping her arms around me, she more than surprised me. “I’m Mariana. Rudi’s best friend.”

“Rudi?”

It hit me at that second. No teenage girl in America would call herself “Gertrud,” and no punk.

Mariana released her arms.

The two of us eyed each other.

I tried to corroborate the girl in the photo with the image in front of me. “You’re her best friend?”

“She didn’t tell me that she had a brother.”

“Probably no one told her either. Where is she?”

“She’s been calling me from a pay phone. But she won’t tell me where she is. I’m really worried.”

“What about the boy?”

“The boy?”

“The one who got beaten up in that motel room.”

“Yon Levy. He’s at St. Barnabas. That’s a hospital in Livingston. Off South Orange Avenue.”

“He hasn’t left the hospital?”

“I’m not sure what’s wrong with him.”

 

 

“I’VE HEARD THE rest,” Rudi said over the phone.

The moth escaped the light above me. It tore toward another in the distance.

It got to it as a car horn blared down the road, breaking the silence around me.

“You haven’t heard all of it,” I said. “Me neither.”

“It’ll have to wait till tomorrow. I have to get up early.”

“I don’t work tomorrow. I can call you earlier.”

“Call me anytime. After ten in the morning. My time.”

 

 

BECAUSE I WASN’T working the next day, I didn’t need a bus pass from Josh.

But I had no choice but to run into him when I went to bed.

He was standing by the entrance to the women’s quarters. He was leering at the women as they slept. He leered in the way that a wolf does at its prey. His eyes were sparkling with delight.

Who watches the watchers?

I didn’t sleep a lot that night.

But it wasn’t due to a snoring that was louder than normal. Nor was it because of waves of darkness and demons that never came.

With my eyes locked on the nothingness around me, I was figuring out what I should do about Josh.

I wasn’t figuring out much.
