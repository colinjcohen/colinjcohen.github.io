chapter fourteen: out of a wagnerian opera

 

 

HERMANN RAISED HIS eyes through his drowsiness. Toward the clock on the wall.

It was minutes after nine.

A disheveled Klaus was standing in the doorway. He had dressed in a haphazard manner. In a hurry. He hadn’t shaved. He was also more tired than Hermann. He was more tired than he had been the day before.

Hermann remembered the nightmare that Klaus had been having in the car. “You okay?”

Klaus grumbled. “I’m fine.”

Hermann could tell this was a lie. But he pretended otherwise. “What about our average and unimposing lieutenant?”

“Colonel Mueller says three or four men could match the description. He gave me their names. But Colonel Reidl doesn’t want you questioning them. He’s having Captain Kleiner do it.”

“What about the other dead men? Were they at the party?”

“Captain Fischer was. But not Captain Gruber.”

Hermann climbed to his feet. “This morning, the door to the Maisel Synagogue’s gate was ajar.”

“So?”

“It wasn’t like that yesterday.”

“We can check it out if you’d like.”

Hermann sifted through the police reports. “Then may we talk to the man who discovered the body at the Spanish Synagogue? Mr. Hrabal?”

Klaus stepped into the hallway. Hermann followed. The two headed down the corridor.

Hermann couldn’t control his enthusiasm. Or his guilt from it. He couldn’t dampen either. No matter how intense either got, he moved faster. He and Klaus neared the reception area and Marta’s desk.

She stood. A cup of faux coffee was in her hand. “Herr Weiss, I was about to bring this to you. Was I late this morning?”

“I doubt it.”

Marta gave him the cup. He downed it in one gulp.

She blanched. “Herr Weiss, that was scalding hot.”

“I can’t tell you how much I appreciate it.”

Gulping down scorching hot coffee would become one of Hermann’s trademarks. He drank it this way till his death. When I asked him why, he said that eight months of Theresienstadt had made him appreciate hot coffee. No temperature was too hot.

Hermann sensed that Klaus was anxious to get going. He sought a place for his cup.

Marta grabbed it. As she had done many times over the years.

Hermann and Klaus went off.

“Is there anything you want me to do today?” she called out.

Hermann turned toward her and her eager expression.

She had expressed this so many times when they had worked together. He never stopped admiring it.

“I was terribly bored yesterday.”

“You can be of help to our investigation. The Jewish community, before they left town, must’ve assigned responsibility for the upkeep of their synagogues to local individuals or companies. If you could do some research at City Hall, discover who’s responsible for the six synagogues in Josefov, I’d appreciate it. I’d want as well any details you can discover about these people or companies themselves.”

“When do you need it?”

“No rush. Accuracy is of higher importance than speed.”

Klaus and Hermann continued down the hall. They descended the stairs. They passed Hercules and Hydra in the building’s lobby.

Waiting outside was Klaus’s Mercedes. Its engine started on their sight.

They started toward it. But a voice froze them. “Klaus!”

A woman was rushing at them with a paper bag in her hand.

She was in her thirties. She was about Hermann’s height, wearing an expensive black coat and a black purse that was more expensive. She also had a flashy smile that made her look vacuous.

Beyond all this was how she was a character out of a Wagnerian opera. With curled platinum-blonde hair and light-blue eyes and skin so fair that Hermann imagined that this was the first that the sun had set upon it.

At the same time, Hermann sensed that there was more to her than the archetype she was projecting. Despite being unsure of what. So, despite feeling a visceral dislike toward her because of what she represented—and a willful dislike toward the National Socialist Women’s League pin on her coat—she intrigued him. He couldn’t budge his eyes from her.

She stopped in front of Klaus. “You left your wallet.”

Digging in her purse, she whisked out the wallet.

Klaus’s face became flush.

Thanking her with excess terseness, he grabbed the wallet. He stuffed it into his jacket.

The woman scanned Klaus’s disheveled appearance. “Are you okay?”

He grumbled. Like he had when Hermann had asked him this in the office. “I’m fine.”

“Did you have some kind of accident this morning? I heard a crash.”

“I had somewhat of a tumble. I was in a hurry. I woke late.”

“I would’ve had Kamča wake you if I knew you needed to get up early. She was up baking rohlíky.”

The woman took the opportunity to offer Klaus the paper bag. “They’re so good that you don’t need to butter them.”

“Thanks,” he wheezed without zeal. He took the bag with less.

“Do you still want to go out tonight?”

“I told you I did. Now, if you’ll excuse me . . .”

The woman didn’t respond. She was too busy gawking at Hermann.

An uncomfortable silence overtook them. Driving it was the same incongruence: of Hermann and Klaus together.

She shifted her eyes back to Klaus. “So?”

“What?”

“Aren’t you going to introduce me?”

Klaus didn’t want to do this. But he did so anyway, adding, “He, he’s an old friend of mine.”

The woman returned her eyes to Hermann. She returned her gawk. The source of it was that Hermann was as interesting to her as she was to him. She would say that he was so interesting that she couldn’t decide in which way her interest was the highest. Her single certainty was that she wanted to learn more about him. “Klaus has never mentioned you before.”

“He’s never mentioned you either.”

Klaus sneered at Hermann from behind the woman. But it didn’t last. With awkwardness, he put his hand on the woman’s shoulder. “This is my lady friend. Gertrud Gross.”

Hermann feigned a smile in her direction. “Pleased to meet you.”

“I’ve never met any of Klaus’s old friends.”

“Perhaps he doesn’t have many.”

Klaus sneered at Hermann for a second time. Stronger than before. But it didn’t last longer than it had before.

Gertrud whipped herself toward Klaus. “Of course, you’re inviting Hermann to join us tonight. He looks as if he hasn’t eaten in weeks.”

“I’m afraid he’s terribly busy.”

Gertrud was persistent. She wouldn’t give in to Klaus’s resistance. She wouldn’t give up.

Hermann tired of their arguing. He injected himself into the conversation. By agreeing to the invitation.

Klaus sneered at him for a third time. This the strongest and most serious.

Gertrud didn’t catch it. Her eyes were on Hermann. “Six o’clock or so. At U Bílého Lva. It’s near—”

“—I know where it is.”

“You can’t imagine how I’m looking forward to this.”

They weren’t empty words to Gertrud. She meant them. She doubted whether she could make it through the day.
