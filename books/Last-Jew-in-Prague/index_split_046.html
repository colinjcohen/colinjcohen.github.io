chapter forty-six: cutting to the chase

 

 

COLONEL REIDL’S MERCEDES coasted down Dvořák Quay. It hugged the winding riverbank.

Hermann and Klaus were in the Škoda blocks behind it.

Similar to the other times that Hermann had been by the river over the past days, Smetana’s “Vltava” was playing in his head. Though it was louder than before, and he heard more than the song’s melody. The entire symphonic poem was unfolding around him.

The Mercedes stopped. At the foot of what before the occupation had been Čech Bridge and was now Mendel Bridge.

Hermann pulled over too. Well in the distance.

The colonel stepped from his car. He plodded to the riverbank’s railing and the water beyond it.

What was in his thoughts, I can’t say. But I believe that he was making himself believe that all he had planned was not lost. Despite Klaus’s remark at the party and the men killed in the wake of his carrying out his plans.

He was alive. From what I’ve read about him and his military career during both wars, that was the limit to what mattered to him.

Also was the matter of actions that he had taken during this war. Such as at Lidice. Actions that he must’ve known the Allies would judge as serious war crimes after Germany’s inevitable defeat. To hide from these would take money. Lots of it. He couldn’t have harbored thoughts of changing course.

Reidl continued by the water. A barge passed him. With a heavy load on its deck.

He was traveling with it.

Hermann contemplated the meaning of this from his car. He did so while becoming mindful that his anger at Klaus had diffused. He was mindful, too, that Klaus’s had as well.

Klaus covered his swollen eye with his hand. He picked up his cap from the floor. He put it on. “What’s he doing?”

“I doubt he’s admiring the view.”

The colonel returned to the water after the barge disappeared. The quiet river was enchanting him. Along with the quieter docks beside it.

It didn’t enchant him for long.

He returned to his car. He and it sputtered down the quay.

Hermann didn’t budge.

Klaus swung his arm down the road. “What are you waiting for?”

“I don’t want the driver to get suspicious. He might already be.”

The colonel’s car left their view.

Hermann started his own car. He moved it forward.

The Mercedes’ rear reappeared.

Hermann made a left down Břehová Street.

Klaus lifted his hands. “What are you doing?”

Hermann didn’t answer. He made a quick right onto Sanytrová Street.

“I asked you a question,” Klaus said, his fury rising.

“They’re likely driving down the quay to at least Mánes Bridge Road. The driver’s other options are making a left onto Na Rejdišti Street. Or one onto Rejdiště Square. If he does either, he’ll drive into my line of view. No other possibilities exist besides stopping. And if he does that, we’ll discover them soon enough.”

Hermann sped right onto Mánes Bridge Road. He did this as Reidl’s car finished its own right up the street. It mixed in with the light traffic heading to the bridge and over it.

Behind it Hermann trailed. He trailed it a safe length back. On the other side of a trio of cars.

The Škoda reached the bridge and the water. With this, the music in Hermann’s head grew louder. It and its approaching climax were thunderous. He was certain that Karel Ančerl and his orchestra were beneath him.

The Mercedes got to the city’s other side. It glided down Klár Place.

Hermann got to the same street. He made a sharp right onto Strak Quay. He followed it with a sharp left onto U Železné Lávky.

Racing down the road, he eyed the Mercedes along the parallel street through the barrenness between them.

“Now what?” Klaus asked.

“Just watch,” Hermann said. He downshifted. He slammed on the gas.

With his foot to the floor and the music’s dissonance reaching a crescendo, Hermann spun left onto Kramář Quay. He turned right onto Klár. He zipped in front of Reidl’s car.

Hermann’s eyes bolted onto the Mercedes in his rearview mirror. “If you want to follow someone in this part of Prague without being noticed, you have to be ahead of them.”

Klaus shook his head. His eyes were dazed. “If only you weren’t Jewish, Hermann. We could use a man like you in my office.”

“Another reason to be thankful for being Jewish.”

Hermann went on tailing the Mercedes. He anticipated each move it would make as the cars wended their way into Malá Strana. As they drove across a set of twisting, ancient streets.

Both cars came to a cobblestone road called Tržiště. This was where the Mercedes diverted from Hermann’s lead. It veered right with the road. It flowed into a narrow outlet.

Hermann didn’t panic. He continued onto Břetislav Street. He turned around. He stopped by the corner.

Klaus didn’t bother asking Hermann what he was doing. He gave him a look that expressed it.

Hermann hoisted his finger toward the road. “Tržiště is a dead end up there. If Reidl is coming out, he must come back from where he came.”

But Reidl didn’t come back.

Hermann sat back in his seat. “Is there a reason he’d stop here?”

“This is not where he lives. I can tell you that much. But perhaps his mistress lives here. I know he has one. But I know nothing about her. Not her name. Or what she looks like.”

“So we could be here a long time.”

Klaus smirked. “Or maybe not.”

“You want me to take the initial watch?”

“I’ll do it.”

Hermann agreed, disregarding how Klaus was more tired than he was. “Wake me in a few hours.”

He closed his eyes. Sleep overtook him.

But that didn’t mean that he was to get any rest.
