chapter eight: an unexpected kindness

 

 

THE MERCEDES REACHED what had reverted to its state as a former capital. It approached Prague Castle.

Hermann swayed toward an edifice that was a thousand years old. It dominated the skyline. It was a fairy tale that had come alive, shining in the early afternoon sun.

Closer they got to it. Hermann could hear his father describe it in perfect detail. The palaces and churches and towers rising from everywhere. He could recall, too, his youthful daydreams about it. Which helped him forget his present situation. Along with another that he had spent months forgetting.

The castle left Hermann’s view. But he continued to forget. The Hradčany streets that he was on, like the Old Town neighborhood that he had strolled through on his first day in the city, were a snapshot of a lost time. Beyond what the Nazis could corrupt. The district impressed him more than Old Town had on his first day there. He couldn’t have imagined that he would return to it.

Hermann’s awe vanished. The car had parked by a block-long baroque building on Loreta Square called Černín Palace. Before the occupation, the building had housed the Ministry of Foreign Affairs. But now it was the city’s SS headquarters.

Hermann suppressed a sigh. He turned to Klaus, who wasn’t making the slightest effort to leave. He was off somewhere. “What are we waiting for?”

Klaus didn’t respond. He acted as if he hadn’t heard the question.

Hermann re-asked it.

“As you can imagine,” Klaus said, his eyes away and showing no emotion, “this affair has been embarrassing to us. For many reasons. No one outside of a handful of SS officers has knowledge of it.”

“And you want it to stay that way?”

Klaus said nothing.

It was an answer to Hermann. It confirmed what he had believed about his task. What would be its inevitable outcome.

The two left the car. They passed the SS guards standing outside the palace. They dragged their feet through the building’s entrance. Into a marble hallway.

They bumped into a statue of Hercules battling the Hydra. It captivated Hermann.

Klaus crossed his arms from beside him. “Is there a reason you’ve stopped?”

Hermann didn’t reply. A heavyset SS colonel had grabbed his attention.

The man was around sixty. He was marching toward them from the staircase. Alongside a wispy and smug captain half his age.

Both the colonel and his companion slowed. With reluctance.

Klaus saluted the colonel. He conveyed a reverence that he manufactured.

The colonel acknowledged him. He turned toward Hermann. He sneered.

I suppose that he had deemed Hermann a caricature of a caricature. That he represented the sum of what the man hated. That his presence in the building was a personal affront.

Hermann didn’t acknowledge the sneer. Or the man behind it. But he surmised what the colonel was about, having encountered similar men in the previous war. He was someone who could latch onto everyone’s flaws but his own. A trait that did more to help the Central Powers lose the First World War than any measures the enemy had taken.

“So,” the colonel growled, with the type of baritone voice Hermann expected him to have, “this is the Miracle Jew, who’s going to save us from that terrible killer.”

Neither Klaus nor Hermann said a word.

The colonel returned to Klaus. “He’s not much to look at.”

“That didn’t stop the Austrians from giving him the Order of the Iron Crown for his actions at Verdun.”

Hermann stepped from Klaus, feeling overwhelmed by the sounds of battle that the man’s words had roused. Decades after the war, its broaching affected him. This would never cease.

His military service was an aspect of his life that he wouldn’t discuss with me. Apart from at the periphery of an event. Like this one. One that I had to piece together. This period so troubled him that the reading of a newspaper article about some war or the peeking at a war movie was enough for him to later experience such nightmares that he would wake me with his screaming. On a night after my grandma died, this got so bad that I rushed into his room. He was trembling under the bed in tears.

“The emperor himself pinned the medal,” Klaus said. “After he saved a dozen wounded men from a French assault while wounded himself.”

The colonel snarled. “The Austrian army wasn’t at Verdun.”

“Four infantry divisions joined the battle under German command at the end of it.”

“Yes. They were all worthless.”

“Not all.”

“Consider me skeptical.”

“We’ll discover, in short order, if your skepticism is warranted, Herr Colonel.”

The colonel turned toward the man next to him. “Captain Kleiner believes that he can solve this crime in short order. I’m inclined to believe him.”

“It’s a matter of being persuasive with the local riffraff,” the wispy and smug man interjected. “And I can be persuasive.”

Klaus agreed. “It’s remarkable how you’ve persuaded each commanding officer to keep you from harm’s way.”

The remark wiped the smugness from Kleiner’s face. He was about to respond to it. But a commotion down the hall interrupted him.

A frightened and unshaven man was running toward the exits. He came across as if he hadn’t slept in weeks.

“Stop him!” shouted a voice from somewhere unseen.

Klaus whipped out his pistol. He shot the man in the leg, sending him to the floor. He did this with the casualness that he would’ve opened a door for someone.

The colonel projected Klaus’s calm onto the writhing man. “Captain Stamm has earned the right to conduct this investigation as he sees fit.”

He wheeled himself toward Klaus. “But I’ll keep both my close eyes on both you and the Jew.”

Klaus clicked his heels. He saluted the colonel.

“At ease,” the man barked.

He marched through Klaus and Hermann. It was as if he were smashing through a wall.

Captain Kleiner followed him, his smugness having returned. The two left the building.

Klaus edged toward Hermann. “That was Colonel Reidl. He wasn’t enamored with the idea of bringing you in on this case.”

“How did you get him to agree?” Hermann mumbled, fixed on the bloodied man. While this was far from the first shooting he had witnessed in recent years, it unnerved him. What unnerved him the most was how it did not unnerve Klaus. He also made out the ease with which Klaus could shoot him.

“He agreed to this for the same reason I did. The lack of viable alternatives. But you’d best be as invisible to him as you can.”

Soldiers came. They dragged the wounded man away.

Hermann and Klaus went on. They entered the staircase. They climbed up it. With Hermann riveted to the painting on the ceiling.

On the second floor, they made their way into the building’s north wing. To a reception area.

Beside a desk sat a woman in her late twenties. She was wearing a dark-blue dress. She had shoulder-length light-brown hair and doe-like eyes the same color.

She recognized Hermann. She jumped up. She rushed to him. “Herr Weiss!”

The two embraced.

He gasped. “Marta.”

In the years prior to the occupation, Marta Vachoušková had been Hermann’s “partner in crimefighting,” as he would call her. In the face of how she was much younger than him, uneducated, and no more than his personal assistant—whose primary responsibility was to translate for him when his Czech proved inadequate—I could only imagine them as equals by the way he would describe her.

“What have those animals done to you?” she stammered.

“I’m fine.”

They released their arms from each other. Each noted how Klaus had taken a few steps from them. How he had put his sight in the opposite direction.

Marta lowered her eyes. “I was so sorry to hear about Ana. She was very kind to me.”

Hermann was unsure of what to say. “So, they’ve got you working here too.”

She raised her head. “They didn’t tell me why. Or for whom. Only that I was to report here this morning. I had no clue that I’d be working for you. Or that you were . . .”

“They must’ve wanted to bring back as many of my old cohorts as possible.”

“Not many are left.”

The two ran out of words.

Marta broke the silence. “You, you must be starving. I mean . . .”

“It’s okay, Marta. Really.”

She pointed to a window. Toward the view across the square. “I could get you a couple of utopence. U Černého Vola makes them much the way you love. With plenty of onions. And they gave me plenty of ration coupons. The kind they only give to Germans.”

“That would be terrific,” he said with a smile that reflected the warmth of her sentiment. At the same time, he had to halt his tears. From what he would later regard as the beautiful unexpectedness of her kindness. Through this he was able to say, “And perhaps you could get me some—”

“—Some Velkopopovický,” she interrupted.

She lifted a flower vase from the table beside her. She removed its dying flowers. “Řezané, just as you love it.”

He gave her another smile. It voiced what he couldn’t put into words. “You can still read my mind.”

She smiled back while backing up with the vase. “I won’t be but a minute.”

Swirling from him, she sprinted off.

“Oh, Marta,” he called out. “Could you make it—”

“—Three utopence!” she yelled, spinning toward him with three fingers in the air.

He smiled at her a third time. At what made her wonderful and how this wonderfulness had meaning to him. More than that, for the first time in years, he was anticipating a positive.
