the sixth night

 

 

NO ONE TOOK my place on my days off. I would have more work than normal on Mondays.

Amoun wanted it done in the same four hours. I would run around more than normal.

With twenty minutes to go on this Monday, I had caught up. I had to clean the field by the back lot.

Laboring in the brisk midday gloom, my mind wandered to Josh. To what I should do with what I hadn’t learned about him.

Why it had, I can’t say. I couldn’t get it to stop. Bad Brains couldn’t get it to stop.

All that could was Larks on a String, an old Czech movie that I’ve seen so many times that I can recite its lines. From the end to the beginning.

Filmed during the waning days of Prague Spring and set during the early years of Communism, it’s about a group of intellectuals and misfits who had gotten on the authorities’ worst side. They forced them to work in a scrapyard that served as a metaphor. It would smelt them into the type of new men that the regime wanted.

For these men, failure to achieve this would result in a prison sentence and the uranium mines and the unnatural death that would result from it. They were birds perching on the barest of strings. Any breeze could’ve blown them over for good.

Of those who would fall was a former librarian who was there because he refused to destroy the works of Schopenhauer. Despite this (or because of it), he would quote Kant during the movie. Such as the one from the close of Critique of Practical Reason. “Two things fill the mind with ever-new and increasing admiration and awe, the more often and steadily I reflect upon them: the starry heavens above me and the moral code inside me.”

“Why am I thinking of this?” I cried, gripping a diaper that I had pulled from the weeds.

The answer came fast.

It was this “moral code,” the sort that so thrived in my sister, that was keeping my mind on Josh. Against all reason. Practical or otherwise.

A slamming of a door got me out of my funk.

Sania had pushed her cart outside for a cigarette break. The woman with the beautiful name and the voice that you would’ve sworn was Paul McCartney’s.

She gave me one of her soaring waves. She also gave me encouragement with her eyes. She could sense my struggle.

I skirted this. I returned to the search for Yon.

 

 

OWEN TOLD RUDI on the bus about the two places where he bought punk records.

The first was in The Union Marketplace. A flea market that was open on weekends. The other was a quick ride from Columbia. Called Vintage Vinyl.

We went to the latter. We drove to Springfield Avenue in Irvington.

The shop wouldn’t have popped out at you unless you had wanted it to. We passed it twice before we sighted it, amid blight on each side. The sun couldn’t brighten it.

Grandpa parked across the street.

He, Rudi, and I dashed through the busy road. Into the store.

It was empty apart from the compact, muscular man behind the counter. He was wearing a shredded Bay City Rollers T-shirt three sizes his junior and singing with Keith Morris and “Back Against the Wall,” playing on a turntable steps away.

I couldn’t tell which voice was louder. Or shriller.

They smacked against the building’s beams and its columns and its supports. They struck the window in front. They threatened to break it apart.

The man confounded Grandpa. He studied him by the door.

Rudi and I walked along the stacks. I did so agog.

While the locale lacked the area of some Cadillacs, each record was great. It was a faultless medley of music from the fifties to the present. With apparent disregard for whether any of it would sell. It was more of a record collection than a store.

Among the treasures were the thirteen Captain Beefheart studio albums. They had an alternative version of John Coltrane’s A Love Supreme. They had a section dedicated to the bands that had performed in The Decline of Western Civilization. This included the record playing.

But it was the records that weren’t there that stuck out the most to me. The two hundred records in the Billboard Top 200.

They also had copies of Trouser Press on the counter. This was a cult magazine about the independent music scene that I had heard about but had suspected was a legend. It had never been in front of me.

The man behind the counter got tired of Grandpa. He ceased his shrilling. He scratched the needle across the record. He cast himself toward him. “Listen, pops, we have old records. But not that old.”

Grandpa hid his smirk. He pulled out the picture of Yon. He asked the man about him.

The man’s churlishness sharpened. “And you are?”

Rudi struggled for words. “Yon and I are . . .”

He gawked at her. “You and Yon?”

“It’s complicated.”

The man rolled his eyes. “I bet.”

“Please . . .”

“The name’s Butch.”

“Please, Butch. Yon’s sick. He’s sick and alone and . . . I love him.”

Butch’s expression softened. “He comes in here and buys used Yardbirds records.”

“Has he been here of late?” Grandpa asked.

“A week ago.”

“And he bought a Yardbirds record?” was my question.

“Actually, no. It was various sorts of off. He was off. And trashed. But more off was how he pulled out a copy of Damaged.”

“By Black Flag?” Grandpa asked next.

Butch lifted his eyebrows. He revolved them toward Grandpa. “Your knowledge of punk rock is impeccable.”

Grandpa wanted to hide his smirk for a second time. But he couldn’t.

Butch returned to me. “Yon asked me if it was good. So I told him. I told him that it was the best. But I told him as well that it was no Yardbirds. I told him that he’d hate it. But that’s what he wanted. That’s what he bought. He had me make him a tape of it too.”

Grandpa took hold of my shoulder. “Apart from it being ‘the best,’ does this Damaged have some special quality to it?”

I went back to Butch. “Do you have another copy?”

He craned his head toward a set of albums.

I pried out the record. I showed Grandpa the image on the cover. Of Henry Rollins with his head shaved and his bloodied fist through a mirror.

Grandpa recoiled. “We better get to this boy quickly.”

I shook the record. “It’s not what you’re thinking. I’ve played this a lot. Sometimes I would play it all afternoon when I came home from school. Not a time didn’t I feel better because of it. While it’s full of anger and rage and self-hatred, many songs have this . . . I can’t describe it . . . a mad desperation.”

“For what?”

“Hope,” Rudi said.

She startled me. But she shouldn’t have. I should’ve figured that what I had experienced when listening to the record hadn’t been unique to me. She had felt the same when she listened to it. So must’ve had others.

If Yon had, we had reason to hope.

Grandpa whirled toward Butch. “I presume Yon did not mention where he is staying.”

“Why would he?” the man snapped.

We had no more questions. I put the record back. The three of us started out.

Sighing, Butch thrust his hand over our heads. “He was swerving that way.”

Grandpa flew this way. “Toward Newark?”

 

 

WE PARKED AT the same decrepit motel in Newark that we had been at before.

No one had fixed the sign. The yellow light coming from it was as harsh as before.

Moreover, Yon’s bike wasn’t there.

Rudi groaned. “I told you that he wouldn’t be here.”

Grandpa opened his door. He took a step onto the lot. He walked off. “But that does not mean that he was not here.”

Rudi turned her groan on me. “What’s he doing?”

I had no answer.

Grandpa shuffled through the grounds. For a reason far from clear.

He stopped for a less clear reason.

Was he having another of his stupors? What would we do if he were?

Grandpa came out of his haze. He hurried to a parking space.

Kneeling on the ground, he took out the Polaroid of Yon’s tire tracks from his jacket.

Rudi and I charged from the car. We sprinted to him.

He waved the photo, at a track frozen in the dirty snow. “Yon was here. Recently. Because this was not here the last time I was.”

“He could be staying here and be out somewhere,” I said.

Rudi groaned some more. “Or he could be anywhere.”

Grandpa wriggled to his feet. He resumed his shuffling.

I went in a different direction. I pictured what I would be doing if I were Yon. The boy who had been so different from me when I’d met him. But who was no longer different in the least.

My eyes closed. I focused on the album he had bought.

I shuttled through the songs. I was seeking the one that could’ve been lingering him above ground. The one that could’ve prevented him from hitting that tree.

One stood out. It opened my eyes. Upon Rudi. “What was Yon singing in the cafeteria?”

She whined. “What does it matter?”

“I’m not sure.”

“It’s the song where the guy screams, ‘Keep me alive.’”

“‘Room 13.’”

“What was that?” Grandpa asked.

I lifted a finger toward him. I checked the rooms. Twenty spread across two floors.

The numbers of those at street level began at 101. The units above did at 201. “The song that he was singing is ‘Room 13.’ But no Room 13 is here.”

“But at another hotel . . .”

Rudi and I ran to the car. Grandpa was behind us.

 

 

THE MATADOR SKIDDED from the lot.

Rudi and I read through the printout for the nearest hotel.

She grunted. “These can’t be the fastest routes.”

Grandpa disagreed. “I mapped the geocoordinates of each lodging. I calculated the distance between them all.”

“Did you use a Mercator projection?”

He vaulted back in his seat. “They teach you about Mercator projections?”

“Did you use one?”

“I wanted to use one. But I could never do partial fractions. So I used an orthographic projection.”

Boys are bad at math.

Rudi took the printout from me. “I need a pen.”

I opened the glove compartment. I got her a Bic.

My eyes bulged from the equations spewing over the page. “Are you familiar with Dijkstra’s shortest path algorithm?”

 

 

RUDI AND I got us to the lodgings faster.

But it wasn’t getting us to Yon.

Not a single place had a Room 13.

Rudi slammed the side of her head against a window. She sulked. “Thirteen is an unlucky number in America. Nobody would want to stay in a Room 13. That’s probably why the song’s called that. This is hopeless.”

This didn’t change how we hadn’t other clues. We had to chase this one.

We got to a red light.

Grandpa couldn’t ignore Rudi’s panic in his rearview mirror.

He decided that this was a good time to continue his Kafka story.

 

 

HERMANN IMPELLED HIMSELF toward a frail man in a spotless nightshirt, whose lengthy feet were hanging over a mattress.

He braked beside him. In the dull light that was awash around them. It was reflecting off the river behind the man through a row of windows. “Franz Kafka?”

Kafka answered with a string of coughs. Each harsher. With snowballing degrees of murk.

Hermann reached for a hat that wasn’t there. He tipped his hand instead. “Servus.”

Kafka’s murk thickened. He became alarmed.

Hermann eased himself onto the empty bed next to Kafka’s. “It’s an honor to meet you, sir. I enjoyed that book of yours. The Metamorphosis, wasn’t it?”

“Who told you that I was here?”

“Your family sent me.”

“There you are!” boomed a voice.

Following it stomped an enormous woman with cropped black hair and colossal black eyes. “I’ve been hunting all over for you, Herr Weiss. I’ll get your stretcher. So I can bring you to your bed.”

Hermann patted the cot he was on. “What’s wrong with this?”

“I guess it would be all right.”

“Am I all right?”

“You’re lucky that Mrs. Veselá was around when you fell down that cliff. You can bet that ferryman wouldn’t have helped you. He wouldn’t have helped you if you had your heller with you.”

“I must’ve lost mine in the fall.”

“You shall not be needing it. Not for years to come. The doctor says that, with bed rest, you’ll be fine in a few days. And when you do go home, you can bring Herr Kafka with you. He belongs here as much as you do.”

“I must be daffy from my fall, nurse. You’re not implying that ferryman is Charon?”

“I care not what his name is. He’s a vůl, I tell you. A velikánský vůl! What right did he have to take my mother before her time? I was a girl. I needed her.”

The nurse became overwrought. She scooted away.

Hermann leaned toward Kafka. “I’m in a madhouse. The inmates are running it.”

“That’s what I believed. But now . . .”

“Don’t tell me you believe that boatman is Charon.”

“Is it crazier than airplanes or radios? When I was a boy, I would’ve believed such to have been as fantastical as Charon. But . . .”

The spinning of wheels interrupted him.

A scowling Hašek was straining toward them in his wheelchair.

He got to their beds. He quaked. “Just what we need here. More Jews. Can a Czech go anywhere these days to escape you? Perhaps not in death!”

“What did I tell you, Mr. Hašek?” howled the nurse.

She returned with Hermann’s chart. She flipped it onto the railing of his bed. “Keep your prejudices to yourself. You’ll soon discover how hollow they are. Very soon!”

The nurse sped out. Hašek sneered at her the entire way.

“This is the famous Jaroslav Hašek,” Kafka said to Hermann. “Or should I say, infamous?”

“I gathered that.”

Hermann frowned at Hašek. “I can assure you, sir, that Czechs and Jews have shared death. I’ve witnessed it.”

“Are you telling me that you served in the army, little man?”

“And I have an Order of the Iron Crown to show for it. Which I’d gladly chuck into that river to get any of my friends back. I wouldn’t care if they were Czech, German, or Jew. The bullets didn’t.”

Hašek switched his antipathy to Kafka. “Did he call you ‘Franz Kafka’?”

“I did,” Hermann said.

“Franz Kafka, the writer?”

“You’ve heard of me too?” Kafka yelped.

“I’ve read some of your stories. If you can call them that. ‘Absurd nonsense’ is a better name . . . men turning into bugs and ridiculous penal colonies.”

“You are one to judge, Mr. Hašek. A peddler of dreck. Cataloguing the babblings of a drunkard. A common street urchin can write more coherently than you. Hell, my Czech is terrible, but I can write it better than you.”

“You filthy . . .”

Hašek stumbled from his wheelchair. He stumbled to Kafka. He swung his fist at him.

It missed Kafka. It missed him by a lot. Though it knocked his end table onto the floor. Along with the lamp and the bell that was on top of it.

This didn’t deter Hašek. He rolled up the sleeves of his nightshirt, panting for breath. “It’s time for a pogrom.”

Kafka didn’t flinch. “I should warn you. My father was a competent boxer in his day. He taught me well. Regardless of my pathetic state, I can put you down. Especially in your pathetic state.”

“Boxer? Your father . . . your father isn’t Hermannek, is he?”

“He is.”

“He’s got a shop on Staromák?”

“That’s right.”

Hašek couldn’t corral his humor. It stampeded from him.

He hobbled back to his wheelchair. He plopped onto it. “That man, he’s more Czech than me.”

Kafka stayed silent.

Hašek eyed him. “He’s the one person in Prague who can drink me under the gutter. He’s done it to me twice. And those are the times I remember.”

Kafka stayed silent some more. But he couldn’t corral his humor any better than Hašek had.

“How come you’ve never been out with him?”

“I’m afraid that we are different men.”

“Yeah. I bet you’re a disappointment to him.”

Kafka cringed. But he kept quiet.

Hašek raised his arms to his shoulders. He flapped them. “You, a literati, snooting too high above to lower yourself to us ordinary folk.”

“Listen to you talk. As if you walk arm-in-arm among the proletariat. You must be the richest novelist in the country. Especially with no publisher to pay. I bet Čapek doesn’t earn what you do. Why, what you make off Švejk in a single day must exceed my writing earnings for a lifetime.”

The nurse pounced through the doors. “What’s wrong? I heard a bell.”

“I’m sorry, nurse,” Kafka said. “I knocked the table over by accident.”

The woman rambled to them. She swung her fists as she went.

Glowering at Hašek, she lifted the table. She set the lamp and the bell onto it. “Don’t let this ruffian bully you, Herr Kafka.”

Hašek dismissed her with a sweep of his arms. “Have no fear of that, Nurse Černá. He’s the son of a Hermann Kafka of Old Town Square, Prague. A man who stared down a whole street of rioters. And I should know, I was one of them!”

The nurse stewed at Hašek. She scurried off.

Hašek let loose some more antipathy. At both Kafka and Hermann. He scurried off himself.

 

 

HERMANN SPENT THE day persuading Kafka to go home with him.

Kafka coughed. He coughed and coughed. “I’m a dead man. Whether I die tomorrow or in six months makes no difference.”

“It makes a difference to your sisters. And to your girlfriend.”

“I’m a burden to them all.”

“It didn’t appear so.”

“They’ll be better off when I’m gone. Especially Ottla. She’s in a bad marriage. With a horrible man. She’s only staying with him so I’ll have a place to stay. She’d leave him otherwise.”

“Can you have surety of that? Your death could make her life worse. None of us can have surety over what comes after us. That could be the point of living. To discover what happens and be a part of it. To influence it and affect it. Don’t you want to be a part of what happens next?”

“Not anymore.”

“Some ‘next’ must be worth living for.”

“I can’t imagine one.”

“But if I can, will you come back with me?”

Kafka began to answer.

But the nurse cut him off. From the door. “You have a visitor, Herr Kafka.”

A well-dressed man Kafka’s age traipsed toward them.

Kafka rose from his bed. “Thank you, Max. Thank you for coming.”

Hermann leapt to his feet. “Max? Are you Max Brod? The author of Nornepygge Castle?”

“You’ve heard of me?”

Kafka snickered. “He’s heard of me.”

Hermann wanted to give the men some privacy. He checked the exits. “I could use a walk.”

He didn’t go far.

Unsteady from his fall, he took a seat on a plush chair by the doorway. He became drowsy. He dozed off.

 

 

HERMANN WOKE. A fracas was coming from Kafka’s bed.

By the man was both Brod and Hašek.

Hašek writhed at Brod. “You goddamn Jew! For years now, you’ve been a goddamn Jew!”

Not finished with his tirade, Hašek tossed his head toward Kafka. “And you write as badly as he does.”

Hašek didn’t wait for a response. He spun the wheels of his chair. He propelled himself from the two. Doing so with as much velocity as he could muster.

He approached Hermann. He slowed. His ire rose.

A gravelly voice grated from Hašek’s right. “Don’t you worry about them.”

Hašek sought the voice’s source.

An unshaven man was lying in bed. He was scoffing at Kafka and Brod. “They’ll get theirs sooner than soon.”

This threw Hašek off balance. “What do you mean?”

The man’s eyes darted about.

Lifting the lapel of his nightshirt, he exposed a swastika pin.

Hašek turned white. “You, you stay away from me.”

“What are you getting so upset about, Jarda?”

“My name is Jaroslav. But don’t you call me that. Don’t you call me ‘Mister Hašek.’”

“You and me, we’re the same.”

“We’re not the same. I may be a bigot, but I’m not you!”

Hašek took off. He pushed himself through the room’s doors.

He left Hermann with the man, who broke out in laughter. He wouldn’t stop.

 

 

KAFKA AND BROD continued talking by Kafka’s bed.

They became agitated. They argued.

It got louder upon Hašek’s reentering the room and his pausing by Hermann.

Brod rushed toward the exit.

Kafka shook. His whole self shook. “You must do what I say! You must burn them!”

Brod didn’t respond. He got to Hermann and Hašek.

Hašek lifted himself at Brod. “Burn what?”

Brod didn’t answer. He burst through the doors.

Conflict fell over Hašek.

It kept falling. It wouldn’t ebb.

With his own burst, he followed Brod. Into the hallway.

 

 

GRANDPA LEFT OFF from his story.

With the afternoon dipping below an industrial landscape that was inseparable from the New Jersey Turnpike ahead of us, he suggested that we go to the waterfall before dark.

We turned around. We came to a country road.

It was as different from the turnpike as the day was from the night switching places with it. It passed through the reservation’s length and the trees. Along with the quiet passing through them.

Rudi pressed her nose against a window. She hummed the same song that she had been humming by the waterfall.

Her voice faltered. We reached a body of water that fell into our view from nowhere.

“Reservoir,” she said.

Grandpa sneaked a peek at it. “What about it?”

“Yon told me that his father grew up in Newark. By the old reservoir there.”

“No reservoir is in Newark.”

“But one must’ve been.”

 

 

SO NEAR WERE we to the waterfall that we went anyway.

Yon wasn’t there. But that didn’t mean that no trace of him was.

Grandpa weighed the ravine with concern.

I snuck beside him. Out of Rudi’s earshot. “What?”

He wiggled his head. At a smashed vodka bottle below. “That was not here the last time I was.”

I had come across the broken glass the last time I was there. But it hadn’t dawned on me as having meaning. “So?”

He didn’t answer.

 

 

WE RETURNED TO Newark. In pursuit of a reservoir that didn’t exist.

The proof of this came at the Newark Public Library’s main branch. From a woman locking its doors.

She told us that she hadn’t heard of an old reservoir either.

Everyone we asked in the streets said the same. Or not far from it.

Grandpa, during his decades as a police detective, had been on countless quixotic ventures. But the great knight-errant himself would’ve given up on this one. If for no other reason than that it was in a neighborhood distant in countless ways from La Mancha.

The chagrin on Grandpa’s face made plain that he wanted to give up. He only didn’t because there were no alternatives. We needed to get to Yon now.

He kept pulling the car over. He kept asking people.

Including a middle-aged woman pushing a shopping cart of groceries down an empty block.

“I’ve never heard of any reservoir,” she said. “But I remember Reservoir Pizza. My brother cried when they left town.”

Rudi dove toward the woman. “Do you remember where it was?”

“I sure do. On the corner of 14th Avenue and 9th Street.”

 

 

THE MATADOR SWIRLED right. Onto 14th Avenue.

No sign that a reservoir had been in the area was anywhere. Or any motels or hotels.

But Grandpa drove on. He drove through each street in the vicinity.

We came to a complex. It offered furnished rooms by the week.

Grandpa slapped his forehead. “I did not consider apartments.”

He raced onto the parking lot. To a set of ramshackle units opposing each other.

Beside Apartment 13 were the remains of a motorcycle.

Rudi swatted her finger at it. “That’s Yon’s!”

Grandpa parked by the unit.

Rudi exploded from the car before it stopped.

She tried the door.

Unsuccessful, she banged on it with both fists. “Yon!”

No answer came.

Grandpa and I rushed toward her.

I leaned against him. “Now what?”

He didn’t say. He examined the area.

Wrenching a set of lock picks from his jacket pocket, he told us to cover him.

Rudi sprang his way. “What?”

He slapped the picks onto his hand. “Honem! Šup-šup-šup!”

Fast was how Rudi and I crouched around and over him. We shielded him.

He picked the lock. He opened the door. We surged inside.

Yon was on top of a creaky Murphy bed in the cramped studio.

As Rudi had described, he had shaved his head and was drunk. He was dangling from consciousness while thrashing to the music playing on his Walkman.

This was so loud that I could tell it was “Room 13.”

Rudi plunged onto him.

He didn’t react. He didn’t when she jostled him.

I crept to him and the sweating he was doing.

He had soaked his bedsheets. His pillows and his clothes too. He was swimming in them.

Rattled, I shifted toward Grandpa.

He raised an empty vodka bottle off the floor. The same brand that had been by the waterfall.

Tossing the glass into a garbage pail, he scampered to Yon. He checked his pulse.

His concern grew.

Lurching to his right, he grabbed a phone off a table. He dialed 911. “I need an ambulance. I need it sooner than now.”

I continued my shifting. This time around the room.

Yon had broken the four walls. He had been smashing into them. He’d also smashed the full-length mirror by the bathroom. With more than one fist. Blood was on it.

“Yon!” Rudi pleaded. She was desperate to get him to respond.

He wouldn’t.

The song ended.

Yon hit the stop button on his Walkman. He reversed the tape.

Pressing play, he screamed “Keep me alive!” with Henry Rollins.

He screamed and screamed it.

 

 

GRANDPA, RUDI, AND I sat in the emergency room of St. Barnabas. One of us was sitting with impatience.

Grandpa took Rudi’s hand with both of his. He took it in the same way that she had taken his in the car a week earlier.

In the same way that he had done, she calmed. She also expressed a reverence as jolting to me as her nurturing had been the week before. “I’ll never forget what you did. For a nobody like me.”

Down the hall, a jaundiced man in a white coat exited a door. He stepped toward us with the same nurse who had been in Yon’s room when I had met him.

She had on another mask. Another pair of gloves. She was as frightened as before.

Rudi jumped up. “That’s Dr. Kleinsten.”

Grandpa and I stood.

The doctor ceased in front of us. While the nurse whisked by.

“How is he?” Rudi asked. She was begging him.

“He’s determined to kill himself, and succeeding.”

“What can I do?”

“Ever heard of ‘tough love’?”

Rudi drifted toward Grandpa.

“You’re the only person who can give it to him.”

Rudi wandered down the hall. Toward the room the doctor had left. Her feet never left the floor.

Grandpa inched to the doctor. “What are his chances?”

“Chances?” the man repeated. It was as if the question had been in a language he didn’t speak.

“His chances for survival.”

“No one has survived this. Or has experienced any remission.”

“How long does he have?” I asked.

“I know of no case where the patient has lived for a month. And his is as bad as there is. His immune system has shut down. If the pneumocystis doesn’t kill him, some other complication will. At any time.”

The doctor trod off.

I called out to him.

He came to a reluctant halt. “What?”

“If it’s true that you’re not aware of what causes the disease and how it spreads, how is it that you are not wearing a mask and gloves like the nurse you were with?”

The man thundered down the hall. “I didn’t become a doctor to hide from sick people.”

Grandpa and I went back to our seats.

In the quiet that followed, I wanted to thank him for what he had done.

But it was he who thanked me.

I reeled. “For what?”

“For dragging me to my granddaughter. It would have been a tragedy if you had not.”

“What about Grandma?”

“She will not be thanking you. Or me. Especially me. I might never leave the living room couch. And she might move this outside.”

 

 

I GOT TO the shelter late in the day. The night was falling onto the alley.

Willy slammed the hood of his Civic. He swore.

I eased beside him and the headlights that were beaming down the road. “What’s wrong?”

He kicked the car.

“I bet Mateo can fix it.”

He kicked the car another time. This time with the full force of his weight. “I got fired today. I couldn’t get to work. I can’t get anywhere now. I’ll be stuck here forever!”

The kicking continued.

But he didn’t make a mark. He wasn’t there.

A truck staggered beside us. It squealed to a rest.

It was donating food from a local restaurant. An event not uncommon at the shelter. But it continued to surprise me how generous people were in a city that wasn’t thriving.

Church groups would drop off food each weekend. On multiple occasions, carloads of pizzas had arrived uncredited. As they had in a Jason Statham film called Redemption.

After the second time it happened, questions came with it.

Was life imitating art? Or was it the other way around? Did this occur everywhere?

The Rusty Bull’s donation that evening surprised me in particular. Dozens of cartons of choice steaks and ribs. Along with boxes of fresh fruit and vegetables.

The gift overwhelmed everybody.

That’s why Josh didn’t have to ask us to unload the truck. On our own, we formed a chain. From the alley through the mess hall and into the kitchen. We passed the boxes between us, talking of what a wonderful Christmas meal it would make. Not only on Christmas Eve and Christmas Day. But through New Year’s. It would be a full week of Christmas.

It took a half hour to get the boxes off the truck. But no one complained. For one night, fortune hung over us. It didn’t favor the bold. It refused to go anywhere. It hung over Willy and Dzsenifer, despite losing their chance for escape.

It made each of us feel that the gift was in part from them.

 

 

I MADE IT into the check-in line after cleaning the rec room.

An uproar was coming from behind the office’s closed door.

Josh was arguing with Héctor. They were shouting at each other. With their voices and their hands.

Héctor shot from the room. He passed me. He went to his cubby.

He flung his belongings onto the floor.

“What happened?” I asked.

He crammed his stuff into a dusty silver suitcase. “I got thrown out.”

“Why?”

“I wouldn’t do his wet work.”

“His what?”

Héctor didn’t answer. He blew by with his suitcase. He returned to the office. “I need my passport.”

Josh wrangled to his feet. “Your what?”

“I gave you my passport for safekeeping when I got here.”

Héctor pointed toward the mess hall. “You put it in the vault.”

Josh left the office. He was choking on his animus. “Wait here.”

 

 

JOSH CAME BACK.

He cocked his eye at Héctor. “Your passport has gone missing.”

Héctor got madder. But he couldn’t stay that way.

He was about to cry.

Escaping this, he dragged his suitcase along the ground.

He disappeared into the night.

 

 

ANOTHER LETTER FROM Yon came that day.

I brought it into the courtyard. I called Rudi.

Eagerness was in her voice. She couldn’t hold it down.

 

 

IT WAS DIFFICULT getting through the first pages Yon had sent me. He had garbled most entries.

In a quasi-coherent one, he described his transfer to St. Barnabas after his original hospital refused to treat him upon learning what he had. In another, he touched upon an encounter with his mother in the hospital.

“I blame myself,” she said to him. By the door and the whiteness around her. “I should’ve sent you to Pingry.”

Yon rolled to the other side of his bed. He coughed.

The woman puffed, and she huffed. “You must promise me that you’ll stay away from her.”

“I won’t.”

“After what she’s done to you? After she’s ruined your life?”

“It’s you who should stay away,” he growled, his eyes away from her. They had locked onto the woods through his windows and onto what was behind them.

“If I go, I’m not coming back.”

He coughed some more.

She fumed from the room. Her steps faded. They became deafening.

Yon wasn’t sorry about it.

 

 

WHAT YON DIDN’T describe were his encounters with Rudi.

All he wrote about her was a brief passage. He lamented how he would never make love to her.

He wrote less about the disease ravaging him. The single mention of it was that his countdown had begun. Only the units of measurement were not known.

What came after this was a fractured sentence about leaving St. Barnabas. Coming after that were but streams of semiconsciousness.

I inferred from these that he moved from motel to motel. He drank himself to sleep. He woke this way.

On a grim morning, he got on his bike and drove anyway. One moment he was swerving through traffic on Route 22. The next he had made it to the waterfall.

He spent the day on the rocks. He cursed himself and his fate and who he blamed for it. He screamed this into the skies and above. He only let up when he ran out of vodka.

Smashing the bottle onto the ravine, he left the forest.

He returned to the roads. He swerved through them. Worse than before.

Vintage Vinyl intruded on this.

He bought the Black Flag record because he remembered the T-shirt that Rudi had been wearing on her first day of school. He remembered, too, the tattoo that had been on Owen’s arm.

But, most of all, he bought the record because staring at its cover was like doing so into a mirror.

Though he had no doubt that Butch had been right. That he would hate the music.

Still, back in his room, he slid the tape into his Walkman. He tossed his headphones on. He hit play.

Henry Rollins crawled into his head. He took over the screaming.

My name is Henry, and you’re here with me now.

Rollins’s screaming sent Yon teetering. It knocked him from his knees. It knocked him from what was below them.

He would swear that Rollins was screaming at him. At him alone. Screaming what he was feeling but not able to express.

What toppled Yon over was how Rollins was screaming about more than how life sucks. He screamed more about how he wanted to change it. How he wouldn’t give in to it. Or stop screaming about it.

This inspired him.

“Room 13” inspired him. But so did “Rise Above” and “Life of Pain” and “Damaged II” and “What I See.”

Each song focused on a different facet of his bile. Till there wasn’t a sliver that Rollins hadn’t dissected and tossed out. Bleaker songs such as “Depression” and “Thirsty and Miserable” offered traces of promise too.

The tape finished.

Yon played it another time. He played it while smashing into the walls and punching the mirror.

Each pass through, his anger lessened. By the fourth, his darkness had gone. What remained was the mad desperation to hope.

He sobered the next morning. He played the tape over. Many times over.

It was his one substitute for self-pity. His one means from it. The one substance that wouldn’t let him have it.

It was what moved him forward. It got him up in the morning. It got him to sleep each night. It got him through his tries at killing himself. It led him to a punk club in the Alphabet City section of Manhattan called A7 and a band that must’ve been Ism. He wrote how they had played a cover of “I Think I Love You” with bodies flying across the stage and the room.

Yon collapsed onto a corner of this room. Invisible to everyone and himself.

 

 

YON’S FIRST LUCID moments came after he returned to St. Barnabas.

He woke to the angel hovering above him. The one in contrasting black and white. With no shades of gray.

Forgetting what had happened, he whispered her name.

His forgetting didn’t last. He remembered it all. “I told you to leave me alone.”

Instead, Rudi grabbed his hand.

She was breaking it apart. She was angrier than ever. The fury was firing through her.

He went nuts. He squirmed his hand. “Are you crazy? I’m infected!”

His squirming didn’t work. He couldn’t yank himself away. He could only twist from her. “Go. Please go.”

“Not till we have it out.”

He wouldn’t. He clenched his eyes closed. He prayed for her to leave.

But she wasn’t going anywhere anymore.

He had no choice but to go back to her.

Her voice lashed out at him. “Gonna be some changes around here. From now on, you’re gonna take care of yourself. And you’re gonna do what that doctor tells you! And . . .”

She couldn’t finish. Her lips trembled. The raging punk towering over him changed back into the frightened child. The girl who had been crying inside the motel room. She fell apart. Seam by seam.

Till none were left.

Rudi’s knees buckled. She tumbled onto Yon. She took him into her arms. She held on to him for her life. “And you’re gonna love me. You are. Because I love you. God, how I love you!”

Yon swelled up. “How could you love me?”

“I didn’t want to. I didn’t want to love anyone. Including me. But you’re the most magnificent person I’ve ever met. Or will ever meet. You make my whole rotten life worth it.”

Yon didn’t speak. But tears ran down his cheeks.

She whimpered. “I’m sorry. I’m so sorry.”

“Sorry? You think I’m mad at you? Don’t you get it? I don’t want you to watch me die.”

“But I’m gonna watch you live. I am. There’s nothing you can do about it.”

Yon willed himself to resist her. He willed it with all he had left.

But he couldn’t do it.

He no longer belonged to himself. Or would.

So he grasped her. He grasped her harder than she was grasping him.

“Yoni!” she shrieked. She cried with him.

Her tears mixed with his. They became one.

“I can’t live without you,” he said. “It’s not a figure of speech. Or a hyperbole. Or whatever the right term is. It just is.”

 

 

NEITHER GRANDPA NOR I expected that Yon would make it through the night. The doctor expected it less.

Rudi refused to leave Yon’s room. She flouted a nurse and her threat to call security.

The cops couldn’t have dragged her away.

Through the night she clutched Yon, humming “Moonlight Serenade” during each of his bouts with the shivers.

“I’m so scared,” he stammered to her.

“Me too. But not as much as when I was alone.”

Yon gripped Rudi’s arms. He held on to her for his life.

 

 

THE NURSE SHOVED her medical cart from Yon’s room.

As typical, she had on a mask and gloves. She was wanting to be anywhere but there.

On most days, this bothered Yon. No matter how he pretended otherwise.

But on this one, it didn’t matter.

The reason was the man next to him on his bed, who was wearing a suit and a kippah but no mask or gloves and was holding his hand.

Yon wrote how floored he was that this man he had revered since childhood was touching him and not afraid.

“Is there anything I can get you?” the rabbi asked. With a gentle voice that belied his size.

Yon opened his mouth. But he had to pinpoint the words. “Could, could you bring me a Bible?”

Where did this come from?

It was a question with no answer. Never had Yon an interest in the Bible before. He couldn’t say that he had one now. But the spark had come. He’d let it out.

The rabbi’s head tilted. “I’ll bring one tomorrow, Yonatan.”

He released Yon’s hand. He stood.

Turning away, he did a double take. Twice he did.

Yon went after the cause of it.

Rudi was in the doorway. In his football jacket.

She was holding a cold compress. She was aloof.

“Hey,” he said to her.

“Hi,” she said back. Her voice was a notch above audible.

“This is Rabbi Orenstein. From my temple.”

Rudi forced a smile.

Yon lifted himself toward her. “This is my . . . my . . .”

“I’m his girl,” she said. She must’ve said it not only to the rabbi and to Yon. But to herself.

She plodded to the rabbi.

Faking confidence, she told him her name. She offered him her hand.

He shook it. With his own forced smile. “Nice to meet you.”

The man’s daze over Rudi’s appearance heightened. He hastened back to Yon. “You must be excited about going home.”

Yon didn’t answer. His eyes sailed elsewhere. Onto the waterfall.

“We’re getting a place,” Rudi interjected.

Yon swung himself toward her. “We could get one.”

“We are getting one.”

“You’re eighteen, Rudi. I won’t saddle you with all this.”

“It’s my choice. Not yours!”

“What if, what if I don’t get out of here?”

“You’re getting out of here.”

The rabbi stifled in his discomfort. “Nice apartments are across the street. I’ve been inside a few. Some you can rent furnished.”

Yon moaned. “They’d be out of our price range. We don’t . . .”

“The synagogue has a fund for these kinds of circumstances.”

Rudi stretched toward the man. “You do get that we’re not married.”

“Under normal times, that would be a problem. But under these . . .”

Yon moaned louder.

The rabbi waved him off. He started toward the door. “Let me handle it.”

He walked out.

Rudi peered into the doorway. She was peering into it after the man had gone. “Are all rabbis like him?”

Yon peered with her. “They aspire to be.”

Rudi climbed onto the bed. She applied the compress. To Yon’s face and his forehead. “I have a surprise for you.”

“Should I be worried?”

“Yup.”

“Well?”

“You’ll have to get out of here to get it.”

 

 

RUDI AND I stood in front of Dr. Kleinsten. She wasn’t breathing.

The doctor was reading a chart. Several times he read it.

He lowered it. He did so with a shock that shone through his restraining of it. “The pneumocystis has passed.”

“Yeah?” Rudi blurted out. “So he can leave?”

“I guess. But you need to get realistic about this, Ms. Weiss. I keep trying to tell you that. It’s not that there’s no cure for what Yon’s got. But that there’s none on the horizon. It’s not that we have no name for his disease. But that we have no understanding about the virus underlying it. We can’t say with certainty that it is a virus. I have a duty to be direct with you. In cases such as his . . .”

Rudi wouldn’t listen to any more. She roared down the hall. She refused to let reality take a foothold in her way.

I followed her. We entered the elevator.

The rabbi stood in front of us.

“Hi,” he said with awkwardness.

Rudi said the same. With the same awkwardness.

The two remained rigid. The elevator descended.

She lowered her eyes. “I want to thank you for the apartment. It’s terrific.”

“You’re welcome.”

“I have questions.”

“Questions?”

We got to the ground floor. We exited.

Rudi raised her head toward the man. “This isn’t the place. Or time.”

“Our synagogue, Beth El, is on Irvington Avenue. Close to the intersection of South Orange Avenue. I have office hours each morning.”

 

 

RUDI HELPED YON inside a door. She switched on the light.

It exposed the same one-bedroom apartment from Yon’s dream. As well as the same pint-sized golden-haired mutt that had also been in it.

Yon felt surprise at the dog. At her running toward him with her tail wagging.

But the surprise didn’t last. The apartment and the dog’s presence in it couldn’t have been otherwise.

The dog jumped into Yon’s arms. She licked his face.

He gushed. “She remembers me.”

Rudi pressed herself against him. “How could a girl forget a hero?”

“How did she wind up here?”

“Nobody claimed her. I talked to Dr. Kleinsten. He agrees that a dog could be good for you. She’ll give you company when I’m not around. She’ll give you to-dos. And, to be honest, I could use an unconditional friend. So we’ve adopted her.”

“We?”

Rudi scratched behind the dog’s ears. “We’re calling her ‘Flutter.’”

Yon laughed. “What about the matching collars?”

“They’re in the living room,” she said with her own laugh.

“All right.”

Flutter licked Yon’s face more. A lot more.

 

 

THE EXCITEMENT OF having a home, and all the love inside it, was too much for Flutter.

She fell asleep before dinner was over. She was snoring on the makeshift bed that Rudi had set up for her on the kitchen floor.

At a table steps away were Rudi and Yon. Beside the leftovers of a Reservoir pizza and some Cokes. Like what they had on their first date.

Or whatever it had been.

Rudi stood. She grabbed Yon’s hand. She lifted him to his feet.

She led him to their bedroom door, blushing as she had outside the restaurant.

Yon’s movements were as halting as hers.

The two tiptoed into the room’s threshold.

Yon’s copy of Damaged sat against the wall on the floor.

He picked it up.

With it came a joy that was sparring with the tears pushing their way out. Only now had it come to him how it had saved him.

Rudi laid her fingers on the record. “That you were listening to that was a real shock to me.”

“It’s the best, and I’m not the only one who believes it.”

She released Yon’s hand. So she could walk to the portable stereo on a nightstand by the bed’s far side. The portable stereo that had belonged to Mrs. Criss, who’d given it to Rudi as a going-away present.

Rudi hit play.

“Moonlight Serenade” filled the room.

Yon pointed at it with his mouth open. “Where did you get that?”

“I got the record at the library. I made an infinite tape of it. You can listen to it all day if you want.”

“And never get tired of it.”

Yon checked out the room.

On top of a pillow on the bed was a wrapped package. “What’s that?”

“A housewarming gift.”

“Rudi. I should be the one giving gifts. You’ve given me too much.”

“It’s no big deal. Go on, open it.”

Yon put down Damaged. The two sat on the bed.

He opened the gift, careful not to tear the wrapping. He uncovered her crumpled drawing of him, set inside a simple black frame.

Hiking it up in his hand, he smirked. “I’ve always wanted a picture of Lee Ving.”

Rudi smirked back. She shoved Yon onto the bed. She leaned down to kiss him.

He stopped her. He shook his head. “I can’t.”

She snarled at him. She snarled as she had on the day that she’d met him and on so many after it. “You only have a few can’ts. And jillions of cans.”

This time the tears got past Yon. He couldn’t prevent them.

He caressed her cheek with the back of his hand. “The infinite possibilities.”

 

 

YON DESCRIBED HIS first night with Rudi in colors.

They trickled over him with each kiss and touch. Blues and yellows and violets. Colors as vivid as those in the Vermeer painting that his parents had taken him to see.

The colors not only flooded Yon’s eyes. He felt them in Rudi’s lips and her fingers and her toes. He heard them in Glenn Miller’s trombone.

Ivory, too, was in plenty. Michelangelo had carved Rudi from it.

She was perfection. His perfection.

By the time Yon had passed out, Rudi had become his lover.

Not in all senses. But in all the ones that mattered.

 

 

YON WOKE.

With fright he woke.

It was dark. He couldn’t tell whether Rudi was there.

She was. She was sleeping in his arms. She was clinging to him. She clung as if he would slip away if she let go.

This recalled to Yon the afternoon by the waterfall. When Rudi had put her head on his shoulder.

He had believed that this was the greatest he could feel. But it was child’s play compared to now. To holding her and protecting her. To feeling her breathe against him.

This would be his reason to live. So he could keep feeling her body next to his.

“I’m never gonna die,” he said to himself.

He repeated it and repeated it.

Until he believed it.

 

 

I RANG RUDI and Yon’s apartment doorbell, carrying an oversized ceramic bowl covered with tinfoil.

Rudi opened the door.

She had a Bible in her hand. Her finger was marking the spot that she had been reading.

It wouldn’t be the last time that I would catch her with a Bible. Or with a similar book. It would embarrass her.

Fumbling about, she hid the book behind her back. Like it were a Playgirl.

What led her to it? Was it the same spark as Yon’s? Or was it a desire for meaning from what wasn’t having any?

Her eyes set upon my bowl. “Whatcha got there?”

“Grandpa wanted to give you guys a housewarming present. A jar of his utopence.”

“His what?”

“Pickled sausages.”

I wouldn’t let a grimace form on Rudi’s face. I elevated the bowl. “But we made you a salad instead.”

Rudi hugged me in the doorway.

For God knows how long.

 

 

“HOW’S NEXT SATURDAY?” I asked from the dining room table across from Yon.

“I’m not sure,” griped Rudi’s voice from the kitchen.

“You can’t avoid her forever.”

“It’s not me who’s been doing the avoiding.”

Yon sat back in his chair. “Saturday will be fine.”

Rudi marched into the room.

She was carrying the salad bowl in her right hand. A plate of omelets in her other. She crooked her face at Yon.

Flutter jumped onto her. At the food.

Rudi wheeled from her. “Rule number one here: no feeding Flutter from the table. Breaking it will be a capital offense.”

Neither Yon nor I dared a reply.

Rudi served the salad and the omelets. She joined us.

Yon and I picked up our utensils.

Noting each other’s doubts, we tasted the omelets.

“This is good,” Yon gasped.

I gasped too. Louder than he had. “Yeah.”

Rudi slammed her forearms onto the table. “What were you guys expecting?”

“Glass and nails?” I mumbled. “And rusty ones.”

Yon and I laughed.

Rudi laughed with us. She also slapped my arm.

I fell to the floor.

Below the table, she was feeding Flutter a tomato.

 

 

IT TOOK A lot of cajoling from Grandpa and me to get Grandma to meet Rudi. That was only after she finished throwing stuff at us.

She agreed to lunch. But she told us that it would be a one-time event.

The three of us waited outside the Matador by Rudi and Yon’s apartment building in the early afternoon chill. We waited awhile.

Grandma’s discomfort was rising. A bomb was about to go off.

Rudi and Yon slithered from the building’s front door. They neared us with wariness.

Grandma sneered at Rudi. “Look at her. She . . . I cannot conceive of what she is.”

It wasn’t how Rudi looked that upset Grandma. It was how she reminded her of my mother. More fear was on display than disgust.

Yon and Rudi made it to us.

“Hi,” Rudi said.

Grandma didn’t respond.

It was iffy whether we would make it through lunch. Or would get there.

But this would prove minor compared to what was to come.

“Jeeeewww!” someone squawked from alongside us.

It came from a car of hooting boys slowing at a red light down the road.

We turned toward Grandpa.

He played it down. As he did whenever his Jewish looks got him notice.

I had a view to this multiple times. But never was it as bad as this.

He wanted to disappear.

Everyone wanted to pretend that it didn’t happen. Everyone but Rudi.

At the time, I supposed that how she would react had stemmed from the number tattooed on Grandpa’s arm. But now I suppose that she was recollecting when she hadn’t reacted. When she hadn’t stood up to hate.

Whatever her reason had been, she stormed toward the car, her fists drawn.

“Where you going?” Yon called out.

She went on storming.

“You can’t change the world!” he hollered.

“Just watch me!” she hollered back.

Rudi got to the car.

Hoisting a rock, she smashed the front passenger window with it. She shattered the glass to pieces.

It didn’t end there.

She edged toward the boys and their frightened faces. She brandished the rock. “That’s my grandpa you’re laughing at.”

The driver didn’t wait for the light to change. He sped through it.

He sped through the next one.

Grandpa whispered into Grandma’s ear. “Not much of a Nazi, I would say.”

She wasn’t convinced.

But we did make it through lunch. We also made plans for a Sabbath dinner the following week.

 

 

I SWEPT THE night’s pages off the table. Along with the envelope.

Saying goodnight to Rudi, I started toward the dorm and the shimmer that was trickling from it.

But I didn’t get there.

Josh exited the mess hall, hauling a carton of ribs. One of those that we had brought inside.

He carried it through the courtyard. To the back of his bright-red pickup truck parked outside the gates. He had packed a dozen other cartons inside it.

Screeching, he heaved it beside the rest.

A thhhuuddd rang out. People could’ve heard it in Hesperia.

Josh went back to the mess hall. He went into the kitchen.

He opened the freezer. He removed another box.

Coming outside with it, he came upon me. He did without worry. I was a fly who couldn’t harm him.

Or a ghost.

He strutted off, rendering the gift that we had received and the effort made by many to make it happen to nil.

That’s when that pesky “moral code” began gnawing at me.

 

 

I NEEDED A bus pass for the next day.

So I had to wait for Josh to remove the cartons. I had to wait for him to return from wherever he took them.

With this, my gnawing deepened.

It was past three when he led me to the vault.

A passport was inside it. I didn’t need to guess whose it was.

Josh whipped out the passes. He offered me one. He snorted at me. “Is there a problem?”

I didn’t answer. I snatched the pass. I scrambled off.

So I couldn’t answer.
