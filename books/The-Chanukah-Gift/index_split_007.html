the seventh night

 

 

I WOKE BEFORE dawn.

It wasn’t the waves of darkness that woke me. Or the demons pushing me down. I hadn’t felt them in days.

I couldn’t figure out why.

Was it because, unlike the times before, my mind wasn’t on myself?

 

 

I GOT MY cup of green tea from the kitchen. I dawdled into the courtyard, beneath the bluish clouds passing over it. I made my way toward the corner table and the hazy yellow that was scaling the canyons in back of it.

Wendy was in the booth by herself. She was upset.

I sat across from her. I asked what was wrong.

She wouldn’t tell me.

A woman in braids named Zara stopped by her. “I heard that you got a job at Amazon. Congrats.”

Wendy wouldn’t talk to her either. She got more upset.

The same wasn’t true of Zara. Joy shot through her. “Josh said that he could get me a job there too. A late shift.”

Wendy winced. “Don’t.”

“Why?”

She didn’t answer. She grabbed her coffee and cigarettes. She huffed off.

“It’s gone,” Mitchell yelled at Mateo, Patrick, and Gerry, who were sitting in a booth across from me. “All the ribs and all the steaks that we schlepped into the freezer last night. Each of those cartons. Somebody stole them all.”

Everybody swayed toward Gerry.

He lifted his sweatshirt. “Where could I put them?”

Mateo’s eyes wandered. They suggested that he had expected the food would disappear. “So much for that Christmas dinner.”

“All they left was the fruit and vegetables,” Mitchell added.

Patrick did a drumroll on the table. “They must’ve been thinking of our health.”

The four laughed. I laughed.

We were like those creatures in Whoville. The ones who woke to empty houses on that famous Christmas morning.

It didn’t matter that the Grinch had stolen our gift. What was behind it mattered.

Josh couldn’t steal this.

 

 

I WAS POWER-WASHING the walkways outside the motel.

Mateo lumbered by with Lydia, a cleaning woman.

She had been at the shelter earlier in the year. She had gotten Mateo his job.

Life is a series of ever-renewing cycles.

The two were chatting about the company Christmas party. It was happening the following week.

They had been chatting about it since I got the job in the summer. Such longing was in their voices. This was the best they could aspire to.

It made me presume that, from the day after the party, they would be chatting about next year’s.

“Where is my Hebrew?” a voice sprang from nearby.

Amoun was whooping it up as usual. He was skipping and snapping his fingers. “I have good news for you. I’ve decided to make you full-time in the new year. I will raise your salary too. All the way to minimum wage.”

I fell over. In merry.

It wasn’t because of what he had said.

I loved this man. I loved him like I had loved that pastor. He gave me a job when nobody would.

He shared the merry with me. “Happy Chanukah.”

“Thank you,” I said to him. “The Coptic Christmas is coming soon, isn’t it?”

“January 7th.”

“Pikhristos Avmasf.”

My words, however terrible I had pronounced them, heartened him. “Khen Omethmi Avmasf.” He patted me on the shoulder. He swaggered off.

Amoun’s news should’ve made me happy.

With a full-time job and a cheap enough apartment, I could’ve left the shelter.

It was possible that I could’ve held back the darkness and the demons for good.

Mann tracht, un Gott lacht.

Grandma’s saying came a-calling.

Two drifters broke my focus. They were in a room across from me. They were breaking the window’s seal.

They pried the glass out.

One by one, they tossed their belongings outside.

Were they evading the reception area? What was waiting for them there?

It was pathetic. But my observing of it was more so.

 

 

I FLOUNDERED THROUGH the alley in the fading light.

A siren roused me.

Racing along with it was an ambulance. It raced from the shelter past me.

I hustled to the gates. I hustled faster through them. I turned to Hank. “What happened?”

“Dzsenifer had a stroke. One second, she’s talking to some people in a booth. A second after that, she’s on the ground.”

Hank’s words drew me to the courtyard. To Willy.

He was on a bench surrounded by others.

They were comforting him. But his life had sped off.

He was desperate for the means to and from the hospital. Without a running car. Or money.

That’s how it can be for homeless people. The badness climbs on top of itself.

Hank headed toward the bench. “It hasn’t been the brightest of days. They kicked Mario out.”

“For what?”

“Josh found a knife in his cubby.”

 

 

IT WAS A somber line of people waiting to check in that night.

I couldn’t tell whether their minds were on Willy and Dzsenifer or on their own fragility.

Mine was on both.

It was a relief to get Yon’s letter from Josh.

With it in my fist, I sprinted into the courtyard to call Rudi.

But I couldn’t do this right away.

I learned that I had “volunteered” for street showers.

Two nights a week, we let people living on the streets inside the shelter for a shower. Most had been clients that the shelter had kicked out for various reasons.

Rather than moving on, they camped out in the alley by the gates. Or down the block behind Starbucks.

What kept them here? Could they have had nowhere better to go? Or did it not matter where they went?

One of these people would help at the shelter with lunch when I was around. In exchange for a meal.

His name was Fred. He had been a lawyer. Elements of it remained below the decay.

My chore that night was to sit in the men’s room.

I had to sit there while they undressed before they showered. I had to remain there while they got back into their dirty clothes afterward.

Why we had to do this to them, why we had to dehumanize them beyond how they were, was anyone’s and no one’s guess.

None could explain it to me.

The men that night were like the drifters escaping the motel. They were pathetic. But not as much as me.

I eluded this. I plugged in my phone at an outlet by the sinks.

Placing my chair in the showers’ general bearing, I pulled out Yon’s letter. I called Rudi.

We would be passing over more dark passages that night. Darker ones.

I asked her if she was ready. But I was readying myself.

 

 

I RANG THE doorbell outside Rudi and Yon’s apartment. My grandparents were behind me.

During the times that I had been in the hallway before, the hospital hadn’t stuck out through the windows.

But at night, with the building’s lights shining, it beat me over the head.

It made me flinch.

Only the units of measurement were not known.

Yon’s countdown was ongoing.

I didn’t want to believe it. I switched my sight onto my grandparents.

Grandma wasn’t any more excited than she had been prior to her last encounter with Rudi.

Though she wasn’t sneering.

I took this as a positive sign.

The apartment door swung open with a sweeping swish over the shag carpet.

Out surged the aroma from the challah and potato knishes baking in the oven. It lured us forward.

A flustered Rudi let us inside. Yon and Flutter greeted my grandparents.

I followed Rudi. Toward the heaping pan of kasha that was cooking on the stove and the pot of soup simmering next to it. “What’s the matter?”

She turned around. She flung her arms at me. “I can’t get the matzo balls right.”

Grandma cleared her throat. “We call them ‘knaidels.’”

“I can’t get the knaidels right.”

“How did you prepare the chicken fat?”

“They’re vegetarian.”

“Vegetarian knaidels? In chicken soup?”

“Parsley soup.”

Grandma staggered. Her legs wobbled. She had to grip the sofa with both her hands. “Vegetarian knaidels in parsley soup?”

She steadied herself. She captured Grandpa’s eye. “Have you ever heard of such?”

“Just now,” he deadpanned.

Grandma hissed at him. She shooed him away.

Taking Rudi by the arm, she led her into the kitchen. “Let us see what we can do.”

“At least, Gertie,” Grandpa called out to her, “at least you will not have to worry about eating unkosher.”

Rudi twitched. “Gertie? Your name is . . .”

“Gertrud.”

“So I’m . . .”

“Someone apparently named you after me.”

I was hoping that this would make a crack in the frost.

But the two wouldn’t stop arguing. They argued over the stove while messing with mishmashes of each ingredient in that kitchen.

A couple of times they came close to blows.

But, damn, those would be the best knaidels that I would ever eat.

 

 

BEFORE DINNER COULD start, Rudi had to light the Sabbath candles. She had to recite the blessing over them.

Everyone was nervous for her. Including Grandma.

Rudi placed a scarf over her head. She lit the candles.

She was the most nervous. Her hands were trembling.

Lighting the candles was easy. So was bringing the light’s spirit into her home with her hands.

Harder was singing the Hebrew prayer.

None of us were sure what to expect. Rudi must’ve been the least.

Out came her voice. Along with the stillness that passed through the room as a response to it.

She slid toward us. “That bad?”

No one answered. But “bad” couldn’t have formed in any of our minds.

That didn’t prevent Grandma from becoming pained.

It pulled everyone toward her. Including Rudi.

She lowered her scarf. She stepped to the woman. “What’s wrong?”

Grandma shook her head. “Your voice, it, it is your mother’s.”

The two fell silent. They didn’t stir.

Grandma broke this. She broke down. She took hold of Rudi. She cried.

Rudi didn’t react. She stayed quiet. Motionless.

“I miss her so much,” Grandma said.

Rudi warred with her own feelings. It was all over her face as she wrapped her own arms around the woman. “I miss her too. I don’t want to, but I do. I can never remember the awful. No matter how I try. I remember her buying me cotton candy at the circus. Cradling me after a nightmare.”

“I remember too. Whenever I close my eyes.”

“She . . .”

“She never could let herself be a part of us.”

Rudi’s face lit up. From what she would say next, she had gotten where Grandma was coming from. “‘Your people are my people,’” she murmured, quoting from the book of Ruth. “‘Your God is my God.’’”

Grandma cried harder. She held Rudi harder. She murmured back with a quote from the same verse. “‘And not even death shall separate us.’”

The two continued their embrace. These two women whose differences couldn’t have been starker. The granddaughter of a Nazi and a woman whose life the Nazis had wrecked. A punk rocker and a gentlewoman. Separated by generations and continents and mores.

Yet they were the same.

 

 

SO STUFFED WERE we after dinner that none of us could budge.

None more so than Flutter. She had sprawled herself out on the floor.

Rudi stretched her arms. “Grandpa, I want to hear the rest of your Kafka story.”

“Oy vey,” Grandma yelped. “Do not get him started. You will never get him out of your house.”

The entire room laughed.

None more so than Grandpa.

But he wasn’t going to let this opportunity get by him.

He filled Yon in on the story’s beginning. He brought it to an end.

 

 

“BROD!” HAŠEK YELLED.

His voice was so piercing that it was painful to hear as he followed the man down the hospital corridor in his wheelchair.

None of this was Hermann’s business. But he dipped his head through the door.

His curiosity over why Hašek would care what Kafka wanted Brod to burn had overwhelmed him.

Why would he care about Kafka at all?

Hašek chased Brod in a corridor bereft of light. The two men were figures.

Panting for air, Hašek struggled forward. “If you don’t stop, I will follow you to Prague. I will haunt you to your last days. I swear I will!”

Brod slowed to a halt.

Hašek stopped behind him.

Lifting himself from his chair, he grabbed Brod. “What does he want you to burn?”

Brod was silent.

Hašek shook him. “Answer me!”

“His novels.”

“Novels? He has written novels?”

Brod didn’t answer.

“Are . . . are they like his stories?”

“Better.”

“And you, you’re gonna burn them?”

“What can I do?”

Hašek threw Brod and himself into a wall. He forced the man to face him. “What gives you the right?”

Brod pointed to himself. “They’re not mine. They’re his.”

“What gives him the right, the selfish little yid!”

“Let go of me.”

“Listen to me, you worthless wretch. You’re gonna publish those novels. Every one.”

“No.”

“The greatest writer Prague has set its senses upon, and you’d turn his poetry into ash? You’d be damned. For such a crime there can be no absolution!”

“Let go of me!”

Brod pushed Hašek away. He crashed to the floor. Brod rambled off.

“You will not burn them, Brod!” Hašek shouted. “You can’t do it! His words won’t char!”

 

 

HERMANN WENT BACK to his bed.

Kafka lifted himself up. “What was that about?”

Hermann rolled over. Away from the man. “I can’t say.”

Hašek came back to the room. He was seething at Kafka.

Kafka returned the gesture. “What was that screaming about?”

Hašek waved him off. “I can’t see how it’s your concern.”

The man got near the two and veered right. He thrust himself forward.

But he didn’t go far.

He paused, his back to both men.

“What is the problem?” Kafka asked.

Hašek lowered his head.

“Did you hear me?” Kafka asked next. A bit louder.

“You’re looking at a jealous man, Herr Kafka.”

“Jealous?”

“When I told you the other day that I had read some of your stories, I lied.”

“What do you mean?”

“I’ve read them all. Every single one. I have them stacked in my home. I’m jealous of you. Of your talent and triumphs.”

“Me?”

“I wish I could write like you. That my words could flow as yours do. From the page onto someone’s soul. But I’m only a scribbler. It doesn’t matter how many books I sell. Nothing will change that I’m a failure.”

Teary-eyed, Hašek turned toward Kafka. “I never wanted the money. I wanted to be a real writer. Not some ‘peddler of dreck,’ as you called me. That’s why I got so mad at you. You weren’t the first to call Švejk dreck. That’s what the publishers called it. The reason that I had to put it out myself wasn’t because they believed that it wouldn’t sell. They were certain it would. It was because they didn’t want to be associated with dreck. Or with me.”

Hašek yanked his wheels from them. He rushed off.

“Švejk isn’t dreck,” Kafka yelled.

Hašek came to another pause.

“I was lashing out at you. The truth is . . . I loved every page. Every single one. You’re a modern-day Rabelais, Mr. Hašek. Švejk stands eye to eye with Pantagruel and Gargantua. If this happens over the heads of publishers, never you mind. History is never nearsighted.”

Kafka’s face brightened. “Let me tell you a story. I was crossing Palačák when I came upon a group of boys. They were playing out a scene from Švejk. The last one from the first book. If I remember right. The one where Lieutenant Lukáš curses Švejk for selling him the colonel’s stolen dog. These boys, they performed it so perfectly. With such delight.”

A coughing fit struck Kafka.

But he shook it off. His face brightened more. “Through them, Mr. Hašek, Švejk will live on past you. He will live forever. These children, they will pass him onto their children and their children’s children. And were you aware that Max is preparing a German translation of it?”

Hašek jolted in his chair. “He is?”

“Soon the whole world will love Švejk. They will love him as we do. He is each of us. Our follies and our fears. But far more our joys.”

Kafka sank himself. “I should be who’s envious. Of you. You called yourself a failure. But they’ll forget me before they lower me into the ground.”

Hašek rubbed the tears off his chubby face. “Don’t be so sure about that, Herr Kafka. Don’t be so sure.”

 

 

LATE INTO THE evening, Hermann was giving it one final go at convincing Kafka to go home with him.

“If you won’t live for others,” he said from his bed, his eyes on the ceiling, “what about for yourself? What would you miss?”

Kafka said nothing.

Hermann crossed his arms. “Oh, what I would miss. I’d miss the sun setting over the Vltava. I can’t imagine life without it. More than that, I’d miss the marvel in the eyes of a child when you tell them a story. I would’ve given any price to see it in the eyes of my child. The one who was stillborn. And what about the kiss of a woman? You can’t tell me that you wouldn’t miss that.”

Kafka continued saying nothing.

Hermann gave up. He went to sleep, consigned to leaving the following day without him.

 

 

HERMANN WOKE IN the night.

Kafka was reading from a slim hardcover book. A first edition of The Metamorphosis. The only edition at the time.

The man’s gaze was on it as if it were his son or daughter.

Hermann yawned. “I have the best argument yet for you to live.”

Kafka ignored him. He went to the next page.

Hermann lifted his head toward the man. “It’s rather selfish, I’ll admit, but I’d love to read another of your stories. I’m not alone.”

 

 

HERMANN WOKE FOR a second time.

It was morning.

Kafka was standing over him in a suit, his open and packed suitcase on his bed. “You better hurry. A single train leaves from here. In an hour.”

Hermann jumped onto the floor.

But he froze. His nightshirt had jumped out at him. “My clothes.”

“I sent Nurse Černá to get them.”

Kafka’s copy of The Metamorphosis was on Hermann’s end table.

Hermann tilted at it. “What’s this doing here?”

“A belated Chanukah gift.”

Hermann picked up the book. He checked inside its cover.

Kafka had inscribed it to him.

“Thank you, Herr Kafka,” Hermann said. “Thank you.”

Kafka opened his mouth. But the spinning of wheels interrupted him.

Hašek was heading their way in his wheelchair.

Crawling to a stop, the man was in a dour mood. “Would you gentlemen do me the honor of escorting me to the dock?”

He raised his fist. He showed them the one-heller coin between his fingers.

 

 

KAFKA WHEELED HAŠEK to the staircase. The one on the cliff overlooking the river.

Hermann was paces back. He was carrying Kafka’s suitcase.

Hašek leaned over the steps. At the six people boarding the boat from the dock.

He balked. “I had no idea that there were so many stairs. I won’t make it.”

Kafka offered him his hand. “But we will.”

Hašek took the man’s wrist.

Kafka brought him to his feet.

“I see it now,” Hašek sang, his eyes rising toward Kafka.

“What?”

“Your father.”

Glee overcame them both. Neither could hold it back.

They made their way down the steps, their arms around one another. Together, each complemented the other. To the extent that they appeared as one healthy body.

“You think they’ll have some good pubs?” Hašek said.

“No question about it.”

“I’ll save you a seat.”

“I’d like that.”

The two men tired. They rested midway.

“Can I call you Franta?” Hašek asked.

“You may.”

“And you, you call me Jarda. That’s what my friends call me.”

The two soldiered on. They got to the dock.

Hašek paid Charon his fare.

Kafka helped him into the boat.

It pulled away.

“You know what?” Hašek called out, his face reflecting the blinding sun. “I’m no longer afraid.”

“Neither am I,” Kafka said while waving his friend goodbye. “Neither am I.”

 

 

GRANDMA TOSSED UP her arms. At the row of floodlights on the ceiling and at what was above them. “Kafka must be sick of that story. Hašek too.”

The entire room laughed.

None more so than Grandpa.

Flutter leapt to her feet. She joined in. She bayed.

Rudi grasped Yon’s hand. A hint of dampness was in her eyes. “Well, I loved it.”

Grandpa had been right.

The story did have meaning for her. The same meaning that the Black Flag record had for Yon.

 

 

SPRING MADE ITS entrance. With it, Yon’s eighteenth birthday.

This would’ve been a feat for anyone. For him, it was bigger.

To celebrate it, Grandma cooked him dinner at our house.

As a way of welcoming him into our family, she made him the array of Czech dishes that she could make without meat.

This wasn’t numerous. But it was delicious.

Stuffed peppers. Fried mushrooms. Lots of dumplings. Potato and fruit.

 

 

THE NEXT DAY, Rudi held a party for Yon by the waterfall.

She had told us that the only gift we were to bring was ourselves. She must’ve recognized how difficult it would’ve been getting gifts for him.

What do you give someone who’s about to die?

I drove Yon and Rudi and Flutter to the falls late that morning. Along with a cake Rudi had baked. A stack of Reservoir pizzas. Soda.

Owen and Eliot were waiting. Jared and Lis showed up later. So did Mariana.

A motley bunch we were, sitting on the rocks in the sun. Steeped in a tape of Yon’s favorite Yardbirds songs that Rudi had made. It was echoing in the skies.

Over “Happenings Ten Years Time Ago,” and Yon and Owen’s arguing over whether Beck or Page was the better guitarist, I took account of each of us. Myself the most.

Not a shred of grounds existed for us spending this time together.

But here we were. Here we belonged.

 

 

THE PARTY ENDED early.

I helped Rudi carry stuff to the car on Crest Drive.

She asked me to bring Yon back from the falls while she said goodbye to Mariana.

I returned to the rocks.

Yon was hanging over them next to Jared and Lis. Their eyes were dipping into the ravine and the water churning onto it.

“Some people back home have the same,” Jared said. With Lis holding on to him from behind. She held on with all she had. “It’s only dumb luck that I don’t.”

Yon’s eyes dipped deeper into the water. “There could be a reason.”

“What reason could there be for me to live and not you?”

“That’s not what I mean. What you survived could give you a reason to do what you wouldn’t’ve done. Something big. Important.”

 

 

WE LEFT THE reservation in the Matador. We passed South Mountain Elementary, heading to Pathmark and the groceries Rudi needed.

Reading Is Fundamental was having a readathon behind the school. Volunteers were welcome.

“Let’s do it,” Yon cried.

“Are you really up for it?” Rudi asked.

“Come on. It’ll be fun.”

Yon had exhausted himself that day. It was why Rudi had shut the party down early.

But we couldn’t refuse him on his birthday.

I drove down a steep hill. To the blacktop behind the school.

Scores of children were waiting. A trio of adults were organizing them into groups.

It was no surprise that the adults got scared on whiff of Rudi.

But what was surprising was how the kids didn’t share this fear. Her presence drew them as if she were a superhero.

That wasn’t nautical miles from the truth.

All the children wanted to be part of Rudi’s group. They also loved Flutter and the matching spiked collars the two had on.

They encircled the dog. They petted her.

“Be gentle,” Rudi said to them. “She’s been through rough times.”

The kids took heed. Some may have been recalling their own rough times.

 

 

THE THREE OF us ran reading groups on the field below the blacktop. Plenty of people did.

Rudi’s was the largest and loudest. It got the most notice.

The kids in hers took turns on her lap. Others sat next to Flutter.

With patience and fostering, Rudi helped them voice out the syllables that made up each tale they read.

Like she had with Owen, she was repaying a debt that afternoon. For when the librarian took the time to teach her to read.

What I remember is how she made reading magical for those kids. She bestowed upon them a secret wand.

It opened worlds. As many as they could imagine. The marvel on their faces remains with me.

For hours we read with the kids. But Rudi did nonstop with hers. It was impossible to tell who was enjoying it more.

 

 

THE SUN FELL. The readathon’s organizers had to bring it to a close.

Kids in Rudi’s group screeched.

But Rudi was the most disappointed.

“You should become a teacher,” the woman running the event told Rudi.

Earlier, she had been the most terrified person there. Now she was the least.

 

 

YON STAYED HEALTHY in the months that followed. He stayed away from the hospital.

I couldn’t tell that he was sick. Or I could pretend that he wasn’t. I could pretend that these wonderful times, the best that would come my way, would last forever.

But I would learn the truth of this period from his journal.

They still hadn’t given his disease a name. Rather, they hadn’t come up with one that everybody could agree on.

Nor were there treatments for it. Dr. Kleinsten could only treat his symptoms.

These had no bounds.

Among them were the Kaposi lesions that would form on Yon’s face and body.

As Rudi had with Mariana, she taught Yon how to use makeup, after tinkering with foundations and concealers till she got the right blend.

Rudi’s care didn’t stop there. She would often have to change their bedsheets during the night due to the sweating Yon would do. At times, she’d do this twice a night.

On top of that, she had to nurse him through each cold and sickness. Any could’ve been fatal.

I was ignorant of all this.

Rudi never let on to how much they were struggling.

I remember her as happy during this time. I would never see her as happy as she was with him.

 

 

DESPITE THEIR TROUBLES, Yon and Rudi developed the kind of everyday routines that any couple would.

Such as hikes into the woods with Flutter.

“The beautiful afternoons of normal,” Yon would call them.

They wouldn’t stop arguing. They’d argue about the news and about politics and about music. They’d argue about the weather.

But these arguments never mattered to him.

Not a moment with her hinted of dull. Or one when she wasn’t holding his hand.

 

 

RUDI HELD YON’S hand when the two went to Dr. Kleinsten’s office each week.

They would go over his state. They would get the latest test results and prognoses.

No matter how depressing this got, Rudi would glean a positive.

Until she couldn’t.

The doctor gleamed at them from across his desk. “I have some good news. A hospice that’s willing to take Yon.”

Rudi’s mood fell with her head. “Hospices are for people who’ve given up.”

“It would make both your lives easier.”

Yon agreed. He was tired of fighting the disease. He wanted to ease Rudi’s burden. He wanted her to have a life outside of taking care of him.

Rudi would have none of it. “We’re not giving up. We’re not.”

 

 

ANOTHER OF RUDI and Yon’s routines was spending their Saturdays at Vintage Vinyl.

They would bring lunch. They would pass the day playing records with Butch.

Not only their kinds of records did they play.

Some days they would pick a bunch at random. They would play them the whole way through. They played records that they wouldn’t have otherwise. Like Johnny Cash at Folsom Prison and Patti LaBelle.

Falling for the latter, they bought it. They had Butch make a tape of it for them.

On other days, the two would binge on the British imports that Butch would get because he liked the bands’ names. Records from the likes of the Cure and the Psychedelic Furs and Echo and the Bunnymen. All years away from becoming known in America.

Another was a single from a group as obscure in England as they were here.

It got Rudi so worked up that she called me. “You’ve gotta listen to this. The guy sings, he sings as if he means every word.”

She put the phone to the record. I got my baptism to the voice of Morrissey and “Hand in Glove.”

The pair also spun the Coltrane album that I had happened upon during my maiden visit to the store.

Yon loved the music. But he loved more the poem that Coltrane wrote in the liner notes. The one that he played his saxophone to in the record’s final movement.

Two lines of it, in particular, took hold of him. They wouldn’t let him go.

 

God breathes through us so completely . . .

So gently we hardly feel it . . .

 

 

THE ENTRY PUZZLED me.

How could Yon’s faith have grown the closer he got to the bottom? How could he have had greater hope the greater his hopelessness got?

I’ve gone through nights answering these questions. I don’t believe that I’ll do so in full.

But could this eighteen-year-old boy have tripped upon the purpose of faith? Was it to raise us when we have no place left to plunge?

 

 

RUDI AND YON didn’t spend their time at Vintage Vinyl only for the music. Or for the poetry.

They also went for Butch, who would make them laugh through the day.

This was possible because Butch didn’t care about Yon’s disease. He refused to treat him in a different manner because of it.

He was as rude to him as he was to everyone.

 

 

THE LAUGHTER AND music and hope couldn’t fill the scope of Yon’s days.

On a gray and drab morning, he sat beside his bedroom window as the leaves of fall tumbled from the trees.

He sat beside the window into the afternoon. He was sitting there in the dark.

Rudi slipped through the door.

That summer, she had graduated from high school. She’d gotten a job. One with personal meaning.

She was working at the first counseling center in the state to help the increasing number of those like Yon. Those diagnosed with what they had just named AIDS. She often worked late.

“What is it?” she asked.

He wouldn’t tell her. He kept fastened to the window. He kept himself on it after she helped him from the chair into bed.

She joined him. She wrapped her arms and legs around him. “Tell me. Please tell me.”

He wouldn’t.

But she wouldn’t stop pressing.

He relented. “Today I realized, I realized that I’d never see the leaves grow.”

No words from Rudi came back.

The last Yon would pick up on before nodding off was how she was struggling not to fall apart.

 

 

YON WOKE WITH the sun.

He woke with a jarring.

It came from Rudi’s absence and the pots in the room.

They were everywhere. Filled with young and green and growing life.

Bringing another into the room was Rudi.

She was filthy, having spent the night hauling plants from a nursery down the road. She must have spent herself too.

But she refused to show it.

“It’s spring,” she said with her eyes matching the morning in the window. “It will always be spring.”

 

 

RUDI HAD TO work on select Saturdays.

On one, I drove Yon to Vintage Vinyl in her place.

We left the car with a pizza. I couldn’t avoid how slight Yon had gotten in the face.

It must’ve been why he would wear an Oakland A’s baseball cap when he went out. So others wouldn’t have to avoid it as much.

The cap couldn’t hide Yon’s hair. How it wasn’t so wavy.

But he wasn’t so doomed either. He stood in defiance of his fate.

This was on display to the world while we entered the empty store.

“You’ve got the new DKs album,” I blurted out, laying the pizza onto the counter.

Butch pulled out a slice. “Go ahead, put it on.”

I whipped out Plastic Surgery Disasters. I hurried behind the counter with it to the turntable.

Yon stopped by Butch. “Rudi’s having a birthday.”

“Me too,” I said while dropping the record onto the player and the needle on top of it.

“Him too,” Yon continued as the Dead Kennedys filled the store. You could tell that it was them from the opening chord. “But I’m at a loss as to what to get her, and he’s no help.”

Butch lowered his pie. He swallowed. “Don’t look at me. There’s a basis for why I’m single.”

“But you guys love the same stuff.”

“Except you.”

“Come on, if you were having a birthday and could have whatever you wanted, what would it be?”

Butch didn’t need an instant to answer. “To see Bad Brains live.”

“They’re good?”

I slanted toward Yon. “They’re good.”

“Do they play at that A7 place?”

“They usually play at CBGB,” Butch said. “But they left town.”

“What about . . . what about Black Flag?”

“They’re in LA. They do come out here to play. But not now.”

“Lee Ving?”

“You mean Fear? They’re also in LA.”

Yon tossed his head at the record I was playing. “Them?”

“San Francisco.”

“Is there anyone good playing around here?”

“I’ve got flyers somewhere.”

Butch checked underneath the counter. He checked through the clutter.

He yanked out a bunch of papers. He set them down.

One made Yon shine.

 

 

RUDI DRAGGED HERSELF into her apartment. This after a longer than a full day at work.

Yon dragged her out.

She whined. “Where are we going?”

He dragged her into the corridor.

She shut their door. In front of a charging Flutter.

This didn’t keep the dog from making herself heard.

Yon’s dragging continued. The two got to the building’s front door.

Rudi resisted. “I’m beat.”

He flung himself and her outside. “You won’t be.”

A driver in a black uniform was standing by a white limousine. The kind that she might’ve ridden in if she had gone to her high school prom.

Her head went sideways. “What’s going on?”

“Only one way to get an answer.”

 

 

AMID HEAVY TRAFFIC in Lower Manhattan, the limousine idled beside a parked car by CBGB.

Rudi put her nose to the window. “Why are we stopping here?”

Yon guided her chin. Toward a poster on the wall.

It announced that Richard Hell was performing that night.

“Happy Birthday,” he said.

Rudi staggered.

But through it she kissed Yon. She kissed and kissed him. Nothing could get her to slow.

 

kisses are a better

fate than wisdom

 

Yon recalled the poet’s words. The ones that had come from the classroom on the day he’d met Rudi. He recalled, too, the man’s overromanticized notions of life.

He was living them.

 

 

HAND-IN-HAND, RUDI AND Yon passed through the crowded bar. On the way to the back where the bands played.

“Rudi?” a voice bellowed.

She released Yon’s hand.

To her left was a young woman with blue hair and facial piercings. Next to a group of punks that made Rudi conservative looking.

“Leila?” Rudi gasped. She squeezed the woman.

“Where have you been?” Leila squealed, squeezing Rudi back. “You haven’t been anywhere.”

“I’ve been busy.”

The two let go of one another. Rudi said hello to the others.

Retaking Yon’s hand, she introduced him.

Stinging stares followed. Yon didn’t fit in with her friends any more than Rudi had with his.

Yon didn’t take offense at this. He whispered in Rudi’s ear, “I’ll meet you by the stage.”

“I’ll go with you.”

“Stay with your friends.”

“But . . .”

He swung his head at a door. “I’ve gotta go to the bathroom.”

 

 

YON DODDERED INTO the graffiti-covered men’s room. He was burning up.

So sick was he that he had to lean against a wall.

He slid down it to the floor. He couldn’t say if he would get up.

“You missed Bad Brains,” Leila wailed at Rudi outside the door. “You missed every show.”

“I had no clue they were in town.”

“You said you loved them.”

“I do love them. I play that tape you gave me eternally. I couldn’t get through a day without it. Remember how we would skip over the slow reggae songs? I don’t anymore. I especially don’t skip them.”

“We’re gonna follow them down the coast. Straight to hell. We’re leaving after last call. Why don’t you come with us?”

“I can’t.”

“Why not?”

“I’ve got a better to-do.”

“What could be better than Bad Brains?”

“There’s only one ‘what.’”

“It’s got to do with that guy, doesn’t it?”

“It’s got to do with that guy.”

“Who is he anyway?”

“I told you, his name is Yon.”

“Yeah, but who is he?”

“The boy I love.”

“The boy you what?”

“He’s what gets me through the night.”

No longer was Yon sick. He was rising from the floor.

 

 

YON TOOK A seat at an empty table by the stage. As a peak plummeted onto him.

He had believed that he needed Rudi more than she needed him. That their relationship was one-sided.

But after hearing what she had told Leila, he had to reject this.

It didn’t make him happy. It scared him that he wouldn’t be around for her. It shook him into a daze.

He came out of it. Rudi jumped onto his lap.

He grumbled. “Rudi.”

“What?”

“It’s your birthday. Hang with your friends.”

“They’re not my friends. We just love all the same music and hate all the same wrongs. That doesn’t make you a friend.”

She added to this by singing to him. “You are my friend.” She sang it like Patti LaBelle had on the record the two had fallen for.

Laying her head on Yon’s chest, she said, “You’re my friend, despite not loving all the same music. Or hating all the same wrongs. You’re my friend in spite of it.”

Rudi touched Yon’s face. She vaulted to her waist. “You’re sick. We’ve been through this. You’ve gotta tell me when you’re sick.”

“I’m okay.”

“We’ve gotta go home.”

“We’ve gotta stay.”

“Do you want to go back to the hospital?”

“I am going back. We both know it. But not tonight. Tonight we’re watching Richard Hell.”

“Why?”

“Because it’s your birthday!”

“Each day is my birthday! Each day I come home to you, it’s my birthday. Each day I wake to you, it’s my birthday. I don’t need this.”

Yon wouldn’t yield. He wouldn’t let her make a fuss over him.

She could only hold onto him and hope for the best.

They waited for Hell to play. This went on.

 

 

HELL AND HIS band came on stage.

They performed “Blank Generation.” The song that had helped two lonely people see that they weren’t alone.

Yon wrote—to Hell singing for the jillionth time about the feelings that marked the passing of our youth—if he had spotted the two below him, he might’ve spotted how their eyes were stuck on him. With their arms and bodies entwined.

To them, he was singing the most beautiful love song ever written.

 

 

YON HAD BEEN right. He returned to the hospital.

Would he ever leave?

It was a question that he asked on most days.

We were determined to make these days happy.

For Yon, that meant reading as many classics as he could. Both old and new.

I raided my grandparents’ library to make this happen.

On one raid, in a darkened nook, I came upon a tattered and slim hardcover book that startled me.

An original edition of The Metamorphosis.

I snatched the volume. I opened its cover. “It can’t be.”

Despite my bad German, I could read the inscription.

 

To my true confidant Hermann,

 

Franz Kafka

 

From when I had first heard Grandpa’s Kafka story, I was doubtless that truth was in it. But I was also doubtless that this was well at the bottom of it.

Now I wasn’t so doubtless.

 

 

I BLUNDERED INTO Yon’s room, grappling with a heap of books.

Rudi was by him on the bed.

Above her was a picture on the wall. Of a boy who longed for what he couldn’t picture.

She was reading aloud. From The Collected Poems of E. E. Cummings.

 

here is the deepest secret nobody knows

(here is the root of the root and the bud of the bud

and the sky of the sky of a tree called life;which grows

higher than soul can hope or mind can hide)

and this is the wonder that’s keeping the stars apart

 

i carry your heart(i carry it in my heart)

 

Yon crept his head over the book. “I wish I could write those kinds of words to you. Words that speak without spoken.”

“Yours speak plenty.”

I knocked on the door. The Master and Margarita was wiggling from my grasp.

The two greeted me.

Rudi climbed off the bed with her book. She sat in a chair beside it.

Behind her was a bureau and a box of Reservoir pizza. Next to it was a pair of Cokes.

She took out a slice. She offered it to Yon.

He couldn’t eat. All the weight that he was losing couldn’t influence this.

I released my books beside another heap. On the bureau by the other side of Yon’s bed.

From the old heap I lifted The Manuscript Found in Saragossa. “You done with these?”

“Yeah.”

My head swayed. Toward a copy of Ficciones under the pizza box. “What about the Borges?”

“Nah.”

“You’re telling me that you can get through Boccaccio and Potocki in a couple of days but can’t finish that puny one in a week?”

“I can’t stop reading it.”

Rudi spied the book. “What’s so special about it?”

Yon reached for it. “I’ll show you.”

His reaching lingered.

Rudi put down her pizza. She got the book for Yon.

He flipped through the pages. He halted in places. He came to the story that he wanted. “This is my favorite. ‘The Secret Miracle.’”

“That’s Grandpa’s favorite too,” I said. “He reads it each anniversary of his first wife’s death.”

Yon lifted his head at Rudi. “It’s set in Prague.”

She took the book. She faced the story’s opening page. “What’s it about?”

“This playwright. Months before World War II. The Nazis arrest him. They sentence him to death. And he’s upset about it.”

“I should hope so.”

“But he’s not upset about dying. Well, he is kind of upset about that and about how it will happen by firing squad. But what upsets him more is that he’ll never write his opus. His reason for being. So he begs God for a one-year reprieve. So he can write his play. And, guess what, God grants him his wish.”

“He doesn’t get shot?”

“Oh, he gets shot. Right on time.”

“I don’t get it.”

“On the morning of his execution, the soldiers stand him against a wall. They fire their guns. But the bullets freeze. The world freezes. The playwright freezes. Though he’s conscious throughout it.”

Yon paused. He stepped into the story. “What’s a fraction of a second for everyone becomes a year to him. He gets to write the play in his head. He doesn’t care that it won’t be famous. Or celebrated. Or known. Achieving greatness is enough. He dies happy. Isn’t that wonderful?”

Rudi squirmed in her seat. “I guess.”

Yon edged toward me. “She’s not much of a believer. No matter how often her nose is in that Bible.”

Rudi stewed. She rocketed to her feet. “How could I believe in a God that would let this happen?”

Not waiting for an answer, she scampered from the room. She might not have been aware that she was clutching the book.

Yon’s eyes followed her out the door. “I have my reason for being. My greatness.”

 

 

I STAYED WITH Yon late into the night.

We discussed the books that he had read. He had so much fire for them that I felt guilty for taking them for granted.

I felt guilty for taking so much for granted.

He dozed off.

I took the old books from the bureau. A paper that had been on it flew to the floor.

It was in Yon’s handwriting. Titled “angels wear spikes.”

I placed the page, along with the words that spoke without spoken, beneath the new heap of books. I left the room.

Rudi was on the floor outside the door. She was gripping the Borges book. It was gripping her too.

I stepped to her. “You okay?”

She didn’t answer.

I sat with her. Her eyes were on the last page of “The Secret Miracle.” “Did you read it?”

She shook her head. But not at my question. “It’s not a story. It’s not fiction. It’s not.”

“That’s his style. He writes as if he were making authentic and objective observations.”

“No, it’s true. All of it.”

I said nothing. Years later, the truth in Borges’s works would overwhelm me. In this story the most. But at that moment it was only a story.

Unsure of what to do, I took Rudi’s hand.

She pressed her shoulder against mine. She cried.

I wanted to cry too.

 

 

I WOKE BEFORE dawn.

Rising to my waist, I made out voices and music from downstairs.

“It is heroic what you are doing for those people,” Grandma said in the kitchen. Over a scratchy German cabaret record that was playing in the den next to it.

“They’re the heroes,” Rudi countered. “Not a day passes that they don’t humble me.”

Hearing the two this early didn’t shock me. They talked over the phone a lot. Late into the night. They got together as often as possible.

They were making up for lost time.

“How are you holding up?” Grandma asked.

“I’m okay.”

“You forget that I raised your mother. You lie as badly as she did.”

Rudi was silent. I could sense that she was wrestling for words. This carried on. “What am I gonna do? What will I do when he’s gone?”

Grandma wrestled with her own words. She did so longer than Rudi had. “You will do what I did after my first husband passed. You will live. You will live big. You will live so big that when the two of you meet afterward, when you meet for good, he will be proud of you. You will be proud of you.”

She and Rudi must’ve hugged. No further words passed between them.

Till Grandma’s. “You are yet grating the potatoes too finely.”

“Sakra!” Rudi cried.

“You speak Czech! Did your mother teach you?”

“My brother.”

“Oh, do not learn it from him. He tortures it as much as your grandfather does. I will teach you. I will teach you its poetries. It is so pretty that people do not speak it. They sing it.”

“What’s that music you have on?”

“Dora Gerson. Artur and I, we would drive to Berlin to hear her.”

“It’s wonderful.”

“You like it?”

“I’m gonna make a tape of it. Yon will love it too.”

“You keep surprising me, Rudi. It is such a joy at my age to be surprised.”

“What’s the song about?”

“Times that cannot return.”

The two danced. Their footsteps bounced off the linoleum floor. Onto the walls and the ceiling. To the beat of Grandma’s voice singing with the music.

 

Vorbei, vorbei, vorbei—

Ein letztes Wort,

Ein letzter Gruß—vorbei.

 

Grandma’s voice cracked. I wasn’t the only one who picked up on it.

“What’s wrong, Grandma?” Rudi asked.

“They burned her,” she said, her voice cracking more. “They burned her at Auschwitz. But they could not burn that voice. Listen to it. It outlives them all.”

 

 

I LEFT MY bedroom that morning. I reached the landing.

Rudi and Grandma were putting on their coats in the foyer.

Grandma reached her hand into her purse. She took out a check.

She offered it to Rudi. “Your grandfather and I want to give this to you.”

Rudi wouldn’t take it. “We’re doing okay.”

“Why will you not let me spoil you?”

“You already spoil me, Grandma. With the one thing I need.”

 

 

GRANDPA AND I poked ourselves into Yon’s room.

He wasn’t alone.

A sea of patients had joined him. In wheelchairs and IVs. For their daily afternoon “rag session.”

The men were tossing a football around. They were teasing each other. They were flirting. They were singing along with The Gap Band’s “You Dropped a Bomb on Me,” playing on a handheld radio.

They were living.

Grandpa and I came to Yon’s bed.

The Master and Margarita was in his hands.

“We are picking Rudi up for lunch,” Grandpa said.

Yon pointed the book behind himself. “She’s visiting Cary.”

Grandpa tapped the book’s cover. “How was it?”

“It says that Bulgakov spent his last twelve years working on it.”

“He could not get it published while Stalin and his like were around. So he rewrote it and rewrote it and rewrote it. That is why it is so unmatched.”

“He couldn’t have suspected that it would ever come out. What motivated him through each page? What got him up each morning?”

“It must have been the woman that he based Margarita on.”

 

 

CARY WAS ONE of Rudi’s clients.

Grandpa and I passed through the door of a tallish man with a thinnish beard. He had the same tubes coming in and out of his body that Yon had.

“I’ll be ready in a moment,” Rudi said.

She introduced us.

Grandpa held out his hand to Cary.

The man didn’t take it. He slunk Rudi’s way. “Have you told him about me?”

Grandpa’s hand stayed out. “There is nothing that she could say that would make a difference.”

The two shook hands. Neither wanted to stop.

Grandpa leaned over the bed. A box behind Cary on a dresser had drawn him. “Is that a domino set?”

“Yeah, unfortunately it’s a lost art. Nobody can play.”

“I love dominoes.”

 

 

NO ONE COULD have predicted what would happen. How Grandpa would show up at the hospital a lot.

He lavished his time not only on Cary. But on all the AIDS patients. He became a surrogate grandfather to them. The most to those whose families had shunned them. He would be there whenever they needed a kind word. He’d play board games with them. He’d bring them gifts. He did what any grandpa would do.

More than that, he listened to them. He never judged.

In return, Grandpa got a receptive audience for his detective stories. More than that, he got purpose. No longer did he waste his afternoons with a TV.

Never did I hear him listen to Pergolesi anymore.

 

 

ONE PATIENT WAS immune to Grandpa’s charms.

His name was Tony. He wouldn’t respond to anyone.

He was already dead. He was waiting for his body to accept it.

Grandpa refused to give up. He would sit by him. He would tell him his stories. The length of afternoons at times.

This didn’t work.

So he told him the story that he seldom told. Of Ana’s death.

He cried himself to sleep.

 

 

GRANDPA WOKE WITH a startle.

It came from a tray that a nurse had dropped when she entered the room.

Grandpa hadn’t been the only one crying. Tears were running down Tony’s face.

The nurse shook her head at them. “How?”

“I suppose,” Grandpa said, “I suppose this terrible disease cannot rob you of what makes us human.”

 

 

RUDI CARRIED ANOTHER box of Reservoir pizza into Yon’s room. With the daylight disappearing in his window.

She brought him pizza whenever she could. She brought it that day, disregarding how they had connected him to a feeding tube, in addition to those that helped him breathe and get fluids. She brought it because it connected him to his old life. To being alive.

Yon’s mind was on a bureau. On the simple metal menorah on top of it that the rabbi had left that day.

“It’s gonna be Chanukah soon,” he said when she didn’t say a word. She didn’t say hello.

Rudi swayed to the menorah. She lifted it. “Grandma’s been showing me how to make potato latkes. For the first time ever, we didn’t have to substitute ingredients.”

“I must admit, when I was a kid, I had serious Christmas envy. My friends, they had Santa Claus and reindeer and Christmas trees. And we had a bunch of candles. So the oil in the Temple burned for eight days?”

Yon pushed himself toward Rudi. “But I get it now. The miracle wasn’t the oil lasting eight days. It was that it lasted longer than what anyone could expect. That’s what we have to be thankful for. That’s what we always have to be thankful for. The exceeding of expectations.”

Rudi didn’t speak. She didn’t have to. Her glazed-over eyes said it.

 

 

YON SET ASIDE how sick he was. So he could celebrate Chanukah at home.

From the way that his story had begun, he must’ve been curious as to what would happen during the replay of it.

I, too, was curious.

But Yon made but one entry during the holiday.

 

Chanukah was different this time, and I got a better gift. But Rudi’s holding back.

 

 

“WHAT DOES THIS mean?” I said to Rudi over the phone.

She grumbled. “Why are you asking me?”

“What was it that you were holding back?”

“I can’t guess what he’s talking about.”

“What was the gift you gave him?”

“I don’t remember. Not only the gift but the holiday.”

“What about the old man in the store at the story’s beginning? The guy who slipped the envelope into your bag? With the wrapping made from gold stars?”

“That’s not how I remember it.”

I didn’t believe her. Not a word.

But I continued with the entries.

 

 

YON RETURNED TO the hospital after Chanukah.

On his first day back, I staggered to and past his door.

In my arms was a collection of tomes from an unabridged edition of Burton’s translation of One Thousand and One Nights. It was anchoring me to the floor.

Yon was sealing a bulging and blank envelope.

I nodded at it. “You want me to mail that for you?”

“I’ve got it.”

I strained to lift the books. “This should hold you awhile.”

The telephone rang.

I unloaded my bundle onto the bed. I hurried to the phone.

Beside it was an oversized book. A multilingual volume from the Talmud.

These are teachings on the Torah. The Bible’s first five books.

They apply what’s in them to solve unsolvable problems. Such as, “why does God allow suffering?”

The book confused me.

It hadn’t come from my grandparents’ library. Nor had Yon requested it. It wasn’t the type of enjoyable reading that he enjoyed.

I answered the phone.

“It’s me,” Rudi said. “I had to work late. I missed the last bus. I hate to beg . . .”

“I’ll be right there.”

 

 

I TOOK A step from the elevator. Into the hospital lobby.

Rabbi Orenstein was on a sofa in the distance. Across from a middle-aged woman who had her dirty-blonde hair tied back.

I set out toward them. I wanted to say hello to the man.

He cringed. At what the woman had said to him. “I told you that I would keep it a secret. But I shouldn’t.”

She affected disinterest. “How’s he doing?”

“Better than I would’ve presumed. Rudi has—”

“—I don’t want to talk about her. Or hear about her.”

“But you should.”

The woman furrowed her brow.

The rabbi sighed. “I’ll admit that she’s challenging to the eyes. But beneath that . . . I’ve been a rabbi for decades, I visit families caring for their loved ones without end. But none as devoted as that young lady.”

The woman got madder. “She feels guilty.”

“Guilty?”

“She’s the one who did this to my son. By dragging him into the sewer with her.”

I wanted to rage at the woman. I wanted to tell her what an idiot she was. How wrong she was. How she couldn’t match Rudi’s height if she climbed the Empire State Building.

But this wasn’t what Rudi would’ve done. Or what she would’ve wanted me to do. Beyond that, I didn’t have the guts to say it.

Instead, I raced outside.

This was when I raged.

 

 

RUDI WAS WAITING for me on a street corner when I came to her office building in Florham Park.

I never got how she got her job. She couldn’t have been qualified for it out of high school.

But her personal experiences must’ve counted more. Those she had in excess.

Also, not many could’ve wanted the job.

This required her to reach out to people with the disease. Both at the office and in the hospital. She would will her days to men who had lost hope. Despite how she was in short supply herself.

That could’ve been the point. To raise all boats in the rising tide.

What they would talk about, she never told me. What she did say was that she would often do nothing but hold their hands. Or hug them.

She’d make them feel that they mattered. That someone loved them. That they cared whether they were alive.

That evening, through the darkness, the months of doing her job, along with taking care of Yon, were weighing on Rudi. She was about to fall over.

Part of this came from what she was carrying. A few of those oversized Talmud volumes.

She plopped them onto her lap as I drove off.

I tapped the top book. “There was a time when they didn’t allow women to read these. Recently.”

“Those times aren’t coming back.”

“Well?”

“Well, what?”

“Have you gotten any answers in them?”

She turned from me. Into the emptiness in her window. “No matter how I try, I can’t stop searching.”

 

 

“PIZZU DO NEMOCNICE přivezu,” I said to Rudi while driving down South Orange Avenue.

She gave a shot at repeating it.

But she got stuck on the “ř.” A sound that can take English speakers months to get right. If ever.

I exhaled. “You sure you want to learn this?”

She heaved a breath. “Under the ridiculous consonants and the grammatical absurdity lies a chunk of me.”

All her life, Rudi had been a leaf blowing in the wind. Now she had uncovered the roots. But she was figuring out how to plant them.

I smirked. “You’ll need nine lives before you can speak Czech.”

“It’s no harder than Hebrew. Or Aramaic.”

I checked out the books in her lap. “Are you reading these in the original?”

“It’s hard enough getting at the truth. But when you’re not reading the right words . . .”

 

 

WE GOT TO Reservoir.

I parked outside it. So Rudi could get dinner for Yon and herself.

“Stay here,” she pleaded when I opened my door.

“Why?”

“Please.”

Puzzled, I stayed in the car.

She slithered into the busy restaurant.

In its window, her motive for not wanting me with her jutted out.

The workers were glowering at her.

Among them was an old man in an ill-fitting suit by the register. As well as the woman behind the counter, who dropped a pizza box onto it in front of Rudi.

It bounced multiple times.

Heads in the restaurant sought why.

Rudi ignored the bitterness.

Fighting to project apathy, she paid for the pie with haste.

She left with more.

 

 

I LEFT RUDI off at the hospital.

She stayed up late with Yon. They shared their day of hopes and fears.

They weren’t the only ones sharing.

“I can’t wait to meet her,” Rudi said into the phone. From the chair beside Yon’s bed, where she was reading a volume from the Talmud.

She listened to the reply. She continued to the next page. She got annoyed. “I am going to college. I want to be certain for what.”

A voice answered hers.

Rudi paid more notice to Yon and his stupor. “I’ve gotta run. I’ll call you next week.”

She hung up. “Mariana says hi. So did Owen, Jared, and Eliot. I had breakfast with them this morning. With Lis away, they’ve literally become the Three Musketeers. They go everywhere together.”

Yon raised his eyebrows. “Who could be responsible for that?”

Rudi sneered.

It didn’t last. She let her book down onto the other volumes.

Taking out a slice of pizza, she brought it to Yon’s mouth.

He whisked himself from her. He knocked his water tray onto the floor. “I hate it. And what I hate worse is that I hate it. I hate this place. I hate this bed. I hate me.”

Rudi wouldn’t give up. She had become used to these outbursts. “You don’t want them to put back the feeding tube, do you?”

Feeding tube.

The two words were enough for Yon to gobble pie. The tube made him feel more machine than human. He gobbled as much pizza as he could.

But the words weren’t enough for the pizza to remain down. He had to clench his eyes closed. He had to force his body to obey.

Sweat poured down his face. His body shuddered.

The misery eased. But it took its time.

Yon opened his eyes. To Rudi.

She was holding his hand. Greater pain was on her face than what he felt. She wanted to take his pain. She would’ve taken it all if she could.

He fought for what to say to her. He was flailing for it. “Why, why do you go through this?”

“Through what?”

“You could’ve sent me a card. No one would’ve blamed you.”

Fury fired through Rudi. The same that had been there when the two had it out.

She let go of his hand. She let the pizza fall onto the box. She stood. “We need to talk.”

Yon gave his grudging assent. He squirmed his frame to his left, careful not to let his IV come out.

She joined him. She draped her arm around him.

He followed suit. He lowered his head onto her chest. He grabbed her hand with both of his.

She didn’t talk. She was waiting for inspiration.

It came. “Let’s be clear,” she growled in a tone desperate to sound true. “I didn’t do any of this for you. I did it all for me. I’m the most selfish person on earth.”

Yon responded in caresses. He caressed her hand with his. He caressed her arm with his cheek.

She waited for more inspiration. She waited more than she had before.

Gulping a breath, she raised her head toward the ceiling. “I . . . I’ve become tired of my look. So what do you say I get some new clothes? A new haircut? I could be, I’m gonna be, I’m gonna be someone who won’t embarrass you.”

This time, Yon didn’t respond. In any way.

“Did you hear me?”

He took his time replying. He had to make each timbre of his voice strike the right note. “I fell in love with a punk rock girl. A girl who was nobody but her. Nothing less will do.”

Rudi buried her head in his shoulder. I suppose to stop herself from crying.

Clutching him with all her might, her voice rasped, “You were wrong about the falls. They’re not ‘the best anywhere.’ You are. You, you’re my fairy tale.”

 

 

GRANDPA AND I stopped by Cary’s room. Grandpa had wanted to finish a dominoes tournament that the two had begun days before.

Cary wasn’t there.

Rudi was on his stripped bed.

Her arms were around her legs. Her apathy was full on.

“Where’s Cary?” Grandpa asked.

“Gone.”

“Gone?”

“He passed away last night.”

Grandpa shuffled toward the bed. Ages went by before he got there.

Sitting next to Rudi, he held her. “I have seen too many loved ones go. I wish I had some potion to give you. But what I can say is, if these people, if they stay inside you, they never pass.”

Rudi rested her head on Grandpa’s shoulder. “He’s the third this month. I can’t keep doing this.”

“If you do not, who will?”

 

 

YON NO LONGER had the energy to read.

We took turns doing it for him.

Grandpa’s Kafka story had gotten him interested in The Good Soldier Švejk. He asked me to tell it to him.

“‘Do you know, Švejk, what a march battalion is?’” I read from Grandpa’s cherished copy one afternoon.

“‘I respectfully report, Lieutenant, sir, that a march battalion is what we call a maršbaťák, as a marška is a march company. We are forever shortening.’

“‘Well, Švejk,’ came the lieutenant’s grave voice, ‘since you enjoy such shortenings, I will inform you that you’ll be joining me in a maršbaťák. But don’t think that on the front you’ll get to partake in the kind of stupidities you have here. Does that bring you joy?’

“‘I respectfully report, Lieutenant, sir, that it brings me tremendous joy,’ answered the good soldier Švejk. ‘It’ll be something truly wonderful when the two of us fall on the battlefield for the emperor and his family.’”

Yon and I laughed. We couldn’t do otherwise.

Rudi popped her head into the room.

Her eyes were on Nurse Templeton, who was checking Yon’s vital signs.

She did this without a mask. Or gloves. Or fear.

Petite and a few years older than us, the nurse had curly blonde hair that settled below her shoulders and pale blue eyes. She was among the few in the hospital who wanted to take care of Yon.

She requested the assignment, becoming a personal nurse to him and to other AIDS patients.

She walked past Rudi on her way out.

“Thank you, Kim,” Rudi let out, meaning each word.

I offered Rudi a book.

Pohádky Boženy Němcové.

She took it. “What is it?”

“Fairy tales. Grandpa would read them to me when I was a kid. They’re so famous over there that the woman who wrote them is synonymous with the genre.”

She opened the cover.

“It’s not exactly your taste,” I said, “but it’ll help you with learning—”

“—It’s exactly her taste,” Yon interrupted.

Rudi blushed.

But she shook it off. “You up for company?”

Yon sat up. “Sure.”

My grandparents came into the room.

Grandma was carrying a casserole dish. “You must be tired of hospital food and that pizza. So we brought you some noodle kugel. Vegetarian, of course.”

She took it over to Yon.

To make room for her on the bed, he shifted over.

His IV fell out.

Blood dripped down his arm. “Stay away from me!”

Grandpa wouldn’t. He snagged a towel. He used it to clean Yon’s arm. “Blood cannot frighten me.”

Grandma wasn’t frightened either. She sat next to Yon. She fed him.

The phone rang.

Rudi picked it up.

The voice ascending from the receiver dumbfounded her.

She walked the device to the room’s corner. Her dumbfounding was doubling with each step.

“Mrs. Levy?” she whispered. She whispered it at a volume so low that, if I hadn’t seen her lips move, I wouldn’t have gotten what she had said.

The lone response was Rudi’s disappointment.

She turned to Yon.

Somehow he could tell who she had been talking to. The disappointment was on his face too.

 

 

RUDI WORKED LATE so often that my picking her up became a daily ritual.

Part of this entailed getting a pizza at Reservoir and the bitterness that went with it.

It never wavered.

On one gloomy night, a sheen rose over it.

Rudi collected the pizza at the counter. She started off.

But she froze.

Rabbi Orenstein and his eyes were embracing her from tables over. They embraced her with pride and ardor. As if she were his own child.

This wiped away the apathy she was feigning. She battled to make it out the door.

 

 

RUDI AND I got off the elevator at Yon’s floor with the pizza.

We started down the corridor.

“Rudi?” Dr. Kleinsten called out.

His voice was ominous. So ominous was it that Rudi had to force herself upon the man.

He was forcing his feelings down his throat.

“What is it?” she muttered.

“The pneumocystis has gotten worse. A lot worse. If, if you have special plans . . .”

“Have you told . . .”

“Yes.”

“When? When will he . . .”

“I wouldn’t leave him tonight.”

Rudi meandered toward Yon’s room. I followed her.

The doctor took a few steps with us. “I have to say this. You gave that boy a year of life. You alone. That doesn’t happen every day.”

She said nothing.

We approached Yon’s room and the coughing and labored breathing inside it.

I wanted to tell Rudi words too. I wanted to tell her how I loved her. How wonderful she was. How all the ugliness I had been through didn’t matter.

Without it, there would be no her.

I wanted to tell her this so much. But the words wouldn’t come.

We got to the doorway.

Some words came to her. “I’ll call you tonight.”

She took a step into the room.

But she stopped. She hugged me.

“I wish I could say . . .” I stuttered while struggling to hold myself together. For her sake.

“You’re saying it.”

 

 

YON WROTE OF how he had taken the news the doctor had given him.

He had taken it better than Rudi had. He had been waiting for it. From his waking in the hospital after the beating he had taken.

In a way, he was relieved. His suffering would give way.

The only pain after he died would be Rudi’s.

For stretches of time, he foraged for the means to lessen it.

But none showed up.

 

 

RUDI MADE IT into Yon’s room.

He was turning a page in the Borges book.

Hearing her footsteps, he hid the book under the covers. “Sorry.”

“You don’t have to hide it. Or be sorry for it.”

“I don’t want to upset you.”

“You can’t.”

“I bet I can.”

“How?”

“I talked to Him today.”

“Him?”

“I believed that I had all that I wanted. I swear I did. But today I realized that I didn’t. So I asked Him.”

“Asked Him what?”

“I asked Him . . . I asked Him if I could have you.”

“You have me.”

“I mean after.”

Rudi couldn’t make her lips move. She couldn’t breathe.

She kept bolted on him until she could do both. “What did He say?”

“He hasn’t gotten back to me yet.”

“He must be busy.”

“Yeah, and there was that, there was that I cursed Him pretty badly a while back.”

“He must be forgiving about that.”

“I hope so.”

Rudi chased another round of inspiration.

It took her lifetimes to catch it.

Her eyes fell upon the door. “I need to go somewhere. But you’ve gotta promise me that you won’t go anywhere.”

“Go?”

“Promise me.”

He measured what she had asked. For moments he did. “I love you.”

She sprinted from the room. “I love you too!”

“Where you going?”

“I’ll tell you tomorrow!”

Yon hoped she was right. He hoped through the night.

 

 

I LOWERED THE last of Yon’s pages that evening.

Tears were running down my face. I couldn’t avert them.

Scrambling, I wiped the moisture away with the back of my hand.

I made out that the street people had wrapped up showering. They must’ve had for a while. Though the floor was wet with their footprints.

It sparkled from them.

“One night is left,” I said to Rudi while struggling to hold myself together. For her sake.

“Call me tomorrow. But hours later than normal. I have a dinner following service.”

I hung up. I left the bathroom.

The women’s shower was on in the room steps from me.

“You owe me,” drummed Josh’s voice from inside it.

“Not like this,” Wendy bawled. Fright was overwhelming her voice.

“I could throw you out,” Josh said, his tone yielding but in control. “I could throw you out for just about any reason. Any at all.”

Quiet followed. Too much of it.

I wanted to bang on the door. I wanted to scream at Josh. I raised my fist.

But the weaker part of me was stronger. Despite the so-called “moral code” within me.

I took the path of ease. Of cowardice. I wormed down the hall. I convinced myself that I could do nothing for Wendy. That I’d only get myself thrown out and put on the streets. Without work. Or escape from myself.

Worse, Josh would get away with it. He would continue to get away with it.

I reached the men’s quarters. I fell to my knees.

A volley of hate had sent me to them.

It was directed more at me than at Josh.
