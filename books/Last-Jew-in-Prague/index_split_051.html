chapter fifty-one: good king wenceslas

 

 

GERTRUD TOOK A stroll around town.

She would say that she did this to forget the night before. To cleanse her soul and body.

But those weren’t the only reasons.

The reasons didn’t matter. What did was that the early spring air was fresher than the day before. Each building was prettier than normal.

At the foot of Wenceslas Square, she hiked toward the top. She did so remembering the walks that she and her husband had taken on it.

On most evenings they took one while discussing the day’s happenings. No matter how busy he was, he’d carve out an hour for it.

Often it was late at night. But he’d make time for it. He’d make time for her. Even when the Wehrmacht was massing on the border.

Only now did Gertrud realize how spoiled her husband had left her.

No man she’d met since was like Artur.

Though she would say that Hermann shared some of his better qualities. Such as his intelligence and his strength of character and his reverence for women.

She had discovered that Hermann was more interesting than how she’d discovered him outside Černín Palace. She lamented how she would never see him another time.

Gertrud reached the square’s top.

She faced the statue dedicated to the person that the square had been named after, who straddles his galloping horse there to this day.

Humor struck Gertrud.

It came from how the man—who we in the English-speaking world know as Good King Wenceslas—was nearer in appearance to Don Quixote than to the patron saint of Czechs.

Was this what Myslbek had intended when he sculpted the statue over the decades that he labored over it?

Gertrud saw a lot of herself in both the star-crossed monarch and the starry-eyed knight-errant. This must’ve been why she laughed.

She didn’t tarry.

Aware that she had a lot to do that day, she strode down the square to her home.

A note that Kamča had left was waiting for her. It mentioned that she’d gone food shopping.

Gertrud penned her own note for Kamča.

This took time. She had to forgive the woman for the spying that she had been doing while in her employ. She needed this to come across with sincerity. For both their sakes.

She never got the chance to finish the letter.

A heavy vehicle screeched to a stop outside. It sent her to a window overlooking the street.

Three Nazi soldiers were rushing from a truck. Into her building.

This was not a surprise to her.

So, collecting her coat and purse—inserting into the latter an old jewelry box that her mother had given her ages earlier—she marched into the spare bedroom. She leapt out a window facing the courtyard.

Concealing her fright from herself, she climbed the wall to the building’s third floor. To a window that she had left ajar for this purpose.

She sprang inside it. She zoomed to the unit’s entrance.

The soldiers were banging on the door below.

They didn’t keep this up. They broke through. They stormed inside.

Gertrud whisked off her shoes.

With them in hand, she swung open the door. She soared down the stairs. She left her building.

While in motion, she put her shoes back on in the courtyard. She jumped into her car.

She drove to Národní. She stopped across from the Palác Dunaj.

Tonda’s car was gone.

Gertrud couldn’t dally. But she also couldn’t leave right away.

She flew into the boarding house.

A sight by the window did surprise her.

Klara was boring her eyes into the street. Much as she had the day earlier.

The difference was that she was smiling.

“Klara?” Gertrud cried.

Klara didn’t respond. But she didn’t stop smiling.

Gertrud hopped to her. She sat on the armrest of her easy chair. She smiled too.

“Have you a reason for smiling?” Gertrud asked, despite having seen this smile on her own face.

Klara kept silent.

“It starts that way.”

“What does?”

“You convince yourself that you are lost. That you’ll never be the same. But one day you smile without realizing it. This is how it starts.”

“What?”

“The road home. It’s a long journey. But it has to start somewhere.”

“I relive what happened to me each day.”

“As strange as it may sound, so do I. But instead of it horrifying me, it now motivates me. All that I do, including helping you, comes from the hate that I’ve turned on its head.”

“I could never do that. Or begin to. I’m not as strong as you.”

“That’s what I believed about myself. I believed it right up until I discovered, in the light that was shining onto me, that I was stronger than I had believed. This moment will happen for you too. I guarantee it.”

Gertrud hugged Klara. She kissed her cheek.

It didn’t matter that the warmth wasn’t returned. This would occur in the future when Klara would turn the light given to her upon another.

For now, Gertrud took comfort in Klara’s smile.

She sprinted from the house with her own smile.

Noting that Tonda’s car was still not in front of his building, she dove into her own car. She sped off.

She didn’t care where she was going.
