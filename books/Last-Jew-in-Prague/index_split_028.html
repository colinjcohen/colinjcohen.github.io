chapter twenty-eight: under the glare of jan hus

 

 

THE RAIN HALTED.

Klaus’s Mercedes idled by Hermann’s apartment building. Next to the two guards, who were under an awning. As they had when the pair left them earlier.

Hermann opened the car’s back door. With a drawn-out moan, he lifted himself outside.

This moan would become another of Hermann’s trademarks. I would run into it a lot in the downward spiral that defined my teenage years. It would precede a talk about how disappointed he and my grandma were in me.

Hermann twisted back to Klaus. “Would it be okay if I took a short walk? I need to unwind.”

Klaus waved his hand in assent.

Hermann closed the door. He started down Eliška Krásnohorská Street.

Passing the woman’s street sign, he recalled how much she had meant to Ana. How her oversized presence had inspired Ana. During her time at the woman’s prep school and after it. It had been the reason that she and Hermann rented their apartment on her street, named after her years before she died. In the same way that they had named the train station after President Masaryk before he passed.

“Čest její památce,” Hermann murmured. Honor to her memory. A saying Ana would murmur when they walked by the sign together.

Hermann continued down the street.

Klaus must’ve signaled the guards. The two tailed Hermann.

He didn’t notice them. Or what was in front of him. His notice was on a shuttered art gallery. He stopped beside it.

What was on his mind, he never told me. But I have a conjecture. Based on a story that Věra had told me. A story that Ana had told her.

 

 

ANA HAD HER arm wrapped around Hermann’s. They were strolling down a busy sidewalk, a couple of years after they had been married.

It was a hot and bright summer day. Ana was holding a pink parasol in her free hand. It protected both their faces.

People gawked at the two and their difference in height. This would happen whenever they were out together. On most days, it would amuse them. But on this they weren’t paying mind to it.

Their reasons weren’t the same. Excitement had overcome Hermann. While gloom had Ana. The same that had consumed her since graduating from school and discovering no market for her art. He had to drag her from the house that day.

Her eyes drifted from him. “You must hate me.”

“What are you talking about?”

“You gave up your dreams for me. And for what? I’m good for nothing, it would seem.”

They reached the art gallery. Hermann stopped in front of its window. “Wouldn’t this be a great spot for that self-portrait of yours?”

Ana skimmed over an empty easel. She made no comment. She expressed no emotion.

“So, some weeks ago, I went inside. I told the owner this.”

Ana gasped. “You didn’t.”

“I did.”

Ana shook with anxiousness. “And?”

“And she kindly showed me to the door. That’s when I saw the store across the street.”

Ana turned, releasing Hermann’s arm. She faced an empty shop window. “So?”

“So, I figured if the painting were in that window, she’d have to see it.”

Ana gasped. “You didn’t.”

“I did.”

“You rented that store? With what?”

“With the money I’d been saving.”

“You were saving that for Bled. You almost had enough.”

“We’ll get there one day. You need to get here right now.”

Ana’s face reddened. “That’s just wonderful, Hermann. You wasted that money, and I don’t see my painting anywhere.”

He returned to the gallery window. “You need to take another look.”

She took one.

A smiling woman was placing the self-portrait in the empty easel.

Hermann nodded at her. “She represents select painters. She wants to see all your work.”

Ana didn’t speak. She couldn’t. She teared up. She gripped Hermann’s arm. She gripped it like her life depended on it.

I bet it did.

 

 

HERMANN TOTTERED FROM the gallery. He resumed down the empty streets.

He got to an Old Town Square that was empty as well. Apart from the statue of Jan Hus.

Drawn to it like it was the universe’s epicenter, he scrambled in front of it. He re-experienced a moment from the night that he had met Ana.

 

 

HERMANN WAS KISSING Ana. He could only do this because she was tipping toward him so their lips could meet.

He was also clutching her hand. Which was clasping the single white rose that he had bought her while her other hand made its way through his “impossible brown curls.”

Their bodies were apart. Their lips and eyes were closed.

It was Hermann’s first kiss. Despite him not being much of a boy. He would say that it was so powerful that it created a sense of calm in him from which he never wanted to return. It also made him content with himself for the first time.

Some intangible opened his eyes. To the rose in Ana’s hand.

He plucked a petal from it.

Stuffing it into his pants pocket as a remembrance, he shrunk under the towering figure of Hus. A pre-Reformation martyr. A Czech national hero.

The man was glaring at Hermann. He was far from pleased.

What would happen to Hermann if Hus were alive? Would he be the one who got burned at the stake?

Ana opened her eyes. Her face became flush. She broke their kiss.

She ran toward the safety of Železná Street.

Not sure what to do, Hermann followed her. Like a puppy dog he did. “What’s wrong?”

“I was not expecting that kiss, Herr Weiss.”

“I can’t imagine what came over me. I’m not like that. Really.”

Ana pointed behind herself. At the statue. “And under Master Hus, no less. My father would kill me. We’re Hussites, you know.”

“I didn’t. Je mi líto.”

Ana, hearing Hermann apologize in Czech, hearing the sincerity in his voice, slowed.

So did he. But he kept a safe distance back.

“It’s okay,” she said. “But give me some warning next time.”

“I will. I promise.”

She ceased to a halt. She spun toward him.

He halted with her.

“What was it like?” she asked.

“What was what like?”

“The war.”

He didn’t answer. He was surprised that he wasn’t hearing the sounds of battle in his ears. He hadn’t left the calm state that he had experienced in her kiss.

She whined. “Did you hear me?”

“What?”

“What was the war like?”

He eluded her eyes. “It, it was nothing like those romantic novels you read when you’re a kid. It was nothing like them in the least. For months, we’d fight over the same trench in the middle of nowhere. At Verdun it was the worst. We weren’t trying to win there. It was about killing as many people as possible. On the premise that none of us mattered. Not only the enemy. But our own people. I saw so many die for a trench, Ana. A lousy trench. I was scared the entire time. I tell you that not for a second was I brave.”

“But you saved those men’s lives, didn’t you?”

“Not as that newspaper let on. It wasn’t my intention to save them. I was trying not to get killed. Nothing further than that. I don’t deserve any of those medals. I can’t tell you how often I’ve tried to throw them away. Including the one that the emperor pinned on me.”

“But you didn’t throw them away, did you?”

Hermann shook his head.

“Because you are a hero. You acted in disregard of your fear. That’s what matters. So I never want to hear you say that you weren’t brave. Or have you imply that you aren’t a hero. Not if you want a relationship with me, Herr Weiss. Is that understood?”

He choked on his words. “Yes, no . . . relationship?”

She kissed him. It was more passionate than before.

Shocked, he broke their embrace.

She, too, became shocked. “What’s wrong?”

“You’re Klaus’s girl.”

Ana laughed.

“What’s so funny?” he cried.

She tossed her hands onto her hips. “Here you are, this brave war hero, wrapped inside a naive boy.”

He mustered outrage. But it wasn’t in him. “What do you mean?”

“It’s not me Klaus is in love with,” she said.

This was in a tone that made clear that he had been oblivious to the obvious.

 

 

IN A FLICKER, Ana was gone. What remained was Hermann and the empty square. Along with the statue of Hus.

He was glaring at Hermann. As he had two decades earlier.

Hermann headed back. His two guards were waiting for him at the square’s edge.

“It was real,” he said to himself while clutching the rose petal in his pocket.

He persuaded himself of what he was saying.

 

 

HERMANN RETURNED TO Eliška Krásnohorská Street.

The Mercedes was by his building. Klaus’s sad and lonely eyes were on him.

It’s not me Klaus is in love with.

Unlike when Ana had said this, Hermann was no longer oblivious to the obvious.

But he wished he were.
