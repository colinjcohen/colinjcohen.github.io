chapter forty-nine: the papers on the floor

 

 

HERMANN WOKE WHEN the sun blistered through the car’s windshield.

Klaus was asleep next to him. He was shaking in fright.

This was worse than what he had been experiencing on the ride from Theresienstadt. He was struggling for his life.

Beside the man’s feet were papers. They must’ve fallen during the fight the night earlier.

Hermann hesitated. But he picked the papers up.

Could Klaus have been truthful about what he had promised him in Janeček’s office?

Hermann’s hesitance heightened. But he unfolded the top page.

It was a bill of lading. For a shipment that Klaus had sent to a Karl Schmidt in Basel, Switzerland. Described as “a belated wedding gift.”

The document puzzled Hermann. He had no idea that it was how Klaus had saved Ana’s two paintings from the flames. The pair that I had seen at the National Gallery, whose existence Hermann would never learn.

The paper below the bill of lading was more interesting to Hermann. It was a pension request that Klaus had filled out for Dita. A woman whose role in this story was minor. But whose role in my life would be great. She would give birth to the girl who’d become my mother.

Hermann smiled. At both the form and Klaus.

An engine started up the road.

Hermann stuffed the papers in Klaus’s jacket pocket. He shook him awake.

“What?” Klaus babbled, stuck in his nightmare.

“You fell asleep,” Hermann said.

Reidl’s Mercedes drove past them.

Hermann followed it with his eyes. “But you got lucky.”

“It’s about time.”

Hermann started the car. He trailed Colonel Reidl.

He led the two through the ancient, winding streets of Malá Strana.

Unlike the night before, Hermann was behind the man.

Due to the daylight and his belief that Reidl would be driving to the palace, he didn’t apply the same maneuvers that he had the night before. He sat back. He enjoyed the goldish tint that was coming over the buildings. It made them more fairytale-like than normal.

But Reidl wasn’t heading toward the palace. His Mercedes led Hermann and Klaus back over the river. To the other side of Prague. He crossed a bridge south of Mánes Bridge that had been Legion Bridge and was now Smetana Bridge.

The Mercedes parked in front of Café Slavia on Národní Avenue. Reidl got out of his car. He went into the building.

Hermann made a left at the intersection. He parked on Smetana Quay. It left him and Klaus facing the café’s other side.

The two peered into the windows. Their eyes fluttered.

Colonel Reidl sauntered to Mr. Malý. The men greeted each other like the best of friends. They sat and talked.

Hermann opened his door. He took a step outside. “I’ll be back in a minute.”

Klaus rose in his seat. “Where you going?”

“To have a look around. Keep watching them.”

 

 

HERMANN RETURNED TO the car. “Anything happen?”

Klaus shook his head. “Where did you go?”

Hermann sighed. “No one’s watching the building. From either side. Didn’t you say that you arranged a tail on Malý?”

Klaus shrugged. “You’ve read The Good Soldier Švejk too. I guess this is part of their passive resistance.”

Hermann smirked. Despite what had happened the night before, he liked Klaus. This was mutual.

The men returned their focus to the café.

A waiter dropped a cup in front of Malý. He rushed off without paying heed to the colonel, who had raised his hand. He had opened his mouth to order.

Klaus yawned. He stretched his arms. “I could use some of that myself.”

Hermann smirked some more. “You’d get it faster by flying to Turkey than you would by walking into that building.”

Both laughed.

They laughed more when Hermann added, “And you’d get real coffee.”

The laughter came to a close.

Reidl reached into his coat for an envelope. He placed it on the table. He slid it toward Malý.

The man snatched it. He stuffed it into his jacket pocket. He checked the café. Each corner of it.

Klaus leaned forward. “What could be in that?”

Hermann leaned too. “It could be the key to the mystery. Or nothing but payment for some legal services that Malý had provided.”

The colonel and Malý stood. They said their goodbyes. They said them as they had said their hellos.

Reidl exited the café. He returned to the Mercedes.

Hermann started the car. “It’s decision time, Klaus. Either we go on following Reidl or we follow Malý. It’s your choice.”

Klaus couldn’t decide. “What do you suggest?”

“Malý.”

“Why?”

“Because if Reidl had had some legal affair that he’d wanted handled, he would’ve gotten himself a better lawyer.”

The Mercedes took off.

Hermann huffed. “What’s it going to be?”

“Malý. Let’s follow Malý.”
