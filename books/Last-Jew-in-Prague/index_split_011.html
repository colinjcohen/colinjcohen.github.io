chapter eleven: the pickle jar by the window

 

 

THE DOOR SQUEAKED open.

Hermann took a step into his old apartment. With heaps of hesitation.

His first reaction was that Klaus had been right.

The place was a mess. Broken walls. Garbage strewn everywhere. The Nazis had also stripped it of value. Apart from the memories. They were in abundance.

Hermann ignored them. Or pretended that he did.

He took another step.

The guard in the hallway closed the door behind him.

Hermann had become a prisoner in his own home. But it was a better prison than his previous one. It was better than sleeping in the office too.

He went on.

Reaching empty easels on the living room floor, he turned toward the kitchen.

The empty pickle jar was by the window where Ana had left it. An upside-down picture frame was close to it on the floor.

Hermann crept to the frame. He picked it up.

Through the broken glass was a photo of Ana and him on their wedding day.

A day that came alive.

 

 

HERMANN WAS BACK in Mr. Jacobs’s suit. He was carrying his bride across the threshold, who was wearing a simple white dress. She was gripping a simple white rose bouquet.

They had arrived after a basic ceremony at City Hall that both their families had boycotted. Hermann’s mother had been so upset by the wedding, by his marrying outside his faith, that she held a funeral for him. She sat shiva in mourning.

Not a single guest had been at the ceremony.

But the two didn’t care. They were overcome with the promise ahead. Hermann the most. He would say that he was glowing more than the bride. He was also punch-drunk from the kissing that they had been doing in the ceremony’s aftermath. He was so intoxicated with his new life state that he couldn’t feel the burden in his arms. He had only a vague sense from the scent of Ana’s favorite perfume ascending to him.

“It’s not Bled,” he said to her, referring to the mountain resort town in what was Yugoslavia. He had promised to take her to it for their honeymoon when they got engaged. But this didn’t happen. They ended up getting married much sooner than they had planned. He had no time to save for it.

She locked her eyes onto his. “I don’t need any Bleds.”

I can only imagine what she was feeling. But from the way Hermann would describe her on that day and from my conversation with Věra, I bet he wasn’t so much holding her up as he was preventing her from reaching the ceiling.

Hermann squeezed Ana. He squeezed her with all he had, making certain that she was real. “We’ll make it there one day. When I’m the richest lawyer in Prague and you’re the most famous artist in the city. We’ll stay right on the lake. At the best suite in the Grand Hotel. And I’ll carry you right up those 99 steps I told you about. Přísahám!”

Ana glowed more. At the vow that he had sworn in Czech. While I’m sure believing that the only vow that mattered to her was what he had taken earlier.

They both laughed. For no reason.

Hermann spun his bride around. His heart beat to the point that he was positive it would explode from his chest.

She began to fade.

 

 

HERMANN WAS FACING the broken picture.

Back when it had been snapped, he’d been positive that he would never be happier.

Depressed by this, he removed the shards of broken glass from the frame. He placed the picture on the empty mantle. Where it had been before. Where it belonged.

“Miláčku,” cooed Ana’s voice, expressing her pet name for him.

A recent and teasing edition of Ana was skipping into the bedroom.

Hermann rushed after her. He sprinted into the room.

Nothing but additional mess greeted him. Among it were the remains of their featherbed on the floor. Along with a single, bare pillow on top of it.

He stood above this. His body hollowed out. It hollowed from top to bottom.

His legs collapsed. He crashed onto the bed.

Grabbing the pillow, he clutched it. He rolled himself into a ball. “Aničko moje!” he cried out to his wife in her mother tongue. “Proč?”

He wanted her to tell him why.

But he didn’t get an answer. Instead, his past reanimated itself.

He was back on his first night in Prague.

 

 

HERMANN WAS RUNNING.

He was an hour late for his get-together with Klaus and Ana. The result of him getting lost in the city’s twisted streets for the first of untold times.

Fed up with the conflicting directions passersby had given him, he went into a bookstore. To buy a map.

They didn’t have any. But the shop owner took pity on him and his exasperation. He gave him an old brass compass from a junk drawer. A compass that I would know well.

The man waved Hermann in the right direction. “Head due west, young man. You’re bound to reach Hus Street eventually.”

Hermann got to both the street and U Zlatého Tygra. He stuffed the device into his jacket pocket. He stormed into the pub.

Klaus was in a jovial mood. He pointed to three full řezané beers on the communal table in front of him. “You’re way behind, my friend.”

Hermann trotted to the table, relieved that Klaus wasn’t mad at him.

A waiter brought a fourth beer.

Hermann waved the man off. “I have enough.”

The waiter gave him no mind. He placed the beer beside the others. He left.

“They keep serving them here,” Klaus said to Hermann. “Whether you ask for them or not.”

“Or whether you’re here or not?”

“We have three seats. So we keep getting three beers. You either drink up in this place or you get the hell out.”

“I guess I’d better drink up.”

He grabbed a beer. As the woman across from Klaus rose from her seat.

Hermann couldn’t have known from Ana’s picture that she was a head taller than him. But he had no doubt that it should’ve bothered him. It should’ve diminished the way that she had aspired him.

He wanted it to. He wanted to feel unaspired. He wanted this so much. For lots of reasons.

But nothing could hold down his enthusiasm for her. Including when his single bare pillow replaced her. What she had invoked in him years earlier was lingering and alive in him.

So was she.
