chapter forty-two: the circumstances of his death

 

 

KLAUS WENT TO his office while telling Hermann that he would get him at six.

Hermann headed to his own office. On the way, he stopped at Marta’s desk.

Inspecting the empty hallway in both directions, he opened the top drawer. He took out the folded-over piece of paper that she had left him.

He unfolded and read it.

 

Herr Weiss,

 

I went through police records. To check if we had a file on this Gertrud Gross. It turns out that we did. But the Gestapo pulled it soon after the occupation, and nobody could tell me why. Nor could anyone recall what was in the file.

 

Of course, I did not let this deter me. Because you said that she was a socialite, I called Pavel Bezstarosti. He’s the society editor at Lidové noviny, and he provided me with details about her.

 

He couldn’t speak about her background. But he said that she was married to Artur Gross, whose family has plenty of both money and royal blood. The man didn’t need to work. But he was a senior deputy in one of the government ministries. He was also well respected by both Czechs and Germans. According to Bezstarosti, despite Herr Gross’s German heritage, the man was the most patriotic person that he had ever met. People were talking of him one day becoming prime minister. Or president.

 

Bezstarosti further told me that the couple lived at a beautiful villa in Vinohrady and that they were known for giving some of the best parties in the city. He had been to a number and says that it was impossible to dislike either one.

 

He says, too, that Herr Gross died soon after the occupation. Though the circumstances of his death are murky. The predominant rumor is that he shot himself after discovering that his wife was having an affair with Reich Minister Frank. But Bezstarosti is dubious of this. At a minimum.

 

After her husband’s death, Frau Gross sold the villa and bought a building in Old Town, where she continues to hold lavish parties. An invitation to one is said to be prized among the Nazi elite. She’s also known as someone who goes through men faster than the weather changes. But Bezstarosti is as dubious of this as he is of her alleged affair.

 

The note was interesting to Hermann. It was so interesting that he kept it in his face after he had finished reading it.

Tearing it into shreds, he dropped them into a garbage pail.

He wasn’t certain whether what he had read would be useful. But he was certain that Gertrud had fascinated him more. He was almost anticipating the dinner party.
