chapter fifty-four: limitless unknowns and no equations

 

 

IN THE DARKNESS, Hermann focused the flashlight’s beam onto the arched door’s handle.

He unlocked and opened it. Klaus was on the other side of it.

The two hurried down the stairs. To a cellar like the one in the Pinkas Synagogue. It was packed with supplies. It had the same flat, deep, and immense footprints in the dirt floor that had been at the Pinkas Synagogue. These, too, led to a stone wall upon which someone had painted a Star of David. Leading to it as well were wheel marks and two pairs of normal footprints.

“This was where our friends went,” Hermann said.

He and Klaus stopped at the wall. Klaus gave it a once-over. “Another dead end?”

“Some matter must be behind it. Like what must’ve been behind the wall in the Pinkas Synagogue cellar.”

Hermann felt along the stones. He pushed and slid the wall in many places.

He couldn’t get it to budge.

Klaus pounded his fist against the wall. “It’ll take more than a battering ram to bring this down.”

Hermann’s pushing and sliding went on. “An easier way must be.”

He sought one out. He sought and sought it. He was certain that the only path to solving the mystery lay through the wall. It was also the only means for him to reach wherever fate had been directing him.

The minutes passed. The hours followed. But Hermann didn’t care. Only the wall mattered, and what was behind it.

Klaus didn’t have the same problem. He cared too much about time. He made Hermann aware of it.

Hermann ignored him. So obsessed was he with the puzzle in front of him. With its limitless unknowns and lack of equations.

His obsession had limits. He became compelled to give up.

He couldn’t voice this. Methodical and heavy footsteps interrupted him. They were descending a wall.

Klaus wheezed. “What’s that?”

Hermann opened his mouth. As the sound changed. To that of someone coming down a wooden ladder. “Metal rungs are on the synagogue’s back wall. They used to run up the entire wall. But now start three meters from the ground.”

“What happened to the others?”

“They removed them.”

“Why?” Klaus asked, fear scratching his voice.

Hermann gave him an accentuated grunt. “Because, because people kept climbing up them. To break into the attic. To see if a golem was hidden inside it.”

“That’s where he’s coming from. The attic.”

“It’s a legend, Klaus.”

“So how come we didn’t hear footsteps going up?”

“That’s a good question.”

Hermann started toward the stairs. But he stopped when Klaus didn’t follow. He shined the flashlight onto him and his fright.

Grunting for a second time, Hermann went back for Klaus. He snatched his arm. He dragged him up the stairs.

Reaching the ground floor, he dragged him outside. Into the evening’s darkness.

Stomping feet echoed in the distance. The same from the night before.

They thinned out.

The two ran to the building’s back. They halted by the ladder, the metal rungs, and the closed attic door.

Hermann directed the flashlight at the door. He started up the ladder.

He stopped when Klaus didn’t follow. His exasperation multiplied.

“I’ll wait here,” Klaus slurred, more afraid than ever.

Hermann sighed. He climbed the ladder’s remaining steps. As well as the metal rungs. He reached the attic door.

He gripped the handle. Unlocked, he opened the door. He lurched inside.

Moving the light across the room, he discovered that it was uninteresting apart from the floor. In addition to immense footprints of dried mud—those in the same shape as the ones in the cellars—were slippers.

Ivo’s.
