chapter twenty: three plates of venison

 

 

HERMANN BENT TOWARD the throat-clearing sound.

Klaus and Gertrud were standing beside his table.

Hermann got to his feet.

“I do hope that we didn’t keep you waiting,” Gertrud said. In a blue gown that couldn’t have had steeper contrast with the suit that Hermann was wearing. Each trait of hers contrasted with him.

Yet Hermann got the feeling that they weren’t that different. Also, as when they had first met, he felt more was going on with her than what she was projecting. This intrigued him.

“I got here early,” he stammered.

She took her seat. “I’m just glad you made it.”

Hermann intrigued Gertrud too. He was as intriguing to her as she was to him. She wanted to discover the source of this. “I’ve been so looking forward to tonight. You can’t imagine how much.”

Hermann and Klaus joined her at the table.

Joining the three was the waiter. He left three menus and a wine list.

Gertrud made not the slightest advance toward her menu. “They have terrific peppered venison.”

Hermann agreed. He remembered the evenings that he had eaten the dish here over the years. “My wife and I . . .”

He stopped himself. Klaus was glaring at him.

“Go on,” Gertrud said.

Hermann demurred. “It’s not important.”

“You two share some mysterious past, don’t you?”

“It’s not so mysterious.”

“If only finding the truth in men were as easy as finding the area under a curve. I’d take the definite integral between the two of you and discover what I wanted to learn. But I am going to find out about you two. You can trust me on that.”

Another objective drew Gertrud.

The waiter was shuffling past them.

She called out to him in Czech, “Pane vrchní!”

The man halted. He marched to their table. He fished out his pad and pen. “Ano?”

She ordered the meal. “Srnčí na pepři, prosím. Třikrát. S rýží.”

The waiter picked up the wine list. He showed it to her. “Něco k pití?”

“Váš nejlepší pinot noir.”

Klaus shied away. “Mléko pro mne.”

“Dobře,” the waiter let out. He grabbed the menus. He scampered to the kitchen.

Gertrud turned to Hermann. “I do hope you don’t mind that I ordered for you.”

“Not at all.”

She grasped Klaus’s wrist. “It annoys him to no end. He’s not used to a strong woman.”

Hermann shook his head. At how well Gertrud had spoken to the waiter. It embarrassed him too. He couldn’t have spoken anywhere close to as well. “Your Czech is remarkable. Not even Klaus can pronounce his ř’s like you. And I’ve forever envied his. Despite living here for twenty years, I can’t say them right. My wife . . .”

Hermann stopped himself for a second time. For a second time Klaus was glaring at him.

“I was born here,” Gertrud said.

The waiter brought Klaus a glass of milk. He brought Gertrud and Hermann a bottle of French wine. He showed it to her.

“Dobře.”

He uncorked the bottle. He served it to them. He left what remained on the table.

Hermann leaned back. He took Gertrud in. “I’ve known plenty of native German speakers who were born in Prague. Plenty can barely say hello in Czech. Most haven’t shame from it. In fact, a good friend of mine named Kafka was taught Czech from his earliest years. But he spoke it as haltingly as I do.”

“In my father’s house, the entire staff was Czech. I had to learn it if I wanted to keep up with the goings-on.”

“I bet it’s more than that. I bet this is more stimulating than my past with Klaus.”

At that split instant, the gaiety that had flowed from Gertrud from the instant Hermann had met her emptied out. Someone had popped her balloon.

She fumbled for words. “My husband, he was a native Czech speaker. While German, he insisted that we speak it whenever possible.”

The loss of air wasn’t the limit of what was afflicting Gertrud. Sounds and images overwhelmed her. Of her tear-soaked visit days earlier to Vyšehrad Cemetery. As well as to the terrible morning years before that. The morning she wanted to forget. The morning she had to forget.

But no matter how much she tried, or what she tried, she couldn’t stem the flow of images and sounds. The horror from them seeped from her.

Klaus cleared his throat. “We should change the subject.”

Hermann averted his eyes. From them both. What he had seen on her face he fathomed too well. “I agree.”

Gertrud struggled to inflate herself. “Klaus tells me that you’re a police detective. He also says that you’re helping him solve an important case.”

Hermann did a double-take. “He said that?”

“He actually said the opposite, leading to my conclusion. Which your face has confirmed. Thank you.”

“You’re welcome,” he babbled, shifting his eyes to Klaus, who shrugged in defeat.

The exchange forced Hermann to infer a truth about Gertrud.

Earlier, he had believed her beautiful and alluring but vapid. But he was now sure that she was a whole lot better. A human being was behind the caricature. A powerful mind was lurking beneath her beauty. It was as adept as what was on the exterior of her. If not superior to it.

Gertrud went on. “I’ve never heard of a Jewish policeman. I’ve heard of Jewish accountants and Jewish bankers and lots of Jewish lawyers. I’ve heard of a Jewish boxer. But never a Jewish policeman.”

“A greater number of us exist than you’d expect. Police serve best in the communities in which they live. And Josefov, where I lived and worked, was a community like others. With the same problems and crimes.”

“But there is no more Josefov.”

“Jews may no longer be in Josefov. But Josefov will never cease to be. Nor will crimes there. That is why I’m here.”

“So I assume that you haven’t been in Prague all this time?”

“I, I’ve spent months in Theresienstadt.”

Klaus was getting antsy.

This amplified when Gertrud sat up in her chair and said, “It must have been some crime for them to drag you out of Theresienstadt to solve it.”

“Crime is relative. Especially in moments like these. What may appear a major crime to some people, to others is minor in comparison to other crimes.”

Gertrud could feel her interest in Hermann rising. It rose so that she was falling toward him. “I’m in complete agreement. So, are you hot on the trail of this culprit?”

“I began looking for him yesterday afternoon.”

“I bet if I were your villain, I’d be worried about tomorrow morning.”

“Perhaps the morning after tomorrow.”

Gertrud laughed.

The waiter stopped by. He brought three plates of peppered venison and white rice.

Gertrud picked up her knife and fork. “Dobrou chuť.”

Klaus and Hermann answered in kind. They picked up their own utensils.

Hermann, overwhelmed by the smells soaring toward him, tore off a chunk of cutlet. He brought it to his mouth. He savored each spice and flavor that fell upon his tongue.

The food made him tremble. From both the taste and the excitement of experiencing such pleasure.

He came out of this. A man had screamed, “Heil Hitler!”

Hermann didn’t want to seek the source of this. But he couldn’t help himself.

A young soldier was saluting Klaus. His eyes were the size of pears.

“What is it?” Klaus growled, with Gertrud more perturbed than him, having had too many meals with Klaus interrupted in a similar manner.

“Colonel Reidl requests your presence in his office. On a most urgent matter.”

Klaus sighed.

Wiping his mouth with a napkin, he climbed to his feet. He bowed to Gertrud. “I hope you’ll forgive me.”

“I always do. But I shouldn’t.”

Hermann took one last bite. Pretty much the bounds of what he would’ve been able to eat that night.

Washing it down with his entire glass of wine—wine that exhibited an excellence that he had never imagined wine having—he stood.

“But Hermann doesn’t have to go too, does he?” Gertrud said to Klaus. “I’ve been so enjoying our talk.”

“I’m afraid so. This probably concerns him.”

“All right, gentlemen. We’ll resume this tomorrow night. I’m having a dinner party at my home, and I won’t take no for an answer. I’ll even serve venison, Hermann. So you can get your proper fill of it this time.”
