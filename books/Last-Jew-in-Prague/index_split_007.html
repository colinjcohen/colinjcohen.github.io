chapter seven: a spark of aspiration

 

 

KLAUS’S MERCEDES CONTINUED down the road.

Hermann eyed the SS flag adorning the car’s hood. It was standing against the wind. In defiance of it.

His eyes shifted.

A truck was making a wide right turn ahead. It was coming their way.

Shrieking its tires, it roared at them.

Hermann, like everyone who had come of age in the Austro-Hungarian Empire, had learned to drive on the left-hand side. He would drive like this during his years in prewar Czechoslovakia. It was so a part of him that, when I was growing up, I would have to remind him to move our car into the right lane. Multiple times, we came within a sliver of certain death.

At the time he left Theresienstadt, Hermann’s newness with vehicles driving on the right-hand side was at its peak. He flinched at the truck barreling toward them. He was sure that it would smash into and over them.

It didn’t. It made its way into the right lane as if this were normal.

At the occupation’s beginning, it had been startling to Hermann how it had taken the Germans a few days to make an entire country drive on the opposite side. But more startling was how fast they had made it part of everyone’s nature. It was startling how an entire country had conformed to the will of another.

Hermann hid from this.

He returned to his past. To the day that he had met Klaus. In the room that they’d shared twenty years earlier.

 

 

KLAUS MADE NO response to Hermann’s greeting. He paid him no mind.

He kept doing those push-ups in his underwear. Up and down he went. He was racing against someone unseen. He was afraid of coming to a rest. Of what might happen if he did.

Hermann wasn’t sure what to do. He introduced himself.

Klaus didn’t speak. But he did respond. He did a dramatic backflip to his feet.

With toothy glee, he approached Hermann. He shook his hand with a firm but moist grip.

Daze fell over his face.

It alarmed Hermann. “What’s wrong?”

“Nothing,” Klaus said, his daze having turned into dismay. “I wasn’t expecting . . .”

Hermann panicked. He had been to this opera before. “You were aware that I’m Jewish?”

Klaus gave him a dismissive wave. “Oh, it’s not that. I was expecting from what I had read about you . . .”

“Expecting what?”

“That you’d be a giant warrior.”

“Looks can be deceiving.”

“Did you bring your medals?” Klaus blurted out, gawking at Hermann the way people today gawk at celebrities.

“It was nothing,” Hermann said, I bet displaying several levels of embarrassment and hoping that they could change the subject. He displayed this when anyone broached the subject. Including me. Worse, the war’s mention was enough for the sounds to return. Of gunfire and screaming coming from all points. From him the loudest. They refused to pause.

“I served too,” Klaus went on, causing the sounds to stop ringing in Hermann’s ears.

Hermann pointed at the man’s shorts. “I could tell.”

“But not on the front lines.”

Hermann plopped onto his bed. “You were lucky.”

He wished that he had been so lucky.

Klaus was ignorant of Hermann’s unease. He swaggered to his bed. “It’s odd how many first-year students here are in their twenties. Some seniors are pushing thirty. And some doctoral candidates, some will be pensioners before they get their degrees.”

Both men laughed.

Klaus took off his shorts. “You like beer?”

“It’s gone past ‘like.’”

Klaus grabbed a towel off a closet door. He wrapped himself with it. “How about joining me and my new girlfriend at U Zlatého Tygra tonight?”

“Could we do it some other time?” Hermann asked. With further embarrassment.

“What’s wrong?”

“I need to get a job first.”

“You have no money?”

“Sure, I have money. But not a whole lot of it.”

“But you people are all rich.”

“Unfortunately, it’s not so. I can’t tell you how much I wish it were.”

“Well, the wrestling club needs a manager. I’m already the captain. So consider the job yours if you want it.”

“That would be terrific.”

“And I’ll front you some money till then.”

“I couldn’t let you do that.”

“You can and will. You’re coming out with me and Ana tonight. I won’t listen to another sentence about it.”

Klaus marched off.

Hermann hid his envy of him. He called out, “You have a girlfriend already?”

Klaus slowed by the door. “I’m surprised that it took me so long.”

He waved toward a bureau by his bed. “Her picture’s over there.”

Klaus left the room. But Hermann didn’t give heed to it. He was holding down his conflicting feelings.

He told himself that he wasn’t going to peep at the picture of Ana. He insisted that it didn’t matter what she looked like. That she had nothing to do with him.

But he made his way toward the wooden dresser and the picture on top of it. Which was facing away from him. It both enticed and excited him. This increased with each step closer to it.

He got within reach. He grabbed the frame.

The photograph inside it was of a nineteen-year-old woman. She had her dark-flaxen hair styled in the type of bob that was popular everywhere at the time.

Hermann would tell me that the image made “the majesty of Prague bland and ordinary.”

It wasn’t Ana’s beauty that sent Hermann tumbling. Though from the pictures Věra showed me of her, I can say that she was beautiful.

What sent him tumbling was the spark in her eyes.

It was aspirational. Ana was a woman who could lead a man toward what was greater than himself.

He so wanted this.

 

 

HERMANN RETURNED TO the Mercedes.

Another image had replaced that of Ana. The Golden City, as Czechs call Prague, was on the horizon.

Klaus was waking. His face was full of fright.

The city didn’t ease it. It made it worse.

You couldn’t say the same of Hermann. As on his first day in the city, he felt aspired.

For what?
