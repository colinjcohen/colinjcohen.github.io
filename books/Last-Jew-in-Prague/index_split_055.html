chapter fifty-five: a burst of excitement

 

 

HERMANN RETURNED DOWN the metal rungs and the wooden ladder. He told Klaus what was in the attic.

Klaus shook his wrists. “I should’ve arrested him.”

“I still don’t believe that Ivo committed the murders.”

“But he must be involved.”

“I told you that from the beginning of this. But we’ll only learn how he’s involved by getting through that wall downstairs.”

“We should wait in the car for those two to return. They have to come out sometime. At least it’s more comfortable in the car.”

“What if they don’t come out?”

“Where could they go?”

“That wall downstairs leads somewhere, and I have an inkling where. Like the cellar in the Pinkas Synagogue, this one isn’t at river level. Another structure is likely underneath it. This likely leads somewhere. It would explain why we’ve been seeing footprints leading to the walls but not from them. We must get through that wall downstairs. We must do this now.”

“Why now? If you’re worried about my replacement—”

“—Kant quantified two types of knowledge in the book that Dita wrote her paper about: a posteriori and a priori. Knowledge gained through experience and knowledge that exists outside of it. From both—both my instincts and my experiences as a police detective—I’m positive that if we don’t follow them tonight, we’ll lose the trail. Possibly for good.”

Klaus grimaced. At the mention of Kant’s name. “And how are we supposed to follow them?”

Hermann didn’t answer. For the obvious reason. He shuffled back into the synagogue, followed by Klaus. The two went down the stairs to the cellar. Hermann walked to the wall and the mystery behind it.

This time his mind wasn’t so stuck. Having given it time to unwind, it grasped at what he hoped was a step toward the solution he had been seeking.

I shall die in my nest, and I shall multiply my days like the phoenix.

Hermann recalled the passage that he had read in the Pinkas Synagogue cellar. He mused, “I wonder if the means to open the wall is related to the Bible. What in that book deals with walls?”

Nothing came to him. “If only I hadn’t daydreamed so much in cheder when I was a boy.”

“Joshua,” Klaus said.

Hermann spun toward him. “What?”

“The book of Joshua deals with walls.”

“That’s right. Joshua and his army brought down the walls of Jericho. But how?”

“Horns. They blew horns, and the walls tumbled down.”

“Sound. We heard a sound when we were at the synagogue doors. The wall’s opening could be sound-activated.”

Hermann, with a burst of excitement, rummaged through the boxes on the floor. Guided by the flashlight.

“What are you looking for?” Klaus asked.

Hermann humphed. “I’ll know when I find it.”

He did just that. He found a box with a ram’s horn. He brought it to Klaus. He handed him the flashlight. “Point it at the wall.”

Instead, Klaus pointed the light at the horn. “What’s that?”

“A shofar. We use it on the High Holidays.”

“Use it for what?”

Hermann took out the shofar. He blew into it.

A roar let out. With it, a wall’s section separated.

Hermann lowered the shofar into its box. He and Klaus rushed to the wall. Which they slid open.

A shaft was behind it. This led to an area below the cellar. As well as a cable made of rope.

Hermann took the flashlight from Klaus. He used it to follow the cable downward. To a wooden enclosure meters below. “It must be an elevator. Like what they use in mines.”

“How do we call it?” Klaus asked.

Hermann moved the light around the area. He stopped by an inset on the wall’s side. In it, a metal dial had been mounted on an arched concrete slab.

Over and over, he moved the dial. To various spots along the slab.

This did nothing. He got the same result when he moved the dial along the slab’s entire surface in both directions.

Klaus slapped his thighs. “Now what?”

Hermann didn’t reply. Like with the wall earlier, he was too obsessed with producing one.

His head throbbed. To the point that his focus shifted to the concrete slab. Despite not getting why it had.

If only finding the truth in men were as easy as finding the area under a curve.

Gertrud’s words from their dinner nights earlier echoed in Hermann’s head.

He tapped the slab. “How would we calculate the area under this curve?”

Klaus moaned. “Why are you asking me?”

“You studied geophysics.”

“That was twenty years ago. And if you recall, I never did much studying.”

I’d take the definite integral between the two of you and discover what I wanted to learn.

The words Gertrud had further said that night echoed in Hermann’s head too. “Integration. We need to integrate f(x). But what is f(x)?”

Klaus poked the slab. “This is a sine curve.”

“So we need to integrate sin(x) from zero to . . .”

“From zero to pi.”

“But how? What is the antiderivative of sin(x)?”

Klaus didn’t respond. So Hermann asked a second time and louder.

“I don’t remember!” Klaus shouted.

“You must!” Hermann shouted back.

The only response was silence.

“Is it cos(x)?” Hermann screeched.

More silence came back.

It ended when Klaus muttered, “−cos(x).”

He pulled out the papers from his jacket. He withdrew a pen that was there as well. He used it to write against the wall in the light. “So we need −cos(π) − −cos(0) . . . which gives us . . . −(−1) − −(1) . . . 2. The answer is 2.”

“2?” Hermann yelped, his eyes drilling into the dial. “What am I supposed to do with that?”

“In degrees,” Klaus said, returning to his scribbling, “2 radians is . . . it’s 115 degrees.”

Hermann moved the dial 115 degrees. He held his breath the entire way.

Nothing happened.

So he jiggled the dial. He jiggled it and jiggled it, moving it by the slightest amount possible.

He felt a slight click.

With it, the elevator rose.

Hermann laughed. “I knew you could do it.”

Klaus laughed back. “Let’s hope the math doesn’t get harder than that.”
