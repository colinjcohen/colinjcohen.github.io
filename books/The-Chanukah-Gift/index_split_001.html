the first night

 

 

HOW DO I begin the story of a snarling, quick-fisted girl named Rudi?

That destiny fated her to Yon, the Polo-wearing boy she otherwise would’ve hated? That she fought dragons for him and defied death? That she was my sister and my twin and my salvation?

No, this flipside-up fairy tale, this punk rock Cinderella, must start thirty-five years later. On a day that started like others.

 

 

WITH THE SUN slinking above I-15 blocks away and my jacket zipped beyond my chin, I rattled a garbage can forward.

Passing Mateo and his fixing of a heater behind the motel where we worked, I closed in on a duo of dumpsters and the scruffy man sprawled against them.

The barrel and I halted. By a dirty needle lying among the sand blowing across the cracked asphalt.

It was begging me to react. With disgust. Or shock. Or surprise.

But the single one I could muster was to grab the needle with my gloved hand.

“Where is my Hebrew?” a voice sprang from nearby.

Amoun was whooping it up. He was skipping and snapping his fingers.

He whooped it up a lot. He got a kick from an Egyptian bossing a Jew. He was reenacting Exodus.

In a sense, I was his slave. He had me do eight hours’ worth of work in a four-hour shift. For less than minimum wage. He’d discovered a loophole around it.

But I didn’t care about the money. I couldn’t spend it. I needed the job, the more of it and the more demanding it was.

So I indulged his Ramesside fantasies. I let him revel in them.

“I want you to shampoo the hallway carpets,” he said to me, ours the only Motel 6 with interior corridors. “And power-wash the walkways. Oh, the people in 122 last night left blood everywhere. Clean that too.”

He swaggered off. While I flung the needle into the can.

I continued to the dumpsters.

The scruffy man got to his feet. He was a regular guest who paid his seventy-dollar bill in nickels.

He straightened his clothes and his hair. His finger quivered at the needle. “That wasn’t mine. I would never leave them on the ground.”

I dumped the can. “I appreciate that.”

 

 

THE MORNING WASN’T alone in its likeness to others.

At the day’s end, Mateo and I took a pair of buses from Hesperia. We slogged our way down 7th Street in Victorville. A city that was at the center of a concrete-and-glass oasis, struggling to rise from the common source of both, churning beneath it and around it.

The two of us approached a sun eager to sink below the interstate. I was sinking with it.

Mateo snorted at me. He was the closest I had to a friend and the reason I had a job. He’d managed to convince Amoun that someone with a computer science degree could make a good houseman.

“Get off the road, you idiot,” he directed at my veering and quickening toward the traffic racing past us. Or was it my chasing of its impetus? “You’re gonna get killed.”

Slowing, I shook my head. I tossed up my arms. “If I am to wilt in the desert, I cannot get killed by a car.”

Mateo towed me back. From the path of a blazing red Charger. “Not today, you can’t.”

 

 

MATEO AND I reached the gates of a dusty complex.

In the alley alongside it, Willy was brooding over the open hood of a rotting Civic. Its fender was threatening the ground.

Mateo dragged a foot toward him. “What’s wrong?”

Willy cringed. “It won’t start. If I can’t get to work tomorrow, they’ll fire me for sure.”

Mateo’s face conveyed how tired he was. How he didn’t want to do more fixing. But he lumbered to the car.

“Mateo?” Nichelle called out from inside the gates. “The washing machines are broken.”

“I’ll take a look at them in a while.”

Mateo bent over the engine. He tinkered with it.

Willy lowered his eyes. “Thanks.”

“We’re in this together.”

 

 

THE EVENING ALSO began like others.

I was eating dinner with dozens of men, women, and children from toddlers to teens. At communal tables grouped in pairs.

Across from me was Gerry, a grizzled man my age who had come days earlier.

He was slipping silverware into his pockets.

I pretended to ignore this. “How’s it going?”

He hid his hands behind his back. “The usual.”

I smiled. I didn’t want to. But I did.

Gerry edged toward me. His eyes widened. He forced his fears out. “Were any suspicious characters outside?”

“That’s all that are. Outside and in.”

 

 

I TRUDGED TO a table by the kitchen. I dropped off my tray.

On the wall hung a calendar. Along with what made this evening different.

It was the first night of Chanukah. A night, like any other, I was spending in a homeless shelter.

That it was Chanukah shouldn’t have mattered. I had lost faith years before. Religion years before that.

But the holiday evoked memories. Good ones.

They played through my cleaning the mess hall after dinner. They ran on during my waiting at the head of a winding line of people, in front of a glass-walled office where we would check in for the night. They only faded when Mateo exited the open door with a towel in his hand.

It was my turn. I stepped into the cramped room, where sat Josh crouched over a desk.

A man in his thirties, he had a graying beard. A bulging belly. Red cheeks. He reminded me of Santa Claus. Without the jolly. Or good cheer.

I couldn’t tell whether he had gotten wind of me. The cardboard schedule hanging over the table’s sides had spellbound him. It held the galaxy’s mysteries.

He wrote my name in a slot. “You’ve got laundry tomorrow morning.”

I held back a grunt, coming from explaining to him five nights a week why I couldn’t do this. “I work in the morning.”

He didn’t hold back his own grunt. He crossed out my name. “Kitchen, tomorrow night.”

Check-in was complete.

I grabbed a towel off the stack on the chair next to the desk. I set out.

“This came for you,” he let out.

In his hand was a bulging white envelope.

I didn’t give it much due. I snatched it and left the office. I walked a few paces. To the cubbies.

Tossing the towel into mine, I exited the dorm.

Under bright lights, I entered a courtyard that was passing for a diner. In its dozen weathered booths, twice as many people were passing time in them.

One man was rubbing out a stain from his coat. Over Beethoven’s Grosse Fuge screeching from his phone.

Groaning, he doused the stain with what was in his cup. He rubbed harder.

The stain wouldn’t let up. Neither would he. Nor would the four in the booth ahead. They were swatting flies.

Two women were behind him. They were telling tales. Of jobs they didn’t want. Of plans for leaving the shelter they wanted to believe.

Mann tracht, un Gott lacht, my grandma would say in Yiddish.

Man plans, and God laughs.

He must laugh at women too.

I went on listening to the two. To the normality they projected. To the vanity they applied to it.

It didn’t deter their aspiring for it.

I recalled the envelope.

Having no better to do, I opened it.

With less vigor, I wrenched out dozens of pages.

Someone had handwritten them in ink. On both sides of lined white paper with ruffled edges. What I had last come across in school.

I leafed through the crinkled and aged sheets. My disinterest decreased with each.

The person who had penned them rushed at me. My eyes latched onto the envelope and its return address.

It was from a Yon Levy in Livingston, New Jersey.

More shocking than this was the postmark. The date was from thirty-five years earlier.

 

 

THE WIND WAS bucking. A chill scorched the air.

It was a Southern California night so unlike those in my childhood from the opposite coast. It had been sunny and room temperature in Cali year-round on TV. Nobody wore jackets. Or sweaters.

I was alone at a booth in the courtyard’s corner. Below a full moon that silhouetted the palm trees and canyons to my right and my fathoming the pages on the table.

Failing at this, I took out a plastic phone. What they gave out to folks like me.

“Obamaphones,” we called them. Despite him not having been president in years. He wasn’t the guy who came up with them either. But we had to give them some name. To distinguish them from those that people wanted.

The phone was low on battery. It was low when charged. So I plugged it in at an exterior wall outlet.

I took an extended breath. I dialed a number that I assumed I had forgotten.

It rang.

A sudden and unknown dread overcame me. It pushed my finger over the disconnect button. I willed myself to tap it.

“Hello?” she said.

George W. Bush had been president when I heard this voice last. But I would’ve recognized it after a thousand years.

It was the same as it had been. Confident and unafraid. It was soaring over me.

I reached out to touch it. To be part of it.

But I didn’t speak.

She repeated herself.

Words tumbled from my mouth. “It’s me.”

Rudi had to wait for words too. Longer than I had. “Where are you?”

“It doesn’t matter. I don’t want to take up your time. You must be busy tonight.”

“Service ended a while ago.”

I faltered. I couldn’t get to the point. I couldn’t drag myself from the pages. From the impossibility they were relaying.

But not able to refute their reality, I babbled, “It’s crazy. But, but I got a letter today . . . from Yon.”

“Yon?”

Rudi gasped for air. I could sense she was falling. I was falling with her.

I prevented myself. For her sake. “It’s right here.”

“What is?”

“A journal he wrote.”

“He never wrote a journal.”

“You certain about that?”

She paused. For a while she did. “I’m never certain. The lack of it comes with my job.”

“You want me to mail it to you? Or I could scan it with my phone and email it. There’s this new app that uses a machine-learning technique I’ve been reading about to do character recognition. It might untangle Yon’s scribble.”

“You can’t read it?”

I huffed. “I can. But I skimmed through it. It’s real personal.”

“You were important to him. He must’ve wanted you to have it.”

“But . . . how could he have sent it to me?”

“I can’t remember when I stopped questioning how. Please read it to me. It’ll be the best Chanukah gift I’ll get.”

I dithered. I didn’t want to return to the past. Or the nightmare buried inside it. Nor did I want Rudi to rehash her own.

All this made me quiver.

Yet I ordered the pages by the numbers on the bottom. I read Rudi a story that I’ve narrated with my own views of it.

The first entry was from December 24, 1982.

Its date stuck out. It was in a different color than the rest.

Yon had written it at a different time.

 

 

WHEN YOU READ someone’s journal, you expect that it will start at their story’s beginning.

Yon’s started at the end.

Through the corner of his eyes, the room’s full-length mirror was oppressing him.

It pictured a tall but peaked and skeletal eighteen-year-old figure in a white terrycloth robe. Whose flat chestnut hair was so bare that it didn’t cover his scalp.

The image so troubled Yon that he zeroed in on the yellow ribbon in his hands. He was draping it over a red gift-wrapped box.

Snow was beside him. It fell between the windows and the hospital across the street. The off-whiteness staggered from the skies, in time with a Glenn Miller song from a half-century before. It was playing on a portable stereo by the bed.

A chain jingled.

Yon swayed toward it.

A pint-sized golden-haired mutt in a spiked collar was guarding him by the door. An urgency was in her eyes.

Yon returned to the ribbon.

Mindful that he had to get it right, he tied and untied it.

He had to rest between the two.

 

 

THE AFTERNOON WAS gone. Sweat was pouring down Yon’s face.

He wiped it away with his arm. He tugged at the ribbon’s ends.

Two perfect loops spread across the box.

He gushed at them, raising himself and the box off the bed.

Lightheaded and wobbly—with his legs half-asleep—Yon couldn’t keep his socked feet from slipping.

He collapsed onto his knees on the wood paneling. With a boom and a holler. A set of hacking coughs.

Both he and the dog fell into a panic.

Would he get up?

The finality of his disease never ceased looming.

His panic didn’t last. It eased with the pain.

Yon stood.

He staggered and limped out the door with the box. Into the living room.

The dog followed him. Step for step.

Yon neared a mantle. A simple metal menorah was on it.

Under it, he lowered himself to his knees.

This took time. Longer than it had taken him to lift himself. But he got the gift on the floor.

The front door creaked.

In the apartment’s threshold was a woman Yon’s age. She was glaring at him with her hands on her hips.

The woman couldn’t have been more different from Yon. She contrasted with him and herself. Her powder-white face contrasted with her spiked black hair and with her heavy black eyeshadow and with a glossy black lipstick that matched her nails. Contrasting with all this was her oversized blue-and-white football jacket. “Yon” and a “C” had been stitched into it.

Her presence spread a blanket of warmth over Yon. He forgot his doom.

The feeling wouldn’t wane. So overpowering was it that he wrestled with what to say. “You’ve caught me.”

Her glare didn’t let up. “I didn’t get you a gift.”

“You’ve already given me it.”

“I did get you some . . .”

The woman shut the door. She marched to Yon.

Kneeling beside him, she took a white canvas seabag from her shoulder. She reached inside it.

She pulled out an envelope, wrapped in gold paper collaged from stars.

Her body flinched. Her eyes stretched.

He leaned toward her. “What’s wrong?”

Her startle got starker. She shook. “He must’ve slipped it into my bag.”

“Who?”

“The old man at the store.”

“Can I open it?”

The woman didn’t answer. She kept shaking.

Yon drew the envelope from her. He slid his finger along the seal.

He took out a white card. It could’ve come from a board game.

Now she was leaning toward him. She was toppling over. “What is it?”

“It says, ‘One Replay’ . . . and in fine print . . . ‘Caveat Amor.’”

 

 

THE SECOND ENTRY in Yon’s journal was from December 11, 1981. A year before the first. The remaining entries followed the second.

“Did you hear me?” spoke a familiar female voice from Yon’s right.

He didn’t answer. He couldn’t.

His focus was on what was reflecting off a light-soaked window. Of a dazed but striking and broad-shouldered seventeen-year-old boy.

The boy had a shock of wavy hair. An olive complexion. He was wearing the blue-and-white football jacket with his name and the “C” stitched into it.

“Did you hear me?” the question repeated. This time with a touch of a growl.

Yon’s gaze shifted to two other items in the window: the turquoise school backpack across his shoulder and who was hanging over his other arm.

Her name was Darlene. The prettiest girl at Columbia High School.

With her strawberry-blonde permed hair and legs without end and a billion-dollar smile, she could’ve become a model. “The Jewish Brooke Shields,” people called her.

But her ambitions aimed higher. “Yon?”

He spun toward her. “What?”

“I said, ‘Happy Chanukah.’”

She roused Yon to his new reality.

He was standing by a cathedral-like school, half old and half new. He was by its front doors. He and his friends and dozens of other kids.

They were all gabbing. Joking around. Their breaths were tinting the morning air.

Yon strained to reconcile his present state with his previous. The minutes that he had spent with the crazy-looking girl that he couldn’t recall beyond this.

He grasped at the most logical conclusion. That he had been dreaming.

The girl. The apartment that the two shared. The disease that had been killing him. It had to have been a dream.

But he couldn’t resolve the problem with this.

A dream may appear real while it’s happening. But when you wake, it becomes clear what it had been.

This wasn’t. It had been as real as his now.

The two traits that it shared with a dream were that—as when waking from one—he had a limited memory of it, and he was forgetting it.

He yanked his arm from Darlene. He swung his backpack off his shoulder. He whipped out a spiral notebook from it and a pen. He used the latter to scribble on the double-sided lined white paper.

Darlene retook his arm. She gripped it. “What are you doing?”

He shook her off. “I’ve gotta write this down.”

This got more intense.

He spit out what he could remember. From tying the yellow ribbon. To reading the card. “One replay? A replay of what?”

Darlene gripped his arm harder. “What are you talking about?”

Yon didn’t answer. His mind was on a more troubling riddle.

Shouldn’t he have been happy?

Whatever he had experienced wasn’t real. He was healthy. Death was off in some distant plane.

But he wasn’t happy.

He was what it juxtaposed. The same that he’d been toward whatever had been in his horizon since the summer. Including himself.

Before that, you could say that he had a semblance of happy. From that his heftiest wrangling was over “suede vs. leather.”

Now the weight of his weightlessness could’ve brought Atlas to his knees.

The single time that this wasn’t true, the single time that he was happy, was the time with the girl in his dream.

“Did you guys catch the ‘Pretty Woman’ video on MTV?”

Asking this had been Joey. A boy in the same blue-and-white football jacket as Yon’s.

He was much the same as him. All his friends were. They all had pretty boy haircuts and designer clothes. They were all as good-looking.

By the pictures Yon would show me in his yearbook, they could’ve started a boy band. If such had existed.

Nobody answered Joey. The chatter tailed off. The silence got so loud that Yon gravitated toward it.

He shuddered at what was coming.

Two people were making their way to the school from the parking lot.

The first was a middle-aged man. With a crew cut and a pockmarked face. In an off-the-rack blue suit.

But what the crowd was eyeing was the girl steps behind him. A girl not capable of a quiet entrance.

It was the same girl from Yon’s dream. She had the same wild hair and makeup. She was carrying the same white canvas seabag over her shoulder.

The differences were the Walkman she was clutching and what she was wearing. A navy overcoat. Faded and torn jeans. A pair of black Chuck Taylors.

The last two she had on before either was in fashion. Or was close to coming into it. Yon wrote that he couldn’t remember anyone wearing Converse since he had been a kid.

What was more bizarre was that there were no laces on them.

She must’ve been some fright that morning. The way that she clashed with the suburbia around her.

Punks weren’t a novelty in early 1980s America. Or a lifestyle choice.

They were an aberrance. A popular TV show called Quincy called them a murderous menace.

Today, no one would give the girl a second gawk in the Bible Belt. But it must’ve been as if she had fallen from the stars. The creature from Alien would’ve been less horrifying to everyone.

They had seen that before.

The girl’s appearance wasn’t the trait that stood out to Yon. It was her apathy.

This she was feigning.

You could say a lot about punks. An awful lot. But what you couldn’t say was that they didn’t care. It was the whole point.

The girl’s indifference was hiding too. What was beneath it. The cowering child, desperate for a way out.

It was Yon who bore apathy. He told me that, before this happened, he had battled to wake in the morning.

Closer the girl got to Yon. His knees buckled.

He had suffered this playing football. But at no time did he suffer like this. Or this much. “Who is she?”

No response came.

The girl noted the eyes on her.

Her apathy turned into a scowl. She stared down the entire school. She was ready to fight them all.

At the same time, she cranked the volume of her Walkman. She sang with a song that Yon had never heard. About someone who belonged to a “Blank Generation.”

Yon didn’t care for punk rock. He didn’t get it.

But this song was different. It was different from any other. The lyrics were about him. Or someone who could’ve been him. Someone who had it all and not at all. Someone surrounded by others but alone. Someone frantic to get away. Whether or not this meant never getting back anywhere.

The girl stopped singing. Her scowl fixed on Yon. She snarled.

He was the only one at the school. The only person in the world.

Yon grimaced. But not at her. It was at himself. At the allure of a girl vile in many ways. But fascinating in many more.

It wasn’t because of his dream that she fascinated him. She wasn’t like any girl around.

She stood so uprightly. She cared not how others regarded her.

His heart pounded with each stomp she took toward him. His body tilted forward. He was tipping over.

“Isn’t Halloween in October?” Joey cracked with an elevated brow. The sort John Belushi had made famous years earlier.

A gust of laughter followed. It compelled Yon to join in.

The girl trained her spite onto Joey. “How would you like to go next year as a rug?”

Joey didn’t respond. He couldn’t.

“Them’s fighting words, Joey,” a fellow boy in blue chimed in.

“I don’t fight girls. Hell, I wouldn’t fight her.”

The girl opened her mouth. But the man ahead of her barged in. “That’s enough.”

She sneered at him. She returned her scowl to Yon. Her snarl with it.

More particulars about her jumped out at Yon.

Her bloodshot eyes. The sweating that she was doing despite the weather.

Beyond that was her obliviousness of Mariana and the doors that the two were heading toward.

Mariana was mousy. She had no makeup.

Beneath her plain black wool coat, she wore a white blouse and a plaid skirt that flowed below her knees. She could’ve been going to Catholic school.

She was hurrying to the entrance. Without heeding who was hurrying with her. She and the new girl were about to slam into each other.

They avoided it at the last instant.

Wide-eyed and wider-mouthed, Mariana took the girl in. She was witnessing a new form of life. “Hey.”

The girl took Mariana in. Minus the wide eyes. Or the wider mouth. She raised her fist, insulted by the cognizance of her. “Hey, how about some dentures?”

Mariana didn’t answer. But she did react.

She took a leap back. As far as she could.

This didn’t placate the girl. She went after her.

She didn’t go far.

Her blue escort grabbed her by the arm. “What did I tell you, Rudi?”

The man didn’t wait for a reply. He threw his shoulder into the heavy wooden doors. He tossed Rudi into the building. He followed her inside.

“Who are you?” Yon whispered. At a volume that only he could hear and only a sliver. With his fear hurtling past the red line.

 

 

I BROKE OFF from the story. One too off-the-wall to believe.

But was it crazier than the pages in my hand? Or the postmark on the envelope?

I couldn’t say. I went in a different trajectory.

Yon wasn’t the only person curious about Rudi. She was a stranger to me.

I lowered the page that I had been reading. “Who were you?”

She didn’t want to answer. She wasn’t bashful about it. “This isn’t my story.”

“It belongs to the three of us.”

Rudi was silent. But I was in control. I wouldn’t read further till she relented.

She did so with a grumble. “What I’ll say is that I had bigger worries than some preppy jock.”

“Such as?”

“Such as I was jonesing like hell. I would’ve raced up Everest for a handful of pills. It wouldn’t’ve mattered what. A sword was also hanging over me. It would’ve made Damocles piss in his pants. Or whatever he wore.”

“But Yon got to you.”

“That doesn’t mean it was love. I had no interest in him. However gorgeous he was. Or because of it. He was the best-looking guy ever. Better looking than any celebrity. He could’ve posed for Michelangelo. That shot up my wrath. I was sure that he was stuck-up and boring. Most guys like that are, in my experience. But . . .”

“But what?”

Rudi grumbled more. More than before. “He had this inexplicable. In how those amber eyes of his were cutting through me. It was like he knew me better than I did. That I couldn’t hide from him. This threw me.

“But I brushed it off as biological. I can tell you that, when I stepped through those doors (or flew through them), I had forgotten about him.”

I doubted that. But I continued reading.

 

 

YON AND DARLENE entered the school.

He was there in body. But his spirit was lurching between it and his dream.

Down the corridor, Rudi and her escort stood by the school offices and the principal. A man that was wound tighter than the most inoperable knot, in a tight brown suit and a tighter brown tie.

The principal jabbed his finger at Rudi. While baring his teeth at the man next to her. “I want to reiterate what I told your superior. I don’t care about the importance of your case. We have a sufficient criminal element here.”

Rudi twisted from the man. She slung her arms across herself. She crooked her lips. “The real Nazis run your schools!”

The principal thrust himself toward her. “What did you say?”

“Don’t you worry, Mr. Gonzales,” the pockmarked man hooted, seizing Rudi by the neck. “She won’t be giving you trouble for more than a month or so. With luck, less.”

The man hauled Rudi away. He dragged her past Mr. Criss, a guidance counselor.

He was the lone person that morning that Rudi didn’t put off. Or horrify.

It was all over his face.

 

 

YON WANDERED THE third-floor hallway. Among those shuttling to class and yakking. Without saying much at all.

He hadn’t a clue of where he was wandering through. Or to. He had taken spins in both a washer and a dryer. He couldn’t walk straight.

Coming to his first-period computer class, he stopped outside it.

His mind wasn’t on it. But he poked his head inside the door.

A half-dozen kids were punching cards. For use in the old computer on the room’s other side.

That was how you programmed it. You would type lines of code on the cards and wait for an operator to feed them into the machine. You could wait a week to compile the simplest of programs.

This didn’t make Yon’s heart pound. His knees didn’t buckle. He wasn’t tipping over.

A bell rang.

He took off.

Instead of spending an hour coding what would’ve spewed a bunch of zeros across a page, he went down the hall. He poked his head into each classroom he passed.

At the corridor’s end, he went into a stairwell. He climbed down to the second floor.

He poked his head into the rooms down that hall. He did so with a doubt hanging over him. It was about to collapse onto him.

Had his time in the apartment with Rudi, including the disease that had been killing him, not been a dream?

If it hadn’t, it meant the incredible. Someone had gifted him the chance to replay a part of his life. The chance to elude his fate.

Caveat Amor.

Yon had never taken Latin. But he had confidence that he had gotten the gist from the fine print on the card.

Beware Love.

Was that why his replay began before meeting Rudi? Did he need to beware of her? Would falling in love with her trigger what would lead to his disease and death?

He halted. With haste.

Why was he chasing after her?

No matter how she had capsized him, she couldn’t be worth dying over.

But this could have been what made her so exciting. The risk that came with her. She was a one-woman rollercoaster, and Yon was in need of a thrill.

“What are you doing?” somebody squeaked.

In back of Yon was a teacher. A man whose name he had never bothered to learn, who didn’t come up to his neck.

He had shoe-polish black hair like President Reagan’s. He had on an argyle sweater over a yellow button-down Oxford and a paisley tie.

Yon stammered. “What?”

“Why aren’t you in class?”

“I have a free period. And I was, I was going to Mrs. Elkind’s . . . she promised to help me with calculus.”

“Isn’t her classroom on the first floor?”

“Yeah. I guess I’m out of it today.”

Yon scurried down the hall. He came upon another stairwell.

He checked behind himself.

The teacher was gone.

“If you’re expecting to sleepwalk through this class, Ms. Weiss,” a woman blared from the last room on the floor, “you might as well drop it now.”

Yon strode to the open door. He trembled.

This heightened and widened with each step.

He came upon a setup common at the school. Three tables stretched along three walls. It formed a square with the teacher’s desk in front.

Slouching at the back table by herself was Rudi.

She was wearing spiked bracelets. A tattered white T-shirt held together by safety pins. She had scrawled “Black Flag” on it in marker. Above four black bars drawn in the shape of a flag.

Like outside the school, Yon grimaced.

How could he end up with such a girl?

Forget her outfit. She couldn’t care about anyone.

Yon persuaded himself that his dream had to have been one.

Yet he couldn’t budge. Neither himself nor his eyes. From Rudi and what she was about.

Darlene broke his trance.

She was sitting in the middle of a gaggle of girls. They were congregating by the table alongside the wall that separated the class from the hallway.

Back and forth Yon went. Between Darlene and Rudi. He compared the two.

Darlene was better. In each tangible way. Looks the most.

But it was in the other ways, the ways far from tangible, that she didn’t compare to Rudi.

Were these what mattered? Did they matter more than the danger she posed?

“Modern Poetry is among the most challenging courses in the school,” said Ms. Krasner. In black-rimmed glasses and a black turtleneck sweater.

The woman was both diminutive and towering. She towered bending over a table while rummaging through a stack of textbooks in the room’s far front corner. “You’ll write about poetry. You’ll write some yourself. You’ll do it well. Shelley was publishing verse at your age. So I’ll make no excuse for your youth.”

Darlene smirked. “She had Frankenstein for inspiration.”

The teacher sighed. It seethed from her. “Wrong Shelley. But it was a nice try, Ms. Cantor. Almost.”

Another sigh arose from Krasner. Though not as loud.

She picked out a book. She marched it to the back. “This is an elective. That means no one is forcing you to take it.”

The woman marched the book to Rudi. As Yon slanted from her line of vision.

Krasner sneered. At Rudi’s stab at apathy.

She dropped the book onto the table in front of her.

It bounced.

Rudi yawned. She took her time doing it.

Scowling, the teacher clenched her hands behind her back. “Have you ever read poetry?”

“Can she read?” Darlene peeped.

Laughter cascaded through the classroom. Krasner had to cover her mouth.

“Can you?” Rudi clapped back. “I mean other than Vogue.”

Everyone laughed. Louder than before. Krasner had to return her hand over her mouth. She had to leave it there longer. “Touché, Ms. Cantor.”

The woman galloped to the center blackboard. The poem “since feeling is first” was upon it in perfect script.

She basked in its beauty. “What can anyone tell me about E. E. Cummings?”

Darlene humphed. “He wrote his name in lowercase.”

Rudi slammed her forearms onto the table. “That’s a myth.”

Krasner whipped herself around. “That’s right, Ms. Weiss. Can you tell me more about him? As in ‘more interesting’?”

“He did use unorthodox typography and grammar. It was a product of his hatred of convention and constraints. Of whatever got in his way. Including language.”

Rudi propped her feet on the table. She hung her hands behind her head. She lifted her chair back. “Basically, he was a punk.”

The teacher laughed. Without her hand over her mouth and less at Rudi’s claim than at her. “Is that so? He didn’t dress the part.”

“Punk isn’t about dress. Or hair. Or music. It’s about attitude. He-was-a-punk.”

“You need to dig deeper, Ms. Weiss. Your argument is interesting. But lazy. We’ll have none of that here. Way more is to Cummings than his attitude.”

Rudi and her chair smashed to the floor. She knotted her face. “Too bad the same can’t be said of you.”

The woman blasted daggers at Rudi. Lots.

She returned to the poem. She read it aloud.

Poetry wasn’t Yon’s taste. It was less so than punk rock.

With Krasner’s voice echoing through the classroom into the hallway, he went off.

 

kisses are a better

fate than wisdom

 

Yon froze.

It wasn’t the words that froze him. But the effect that they had on him. Despite unable to say what this was.

His one certainty was that they had affected him. He was slipping toward the class and Krasner’s reciting.

 

the best gestures of my brain

is less than

your eyelids’ flutter which says

we are for each other

 

Yon was back in the room’s doorway.

Rudi had removed her feet from the desk. She was tilting forward. She was pushing the table over.

Krasner’s voice ceased. She whirled toward the class.

Her excitement was exceeding Rudi’s. It swept through the room. “His words mix splendor and dissonance. And he riddles them with the passion he’s unleashed. Cummings was a Romantic. Or a ‘neo-Romantic,’ as some call him.”

The woman paused. For a fraction of a moment. “‘Shelley in a blender’ is what I call him. That’s Percy Shelley, Ms. Cantor.”

Krasner took another pause. She sat on the edge of her desk. “I want to see this passion unleashed in your next assignment. Due a week from today. You’ll craft me a poem worthy of Cummings and his magic. A poem that he might’ve crafted if he were you. And I’ll flunk anyone who does no more than play with their typography. Or their grammar. I want to see passion. I want to see it all over the page!”

The room fell quiet.

Rudi began to drift. Toward the door.

She had registered that someone was there.

This someone split open her jaw.

In less than a flicker, her insides had flipped turvy-topsy. Her world wasn’t conforming to what she had expected.

Yon’s jaw split open too.

It came from her eyelids. From how they had fluttered.

He stumbled away, his mind drowning in an unbearable uncertainty.

Had his eyelids been doing the same?

 

 

IN THE SCHOOL cafeteria at lunch, Rudi paraded through the center aisle with a tray laden with food.

It had erupted over it. Fruit was on top.

Table after table she passed. Waves of heads crashed onto her from all sides.

She ignored them. Or she acted like she did.

I can say that she did care how others regarded her. This would anger her. It must’ve been why her ignoring amplified.

It didn’t last.

She came upon a person she couldn’t ignore. At a table of football players and beautiful girls. A person with amber eyes.

Yon spiraled from Rudi.

He convinced himself that her eyelids hadn’t fluttered. That he had imagined it.

For how could some poet’s overromanticized notion of life be true?

Eyelids don’t flutter. Nor do people blush. Or swoon. We live in a rational world.

2 + 2 can’t equal 5.

So why was he shivering all over?

Yon snapped out of this. He became aware that Darlene was sitting next to him. That her arm was around his.

He wasn’t the only one aware of it.

Rudi lobbed herself around. She stormed toward the back wall.

No longer was she coming across apathetic. Or uprightly. She was about to smash through the bricks.

Beside the empty table by them she plopped down. She picked at her pile.

Dawdling in her path was a boy.

Carrying a more modest lunch, he was in another place. As he often was.

Eliot was bones and skin. He had a pimply face and greasy brown hair. He was sporting a stained purple button-down polyester shirt, tan Haggar slacks whose cuffs dragged across the floor, and dirty white Keds.

He wasn’t cool. He was the kid bullies dream of. To the extent that some felt sympathy for him. Including those who would bully him anyway.

Eliot got to the back table. To who was there.

He stiffened mid-step.

Rudi couldn’t take much of this. Her snarl returned. “Can I help you?”

“I . . . I usually eat here.”

She clenched her fists. “So?”

He didn’t say. He backed up. In slow motion he backed up. He backed up as if retreating from a roaring lioness.

That wasn’t nautical miles from the truth.

Rudi gave him no further notice. She took a notepad and a pen from her seabag.

Laying an eye on Yon, she doodled.

 

 

DARLENE BRUSHED HER hair back.

She stewed at what Yon couldn’t evade. “Can you believe that she’s in my poetry class?”

Yon, of course, could believe this. But he stayed quiet.

“She’s also in my chemistry class. She probably should be teaching it.”

“What do you mean?”

“She’s a big-time druggie.”

“How did you figure that out?”

“Lis has gym class with her. Her NA keychain fell to the floor when she was changing.”

“NA?”

“Narcotics Anonymous.”

Yon wanted this to make a difference. He wanted it to deter him from his snowballing mania of Rudi. He wanted and wanted and wanted.

But it didn’t make a difference.

What could?

 

 

“I WAS DISAPPOINTED with the quiz’s results,” said Mrs. Elkind, a graying woman in a graying dress, handing papers to her students in class.

Counted in this was Yon. He was horror-struck by the D on top of his from his regular front-row seat.

“You should’ve mastered conic sections by now. Few of you, I fear, have a chance of passing the AP exam.”

The woman was about to add further invective. But a disturbance butted in.

Rudi sauntered into the classroom.

She was clutching a crumpled printout of her class schedule. It could’ve been a fast-food wrapper that she was about to discard.

Elkind probed Rudi. Down and up. Multiple times over.

She layered this with incredulity. At the girl’s discordance with the order of her classroom. “Yes?”

Rudi waved her schedule at the woman. She waved it with irreverence. “This is where I’m supposed to be.”

The teacher stormed to Rudi. Her incredulity surged.

She grabbed the printout. She uncrumpled it. Her eyes lasered onto it. “We’ll see about that, Miss Weiss.”

Pain streaked across Rudi’s face. Right and left and crosswise. “Ms. Weiss.”

“Excuse me?”

“Do I look like a ‘miss’?”

The woman pointed behind Rudi. “Solve the problem.”

Rudi skimmed over an equation. It was the length of a blackboard.

The teacher tapped her foot. “Use partial fraction decomposition to integrate the function.”

Rudi went back to the woman. “You’re joking, right?”

“I rarely joke about partial fraction decomposition.”

Rudi sighed. But she plowed to the board.

Picking up a piece of chalk, she weighed the problem.

In less time than that, she answered it. She filled the board’s remaining space with numbers and formulae. Yon gave up following it when she started taking arctangents of square roots.

The teacher stepped back. “That took me fifteen minutes to solve this morning.”

Rudi finished. She squiggled “Q.E.D.” next to the solution. In letters that extended across another board.

Raising the chalk over her head, she dropped it onto the easel. She dropped it much as Ms. Krasner had dropped the textbook.

It bounced as the book had.

Elkind’s face lit up. “Quite easily done.”

Rudi let out another sigh. She frowned. “It means quod erat demonstrandum. That which required proving.”

“A girl after my own mind. You’ve earned your seat here, Ms. Weiss. Maybe you can help others keep theirs.”

Rudi snagged the schedule from the woman. She tramped to a seat by the back wall.

The teacher shuffled to the blackboard. “You can start by explaining to us how you completed the problem. As we’re mostly adults here, you can skip the polynomial division. The factoring of the denominator as well. Go straight to the decomposition.”

Rudi sighed for a third time. Louder than the previous two. “I can’t see over that wavy hair.”

It struck Yon that she meant his hair.

He fell back in his chair. He faced her. “Better?”

“Not really.”

Yon laughed.

It knocked Rudi sideways.

She couldn’t pretend anymore that he was stuck-up. Or boring.

 

 

YON FLOATED FROM a first-floor stairwell. Into the hallway. With hundreds of others leaving school.

He was numb and befuddled from his whirlwind day.

Or had it been longer?

He came to a restroom. A familiar female voice was inside it.

“He doesn’t care about you!” Rudi howled, her voice unsteady.

Who could she be howling at?

“Nobody does! I don’t care about you!”

The door flew at Yon. It smacked him in the face.

Rudi and her muddle dashed from the bathroom in her coat.

The door returned to its frame. It swung like a pendulum.

Yon begged himself not to follow Rudi.

It was his choice in what he did with his life and his so-called replay. It wasn’t hers. He didn’t have to end up that peaked and skeletal and doomed figure in the mirror. He could change his fate. It was so easy.

Still, he couldn’t not follow Rudi.

He followed her down the hallway. He followed her when she went left toward the front doors. He followed her through them. He followed her outside.

She was trotting toward a crowd on the edge of Parker Avenue. Some happening had drawn them.

Rudi approached Darlene, who was standing alongside Lis, a platinum blonde with a ponytail and girl-next-door looks. The two were whispering among themselves. They were snickering at Rudi.

Joey rambled behind them. He tapped Lis’s shoulder.

She twirled toward him.

He pitched his thumb in back of himself. He started off. “Let’s go.”

Lis didn’t linger. She trailed Joey. Doing so as if tethered to him.

She left Darlene to snicker at Rudi alone.

Rudi wouldn’t snatch the girl’s bait. She continued to the throng. She tiptoed over a shoulder.

Swelling with fury, she pushed her way through everyone. Yon wrote in his journal that she was Moses parting the sea. She was an irresistible force.

She got to the street. She stood over a pint-sized golden-haired mutt. The same mutt from Yon’s dream. It was whimpering and bleeding in the road by the curb.

Rudi kneeled beside the animal. She swooped it into her arms.

She rose with it. She cradled it. She comforted it. She calmed it.

Never had Yon beheld such gentleness. It belied what Rudi was about.

But what didn’t?

“An animal hospital is on the end of Valley,” a girl yelled.

Mariana was jumping about. She was flailing her arm down the road. “I volunteer on weekends. The vet, Dr. Winston, is so awesome.”

Rudi examined the dog’s state. She kissed its head. “We wouldn’t get there in time.”

Yon vaulted into her view. He did this in spite of himself. “But we can.”

 

 

RUDI WAS CLUTCHING the dog in her arms as she raced through the school parking lot with Yon.

They raced to a Harley Sturgis.

She caught her breath. Her eyes spread wide. “You drive a motorcycle?”

“I live dangerously.”

“I bet.”

Yon hopped onto the bike.

Rudi didn’t join him.

He groaned. “Come on.”

It took eons, but she crept onboard. As if onto a hot stove. As far from Yon as possible.

She did no more.

He wheezed. “I suggest you put your arm around me. Unless you want to fly there.”

Rudi’s wavering lingered.

But she hoisted one arm around him, keeping the other around the whining dog.

They bolted from the lot. Onto Parker.

Picking up speed, they got to the corner of Valley.

They zoomed left. They zipped through traffic. They weaved around cars and trucks. They ran both lights and stop signs.

Driving with recklessness Yon was good at. He repeated it and repeated it. Wanting to make himself feel alive. Wanting this, in itself, to have meaning.

But never had it brought him any. Coming close to death hadn’t brought it.

Only now had meaning come.

 

 

IN THE PACKED and ultra-bright animal hospital waiting area, Yon and Rudi were scrunching next to each other. Their eyes were straight ahead.

Yon was grappling with himself.

He didn’t want to admit how good he felt pressed against Rudi. Both there and on the bike. “The dog’s gonna be okay.”

Rudi gave him another of her snarls. “You can’t be sure of that.”

“Actually, I can.”

“What?”

“You’ve got some blood on your coat.”

She checked it. “It’s not the first. You’ve got some too. On the back of your jacket.”

“It’s okay.”

“Thanks. I mean, thanks for doing this.”

A veterinarian in a white lab coat verged upon them. She was reading from a chart.

The two stood. They waited for the woman.

Dr. Winston stopped by them. She went on with her reading. “She’s in bad shape.”

Rudi slumped over.

The vet lowered the chart. Her demeanor picked up with it. “She’s got a lengthy recovery. But she’ll recover.”

Rudi lifted herself up. “Yeah?”

“It’s fortunate that you got her here so fast.”

Neither Rudi nor Yon spoke.

Dr. Winston eyeballed them. “I presume that you two can’t pay for this.”

Yon pulled out his wallet. “I can.”

He removed a credit card. He offered it to her.

The woman took it. She took off. She left the two to search for words.

They were out of their reach. Way out of it.

With abrupt unease, Rudi backed up. She dragged her feet from Yon. “I should be going.”

“You want a lift?”

“I’m fine.”

She moved with greater hurry. She bumped into a wall.

Veiling her embarrassment, she fled from the office.

Yon’s eyes didn’t stir from her. They hadn’t stirred after she was gone.

 

 

YON WAS LYING on his bed. He was scribbling in his notebook.

Crumpled pages were around him on the floor. “Over Under Sideways Down” was playing on his stereo.

He scribbled what had happened that day. Many passes of it. He also sketched scenes on the papers’ edges.

Getting the words close to right, he dropped his pen.

The music changed. Into the same Glenn Miller song from his dream.

Or whatever it had been.

But now it was playing in his head.

He was inside it too. He was dancing with someone in his arms. Someone he couldn’t see. They were cheek to cheek.

His heartbeat was matching hers. Both were matching the beat bound by the trombone.

Never had Yon been so perfect. So himself. He prayed for the rising melody to never fall.

“Yon, you up?” his mother said from outside his door.

The music reverted. To the Yardbirds.

Yon opened his eyes.

He was clutching a pillow.

“Yon?”

He heaved the pillow onto the floor. “Yeah?”

“How about inviting Darlene over for dinner tomorrow? I’d love to see her.”

“Some other time.”

The sole response was the steps of Yon’s mother sputtering off.

What he couldn’t hear was Glenn Miller.

No matter what he did, no matter how much he did it, the song wouldn’t come back. Not a fragment.

His mind wandered. To a girl and what she was up to. In the face of how it shouldn’t have wandered there. That his life depended on it not getting within a light year of there.

 

 

MY MIND WANDERED to that girl too. “What were you doing?”

She didn’t want to tell me.

But she didn’t have a choice if she wanted me to resume reading her the story.

She moaned. “I was banging against the walls in the dump that I was staying at.”

“You were what?”

“I was listening to Bad Brains’ Yellow Tape, which a friend had given me after I got out of jail, and I was doing what I did whenever I listened to it. H.R., his voice would reach out and grasp me. It would throw me into wall after wall. It does this today. I bang into walls whenever I listen to them. And I can tell you that it raises eyebrows at work.

“I listen to Richard Hell when I’m afraid. But I listen to Bad Brains when I want to forget. That night, ‘Big Take Over’ was blasting into my head. It was a jackhammer into concrete. I listened to it six thousand times. Only the music existed as I hurled into walls, breaking plaster everywhere. I was in such a lather that my body stopped tormenting me over drugs. Or I could pretend that it did. But . . .”

“But what?” I said.

“A fist hit a wall from the other side. It came close to punching through it. A man hollered, ‘Stop that racket!’

“I didn’t holler back. I fell to the floor. I gasped for breath. With sweat running down my face. With my arms around my legs. I was willing myself to a different place. Any but that one.

“This didn’t work. It never did.

“‘Some fries are out here if you want,’ he said next.

“But I didn’t want them. I especially didn’t want them from him.”

I read between her lines. “Yon wasn’t on your mind?”

She growled. “Why do you think I was listening to Bad Brains? I had been listening to them since I’d left the animal hospital. They had me running over parked cars.”

“But now you couldn’t listen to them.”

“Now I couldn’t listen to them. Instead, I yanked out the drawing that I’d made at lunch. I did with surprise. I had sworn that I’d been drawing Lee Ving. My crush at the time. Me and most punk girls on the planet. Probably lots of punk boys too.

“The drawing did have Ving’s eyes. They were piercing into me when rendered in two dimensions. But the hair was too wavy. The nose and chin were too chiseled.

“It hit me who was staring at me from the drawing.

“Furious at myself, I ripped out the page. I crumpled it. I chucked it across the floor. But . . .”

I tipped forward in my seat. “But what?”

“This didn’t work. So I slunk over to the paper. I uncrumpled it. I forced myself to accept that my attraction was hopeless. ‘You’re so not good enough,’ my voice rasped. It was unsteadier than it had been in the washroom.

“I shook my head. I put it everywhere but on the drawing. And on the mirror across the room and what it would’ve reflected back to me. ‘You’re so not him. And this, this is so not a fairy tale.’”

I exhaled. “I have one last question.”

“What’s that?”

“The person in the drawing, had his eyelids fluttered?”

“Some of this I’ll keep to myself.”

“That was a yes,” I blurted out.

I turned the page. I was back at the beginning. “That, that’s it.”

“What?” Rudi cried.

I fumbled about. I shuffled through the entries. Anxious for more. More anxious than I had been in years.

No more entries were anywhere.

“What do you mean?” she said.

“That was the final page he sent me.”

“But that’s not the story’s end.”

“But that’s all he sent.”

“Today.”

“What?”

“That’s all he sent you today.”

“What do you mean?”

“There are seven more nights of Chanukah.”

“He’s gonna send me more? I mean, he sent me more?”

“Call me tomorrow night.”

Not a doubt was in her voice that I would have a reason to.

 

 

I SHOVELED YON’S pages back into the envelope.

It was past midnight. The moon had buried itself behind clouds. It had taken the palm trees and the canyons with it.

The one gleam shone from the courtyard. It burned into the night. It would through it.

I had to work the next morning. But I couldn’t go to bed. I had to get a bus pass. So I could get to work. Getting it in the morning had been iffy.

This getting was a ritual. Regardless of who was working the night shift.

But with Josh it was the worst. Some nights he would make me stand outside the office till my feet ached. He enjoyed my reliance on him.

He didn’t make me wait much that night. He got up. He tugged the door back. “Yes?”

I told him.

He grumbled. But he led me to a room beside the mess hall.

In the corner of it, he unlocked and opened a vault that loomed over both of us.

He plucked a stack of day passes that the shelter got for free. He offered me one.

But he wouldn’t let it go. “This is a pretty sweet deal you get here. Free transportation each day.”

“I’d buy my own pass if you let me spend the money I earn.”

“That’s right. We’d throw you out if we caught you spending money.”

He released the pass. But not without adding, “We could throw you out for just about any reason. Any at all.”

I didn’t respond. Not in any way.

Putting aside how I wished I could, I went off. I stuffed the pass into my wallet. I returned to the dorm.

I slithered down the hall. I passed an open door to sleeping quarters on my left.

Here not only the women slept but the children. Teenage boys had to sleep there.

This must’ve uneased the women. But leaving the shelter would’ve been worse.

While nobody wanted to be on the streets of Victorville in the winter, the cold was the least bad awaiting women there. It made them more dependent on the place than the men.

Our quarters were past them across the hall.

I pushed myself through the doorway. To snoring so loud that it could’ve woken a cemetery. Including the one in Arlington. I made my way through the dark. In a tomb filled with dozens of bunk beds and the weary men on top of them. I stumbled to the back. To my bed.

With a heave, I climbed to the top. I kicked off my shoes.

I took off my coat and jeans. I slid them under the pillow with Yon’s letter.

Stretching out on the slight and decaying mattress, I struggled to fall asleep.

This struggling was another ritual. On this night, it was the same and not.

The difference was that I was excited about a next day.
