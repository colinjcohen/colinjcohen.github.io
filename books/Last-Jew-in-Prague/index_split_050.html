chapter fifty: in pursuit of mr. malý

 

 

I MET ANTONÍN Malý when he flew to America for my grandpa’s funeral.

This was a difficult time for me. Grandpa and I had become close after the night on the staircase. We became closer after my grandma died.

I felt so alone on that day. While reflecting on the man who had done so much to make me not feel this. The man who really had been my grandpa.

The funeral service was somber. It was held on a snowy morning in early March. With few attending.

At its end, the rabbi recited Psalm 23. He led the others from the prayer hall.

I didn’t give a lot of notice to this. Nor did I follow the procession. I sat in the front pew, clutching an old brass compass. The same that I would clutch on that freezing and rainy desert night.

Knocking me from my haze was a colossal and heavy aging man. He limped into the hall with the aid of a cane. His slow shuffling footsteps swished over the plush carpet toward me.

They stopped beside me.

The man, with a shortage of breath and a German accent, asked me who I was.

I told him.

“I am your Great-Uncle Tonda.”

My jaw cratered. “I was sure that you were dead. My grandparents believed so.”

Tonda laughed. He sat next to me. “Sometimes I wonder myself.”

“How did you know my grandfather had died?”

“I kept ‘close tabs on him,’ as you Americans say. And on your grandmother. I would have been here for her funeral too. If I had not been incognito, if you will.”

It took minutes for Tonda to catch his breath. It took minutes more for him to ask to see my grandpa.

We walked to the altar. I opened the casket.

Two items drew my eyes. My grandma’s gold Star of David pin on Grandpa’s lapel and the fresh white rose petal in his hand. Both of which had been among his possessions after he had died.

Tonda and I left the synagogue. I drove him to my grandparents’ house.

During the day and night that followed, the two of us got acquainted over a bottle of 100-year-old cognac that Tonda had been saving for others to drink at his funeral.

What was interesting about this time was that we didn’t mourn my grandpa. We celebrated his life. We told each other stories about him. We kept telling them well past dawn and well after we had finished the bottle. More important than that, we laughed a lot. I would like to believe that Grandpa was laughing with us. I felt he was.

On my urging, Tonda stayed with me.

He died months later. He’s buried not far from my grandparents.

In the period we had together, he provided me with much-needed comfort. As well as details about this case that my grandpa never learned. This included events after the colonel’s departure from the café.

 

 

HERMANN AND KLAUS didn’t have to wait outside Café Slavia for my future great-uncle to leave.

Tonda drained his cup the moment Reidl and his Mercedes drove off. He tossed some coins onto the table. He rushed from the building.

Scooting across Národní, he dashed into his office building, watched by Hermann and Klaus from their car. Which Hermann had moved from the café’s other side.

Tonda’s heart was racing upon returning to his office. He clutched his jacket pocket. Unable to believe what was inside it, he continued clutching it.

He grabbed his phone. He dialed Gertrud.

No one answered.

He hung up. He stood in front of his window. He argued with himself over whether he should wait for Gertrud to return.

His indecision was short-lived. He made two last phone calls.

“I’m getting it now,” he said to the person on the line’s other end during his final conversation. “I’ll meet you at the Spanish Synagogue in two hours.”

Tonda put down his phone. From a desk drawer, he took out a rusty iron key and a ram’s horn. He placed them both in his briefcase. He left his office with it, taking the paternoster downstairs.

He scampered outside. Into a Tatra 77 that he had parked outside the building. He sped in this across the same bridge that Hermann and Klaus had followed Reidl over. With Hermann following him.

However, instead of leading Hermann and Klaus back into Malá Strana and down its ancient roads, Tonda drove south of it. Into an industrial part of town called Smíchov.

He parked on Holeček Street. Beside a decrepit and empty grocery store.

Hermann and Klaus parked a block and a half back.

The three waited.

 

 

A TRUCK RUMBLED toward Tonda, spewing black smoke from its exhaust.

It persisted in this as it rested across the street from the Tatra.

Tonda jumped from his car with his briefcase. His eyes darted from side to side.

With nothing unordinary in either direction, he ran toward the truck.

The driver’s window rolled down. A scruffy man in his twenties surfaced.

Tonda grumbled. “You’re late, Luděk. You know how important this is for me.”

“I couldn’t help it, šéfe. I had engine problems. You’re lucky I got here at all.”

“You don’t expect more of these problems, do you?”

Luděk couldn’t answer this.

Grumbling more, Tonda climbed into the truck.

It took off, passing Hermann’s Škoda. While continuing to spread smog across the city.

Hermann lingered. He waited for the truck to leave his sight in the rearview mirror.

Making a U-turn, Hermann followed the smoke. For blocks he did. To a building on Plzeňská Street surrounded by a barbed-wire fence, whose entrance a pair of SS soldiers guarded.

At the gate, Luděk showed a guard a piece of paper. Along with both men’s identification documents.

The man read them multiple times. He glared at the men multiple times. He also made a phone call.

Returning the documents and opening the gate, he allowed the truck to stammer inside.

Hermann turned to a wide-eyed Klaus. “What is this place?”

“An SS warehouse.”

“A warehouse of what?”

“Weapons, uniforms, equipment. Any article we could need during our operations.”

“And someone like Malý can drive into it?”

“I couldn’t drive into it. Not without proper authorization.”

“Could Reidl provide such authorization?”

“Yes.”

“So that could’ve been what he gave him inside the café.”

“I should arrest Malý the second he leaves. With whatever he takes.”

“You can arrest him anytime. After he leads us to the killer.”
