chapter twenty-nine: bumping into each other

 

 

ALONE IN THE Pinkas Synagogue, Ivo swept the floor.

He eyed the arched door’s broken lock. He would have to spend a lot of time the following day repairing it. Like what he had done with the one in the Klausen after Captain Fischer shot it apart.

It was fortunate that fixing mechanical devices was a task Ivo excelled at. He would spend twenty years doing it at Tona.

Rabbi Popper had told him that God gave everyone a gift and that this was his. He never forgot it. Nor did he ever cease feeling blessed because of it.

Ivo put his broom against a wall. He turned out the lights. He left and locked both the building and its gate.

He limped onward.

But, on reaching the cobblestones, he had an experience that made him forget his discomfort.

He bumped into the woman with the yellow shawl.

They reacted in the same way. By jumping back, their faces agape.

Klara was about to flee. Which might’ve had to do with Ivo not wearing pants.

But she didn’t flee. She stood her ground. She fixed on the man.

He did likewise to her, feeling more fear than what she expressed.

Ivo whisked his arms behind his back. “Who, who are you?”

She didn’t answer.

He pointed to himself with his shaking hand. He told her his name.

The woman didn’t give an inkling that she would speak. Only that she would run. But she muttered her name.

“That’s a pretty name,” he said.

“Thank you.”

“Your shawl is pretty too. I’ve seen you several times before. But never with that shawl till tonight.”

“A friend gave it to me.”

“It’s good to have friends, isn’t it?”

She said nothing.

“Are you Jewish?” he asked.

“Why would you ask that?”

“You are often by the synagogues.”

“I’m not Jewish. I like being by the buildings. They seem peaceful and safe.”

“They are.”

“I like to be near them. Like I like to be near the river. They both bring me comfort.”

“I do hope that you didn’t get too wet tonight.”

“No, I was standing under a—”

“—Klara?” interrupted a female voice down the street. “Is that you?”

“I better go,” Klara said to Ivo while checking who had called her.

She dragged her feet. She backed up toward a woman standing on the corner of Žatecká Street, a half block away.

“I could show you the insides,” Ivo called out, not moving a toe. “The synagogues, I mean. I could show you any one you’d like. Anytime you’d like. I work in them.”

“I know. I’ve seen you inside them and around them.”

“Me too.”

She smiled.

“Goodnight,” he said.

“To you as well.”

She rushed off.

“I’ll be here tomorrow night,” he shouted.

Her only response was to slow.

Now it was his turn to smile.
