chapter thirty: the joy of potato dumplings

 

 

FOR A SECOND straight night, Hermann didn’t get much sleep. He woke when the sun seeped through his drape-less bedroom windows.

Unlike the morning before, Ana was sleeping in his arms. Like she had done numberless times.

This had been Hermann’s favorite part of the day. He loved the scent of her hair and the beat of her body as she breathed against him. It was what made him believe that he was alive. It also made him “trust the magic of beginnings,” as his favorite philosopher Meister Eckhart had said.

Hermann enjoyed this re-experience more than the others. He so enjoyed it that, while caressing the rose petal in his pocket, he willed it and her to last forever.

Both went away.

He got up. He cleaned himself in the bathroom mirror. The best he could.

His guards drove him toward the palace. They made their way across Maisel Street. They came upon a joyful Ivo sweeping by the Maisel Synagogue.

Hermann asked the driver to pull alongside him. He rolled down his window. He called out Ivo’s name.

Ivo stopped his sweeping. Turning to Hermann, his joy waned.

“Good morning,” Hermann said. “I wanted to thank you for getting up last night.”

“That’s my job. To help you people.”

“You’re working early this morning.”

“I’ve got a lot to do.”

Hermann cracked open his door. “Could I talk to you for a few minutes?”

Ivo limped to the synagogue gate. He put down his broom. He limped to the car and inside it.

Hermann flicked his wrist at the man’s feet. “It must be difficult getting around with these.”

“It was worse before. Dr. Brod, he made miracles for my feet. He’s a feet doctor.”

“What kind of miracles did he make?”

“He made me shoes.”

“Was he a member of the synagogue?”

“Indeed he was.”

“How many years have you worked in the synagogue? I mean the Maisel.”

“Many.”

Ivo twisted his head down the road. “When I was a boy, I was an orphan. I used to beg on Old Town Square. By where Master Hus’s statue is.”

Hermann harked back to a kiss there. It brought him a joy similar to what Ivo had been expressing.

“But the statue wasn’t there when I was a boy. Mr. Šaloun hadn’t built it yet. Still, near where it is now, I’d sit on the cobblestones each day. I’d beg for my meals. Until Rabbi Popper found me.”

A smile shot across Ivo’s face. “I remember the day like it happened now. I remember it so perfectly. The rabbi took me by the hand. He took me with him. He described how he and his people had a responsibility ‘to repair the world,’ as he put it.”

“Tikkun olam,” Hermann said in Hebrew.

“To je vončo!” Ivo cried in Czech, wagging his finger at Hermann. “That’s what he called it: tikkun olam. He said that I was a part of this. That’s why he took me into his home and taught me. Then, when I got old enough, he hired me and got me my own place.”

Ivo’s smile didn’t last. It left as fast as it came while recollecting his life before meeting the rabbi. He was cold all the time. Regardless of weather. He was hungry too. On some days, he was some hungry. On others, he was a lot. But hungry he was all the time.

Ivo and Hermann shared a lot in common. Despite neither sensing it. This created a bond between them. From Hermann’s perspective the most. He wanted to protect the man. But to do so he needed to discover how Ivo was involved in the murders.

“You like Rabbi Popper, don’t you?” he asked, well aware of the answer.

“Oh, do I. And his wife, Mrs. Popperová. She’s who taught me how to make potato dumplings. My favorite dish.”

Ivo’s smile returned. “A patient woman, that Mrs. Popperová. It took her a full day to teach an idiot like me how to make potato dumplings. She did it on a Sunday too. But she did it. Now each night I make them. I make them by myself. And they’re good. As good as what I would eat in restaurants. But much cheaper. Now I can afford to eat as much as I want. Sometimes I eat a lot. Now I’m never hungry.”

Ivo’s smile didn’t last any more than it had earlier. With its vanishing, he lowered his eyes. “I miss them both. I miss many who are gone. I must be too dumb to understand.”

“Understand what?”

Ivo raised his head. “Why everyone hates you so. I hear what they say about you. I’ve heard it since I was a boy sitting on the square. But it seems, it seems to me that they judge in you what they don’t want to judge in themselves.”

“You’re not dumb. You’re smarter than you believe. Smarter than most people around here. That includes me.”

Ivo opened his door. He started from the car.

“One last question,” Hermann said, causing Ivo to freeze. “My understanding is that you were with Captain Fischer at the Klausen Synagogue late in the afternoon on the day he was killed.”

“Who?”

“Captain Fischer. The man who was trying to get into the cellar over there. The one wearing a black uniform. Like what my friend wears.”

“Oh, him. He’s not a nice man. He’s always yelling at me, telling me how he’s going to kill me. He hit me one time. It’s not my fault that I’m dumb. The rabbi never yelled at me. He smiled at me when I did dumb. Mrs. Popperová as well. That Sunday, when she taught me how to make potato dumplings, she smiled at me all day long.”

“When was the last you saw Captain Fischer?”

“When I saw him last, it was five o’clock. Or seventeen hundred. It was right before I left for home. So that I could make my dumplings.”

Ivo stepped from the car. “I must go.” He wobbled his way toward the synagogue gate and the broom.

Hermann closed his door. He was surer than ever that Ivo was involved in the murders. But he was no surer how.
