chapter twenty-seven: the footprints in the cellar

 

 

THE SIX SOLDIERS stood by the arched door. They held a battering ram made from a log. With three standing on each side.

“Now!” Klaus ordered.

The men jerked the ram backward. They slammed it into the door.

It didn’t budge.

“Harder!” Klaus yelled. “Much harder!”

The men obeyed. But the door held.

Hermann snuck behind Klaus. “They’d do better with a running start.”

“Five steps back,” Klaus said to the men.

They did.

Klaus had them charge.

They knocked the heavy door open.

Behind it was an old stone staircase. Along with plenty of darkness. It mixed with the dust from the door slamming into a wall.

Hermann, Klaus, and Colonel Reidl drilled their eyes into the void.

They took a step through the doorway. With Klaus lighting the way with the flashlight.

“Pozor,” Hermann whispered.

With carefulness, they went down the short staircase. To an ancient stone cellar with a dirt floor.

Klaus moved the light over it.

“This place looks older than the building,” bellowed the colonel’s baritone voice.

“Prague was originally built at river level,” Hermann said. “Because of floods, in the thirteenth century they built on top of what had existed. Cellars like this exist throughout the city center.”

Klaus shined the flashlight onto Hermann. “We’re not at river level.”

“You’re right. This must’ve been an upper chamber or an attic.”

“Now what?”

“May I have the flashlight?”

Klaus handed it to Hermann.

He swept brightness across the cluttered room. He stopped at the door of a dumbwaiter on the wall.

Pausing over it, he shook his head.

He continued on. He reached an item more illuminating.

A book on top of some boxes.

He strode to it. It was a Hebrew Bible.

A page was dog-eared. He opened the book to it and an underlined passage.

“What is it?” Klaus asked.

“Job 29:18. ‘I shall die in my nest, and I shall multiply my days like the phoenix.’”

“Does it have meaning?”

Hermann lowered the book. “I can’t say.”

He returned the flashlight across the room. He uncovered a pair of footprints in the dirt. It led to a stone wall with a Star of David painted on it. “Odd.”

“What’s odd?” Klaus asked.

“Look at the footprints. At how deep they are.”

“If the killer was carrying Kleiner . . .”

“How could Kleiner have come down here? How could he have gotten through the door upstairs?”

“The killer could’ve brought him downstairs.”

“But why?”

Klaus didn’t answer. He grabbed the flashlight. He marched to the dumbwaiter.

He opened it. He shined light into the shallow slot. He shook his head. “Not even Kleiner could’ve fit into that.”

Hermann went to Klaus. He retook the flashlight. “Forget about him for the moment.”

He shone the light back onto the footprints. “It’s not only the prints’ depth that is odd. Look how they’re shaped. They have no curves around the insteps. And the lengths are incredible. Have you ever seen feet that size?”

Hermann laughed at his own question.

Reidl bleated. “Is there humor in this, Jew?”

“I was laughing at myself, Colonel. Because an absurdity had occurred to me.”

“What was that?” Klaus asked.

“That these could be the footprints of a golem.”

“A golem?”

“A giant made of clay. With brute strength but limited capacity for thought. Some say that it’s what inspired Mary Shelley to write Frankenstein.”

“I remember seeing a movie about a golem when we were in college. Is that what you mean?”

“Exactly. Well, not exactly. Paul Wegener, the director of that film, took liberties with the legend. In it, if you recall, Rabbi Loew, the Maharal, created an amulet with a magic word that he had gotten from some spirit he’d called. He placed this amulet on the golem’s chest to make it come alive.”

“I remember that too.”

“But in the actual legend, the rabbi used the secrets in that book of Jewish mysticism we were reading at the Spanish Synagogue to create a golem. Also, instead of using an amulet to bring it to life, he carved the magic word into its forehead. The Hebrew word for ‘truth’: אמת. Later, he would turn the golem off by erasing the first letter, changing it into the word for ‘death’: מת.”

Hermann breathed in the stale air. “It’s fascinating how the Hebrew words for ‘truth’ and ‘death’ are related. With the former deriving from the latter. I expect that it comes from our belief that, when we die, our souls enter a place of truth, where we learn the purpose of existence. But more fascinating than this is that Rabbi Loew actually lived. About three hundred years ago. He’s buried in that cemetery we were in. Some people, to this day, believe that the golem was real. I used to hear talk about how it was locked away in the attic of the Old-New Synagogue, a couple of blocks from here. Years back, this old woman marched into my office. She demanded that I investigate whether it was there.”

Klaus gulped. “Did you?”

“Did I investigate it? If I had, I would’ve been fired well before you people arrived in town.”

“You, you believe that’s what killed Captain Kleiner?” Reidl stammered, his voice expressing fear greater than Klaus’s.

“Of course not,” Hermann said, surprised by both men’s fright. “It’s preposterous.”

“It would explain those stomping feet,” Klaus countered, his fright exceeding that of before.

“The killer might want his victims to believe that a golem is pursuing them. To scare them. To scare away anyone who might get in his way. Now that would make sense.”

“But we’re no closer to discovering who this killer is.”

“Before we can answer that question, we will have to answer others. Such as, what has driven the conspirators’ mania with getting into these cellars? More perplexing than that is, why do these footprints lead to the wall and not back?”

Reidl humphed. “For your sake, Jew, you better come up with some answers to these questions. Quickly. This evening, after you two left my office, I arranged for Captain Stamm’s replacement to show up early. He’ll be here in two days.”
