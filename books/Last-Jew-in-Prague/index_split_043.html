chapter forty-three: pretending it all away

 

 

KLARA’S EYES BORE through the front window of a boarding house. From an easy chair steps from it.

Unlike a day earlier, no melancholy was in her eyes. Marvel was in them.

“She’s been acting odd since last night,” Mrs. Jimelová said to Gertrud from the room’s other side. “I mean odder than how she was acting when you brought her here. She’s either been staring into the street through that window or into the river through the window in her room.”

Gertrud swirled toward a woman her age, who had on a black dress and had her dark-brown hair styled in a bun. “What happened last night?”

“She was roaming the streets of Josefov.”

“Josefov? What was she doing there?”

“She wouldn’t tell me. I came upon her as I was returning home after babysitting for my sister. She was talking to some peculiar man. He wasn’t wearing pants.”

“Really? She’s barely said a word to me.”

“Me neither.”

“It took me months to get her name.”

“I took her home afterward. That’s when the staring started.”

“She looks happier.”

“Thanks to you, I’m sure.”

“I have a feeling that she’s been through awfulness,” Gertrud said, returning her focus to Klara and feeling her own awfulness rush back.

“What makes you say that?”

“It’s a feeling.”

“Are you a relative of hers?”

“No. But we have a lot in common.”

“I would’ve never figured that someone like you would have a thing in common with someone like her.”

“You’d be wrong.”

“Well, you needn’t worry. I’ll take good care of her.”

“I know you will. I’m counting on it.”

Gertrud left the house.

Down the street, Mr. Malý’s car was by his office building.

This tempted her to pay him a visit. Stopping her was that she had so much to do that day.

She jumped into her own car. She drove to her home streets away.

In the courtyard, she parked. She grabbed an array of shopping bags from her trunk. She traipsed into her building.

She pretended too. She pretended a lot. Such as that her encounter with Klara hadn’t affected her and that she was in control of herself. Beyond that, she pretended that she was another dumb socialite preparing for another empty night of fun.

“Mr. Malý has been calling you every five minutes,” Kamča said to her while taking the bags in the doorway.

“He can be determined when he wants to be. Which unfortunately isn’t enough.”

“The way you juggle men, Gertie. I can’t handle one husband.”

“I assure you that it’s nothing more than an optical illusion.”

“If you say so.”

“How are we doing for tonight?”

Kamča squinted at the kitchen with her hands on her waist. “An entire deer must be in there. I have enough food cooking to feed a garrison.”

“That’s what’s coming tonight. Except they’re all officers. Apart from the women, of course.”

“Women are going to be here too?”

“A few. Oh, one surprise guest as well.”

“And who would that be?”

“If I told you, it wouldn’t be a surprise, would it? How are we with booze?”

“I restocked the liquor cabinet the other day.”

“We’ll need twice that much for this bunch.”

“I’ll call it in.”

The phone rang in response.

Kamča started toward it. “After I pick that up.”

“I’ll get it,” Gertrud said. “It’s likely for me. Why don’t you put those things away?”

“I’ll call in the liquor order as soon as you’re through.”

“I can’t imagine how I’d get by without you.”

“Please remember that when I ask for my next raise.”

“I’m already looking forward to it.”

Both women laughed.

Gertrud hustled to the phone. She answered it.

Right away, she picked up on the voice and its state. “So where’s the fire this time, Tonda?”

“Your gentleman friend paid me a visit this morning,” he cried.

“Which?”

“You have others?”

“Do you really need to ask such a question?”

“It was Captain Stamm. Along with that Jewish colleague of his you mentioned. A most interesting man. To say the least.”

“You needn’t tell me that either. But why were they visiting you?”

Tonda gasped. “They suspect me of murder.”

“Is that it? And here you had me worried.”

“They’ve also connected me to certain entities in Josefov and to some unvoiced conspiracy related to them,” he said, fear rising in his voice.

“Hermann is indeed a fine detective.”

The apartment’s front door opened and closed. It shifted Gertrud toward a window facing the front of her building. “But I do hope that this won’t scuttle that deal you’ve been working on.”

“It could well affect it. Hence, the call. I wanted to get your expert advice.”

“As I told you, you should hasten that deal of yours. Hasten it as much as you can. Intriguingly, both Klaus and Hermann will be here later for dinner. Along with a big VIP.”

“How big of a VIP are we talking about?”

“As big as they come. In the neck of this jungle.”

“I can’t tell you how badly I’d love to join you.”

“I have a feeling that it’ll be explosive enough without you.”

“Oh, well, I have a never-ending day tomorrow.”

“So tomorrow it will be?”

“Tomorrow it’ll be. I’ve decided, thanks to you. Perhaps I’ll call you after the deal’s complete. So we can celebrate.”

“I’ll be waiting, covered in bells.”

The two said their goodbyes. Gertrud hung up.

But she didn’t move from the window and its view.

Kamča was talking to a Nazi officer outside her home. The woman was also pointing at her unit.

None of this was a surprise to Gertrud. But did she need to prepare herself for one?
