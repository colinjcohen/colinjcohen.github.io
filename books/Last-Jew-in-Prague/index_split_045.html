chapter forty-five: different turns of the same phrase

 

 

KLAUS CONTINUED TO seethe.

He reached the Škoda. He threw open the driver-side door.

Hermann stopped by the car’s other side. “You better let me drive.”

Klaus stomped his foot. “Why?”

“Do you not remember when I asked if you had ever followed someone?”

Klaus sighed. He stormed around the car.

Passing Hermann, he dropped the keys into his hand without saying a word. Or putting his eyes on him.

His coolness went on after the two entered the car. He didn’t stir from the square. He made not a sound.

He broke this while fighting to keep his anger concealed. “We’ll be doing a lot of waiting tonight, I suppose.”

“Your supposition is right. Detective work can be boring. This is as dull as it gets.”

“How do you pass the time?”

“We can talk. If you’d like.”

“About what?”

“Whatever you want. For example, you could tell me what you’ve been doing since that little blow-up of ours. Well, I guess it wasn’t so little.”

Klaus averted his eyes. “You don’t care.”

Hermann also averted his eyes. “You’re wrong. I’ve often wondered what happened to you. Ana wondered too. She told me so.”

“I never graduated from Charles.”

“Neither did I.”

“What was your excuse?”

“Ana got pregnant unexpectedly. What’s funny is that everyone believed that this was why we had moved the wedding date up. It could’ve been why no one showed up for it. But she actually got pregnant right after we married. I had to take a job with the police. It was the only one I was qualified to do.”

“You have a child?”

“He was stillborn.”

“I’m sorry.”

“Me too. I was planning to return to school afterward. Like we were planning to have more children. Like I was planning to take Ana to Bled like I had promised her. But none of this happened. It’s amazing what you put off while convincing yourself that there will be time to do them. What about you?”

“Me?”

“What was your excuse for leaving Charles?”

“I didn’t leave. They asked me to leave. I don’t drink anything harder than milk for a reason.”

“And?”

“And what?”

“What have you been doing the past twenty years?”

“That’s a good question.”

“You don’t know?”

“I have patches of memory. But I can’t tell which are real and which have been distorted. Years passed in which I’m not sure whether I was ever sober. I only became so when I woke in a jail near Dresden. It was early 1936, I believe. It was cold outside. I remember that. I also remember the visit of a local party leader. He told me in my cell that I was diluting the strength of the great and magnificent Aryan race. I could either cease this on my own or the Reich would do it for me.”

“They gave you a choice similar to what you gave me.”

“And I chose similarly.”

“But how did you end up in the SS?”

“I needed work. Like you did. When they learned of my military background, they were too willing to offer me a position in the army. This led to another. Another after that. Once you start rolling down a hill, you’ll likely roll on.”

“But why the SS?”

“Why not the SS? Why shouldn’t I be more respected and better paid than those who commit the same atrocities? It was an easy choice.”

“Easy? It was easy to become a monster?”

Klaus stewed. He clenched his fists. “Surprisingly so. To become ‘a monster,’ as you put it, you only need someone to persuade you that you are above the rules of right and wrong. For them to pat you on the back after breaking them, mentioning each time how good a job you’re doing. With proper affirmation, we are capable of any and all malice.”

“Even braining a defenseless woman with the butt of a rifle. Even dragging her body down the sidewalk like she was garbage.”

“Even killing Ana.”

Hermann jumped in his seat. “You know about her?”

“I read all about you. Including what had happened to her. You can bet that the man who killed her felt that he was doing the right thing. That he was doing a good thing. And don’t think that it’s only Nazis and Germans who can become like him. It can happen to anyone. It can happen to you.”

Hermann shook his head. He kept shaking it. “No.”

“You believe so now. But wait till you’ve committed your first murder. Wait till you realize how meaningless it is. How no repercussions come from it. No remorse. Wait till you realize that life and death are different states of the same rotting. That truth and lies are different turns of the same phrase. You see, Hermann, your Hebrew has got it wrong. Truth doesn’t come from death. It doesn’t come from anything.”

“Speaking of lies, that visa in your jacket pocket, it doesn’t exist, does it?”

Klaus said nothing. But this was enough for Hermann to laugh. “It’s okay. I guessed the truth when you made me the offer. But I was certain of it when you told me that I needed to stay quiet about this case. If you wanted me quiet then, you would want me quiet forever.”

Klaus snarled. “So why did you ask?”

Hermann snarled back. “I wanted to hear you ‘turn the phrase.’”

“Listen to you talk so righteously,” Klaus squalled, with his old hatreds of Hermann overwhelming him. “As if you were different and better. Were you truthful with me? How long were you seeing Ana behind my back?”

“The difference is that I wasn’t trying to hurt you. Nor was I being indifferent toward your feelings. It was the opposite.”

“You stabbed me in the back. Like the blackhearted Jew you are.”

“You never loved Ana.”

“How would you know?” Klaus hollered.

“Because I know who you loved,” Hermann hollered back. “We both know.”

Klaus grabbed Hermann by the lapels of his jacket. His anger swelling, he yelled, “What are you trying to say?”

“You know exactly what I’m trying to say!” Hermann yelled back. For the third time that night, he felt rage. Like Klaus, he could no longer hold it back. Nor did he care where it would lead him. Or whether it would entail complicity in his death. “You never kissed Ana. Or held her hand. Or tried to do any of this. You were using her. Like you’re using Gertrud. Because you can’t accept what you are!”

Klaus hurled Hermann against the driver’s door. Tears were in his eyes. “I could kill you right now! It’d be so easy! I wouldn’t need a pat on the back! Or a good word afterward!”

“So do it! I’m not stopping you! Do it!”

Instead, Klaus released Hermann. “You only want me to kill you because you’re too scared to do it yourself.”

Hermann lowered his head.

Was Klaus right? What about the promise that he had made to Ana not to give up? Was it a way to mask his fear of death?

“Ana,” Klaus said, “she at least had the courage to control her fate. Rather than living in fear of it.”

Hermann began to break apart. Ana’s actions at the train station iterated before him. As well as the lack of his own. With silence and without tears, he wept. For her and himself.

Klaus leapt at this. He let loose twenty years of built-up contempt. “Look at you. Just look. The hero of Verdun, nothing more than a sniveling coward. Ana, she’d be ashamed of you. She’d be ashamed of you!”

In less than a quarter of an instant, Hermann transformed.

Much as how Klaus had broken him apart, he made him roar. Both inside and out he roared.

Releasing a fury not only from that day but from years of days like it, Hermann punched Klaus with all he had in the face. He knocked him backward against the passenger door.

“You can take whatever you want from me, Klaus,” Hermann screamed, grabbing the man by the collar. “Whatever you want. My life above the rest. But I won’t let you take my memories of Ana. I won’t!”

Klaus flung his own punch.

Hermann blocked it. He pounded Klaus’s face with a flurry of blows. It sent Klaus’s cap to the floor and blood down his cheeks.

The latter couldn’t diminish Hermann’s wrath. It drove him wilder. Blinded by antipathy, he hit Klaus everywhere.

The man tumbled about in his seat.

Like outside The Eldorado club in Berlin, Klaus could’ve defended himself. He could’ve halted the onslaught. At least that’s what Hermann would believe. But like then, he was enjoying it too much. He wanted Hermann to pummel him. He wanted him to finish the job that the two men on Kant Street couldn’t. He wanted Hermann to destroy him and all that there was about himself that he hated.

But just as love isn’t always reciprocated, self-destruction isn’t always realized. Hermann ceased his punching.

An engine had started. It shifted his attention toward Colonel Reidl’s Mercedes and its backing out of its spot.

It drove off.

Hermann stayed fixed on it. “Are you going to shoot me? Or are we going to follow Reidl?”

Klaus didn’t answer. He sat by the door dazed. With blood dripping onto his pristine black uniform.

“What do you want me to do?” Hermann cried.

Klaus slumped over. He remained this way. “Follow him.”

Hermann complied. He started the car. He backed it up. He sped off.

With an eye on the Mercedes, he put the other on Klaus.

He was reaching into his jacket.

For a handkerchief.
