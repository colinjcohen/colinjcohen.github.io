chapter twenty-five: in the rain

 

 

“YOU’RE DRIVING TOO fast,” Hermann called out from the Mercedes’ back seat.

Rain was pounding the windshield, making it difficult to see. This included seeing Colonel Reidl, who was standing in the middle of the road by the Pinkas Synagogue.

The driver slammed his brakes. His tires screeched. The vehicle halted by the man’s knees.

Thunder and lightning followed. It exposed Reidl’s face and equal measures of shock and fright. But I doubt either had to do with the car that had come close to hitting him.

From examining the colonel’s record at the Military History Institute, during this war and the one before it, I can say that he wasn’t a man who scared with ease. Each report I read of him depicted someone whose mind stayed calm under the worst of times. Such as at Verdun.

But the enemy he was now facing was different. It was unknown and omnipotent. Fear had overtaken him.

Klaus recognized the man’s fear. Unnerved by it, he reached for his door. So did Hermann.

But Klaus stopped him. “You better wait here.”

Klaus exited the car. He marched toward the colonel.

Hermann cracked open his window to hear the conversation.

Reidl forgot about his fright. Klaus saluted him, but he didn’t react to it. He reacted only to Klaus’s car and the man in the back of it. He shook his hand at him in fury. “What’s he doing here?”

“I was going to take him to Theresienstadt in the morning,” Klaus said. Though he must’ve recognized that this wasn’t an answer.

“But what’s he doing here, Captain? You’ve disobeyed a direct order.”

“He’s a good detective, Colonel. Better than good. In a single twenty-four-hour period, he’s gotten further with this investigation than we had in weeks. If we want to discover who’s behind these murders and stop him before he kills again, he’s our chance. Our only chance.”

The colonel gave weight to Klaus’s words, his face burning with a rancor that wouldn’t diffuse. He may not have been able to tell whether he directed this more at Hermann or at Klaus. “All right. Bring the Jew out.”

Klaus complied. Reidl led the two as well as Klaus’s Mercedes and its headlights along the synagogue gate.

A dead and wet Captain Kleiner was sitting on the ground at the end of it, shot in the right side of his head. He was expressing a horror similar to the other victims.

Hermann, in the subsiding rain, stepped toward the man and his muddy uniform, unsure if there was relevance to the latter.

He pointed at Kleiner’s empty holster. “His gun’s missing.”

Hermann kneeled on the ground. He checked the man’s wound. “It’s self-inflicted. It could’ve happened in the struggle. Or he could’ve done it to himself.”

Reidl clenched both his hips and his jaw. “Why would he do it to himself?”

“Perhaps to avoid worse.”

Klaus walked to Hermann. “But he was seated like the others.”

“It certainly appears that the same man killed him.”

Reidl slung his arms out. “That’s brilliant deduction.”

Hermann turned to the man. “Were you nearby when he was killed?”

“Are you interrogating me, Jew?” Reidl shrieked, his rancor overwhelming him to a higher degree than his fear had.

“Forgive me.”

“You better hope I will,” Reidl snapped. He said to Klaus, “After you left my office this evening, Captain Kleiner wondered about that theory of yours. Of where the men had been killed. He told me that he wanted to search the synagogues for evidence of Maier’s accomplices. So, after getting the keys to the buildings, he went off. With two of my men to protect him.”

Reidl snorted. “Later on, he called me. He sounded frenzied. He prattled about some colossal man lurking around the Klausen Synagogue. They followed him into this synagogue, where he disappeared. I told Kleiner to stay with my men and wait for me outside. But he didn’t do this.”

“Where did he get the keys from?” Klaus asked. “It was my understanding that we didn’t have them, that some civilian in contact with our office had a set.”

“That civilian is a man Captain Kleiner knew named Malý. He got the keys from him.”

“That’s the man who pays the caretaker of the synagogues. You wouldn’t happen to have any information about him? Such as his first name or where he works?”

“I have none.”

“You mentioned that Kleiner was here with two of your men. Where are they?”

Reidl waved down the road. At a pair of Mercedes like Klaus’s. One was Reidl’s, where sat his driver. In the other sat two men. “Those two idiots of mine were with him. Like I said, I sent them with Kleiner to protect him. But Kleiner ordered them to search through the synagogue for the man. They told me that, through a window, they spotted him in the graveyard out there and chased him. They chased him through the cemetery into the streets. But he gave them the slip. Then, when they came back to the synagogue, they found Kleiner where he is now. This was minutes before I arrived.”

Hermann went through Kleiner’s pockets. He fished out a gold coin. He rose to his feet with it. “Take a look at this.”

Klaus stepped toward him. “What is it?”

“A ducat from 1766. Maria Theresa had it struck in commemoration of her husband’s passing. It’s rare.”

“So it’s valuable.”

Hermann flipped Klaus the coin. “I should say. Years ago, there was one for sale at a jewelry store on Pařížská. It cost more than a month’s salary.”

“What would Kleiner be doing with it?”

A sound interrupted the answer. Stomping feet echoed in the distance. It went by them before fading.

Klaus leaned toward Hermann. “Could that be what Mr. Hrabal has been hearing?”

“It’s likely.”

“My men mentioned stomping feet,” Reidl interjected.

Klaus leaned closer to Hermann. “What next?”

Hermann shifted toward the synagogue. “I want to go inside.”
