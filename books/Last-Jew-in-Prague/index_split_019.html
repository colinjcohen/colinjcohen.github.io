chapter nineteen: their own triple entente

 

 

BACK IN THE palace with his new guards, Hermann discovered that Marta was gone.

She and the errand that he had sent her on were the scope of what could’ve occupied his time until dinner.

This meant that he could do no more than sit in his cramped, windowless office. Next to the same police reports that he’d spent too much time by.

He asked his guards if they could take him early to U Bílého Lva, a short distance away on Bělohorská Street.

The men couldn’t come up with any grounds to refuse him. They drove him to the restaurant.

He sat in the center of it. With a beer and hours to kill.

This beer was 12 degrees. Much stronger than the others that he had since returning to Prague.

A couple were enough to send him drifting, along with his surroundings, to happier moments.

Those spent twenty years earlier. With Ana and Klaus at U Zlatého Tygra.

 

 

“NA ZDRAVÍ!” THE three cried, raising their mugs of beer.

They slammed the glasses together. Doing so with such force that Hermann couldn’t imagine that they wouldn’t break.

They didn’t.

Hermann was two beers behind his new friends. He needed to work up a thirst.

He devoured the plate of utopence in front of him.

Klaus grimaced. As he would at Hermann twenty years later when he ate. “How can you eat that?”

“I don’t know,” Hermann said, flinging a chunk of meat into his mouth.

He swallowed it after a pair of bites. “Fortunately, knowing has nothing to do with it.”

Ana laughed. Her upper lip curled.

Hermann would say that he had never beheld a lip do this. But it could’ve been that he had never paid notice to it.

Regardless of which, it riveted him. She riveted him. She was the single element that he could focus on.

Klaus disrupted this. By pointing at the pickle jar behind the bar.

Dozens of sausages were floating in the brine. They were like a bunch of drowned people. Which is what utopence means in Czech.

“Do you have any idea how long they’ve been sitting in that?” Klaus asked. “They’ve probably been in that jar since before the war.”

“They’re like fine wine.”

Hermann wasn’t the connoisseur that he was letting on. This was only the second time that he had had the dish.

The other was during the journey a day earlier. At a stopover late at night in Pardubice. When he had run out of langoš.

Utopence were not only cheap. They were the one food served in the one place open. He had no more choice in them becoming his favorite food than he had in Ana becoming the object of his riveting.

Klaus sneered. At both the meat and Hermann. “They’re hardly kosher.”

“Neither am I.”

Hermann snuck a glance at Ana.

Would his Jewishness affect her opinion of him?

Klaus smirked. “You couldn’t tell that you weren’t kosher by looking at you.”

Hermann shrugged. His Jewish looks marked him wherever he went. It only heightened the older he got.

When I was growing up, antisemitic remarks and slurs would often target him when we were out somewhere. Often it would bring him joy. When I asked him why, he said, “What bothers them is that I’m alive. How can I not be joyful about that?”

Hermann didn’t feel this joy as a young man.

Many times he would deny his Jewishness. When he could get away with it. That he couldn’t with Klaus was the reason he was shrugging.

Klaus wasn’t mindful of how uneasy he was making Hermann. “Look at us. A German. A Jew. A Czech. Out together as if we were one big, happy family. Our own Triple Entente.”

Ana and Hermann laughed at Klaus’s reference to the nickname of their enemies in the recent war. But over this Hermann rolled his eyes. “A joke like that could’ve gotten you shot in my unit.” He meant it too. He could picture the officer who would’ve done the shooting.

Klaus took note of an aging man on the other side of their table. He was wearing a medal from the Austro-Prussian War and glaring at him. “It might get me shot right now.”

Ana took Klaus’s wrist. “Don’t worry. I’ll protect you.”

“But who’ll protect you?”

She spun to Hermann. “The war hero.”

This embarrassed Hermann. More than what Klaus’s kosher quip had. Worse, it made him want to crawl under the table.

The sole matter that held him over it was the pub noise. It drowned out the sounds of war that her comment had evoked.

Ana dipped her head toward Hermann. “I read all about you in Lidové noviny. The local boy who made good.”

Hermann shied away from her. “I’m not exactly a local boy. I’m from a shtetl in Subcarpathia that nobody’s heard of. That includes people from other shtetls in Subcarpathia.”

“I could’ve sworn that I read you were from Prague.”

“My father and his family were from here. So when the propaganda officer asked me where I was from, I . . .”

“You lied,” Klaus interjected.

“I lied,” Hermann said while recalling his father and the stories that he had told him about Prague by a crackling fire. Stories whose number was in excess. In disregard of how he wasn’t older than a baby when he left the city.

This storytelling was an experience that my grandpa and I would recreate decades later. Like on the night of my bar mitzvah.

It’s one that I’ve wanted to recreate with my own child. While talking about the same city. While sitting by the same kind of fire.

Hermann’s eyes seldom blinked during the talks with his father. The city existed past his imagining, not having had pictures of it to root it. Nor could he draw parallels to it in the fields and forests around his home.

This drove his fascinating higher. Some days his wits were only on Prague.

They were there when he was at school. When he was doing his chores. When he lay in bed at night.

Hermann would obsess with Prague. Which was pleasing to his father, who would obsess with the same city.

But it wasn’t so to his mother, who would obsess with a different locale.

She had broadcast her yearning for Jerusalem from Hermann’s beginnings. She would talk about the city during the Sabbath and the High Holidays. She would sing about it. She did what she could to impart onto Hermann a love for a city that she had visited only in words.

But it never sparked his interest in the same way as his true love.

It didn’t spark it at all.

Hermann tapped the table. With the force of his fervor. “It’s forever been my intent to move to Prague. To be from here. To make something of myself here.”

Ana sank herself into his eyes. “I have no doubt you will.”

She aspired Hermann. More than before. She made him want to believe in his words. She gave him the courage to believe in them. In himself too. She also gave him the desire to return what he was feeling.

“Besides,” she added, “now that Subcarpathia is part of Czechoslovakia, you’re as much of a citizen here as anyone. You have as much right to be from Prague as I do.”

Glee shot through Hermann. But with Klaus uneasy, as uneasy as Hermann had been earlier, he changed the subject. “Where are you from?” he asked Ana.

“From right here in Vysočany,” was her answer, referring to a district in the city’s northeastern highlands. “We’ve only recently become part of Prague. So I’m an adopted Pražanka as well.”

“Are you going to the university?”

“I’m attending the Academy of Fine Arts.”

“You’re an artist?”

“She’s a painter,” Klaus interjected. “One of those nasty expressionists.”

Hermann’s excitement poured out. “You mean like Max Beckmann?”

Ana grappled for words. “You, you’ve heard of Max Beckmann?” She would later tell Hermann that she knew no one outside of school who knew of Beckmann and his work.

“I came across some of his paintings at a gallery in Frankfurt am Main when I was passing through there after finishing my military service. Never had I seen anything like it. Especially that painting . . . what was it called?”

Hermann scoured his memory.

He snapped his fingers. “The Night. You know, before I saw it, I thought of art as something to admire only for its beauty. I never thought that it could make you think and feel. I must’ve stared at that painting for an hour. I expected that they would call the police on me.”

“He’s among my biggest influences,” Ana blurted out. “Especially that painting you mentioned. And I can tell you that I’ve stared at it for a lot more than an hour. I mean, I’ve stared at the copy of it we have at school.”

“Really?”

“I’d love to show you my work.”

“I’d love to see it.”

“I should warn you, my paintings aren’t as good as Beckmann’s.”

“Beckmann must’ve been a student too. You must have talent. Or a school like the academy wouldn’t have accepted you. Why, I bet you have twice as much talent as any man there. Or, like I said, they would’ve never accepted you. What you need is further training and life experience and maybe, I don’t know, someone to believe in you.”

The glee was now Ana’s. But it must’ve been less at him than at his words.

Ana would tell Věra and her mother that, on meeting Hermann, she had felt attracted to him, despite their difference in height.

He was handsome and charming. She kept getting lost in what she would call his “impossible brown curls.” Several times that evening she had to stop herself from reaching over the table and wrapping her fingers around them.

But now her attraction to Hermann was deeper than physical.

From the moment Ana had reached adolescence, men had fawned over her. They had drowned her in compliments. But never did these go beyond her looks. Never did men care to notice what was beyond them.

Hermann noticed. He cared.

It made her speculate as to whether he could provide her with the trait lacking in her and holding her back: confidence in herself.

Beyond that, it made her sway toward him and the possibilities in his eyes.

This got Hermann more excited.

But it got Klaus more uneasy. It got him upset.

Hermann supposed that this was from being excluded from the conversation. He directed it to him. “And you? Where are you from?”

“Reichenberg.”

Ana tossed her elbows onto the table. No scarcity of defiance was on her face. “The city today is called Liberec.”

Klaus countered with his own defiance. “You can call it Xanadu if you want. To us, it’s always been Reichenberg. To us, it will always be Reichenberg.”

“Please, you two,” Hermann interrupted, “let’s not start another war. I’ll never finish school.”

Ana laughed. Her upper lip curled. She and Hermann sank their eyes into each other’s.

Klaus got more uneasy. More upset.

He chugged his beer.

Spinning toward the bar and the waiter standing by it, he raised his empty mug into the air. “Ještě jednou!”

Hermann’s mind was distant from Klaus’s demand for another beer.

It was on the woman who was becoming his fate.

But his mind wasn’t so distant from the clearing of a throat.

It brought him back to U Bílého Lva and his present state.
