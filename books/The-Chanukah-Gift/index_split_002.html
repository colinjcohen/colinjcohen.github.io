the second night

 

 

I WOKE IN the night. In the middle of it.

It wasn’t the snoring that woke me. Despite how it was as loud as it had been before.

What woke me was what did each night. It would lurk during the day but come out at this time. When I couldn’t fight back. When it was free to strike.

Waves of darkness swept over me. With them came the demons. They crawled into my head. They pushed me down.

They pushed me below myself. They made me believe that I would never get up while reminding me of how I had messed up my life. Of how I couldn’t undo it. Or make it bearable. I could only give in to it. I could only wilt.

Over and over they cried this at me. In ear-splitting silence.

I turned and tossed. I covered my head. From both them and my fear of escaping them. I prayed too. For the sun to rise. So I could go to work. So I could focus on it.

So it could numb me.

 

 

FLLLIIICKKKK.

Glare filled the tomb. It crammed each nook with a yellow so intense that it forced my eyes open. It also sent the demons scurrying for cover.

I didn’t need to check the time. It was six. When they woke us each morning but Sunday. When they waited until seven.

Some people hid from the glare in their pillows. But I got up with those who had reason to. Or had wanted to pretend it.

I tossed my pillow from my clothes. I hauled out my wallet.

Satisfied that my five dollars were there, I returned the wallet to my jeans. I put them and my coat and my shoes on. I crept through the tomb to the door.

Mateo was sleeping in a bottom bunk.

I shook him.

He groaned. But he did no more.

I recalled an old TV commercial. “Time to make the doughnuts, dude.”

He laughed. But he did no more.

I went into the corridor. To cubbies stacked in three rows.

On the day that I had gotten to the shelter over the summer, they gave me a slot on top, discounting my lack of elevation. I had to imitate a primate to get to mine. To get my towel, my shower bag, and a clean set of clothes.

Into the men’s bathroom I carried them. To a trio of sinks.

I shaved and brushed my teeth beside a fellow riser.

Here I had to accept the decay reflecting off the mirrored wall. No morning went by without me getting grayer and gaunter.

This morning was no different. Yet I was humming, the song my sister had sung on her first day at Columbia. I was humming it as I strolled toward the shower stalls down the way. I did so with a verve that I couldn’t deny was there.

Beside the bench that spanned the stalls, I undressed. I took out my wallet. What we men learned to do if we wanted to have money when we left the stalls.

Having your money stolen while you showered was a rite of passage at the shelter. It happened so often that the staff would laugh when people complained about it. They had laughed weeks earlier when someone lost hundreds of dollars.

What’s funny is that this didn’t happen to me. While the thieves did go through my wallet during my first shower there, returning it to the wrong pocket, they left my five dollars alone. Either it wasn’t worth pulling out or was too pitiful to steal. Or both.

The experience had made me paranoid. It was why I would check my wallet when I woke. Why I would put it in my shower bag.

With this bag, I stepped into the stall. I fired the hot water at me. I enjoyed the few minutes of normal allotted to me each day.

I willed it to last.

 

 

I GOT A bagged lunch from a guy working in the kitchen. I made a cup of green tea. The limit to what I could stomach at this time.

With the mug, I took myself into the courtyard.

I became awash in light and palms. Both were everywhere. So was the breeze that got beneath my jacket. It both stiffened me and made me shiver.

My eyes drifted. To a group of women in the booths.

They were getting their kids ready for school. They were giving them a normal similar to what I had enjoyed in the shower. Beyond that, they were making them feel special in a place where they weren’t. They were making them feel that they mattered. That someone loved them.

One mom was brushing her child’s hair. Others admired theirs while they played. Another was checking her son’s homework. She was on an epic quest.

On the surface, the kids weren’t different from those with homes. But beneath this was what made them different. The rumor was that it was kids stealing people’s money by the showers.

Whether true or not, it wasn’t the worst that they could’ve been doing.

My eyes drifted some more.

Nichelle and Wendy were drinking coffee and smoking. At the same corner table where I had talked to Rudi the night before.

They could do the smoking because the shelter made an exception to its spending ban when it came to cigarettes.

Was it their way of assuring attrition?

I joined the women. Both in their early thirties. I sat next to Nichelle.

She was someone who shattered several stereotypes of a homeless person. She worked two jobs as a manufacturing supervisor. She was as bright as the High Desert days. Also, unlike most of us, she refused to give up on hope, blind to how it never paid her a dividend. She would convey what you could only call delight from her waking each morning.

An additional quality that set Nichelle apart was how she didn’t suffer from any psychological or substance issues that I could tell. She was a victim of events beyond her control. Her husband’s going to prison, combined with medical bills, had overwhelmed her. She was staying at the shelter to save money. To get herself over the water. To get her four kids back from wherever Child Protective Services had taken them.

She leaned toward Wendy. “Saturday night we’re having it.”

My interest piqued. “Having what?”

Nichelle’s head darted around. She tilted it toward me. “I was talking to Lynnette the other night. She’s turning ten on Saturday. And she’s upset about having a birthday here. So we’re throwing her a party.”

“Are you serious?”

“Keep quiet about it. I want it to be a surprise.”

“How will you pay for it?”

“I’ll figure it out.”

 

 

THE MOTEL 6 was a ten-minute drive down the road from the shelter.

But no bus went down the road, ignoring how the only shopping mall in the Victor Valley was on it. Along with any store you could imagine. The two buses Mateo and I would take crisscrossed the valley and the concrete and glass strewn across it.

On both buses that day, I sat by the window under darkening skies as we drove into the wild. It was hard to believe that we were a few hours from Los Angeles. Or from anywhere. It was a setting for a dystopia.

Though a shine was off in the distance. It was striving to break free.

The trip to the motel took an hour and a half. Not including the forty-minute jaunt to the bus stop. But this “hour and a half” was only if neither bus was late. That happened seldom.

On this morning, the buses got us to the motel five minutes early. In time for a rain shower.

I got off into the slosh. “Slota.”

Mateo edged toward me. “What’s that?”

“That’s what you call this weather in Czech.”

“Uh-huh.”

Mateo’s stomach was grumbling.

We scurried to the convenience store down the block. By the 76 gas station.

He remembered what I had told him earlier. He bought a box of doughnuts with his EBT card. This the modern means of receiving food stamps. Another exception that the shelter allowed when it came to spending money.

We left the store. We stood below an awning outside it.

Mateo pried open the box. “If Hannibal’s army had doughnuts, they would’ve won the Third Phoenician War for sure.”

“You’re thinking the Second Phoenician War.”

“That one too.”

He stuffed a doughnut into his mouth. The two of us jogged in the faltering rain to the motel. We punched in on the PC in Amoun’s office.

Leaving it, we ran into the guy with the nickels. He was paying for his room.

The counter became swamped in Jeffersons and Monticellos. The poor clerk had to count each one. More than fourteen hundred after including tax.

The automatic doors opened.

Amoun skipped inside. He was snapping his fingers. He was whooping it up as usual. He was Fred Astaire in Top Hat, and I was his Ginger Rogers. “Where is my Hebrew?”

I raised my hand. “Right here, Pharaoh.”

“Have you been cleaning out the weeds by the back lot?”

“Whenever I get a chance.”

“Be a mensch and get a chance now.”

 

 

CLEANING THE FIELD behind the parking lot in back was a never-ending task.

The field was a magnet. For garbage that guests would leave and for waste that would stream in from the desert surrounding it. Entangling this were the weeds that never ceased to grow. No matter how many I would pull. Or the scarcity of water.

No matter how much progress I would make on a given day, clinging to a pace from my years on Wall Street, days later it’d be worse.

Joining me that morning in the muddy sand was the music that was such a part of my sister’s life. I broke the gloom by playing on my phone the Bad Brains album that she had mentioned.

Not having heard the band in decades, I was surprised that they connected with me. They connected with me as they had when I was a teenager. I strutted about.

The slamming of a door couldn’t disrupt me. Or the cleaning woman who pushed her cart outside.

She had the fairest name of all. Sania. It’s Urdu for “splendid,” and it fit.

Like Nichelle, Sania refused stereotypes. Several you could’ve applied to her.

She was an immigrant. As were most cleaning women at the motel and at others like it in the desert. But she was young and from England. If you closed your eyes when speaking to her, you would swear that you were talking to Paul McCartney.

Sania shared the same delight that Nichelle got from her waking. Defying the bleakness of her job and her environment, she was about to erupt from whatever it was percolating inside her.

She spied on me and my strutting through the toking of her “fag,” as she called it. She gave me the kind of soaring wave I had come to anticipate.

I waved back. I felt my own delight. If only for a moment.

But I also questioned who she was and why she was there. I couldn’t say why I had.

 

 

WORK FINISHED EARLY in the afternoon.

But the first bus going back was late. It made me late for the second. The one that showed up next was later than the first. It took four and a half hours to get to the shelter. A time greater than I had logged at work.

Dinner was ending. People were lining up to check in for the night.

I switched into my highest gear. I made a sandwich. I ate it while cleaning the kitchen and taking peeks at the television in the rec room. I had flipped it on to the revival of Upstairs, Downstairs on PBS. I was identifying with the servants.

 

 

ONLY A FEW people were in line when I got to the back of it.

But getting to the front took forever. I waited for the journal entries that I hoped would be waiting too. I felt the same anxiousness that I had felt each Chanukah day as a kid, waiting for the present that was waiting for me.

I wanted to believe that Rudi had been right. That another letter from Yon would be in the office. I wanted to believe it as much for me as for her. For me more.

This got so strong that I evaded my pessimism. One as pervasive as those weeds in the motel’s back lot.

My turn in the office came.

Josh went through the letters.

None were from Yon.

I hovered over them. “You certain that there was nothing for me today?”

“What do you mean?”

“Like what came for me yesterday.”

He shuffled through the mail for a second time. “You want me to conjure one?”

I didn’t answer. I grabbed a towel. I started out.

“Laundry, tomorrow morning,” he said.

“I work tomorrow morning,” I shot back.

“Mess hall bathrooms, tomorrow night.”

I left the office, my head hanging. I took the towel to my cubby. I tossed it up.

Bumbling forward, I walked into the courtyard. I wrenched out my phone.

My head was hanging lower. It was bringing me to the ground.

Gerry stepped in front of me. In his navy-blue sweatshirt and his hazel Cali baseball cap. A stack of manila folders was beneath his arm.

His face lit up like a Christmas tree. “Remember when I told you how the CIA director is stalking me?”

“Vaguely.”

“I’ve tracked him to Green River, Utah.”

“The CIA director works out of Langley, Virginia.”

Gerry got more joyful. He pointed at me. “That’s what they want you to think.”

“If you’ll excuse me . . .”

“I hear you’re a hacker.”

“Where did you hear that?”

“I hear stuff.”

“I’m not a hacker.”

“But you know computers.”

“Sure, but—”

“—I don’t. Nada. And I need details on this guy.”

“I’m sorry, but—”

“—I’ll pay you.”

“How about on Sunday? I’m off.”

“Terrific.”

I meandered toward the corner booth.

“I almost forgot,” he called out.

Grunting, I faced both him and his bashful eyes.

He was holding out Yon’s letter. “I couldn’t help myself.”

I should’ve been angry. But so giddy was I that I thanked him for stealing my mail.

Hurrying off with it to the corner booth, I ripped open the seal. I yanked out its contents. I called Rudi.

She answered on the first ring. “Yeah?”

“You were right.”

“You sound surprised.”

“You ready?”

“Since last night.”

 

 

THE MORNING ROSE above a three-story white manor in Newstead, the most exclusive section of South Orange. Among the most in New Jersey.

Light had struck the house and the estates surrounding it. The thawing glaze glistened over the grass and hedges. Both of which the owners had trimmed despite not needing it at this time of year.

In a bedroom on the top floor, Yon exited his bathroom in a white terrycloth robe. He loitered in the doorway. Toward a window and the Manhattan skyline twenty miles past it.

The distance from the city miniaturized the skyscrapers. It made them toy-like. He could’ve grabbed them.

It was a million-dollar view, his mother would tell him and everyone she could. But it was ordinary that day. Ordinary was everywhere. Apart from a particular someone.

In his seventeen and a half years, never had a girl stirred Yon. They were accessories. Like he was to them. Darlene was the perfect example of both. His biggest concern over them had been whether they looked good on his arm.

But, in a single day, one had sent him tumbling. A tsunami had whacked him from nowhere.

She couldn’t be worth dying over.

The danger of Rudi roared back. Yon balanced his excitement of her with his fear.

Not able to line up the scales, he started forward. He reached a pair of glass cases.

The first displayed his sporting and academic triumphs over the years. Counted among this was a framed Star-Ledger article from the previous winter that had named him All-State in football. In it as well were certificates naming him to the honor roll in each quarter of his high school career.

In the second case was the comic book collection that he had amassed as a kid. With gems such as the complete set of All-Star Comics and Howard the Duck.

The latter title nabbed his eye. Its telling of a creature stuck in a world absurd to him had fascinated Yon when he was a child.

In recent months, it had done more than that. He could empathize with that duck. Like he would with another fictional character. From a different kind of book that he had been revisiting.

Yon passed the cases. He slid by the Yardbirds posters on his walls. He neared a framed reproduction of Girl with a Pearl Earring.

He wandered to the day he got it. Without grasping why he had.

 

 

YON STOOD OUTSIDE the Metropolitan Museum of Art. With his parents and others. The black-tie event was about to begin.

His father kneeled before him on the sidewalk. He fixed his crooked bow tie.

Yon whined. “I don’t get why we had to dress up.”

“We dress up for special moments. To show them how special they are.”

“What’s so special about a painting?”

The man’s face brightened. “You get one chance to experience Vermeer for the first time.”

The doors opened. The guests spilled inside. To the painting that the museum had gotten on loan.

Closer Yon got to the masterpiece. He dragged his feet.

The colors and their blends were beyond what he could imagine. They exploded at him. They flooded his eyes. He wanted to play in them.

But what hit him harder was the girl’s happiness. He would often wallow in it from his bed.

What was its source?

 

 

YON CAME BACK to his bedroom. He faced a full-length mirror in the room’s corner. A mirror like what he had avoided in his dream. Or whatever it had been.

He avoided this one too. He put his eyes on his gold chai necklace.

The pendant had been his mother’s. Yon had admired it for as far back as he could remember.

So his mother bought a new chain for it. She gave it to him on his bar mitzvah, overlooking how most boys got Star of David necklaces or mezuzahs.

Yon gripped the pendant. The Hebrew word for “living.”

Balancing for a second time his fascination and fear of Rudi, he mulled the word’s meaning.

Was it about pushing yourself from place to place? To keep yourself breathing for as much as possible?

Or was it about achieving some end?

 

 

YON CARRIED HIS backpack down a winding wooden staircase.

He was wearing his football jacket over a white wool Polo sweater. Along with the same brand of starched, pleated khakis and a pair of polished black Gucci penny loafers.

His soles and heels clanked off the gleaming wood. To the beat of Julie London’s velvety voice bouncing off the walls. She was singing “Two Sleepy People” from a record playing in the living room.

It was a flawless song, Yon remarked in his journal. This arrangement the most. But it depressed him because of who was listening to it and why.

At the stairs’ bottom, he went right. He inched toward a wall that separated the hallway from the dining room and from a woman who had turned fifty and had her dirty-blonde hair tied back.

Mrs. Levy was clad in a light-lilac Dior dress. It complemented the floral setting in the center of an oak table.

She was tapping her fingers and toes to the music. By the table’s near end. Her eyes were eluding the empty seat at its far end.

A seat that would forever be empty.

She was so lonely.

Was this how he would be when he was her age? Would he spend his days doing nothing other than revisiting times that could never return?

The song faded. With the tenderest of mercies.

Yon sprang into the room.

Jostling by a cabinet—on top of which stood a sculptured stone menorah and the remains of candle wax from the previous night’s lighting—he plopped down next to his mother. He dropped his backpack by his feet.

He and the woman exchanged good mornings.

Elizabeth swept into the room from the kitchen. In her pristine white uniform.

She served them a breakfast of omelets and hash browns and orange juice and coffee. She left the New York Times and the Star-Ledger on the table with them. Without reason to believe that anyone would touch either.

“Thank you,” Mrs. Levy said to her. In a regal tone that she was well aware she had affected.

Elizabeth nodded. She went off with her silver tray into the kitchen.

Mrs. Levy forced a sigh. She forced herself to pick up her knife and fork. She had to make further effort to eat.

But with her son not doing the same, with him picking at his food, she set down her utensils. “What’s wrong?”

Yon carried on with his picking. “Nothing.”

“You’re a bad liar. Always have been.”

He kept picking.

The woman sighed for a second time. This time louder. With less force. She tossed her arms onto the table. “It must be this diet kick you’re on.”

Now the sigh was Yon’s. “It’s not.”

“So what is it?”

Yon gave up the pretense of eating. He put down his own utensils. “I, I’ve met someone.”

The woman gaped at him. “You’ve met someone? What about Darlene?”

“What about her?”

“She’s perfect for you.”

“For me? Or for you?”

Mrs. Levy beamed. She took a sip of coffee. “Why don’t you invite her for dinner?”

It was Yon’s turn to gape. “Who?”

“This girl you’ve fallen for.”

He tossed up his hands. “I haven’t fallen for her.”

“You’re a bad liar. Always have been.”

Part of Yon wanted to laugh. But the other part snagged his backpack off the floor. He got to his feet. He took off.

“Will she be coming tonight?” his mother said.

Yon slowed. “She’s not easy to like.”

“We have that in common.”

“You have a lot in common. More than I would’ve supposed till now.”

“I’d love to meet her.”

Yon paused by the hallway entrance. “How did you know Dad was the one?”

The woman didn’t need a moment to answer. “When I didn’t have to ask.”

The dialogue ended. Yon left the room.

He hoisted a wrapped white chocolate truffle from a bowl on an end table.

Dropping it in his jacket pocket, he went back to his mother.

She was keeping herself from the empty chair. She was listening to Julie London too.

It didn’t matter that the music was playing only in her head.

 

 

YON PARKED HIS bike in the school parking lot.

A sight made him wobble. He had to persuade himself that it was real.

Rudi and Mariana were striding toward the front doors together. By the way they were talking, someone could’ve mistaken them for friends. If they neglected the absurdity of their presence in the same universe.

Excitement was shooting out of Mariana. “If nobody claims her, she’ll go up for adoption. You should adopt her.”

Rudi made a funny face. “We could get matching collars.”

They both laughed.

This was a bigger surprise to Yon than the two walking to school together. He had met Mariana in elementary school, but never in the years since had she come close to a smile.

The two girls entered the building. Yon headed to it himself.

At the doors, another sight startled him. A red Porsche 924 was stopping on Parker.

No kid at Columbia, regardless of how rich they were, went to school in a Porsche. This included in what was at the time the cheapest version of it.

Kids at Pingry might’ve gone to school in Porsches. But not kids there.

Yon tilted toward the car and the silhouette of someone inside it.

Someone who was tilting back at him.

 

 

I INTERRUPTED THE story. “This raises a question.”

Rudi didn’t speak. She was elsewhere.

“Rudi?”

“What?”

“Are you okay?”

“It’s . . . we’re coming to a not-so-wonderful part in the story.”

“What?”

“It doesn’t matter.”

“I could skip stuff.”

“I want you to tell me it all. I want you to tell me it, however painful or intimate it might be.”

I was averse. More than so. But instead of starting an argument, I changed the subject. “How did you and Mariana become friends? It never made sense.”

“It doesn’t now. That’s the beauty of it. Or one. There are plenty.”

“How did it happen?”

“That morning on the bus, I was in a netherworld. Nothing existed but a pair of eyes. I was also hearing this crazy song that I’d heard in a dream the night before.

“The bus jolted at the stop. Through the window, Mariana was waiting. I guessed so she could learn about the dog. She was so nervous and I was in such a good mood that I had to have fun with her.

“‘You must want those dentures,’ I said with my fists out when I jumped off the bus.

“She just about fainted. So I let up. I told her that the dog was fine. And we talked. And what made us different, and this went on and on, wasn’t important. What was important was that we both needed a friend. Me especially.”

 

 

RUDI TURNED MORE heads at lunch.

She paraded through the cafeteria’s center aisle with her tray stacked with food. In a worn black Springsteen T-shirt that had “sucks” scrawled over it in white paint.

The interest people were showing Rudi that afternoon was different from the day before. The story of what she had done for the dog must’ve spread. No one was in fear of her. Or shocked by her. What they were expressing was nearer to warmth.

Rudi ignored them.

Until she came upon someone she couldn’t ignore.

Yon’s eyes were on her. Darlene’s arm was around his.

Rudi stormed off, I’m sure pretending that she wasn’t storming.

 

 

DARLENE HUMPHED AT the source of Yon’s captivation.

She clenched his arm. Like she wanted to split it apart. “I thought Ms. Krasner was gonna kill her in class this morning. For twenty minutes, she argued with her over some arcane Ferlinghetti poem. Who does that?”

The answer was clear to Yon. As clear as the makeup on Rudi’s face.

Someone who cares does that.

 

 

RUDI SLOWED.

She closed in on the table where Owen Connors was roosting by himself. Apart from his glare and the remains of his lunch.

Owen was the biggest and meanest kid in school. Though they had held him back so many times that he wasn’t much of a kid. He had a receding hairline.

Everybody in school feared Owen. The teachers and the football players feared him. They gave him a berth so expansive that it could’ve filled the sky.

Yon had been afraid of Owen from the first day of South Orange Junior High. He’d stayed multiple distances from him.

But Rudi glared back. She could’ve plowed through him.

She plodded past him. Toward the back table.

Mariana was at one end. She was as excited as she had been that morning. Eliot was at the table’s other end. He was shivering on glimpse of Rudi. He was dying to make himself invisible.

Owen’s glaring at Rudi kept up as she sat across from Mariana.

Yon sensed what he had never sensed before.

He was frightened for someone.

 

 

YON’S FRIGHT HADN’T let up at lunch’s winding down. It had grown.

He followed Rudi from the cafeteria. He couldn’t make himself not do this.

She left at the same time as Jared, a skinny boy with a close-cropped afro, who had transferred to the school a year earlier.

Jared fit in less than Rudi had. But for a different reason.

His family had relocated from rural Georgia. He spoke with what people believed was a funny accent.

But what amused them greater was how he would dress as if he were going to church. He’d wear long-sleeved dress shirts and dress pants and a tie. He’d wear this in the brutal heat.

He’d clutch a dog-eared Bible too. He’d clutch it in gym class, where he did nothing but sit and read.

To anyone at school who gave him consideration, Jared was a caricature. They could dismiss him with ease.

Rudi and Jared ambled down a corridor. They happened upon each other.

Yon was expecting that Rudi would treat Jared as she had with Owen.

But she surprised Yon some more.

Disregarding how she had less in common with Jared than she had with Mariana, Rudi did what nobody in school had bothered to do. She dug beneath the caricature.

What was there jumped out at her. It jumped out at them both. It rippled from their facades.

They rambled off. In different directions.

 

 

YON FLOATED FROM the first-floor stairwell. Into the corridor. His head was spinning from that afternoon’s math class.

On the hallway’s other side was a crowd. They were milling around a toothy Owen and the physics book that he held over someone’s head.

Yon had a good idea of who this someone was. He sprinted down the hall. He didn’t ask himself what he was doing. Or why.

He got to the throng. He pushed through it.

Rudi was opposite Owen in her overcoat. A chemistry book was under her arm. Her face was stages past apathy. She was fighting the urge to fall into a coma.

Yet Yon raged. He leapt toward Owen.

Only to freeze.

It wasn’t fear of Owen that had frozen him. He had an overwhelming desire to go after him in spite of his fear.

But getting beaten up was the least that could happen. It could’ve put him on a path that he couldn’t have gotten off.

Rudi exhaled. “I’ll give you three seconds. One . . . two . . .”

Owen got toothier. “Three.”

Yawning, Rudi dropped her seabag onto the floor. With the same indifference, she took her chemistry book in both hands. She swung it across Owen’s jaw.

She knocked him headfirst onto the lockers. To the floor.

In a state of semiconsciousness he lay, the physics book dangling from his paw.

Quiet was clamoring through the hall. Yon could hear his own heart beat.

Rudi cocked her head at Eliot. He was next to her with his mouth and eyes expanded past their boundaries.

She raised her brows at him. “I suggest you pick up your book. Like, right now.”

Eliot sped to Owen. He whisked the book from him. “Thanks!”

Rudi thundered at Eliot’s cheer. She did so with a mix of a sneer and a snarl. She thrust her finger at him. “This doesn’t mean that we’re friends or nothing.”

Eliot continued in his delight. His delight of her. He continued it in defiance of her while scurrying down the corridor with his book.

Rudi’s sneering and snarling rose. It stayed this way as she fell to one knee.

She grabbed her bag.

Dozens of eyes were on her.

She glowered at them. “What’s the matter with you dolts? Have you never witnessed an ass-kicking? Go on, get outta here!”

Quicker than lightning they fled. All but Yon. He stood steps from her, stunned at how someone such as her could care enough about someone such as Eliot to stand up for him.

A bigger puzzlement came with it.

What further could she care about? And who?

Rudi stood with her bag. Her glower returned. It shone on Yon like a spotlight. “What do you want?”

He sought an answer. “I . . . I was hoping, I was hoping that you could help me with calculus. Conic sections are killing me. But those ellipses inside those rhombuses are nothing compared to partial fractions.”

Rudi glowed. Glee was gurgling from her. “So it’s true what they say . . . boys are bad at math.”

Yon couldn’t respond. Worse, he hadn’t a doubt that he was blushing.

She took off.

He couldn’t fight the yearning to take a chance. It didn’t matter where it would lead him. Or if it meant making a fool of himself in front of someone who might not give a damn. “Please, I’ve never gotten lower than a B+, and I haven’t a hint how I’ll pass the AP exam.”

Rudi paused, paces from him. Her head swung back. “Why is it so important to you?”

“Don’t you fear failure?”

She didn’t answer. The two searched for words.

They were nowhere in sight.

“Weiss!” howled a voice from down the hall.

Rudi squared off with Mr. Gonzales. The man was in another tight brown suit and another tighter brown tie.

He pointed at his door. “My office, now!”

“Oy vey,” she said beneath her breath.

She lurched at the man, who was battling to keep the snicker inside him from the justice Rudi had meted out. “Somebody check if Mr. Connors is alive.”

Yon took a step toward Rudi. “Well? Will you help me?”

She stopped. Flinging her bag over her shoulder, she fingered the frayed rope holding it together. “I’m kinda busy.”

“He’s alive!” a boy called out.

Rudi marched toward the principal.

Yon took another step. “Later?”

She made another pause. Another of her sighs came out with it. “Where?”

“How about Reservoir? It’s a pizza place on—”

“—I’ll find it.”

Rudi restarted her march to the principal and his office. She swung her arms like a gunslinger.

Yon couldn’t budge from her.

Joey disrupted his daze. He seized him by the shoulder from behind. “Coach wants to talk. To the team.”

Yon shook him off. “Not today.”

“What do you mean?”

Joey wasn’t the only one surprised by Yon’s answer. He surprised himself. For he was the type of guy who obeyed authority.

This didn’t prevent him from going through the school doors.

Outside them, he halted before the same red Porsche that had been there that morning. A man in a leather jacket was leaning against the hood with his arms crossed.

He was in his twenties and was lean and wiry. With a shaved head and a neck covered in tattoos. But what stood out more were his cold, blank eyes. They didn’t blink.

Rudi was benign in comparison. He had but a passing resemblance to a human.

 

 

IN A PIZZERIA on South Orange Avenue, Yon sulked.

With the sun setting behind him in his window, he was facing the likelihood that Rudi had stood him up.

Never had this happened to him. But this would’ve been the sole incidence of it that would’ve mattered.

But should it have mattered? Shouldn’t he have been thankful? Shouldn’t he have taken it as a sign to run as fast as he could and save himself?

He went back and forth over this.

Clicking his tongue, he reached for his jacket.

Rudi strutted inside the doors.

Yon spun around. He caught the notice of a waiter.

The man rushed through the kitchen doors.

Rudi got to Yon’s table. Her reticence was sky-high.

Yon wasn’t certain what to say to her. He blurted out the first sentence that popped into his head. “Did you get into trouble?”

“I got detention. It won’t be the last time.”

The two ran out of words. Silence hung over them.

Rudi spotted the chair across from Yon. She whisked it out. She flipped it around.

She plopped down on it, letting her bag collapse onto the floor. But she didn’t take off her coat. “This is not a date.”

Yon convinced himself of this. “Of course not.”

Disappointment crossed her face.

Was it on his?

Also on her face was vexation. “What would your girlfriend say about this?”

“Girlfriend?”

Rudi threw her hands at Yon. “The strawberry blonde who drapes over you. Ms. Cantor.”

“She’s not my girlfriend. She might suppose so, and my mom definitely supposes so. But . . .”

“But what?”

“I’m not sure why I’m telling you this, but would you believe that I’ve never had a girlfriend?”

“No.”

“Don’t get me wrong. There have been girls. At times, too many. It’s the ‘friend’ part that’s elusive.”

“You’re nitpicking over semantics. Over what makes up a girlfriend. And what does, in your opinion?”

The answer was clear to Yon. As clear as the makeup on Rudi’s face. He wanted someone who would make him feel as Cummings had felt when he crafted “since feeling is first.” He wanted this so much.

But he didn’t want to admit it. Not to her. Not to himself.

He demurred.

Rudi pondered him. Her guardedness showed through it.

He tipped forward. “Where you from?”

“Lots of places.”

“Such as?”

“Such as Trenton or thereabouts.”

“What are you doing here?”

Rudi stretched her arms. She plunged her elbows onto the table. Dropping her chin onto her fists, she shut her eyes. “I can’t imagine what I’m doing here. I can’t stay awake in this town.”

Yon dipped his hand into his backpack. “I can imagine what you’re doing here now. Helping me with calculus.”

Rudi opened her eyes. She jumped back in her seat. “You were serious?”

“Why would I ask you here?”

She didn’t answer.

He yanked out a heavy white textbook. Two other books fell onto the floor.

She huffed at one. “You’re reading The Catcher in the Rye? I read that in tenth grade.”

He returned the two books to his backpack. “So did I. But I only read it enough to write an essay. This time I’m reading it for real.”

“Why?”

“I gather you’re not a fan.”

“Why should I care about some spoiled loser from the forties who’s crying about how he can’t get laid? What does that have to do with me and my life?”

Yon was wary of exposing too much of himself. But he couldn’t fight the yearning to take another chance. “That’s not what it’s about. It’s about being alone. The only person in the world. The only person who feels like you. And being so scared because of this that you can’t say whether you want to live. Holden Caulfield, he isn’t some spoiled loser crying about how he can’t get laid. He’s you.”

Rudi crossed her arms. She crumpled her face. “Speak for yourself.”

“I am.”

Fear rained down over Rudi. It stung her eyes.

Yon couldn’t figure out why.

Could it have been that she could no longer make herself believe that she was “so not him”? That she would have no further excuses for not caring about him?

The waiter came by.

Smirking, he slapped Yon’s shoulder with one hand. With his other, he placed a thin-crusted pie on the table. “Here you go, bud. You need drinks?”

“How about some Cokes?”

“You got it. Just give me a few.”

“Yoni!” shouted an accented voice from the restaurant’s other side.

Both Yon and Rudi careened toward Mr. Agnellino, a wrinkled-faced man in a suit that hadn’t fit him in decades. He was overseeing his dominion from the back wall.

Yon waved at him.

Mr. Agnellino waved as well. While doing a double-take of Rudi. Twice he did.

Rudi went back to Yon. “Everyone knows you here.”

“My family’s been coming to this place since the thirties. When it was in Newark. By the old reservoir there. That’s where my dad grew up. Do you know that he only bought a house in South Orange because they moved here?”

Yon lobbed his head. At the mushrooms and red peppers on the pizza. “I hope you don’t mind, I’m a . . .”

A truck dropped onto Rudi. “Vegetarian?”

“You too?”

“I’ve never heard of a football player vegetarian.”

“I’ve never heard of a punk rock girl vegetarian.”

Rudi opened her mouth.

But with nothing leaving it, she shook off her coat. She pulled out a slice. She blew on it.

He pointed at her pie. “It’s the best pizza in the world. Nothing comes close to it.”

She shot him a dirty look. “Nothing is no different from any other nothing.”

“The ingredients, they aren’t different from what you get at other restaurants. It’s the love that they put into it. The pizza has meaning to them. That makes the difference.”

Rudi took a bite. She strained to mask how good it tasted. At how this was exploding in her mouth. It came over her through more and more bites.

Yon had never observed anyone eat. Never had he had a reason to.

He didn’t have one now. But he couldn’t contain his awe at how she made eating unordinary. She could make any ordinary not.

A silliness overcame him.

“What?” she yelped.

 

 

THE PIZZA AND Cokes were finished.

Rudi did a karate chop into the open calculus book. “So you see? Four variations of problems exist. You only have to work out which it is and follow the pattern for solving it. You don’t have to get how you’re getting it.”

Yon fell backward in his seat. Rudi had drawn a curtain that he hadn’t been aware was there. “Wow.”

Rudi bent toward the clock on the wall. “I’ve gotta run.”

Yon’s mind stayed on the book. “Yeah.”

She jumped up. She tossed on her coat.

He did the same with his jacket. He stuffed his textbook into his backpack.

Leaving some money on the table, he staggered toward the door with Rudi. She was carrying her seabag by its rope.

He aspired for words. He clutched the first set that he could. “It’s amazing how easy you make calculus.”

“It is easy. When you stop fearing it.”

The two reached the entrance. Yon reached for Rudi’s hand.

But he stopped himself. He opened the door for her.

She didn’t appreciate the gesture. She shot him another dirty look. She growled what would become a recurring theme through her life, “I can open my own doors.”

Yon held the door open.

Rudi pushed herself outside. Her disquiet rose with it.

She slung her seabag over her shoulder. She slung it with such force that the tattered rope broke.

The bag fell to the ground. Its contents spewed out.

Making matters crueler was a breeze. Enough to blow her belongings around.

She flung up her arms. “Oy vey!”

Kneeling on the sidewalk, she stuffed her stuff into the bag.

A balled page rolled to Yon’s foot.

He fetched it. He uncrumpled it. He exposed her drawing of him.

Never had his image rendered so. She’d captured what no photograph or mirror could. The bright-eyed boy climbing out from the trappings. To discover what was there.

More remarkable than the drawing itself was how she could see in him what he couldn’t. It put him in a fog.

Rudi got him out of this. She yanked the paper from him. “That’s mine!”

She sprinted off. With the bag in her arm.

He stepped toward her. “Thank you.”

She wheeled to him. She quaked. “Thank me for what?”

He flicked his finger at the drawing.

She fumbled about. She whipped the paper behind her back, her face turning shades of scarlet and vermilion. They were burning through her powder-white makeup. “It’s not you! It’s Lee Ving!”

“I couldn’t guess who that is.”

“You couldn’t. They don’t play his music on American Bandstand. Or America’s Top 10. Or whatever garbage you watch.”

Yon laughed.

Rudi became madder. “What’s-so-funny?”

“That punk girls blush.”

Floored, Rudi took a leap back. Followed by another. She awaited a response that wasn’t coming.

He took another step toward her. “Or that they could make me blush. I had no idea that they read poetry and help bullied kids and stray dogs and . . . jaded football players.”

Rudi hid from this. She hid from both him and herself. She raced down the block.

He cupped his hand around his mouth. “How about lunch with me tomorrow in the cafeteria?”

She stopped. But she didn’t turn around. I’m sure she didn’t dare. “You’d want to be with me in front of your friends?”

“I kinda do,” he said. But what he had wanted to say was, “I really do. I want this more than I want to breathe.” He’d wanted to holler this. But the only hollering he did was across a page of his journal.

“You shouldn’t.”

Rudi’s voice echoed into the night. She traipsed after it.

“Who are you?” he whispered. At a volume only he could hear and only a sliver.

 

 

THIS TIME, RUDI interrupted the story.

She told me that “who she was” wasn’t as important as “where.” She had gotten lost.

“Why was that?” I asked. “Why didn’t you walk back the way you came?”

“Let’s just say that my mind was elsewhere.”

“And that was?”

She grunted. “It was figuring out Yon’s angle. Everybody had one. That he didn’t was driving me crazy.”

Rudi paused.

I leaned forward. “And?”

“And, for the second night in a row, I was telling myself that this was no fairy tale. In spite of all that was telling me it was.”

 

 

SO LOST WAS Rudi that it took her twice the time to get back to the school than it had taken her from it.

At the bus stop on Valley by it, she knotted the rope of her bag.

A noise came from behind her. In the school parking lot.

Eliot was getting into a battered, beige Cutlass. One held together by prayer alone.

Rudi crossed her arms. “What are you doing here?”

He did his best imitation of a Cheshire. “Chess Club.”

“What a shock.”

“You do realize that no more buses are running tonight?”

“What?”

“They stopped a while ago.”

“That’s wonderful. Tripleplusgood.”

Rudi motored down Valley. She was flapping her arms.

“Is that from Dr. Strangelove?” Eliot yelled.

“Close.”

“I could give you a ride.”

Rudi slowed. But she shook her head. “It’s okay. I live in a bad neighborhood. You wouldn’t want to go there.”

“Where’s that?”

“Near Irvington.”

“I live in Irvington.”

 

 

ELIOT MADE A left.

The car lumbered that way. Somehow in a single piece.

He spied Rudi. “My dad used the address of a buddy of his at work so I could register at Columbia. He must be under the impression that I could be someone.”

“What’s so crazy about that?”

“Take a gander at me.”

“I am taking one.”

Rudi took one at another object. She swatted her arm down the road. “Behind that blue sedan.”

He parked.

She opened her door. “Thanks.”

“Sure. Anytime.”

Rudi jumped from the car. She halted. For a reason she couldn’t fathom.

She exhaled. “Tomorrow.”

He inclined toward her. He was falling over. “Tomorrow?”

“At lunch.”

Eliot’s delirium surged out. “Yeah?”

“Just don’t push it.”

 

 

YON ENTERED SCHOOL in the morning. He paused in the corridor.

Rudi was outside the girls’ bathroom. Her hands were on her hips. “You have to come out sometime.”

“No, I don’t,” squeaked Mariana’s voice from behind the door. “This isn’t a good idea.”

“The best ones aren’t.”

Mariana stayed quiet.

Rudi pleaded. “Come on, you look amazing.”

The door snuck open. Mariana slithered out. Or someone who resembled her.

This Mariana was wearing makeup. It was but a touch. On any other girl, you wouldn’t have picked up on it. But on her, you couldn’t help it.

“What are you doing with that makeup?” the principal barked from his door.

Both girls rocketed themselves toward him. Mariana dropped her eyes. As far as they would go. “Nothing, Daddy.”

Rudi fell rearward. “Daddy?”

The principal stomped toward Rudi. Yon was certain that he would strangle her. His claws were an instant from her throat. “Is there anything in this school you haven’t corrupted?”

“You.”

 

 

RUDI PARADED THROUGH the cafeteria’s center aisle with her pile of food.

This afternoon, nobody cared.

Yon, well in back of her and straying toward his usual table with his own lunch, sensed her curiosity. Her head rotated left and right.

She discovered the reason behind the lack of interest in her when she got to the table where Owen was roosting.

His head was on his arms. A barrage of paper balls was hitting him from each point.

Rudi lingered nearby. She couldn’t pull herself away.

Yon couldn’t pull himself from her and her growing conflict. He couldn’t guess its source.

She exhaled. “That’s enough.”

The assault went on.

Rudi repeated her command. Louder and firmer.

The paper balls ceased.

Rudi exhaled for a second time. She sat across from Owen. She talked to him.

Yon snowballed past curious. He sat at a table across the aisle from them.

Rudi edged toward Owen. “You need better karma. I’ve been hunting for it myself. The truth is that I need it more than you. A lot more.”

Owen didn’t speak. Or react.

She wheezed. “Come on, lift that chin.”

“So you can punch it?”

“Better karma. I need better karma.”

Owen raised his head. He did so with less enthusiasm than a dog gets into a tub of water. But he got it over the table.

The two regarded each other. They projected a reserve that was uncommon to either.

This ended.

Eliot brought his lunch next to Rudi. His hair was much like hers. Black and spiked.

Rudi held down her laugh. She shook her head. She unspiked his hair with her fingers. “This is the definition of ‘pushing it.’ You’ve become a Quincy Punk.”

Eliot tilted his head. “What’s a Quincy Punk?”

Owen answered for Rudi. “A fake punk. Like on that stupid TV show.”

Rudi spun to Owen. “You’re into punk?”

Owen rolled his eyes. He removed his long-sleeve flannel shirt. Beneath it was a white T-shirt and the Black Flag logo tattooed on his arm.

Eliot dove at it. His eyes went ablaze. He got so excited that he fell off his chair. “I want one of those.”

Owen winked at Rudi. “We’re creating a delinquent.”

The three were creating a scene.

Mariana was pricking up her ears at what they were saying as she carried her lunch to them.

She was more different from before. In addition to that makeup, she had confidence.

Not only Yon noted it. So did a lanky redheaded girl who moseyed by.

Mariana swam in the attention. All the way to the seat across from Eliot. “I’d get a tattoo too. But I’m already grounded till next century. Maybe the one after it too.”

 

 

THE FOUR BANTERED. Nonstop they did.

They weren’t the coolest kids that afternoon. Or the best-looking. Or the most athletic. But their presence drew the entire lunchroom. They were the midpoint of their world.

Included in this was the principal. As he marched into the cafeteria.

He wasn’t pleased. He charged toward the four.

That’s when magic happened.

The four laughed. They laughed the way friends do when they’ve been friends forever and not because of some crazy-looking girl from Trenton.

The principal spiraled to a stop. He came within a split neutron of crashing to the floor.

He was beholding a miracle. A miracle that he had helped create. He forgot about his anger. All that endured was his daughter’s laugh.

The laughter died down.

Rudi got wind of Jared.

He was a few tables over. He had nothing but his Bible to keep him company.

His loneliness reminded Yon of his mother’s that morning.

Jared couldn’t avert his eyes from the four. No matter how he feigned otherwise.

Rudi was ignoring him. But she had no more success at this than she had with Owen.

She waved him toward their table.

Jared pointed to himself. With a meekness that was amplifying itself. “Me?”

“Yes, you!” she roared. Without the slightest meekness.

He rose with unease. He stammered toward them with his lunch. His unease was doubling with each step that he took. It was taking him forever to get there.

Yon made out that Rudi wasn’t going to let anyone eat alone. He bounded for their table with his tray.

Rudi turned his way. The entire table did.

Flustered, Yon scurried off.

“What’s going on with you and Yon Levy?” Mariana yapped.

Rudi took her time in answering. “Would you believe partial fraction decomposition?”

“No.”

Happy breezed over Yon. A gust of it propelled him onward.

 

 

YON’S HAPPY HADN’T tapered at the day’s end.

He had aced a partial fractions quiz. He expected more outrageousness from Rudi. That she would awe him more.

But upon his floating from the stairwell on the first floor, no Rudi was around. Or any chaos or ruckus. An eerie normalcy hung over the hallway.

He wandered through it. He wandered through the building’s front doors.

The normalcy ceased outside. Rudi and the wiry man with the shaved head and tattooed neck were arguing by his Porsche.

Rudi was quelling her fright. She was flailing at it. “I’m not going nowhere with you!”

The man dallied in front of her. But he didn’t keep this up.

He slapped Rudi. He slapped her across the face with the back of his hand. He laid bare a cold-bloodedness that made clear that this was the least he could do.

The blow knocked Rudi off her feet. She teetered.

Yon leapt toward them.

But like with Owen the day earlier, he froze. This time from the handgun in the man’s waist.

It wasn’t the gun itself that froze him. It was the future that it played out in his mind. Of that peaked and skeletal and doomed figure.

The man grabbed Rudi by the ear. He dragged her to his car. “We need to have a talk, young lady.”

She begged him. “Deke!”

Deke threw open the passenger door. He pushed Rudi inside the car. He slammed the door.

Coming around the vehicle’s other side, he scowled at Yon as he jumped into the driver’s seat.

He sped off. From the parking lot and down Parker.

Yon wasn’t positive what to do. Most of him wanted to stay in control of his fate and the replay of his life.

Well after Deke’s car was gone, he was standing there.

Nothing. Do nothing. This could be your last chance to do it.

But the better part of him woke. It got him to his bike. It brought him down Parker. To the end of it.

The Porsche wasn’t anywhere.

Yon went forward. By veering right onto Clinton.

He drove to the intersection of Sanford.

Blocks away was Springfield Avenue. Among the main thoroughfares in the area.

Deke’s car wasn’t about.

Yon couldn’t decide whether this disappointed or relieved him. But he steered onto Sanford.

At the corner of Springfield, he squinted down the busy street.

The Porsche wasn’t to his left. But to his right it was. In the distance.

He raced toward it. Without checking the road. He raced in the path of an oncoming car.

Swinging his body from it, he missed the car. But he headed for a parked one.

He swerved around this. He jumped the sidewalk.

Evading a pair of pedestrians, he hopped the bike back onto the street. He followed the Porsche.

He followed it through roads much the same. He got to a rundown section of Maplewood that he had never been.

This was a fifteen-minute drive from the neighborhood where he had lived his entire life. But it was so different that it might as well have been on the light side of the sun.

The Porsche parked by a crumbling single-story home. Yon hid behind an old burgundy pickup truck a block and a half back.

He stayed hidden. He lost track of time.

Rudi blitzed from the car, her face blanched. Her whole body was shaking.

She shut the door. She ran into the house.

The Porsche took off. It left a parked blue sedan in Yon’s view.

Now what?

He wasn’t sure.

But he got off his bike. He staggered toward the house. He moved as if he were pushing himself through a hurricane. With each step this intensified. He was moving slower than Jared had in the cafeteria.

He reached the door.

It had no bell. So he knocked. A couple of times he did.

This achieved nothing.

Banging on the door achieved the same.

“Rudi?” he yelled.

Stillness came back.

Yon returned to the street. He swayed toward his bike. But he couldn’t say whether this was where he was swaying.

He passed the corner of Rudi’s house. A yard was in back of it.

Not able to do otherwise, he tottered toward it.

 

 

WHAT I HAD read confused me.

I asked Rudi how she could not hear Yon banging on her door. Yelling her name.

“Because I wasn’t there. I was so scared when I ran into that house. The walls were closing in. They were collapsing onto me. I was panicking for a way out.

“I ransacked the place for one. I ripped it apart. But I couldn’t turn up so much as a bottle of aspirin. Or a warm can of beer. So I slunk under the bed. Like I had done when I was a kid. I cranked Richard Hell on my Walkman. I cranked it so that I wouldn’t’ve noticed an apocalypse.”

 

 

“RUDI?” YON CALLED out.

He was creeping alongside her house’s cracked side wall. It might’ve fallen apart if touched.

No answer came.

But sounds were coming from inside the house.

He called her name out for a second time. Louder.

Another sound hurled his way. A clicking sound. From behind him.

He whipped himself toward it. He faced the man who had ushered Rudi to school on her first day. The man with the crew cut and pockmarked face, who was sporting another off-the-rack blue suit and was pointing a gun at Yon’s head.

Yon raised his hands. As high as they would go. In such a state was he that his life didn’t flash before him. Only death had. It was a pull of a trigger away.

The man extended his gun closer to Yon’s head. “You with them?”

Yon willed himself. Not to shake.

Failing at this, he squeezed out an answer. “With . . . with what?”

The man examined Yon. From his shoes to his fingertips.

He hauled out a badge from his jacket. He flashed it at Yon. “FBI. Take off while you can.”

Yon hurried away. He hurried faster when he got to the street. He hurried faster than that after he dove onto his Harley.

He flew down the road. He wanted to fly home. He told himself that this was where he was flying.

But he slowed by the Maplewood Public Library on Baker Street. He rolled onto its lot and parked. He put his hand across his mouth.

Whatever trouble Rudi was in, whatever reason she had for coming into his life, it had to have been major.

Why would the FBI be involved?

His other certainty was that he could only get an answer in the building next to him.

He got off the bike. He scooted up the ramp and into the library and to the reference section. In the days before the web, the lone means of learning about a recent event was the Reader’s Guide to Periodical Literature. It indexed articles from hundreds of magazines and journals.

A librarian directed Yon to an aisle with copious volumes of these going back decades.

In the aisle’s back were lesser versions of more recent editions. He took down the last few months. He lugged them to a table.

He skimmed through them.

Page after page he searched. Book after book. But not clear what he was searching for, he was running in place.

With daylight departing in the windows around him, he was ready to give up.

Such as Trenton or thereabouts.

Yon remembered what Rudi had told him at dinner. He flipped to the back of his current volume. To the T’s.

No articles referenced the city. So he went through his other books.

In one from November, he came upon an entry for a Newsweek story. “Punk Rock Turns Deadly.”

Yon sprinted off, his arms thrashing about. He ran to the magazine stacks near the library’s entrance.

He snagged the issue from a pile. He sifted through its pages.

Passing an article about the mysterious illness that had been afflicting the gay community, he landed upon what he had been seeking.

Below the headline was a mugshot of Deke.

According to the story, someone had beaten to death a nineteen-year-old Jewish student from Rutgers named Mark Shapiro. At a punk show outside Trenton.

Deke Cox, a well-known drug dealer in multiple states—and an ex-con with ties to the Aryan Brotherhood—was the leading suspect of what the police and the FBI had concluded was a crime motivated by prejudice. But they hadn’t charged him, and he wasn’t the only suspect. They believed that an unnamed teenage girl with a similar background was involved in the killing as well.

The “unnamed teenage girl” could be but one person.

Yon lowered his head. The magazine with it. “She is a monster.”

 

 

VOID SEEPED FROM the desert.

It thickened into a bleakness that was engulfing the courtyard. It was suffocating me.

My breath pierced it.

I brought the last page behind the others. “That, that’s it.”

Rudi didn’t speak.

I asked her if she was okay.

She didn’t speak to this either.

“I said it before, I can skip—”

“—I want to hear it all,” she barked.

“But you shouldn’t. It was bad enough the first time.”

“All of it,” she screamed. With a fury that hadn’t changed in thirty-five years.

“No.”

“What?”

“I said no.”

“You can’t . . .”

“I can’t what?”

“You can’t do this to me,” she whimpered, her voice splintering at several points. “He, he was my bashert. My destiny. And . . .”

“And what?”

She struggled to answer. “I miss him. I miss him when I wake. I miss him when I sleep. I miss him in the moments between. I miss his hand on mine. I miss the way he would anticipate my moods. I miss how he made me believe. You’ve gifted him back to me, and this isn’t a gift you can take back.”

“That’s exactly what I’m gonna do.” I was going to do it as much for me as for her. Neither of us needed to go where this story was taking us.

“You’re not the only one who can disappear,” she shrieked. “I can disappear a whole lot better than you. And for longer!”

“What?”

She didn’t answer.

“What does that mean?” I gasped. I had forever accepted that Rudi was stronger than me. But what if she weren’t?

“You won’t get away with skipping a comma,” her voice rasped. “You won’t.”

Now it was my turn not to speak.

“Call me when you get the letter,” she babbled.

She hung up.

I couldn’t move. I could do nothing but curse the pages in my grasp. I prayed them away. Along with myself. I only stopped when the shelter’s gate and its clanging roused me.

Kitty slithered inside.

A woman in her twenties, she was so wispy that a stiff wind could’ve blown her away.

Like some other women in the shelter, Kitty worked at an Amazon fulfillment center down the hill in San Bernardino. They worked ridiculous hours. Getting home late at night.

She neared me.

I tipped my head at her. “Hey.”

She answered in kind, her eyes widened. She was a doe in the road waiting for headlights.

“You okay?”

She didn’t answer. She sped into the dorm.

I pretended to ignore this. I folded Yon’s pages.
