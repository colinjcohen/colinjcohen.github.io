chapter fifty-six: following the suspects

 

 

HERMANN WOBBLED FROM the elevator, his eyes agape. Klaus was in the same state.

The two were facing a narrow tunnel that had a low ceiling. Lit by carbide lamps hanging along its length.

Shocking them wasn’t the tunnel itself. But what was on its sides: a combination of old storefronts and the remnants of buildings.

Klaus raised his arms. “What is this?”

Hermann noted the use of Yiddish everywhere. “I can only guess. Around fifty years ago, the city reconstructed Josefov. They knocked down whole streets. As well as many shops and other buildings. Including a few synagogues. They also knocked down the bordellos that my father gleefully told me about. But the community must’ve made use of the carnage. It’s likely holding up this tunnel. Which must connect this building with others in the district.”

Hermann’s explanation frightened Klaus. He was as frightened as when Hermann woke him that morning.

“What’s wrong?” Hermann asked.

“What reason would they have for a tunnel?”

“That I can’t answer. Why they built it or when, I can’t say. But they could’ve been using this as a means of worship after you closed the synagogues. Like how early Christians used the Roman catacombs. You’d be surprised at the lengths people worship in Theresienstadt. I was.”

Images passed through Hermann’s mind. Of what he had witnessed on Friday evenings at the camp. People would gather each Sabbath. In defiance of both the rules and the misery that they were suffering. Some would gather in an old garage. Others would in a storage space and in the hospital attic. They created makeshift synagogues from each.

Along with the images, Hermann reexperienced the envy that he had for those who could have reverence and devotion at such moments. When he couldn’t have either in the best ones.

Hermann remembered the task in hand. He started down the tunnel’s left side. He did so without making a sound.

“Where are you going?” Klaus whispered.

Hermann pointed to the wheel marks and footprints on the ground. “I’m following our suspects.”

Klaus followed as well. While eyeing the storefronts like they were familiar.

“What’s wrong?” Hermann asked for a second time.

Klaus avoided the question for a second time. By asking another of his own. “Where could this tunnel lead?”

“We’re approaching the High Synagogue.”

This was a block away.

It only took them minutes to reach the tunnel’s end. Which led to another tunnel perpendicular to it. It also led to a closed elevator.

The two stopped beside it. They checked around.

The wheel marks and footprints were heading down the second tunnel to their right. Another set of footprints had joined them. They had begun at the elevator. They belonged to a woman.

“Our suspects have company,” Hermann said.

He and Klaus followed the footprints, staying as quiet as possible. “We’re moving in the direction of the Pinkas Synagogue.”

“Whose footprints could those be?” Klaus asked. In a way that suggested he knew the answer.

“Whose do you think they are?” Hermann asked back. In a way that suggested he also had a strong idea as to the answer.

“If I could think it, I wouldn’t be asking it.”

“We’ll learn soon enough.”

The men went on. This tunnel was longer than the first. But they came upon another intersection and another tunnel.

That wasn’t the bounds of what they came upon.

Voices resonated. Belonging to two men and a woman.

They were faint. But they got louder with the two getting closer. Making them move slower and quieter.

Hermann and Klaus reached the intersection’s edge. With caution they did. The voices were now not only louder. They were clearer. Hermann recognized two before he crept around the corner.

So he wasn’t surprised by Gertrud’s presence. Or by Tonda’s or Luděk’s. But what did surprise him was how the two men were carrying Torahs toward Gertrud, who was standing over a black wooden crate inside the cart that the two men had rolled into the synagogue.

Klaus expressed greater surprise than Hermann. He gasped Gertrud’s name.

“Sshhh,” Hermann whispered.

Tonda and Luděk got to Gertrud.

She surprised Hermann some more. She kissed two pairs of her fingers. She laid them onto the Torahs that the men were placing in the crate.

“What are those?” Klaus’s voice rasped.

“Those are the prayer scrolls that I was telling you about in the Spanish Synagogue. That was what was missing from the ark there. And from the other arks in the other synagogues we’ve been to. Now we know why they were missing.”

Tonda and Luděk went to a table. To the Torahs on top of it.

With care, they each picked one up. They carried them to Gertrud.

Klaus stretched his finger toward the table. “There are more than six.”

Hermann lowered the man’s arm. “Like I told you, the city destroyed some synagogues in the district. Other synagogues are also in Prague and elsewhere in the country. I wouldn’t be surprised if that were the country’s entire collection of Torahs. I bet some are a thousand years old.”

“Are they valuable?”

“They may have nominal value. But their real value is to us.”

Tonda and Luděk brought Gertrud the Torahs. She kissed them.

Klaus tipped forward. “Why is she kissing them?”

Hermann pivoted toward Klaus. “We Jews kiss the Torah. Out of love for it.”

“But why is she kissing them?”

“Apparently, she’s Jewish.”

Klaus shook his head. Several times he did. “She can’t be.”

“Why can’t she be?”

Klaus wanted to answer this. He opened his mouth to express it. But nothing exited. He had to divine that what he had believed about Gertrud had been a lie and that this was the least.

The two men continued packing the crate. They transferred the remaining Torahs into it.

Lowering their arms inside the cart, they strained to lift a heavy object. A black wooden board that had an SS insignia painted on it.

They kept struggling with the board.

But they got it over their waists. They let it down on the crate, aligned with it.

They weren’t done. They picked up some hammers and nails from the cart. They nailed the board onto the crate.

“Unbelievable,” Klaus said.

“What is?” Hermann asked.

“They must have gotten that crate from the warehouse. It’s effectively a diplomatic pouch. Nobody would search it. Not the army. Not the border police. Not the SS.”

Glee overcame Hermann. The mystery had become clearer to him.

“What’s so funny?” Klaus cried under his breath.

“She’s tricked Colonel Reidl. Into helping her transport the country’s Torahs.”

“But how?”

“The treasure must’ve been a ruse. I bet she had Malý feed Reidl information about it, taking advantage of the two’s connection through their responsibilities for the synagogues.”

“But what about that ducat you got from Kleiner’s pocket?”

“I bet they planted it for Reidl and Kleiner to discover. To convince them that the treasure was real. Along with that blueprint Captain Fischer had. Which must’ve acted like a treasure map. In conjunction with this, Malý probably made up some story about needing a way to transport the treasure. Reidl likely supplied it in exchange for a cut. I wonder if those murdered men got too close to the truth.”

“It’s odd that Reidl would trust Malý.”

“I doubt he did. That could be why he was searching for the treasure.”

“But he trusted him with the crate.”

“I doubt that as well.”

Klaus started toward the three. “I’m going to arrest them.”

Hermann stopped him with his hand. “What’s the hurry? Don’t you want to catch the opera’s finale?”

“Listen, Hermann, this has gone on too far already. I have a responsibility to arrest them.”

“We don’t know how they plan on getting that crate out of here.”

“They’d take it up in an elevator.”

“Those elevators only went to the cellars. And they’re not getting that crate up in a dumbwaiter. They’re not going to carry it up stairs either. It must be heavier than a battalion.”

“What difference does it make how they plan on getting the crate out of here?”

“We don’t know who else is involved. We can’t be sure that they’re the murderers. They could lead us to them.”

“Clearly, they’re the murderers. You yourself said that the victims got too close to the truth. Who else could’ve done it?”

“That’s what I want to learn. If we follow them to the end, we’ll know for sure. Isn’t that worth the wait? It’s not as if they’ll escape your grasp. Not while dragging that cart around.”

Klaus lowered his head. With a faint groan. He closed his eyes.

It was as if he were going through the possible permutations. Of what could go right and wrong.

He groaned another time. This fainter. Raising his head, he opened his eyes. “Okay.”

Tonda and Luděk put the last nails in the crate. They covered it with a blanket.

“I don’t get it,” Klaus said.

“What don’t you get?”

“Why someone would do all this for a bunch of prayer scrolls.”

“You couldn’t get it. Nor could I, for that matter. We’d have to have faith.”

Hermann said this. But he couldn’t deny that he had a reaction. He wasn’t positive what it was. But he was positive that he had some reaction after discovering what Gertrud had done and risked. To save items whose value was only spiritual. He might’ve been tempted to call it a twitch of faith. Or an impulse toward it. If he had ever experienced such.

What he didn’t know was that he was taking the first step toward the man that I would know my whole life. A man who was devout, despite his weakness for utopence, who would become moved whenever anyone took out the Torah during service and carried it through the congregation. This wasn’t a ceremony to him. He was reliving the miracle in the tunnel. I’m sure that’s why he never failed to kiss the Torah when it went by.

Tonda and Luděk rolled the crate forward. Gertrud followed them.

Klaus vaulted around the corner. “You were right. They’re not taking it back to the Old-New Synagogue. They must be taking it to the Klausen.”

Hermann joined him. The cart and the three people were far off. They were getting farther. “I don’t think that’s where they’re heading.”

To confirm this, Hermann took out the old brass compass from his jacket pocket. The one that he would give me on my bar mitzvah. It came with the inscription: “May it forever give you direction.”

Klaus eyed the compass with his face agog. “Where did you get that? It’s like what you had in college.”

“It doesn’t matter.”

Hermann studied the device. “The Klausen isn’t that way.”

“What is?”

“Let’s find out.”
