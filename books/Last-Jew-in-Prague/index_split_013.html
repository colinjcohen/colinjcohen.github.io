chapter thirteen: a door ajar

 

 

HERMANN WOKE WHEN the surging sun seeped through his drape-less bedroom windows.

Despite the state of his bed, sleeping on feathers was better than straw. So, despite the mixed feelings he had about returning home—and the bittersweet memories they had evoked—it had been his best night of sleep since his deportation. Not only did he feel rested, but he didn’t feel the odd coldness that he had felt each day in Theresienstadt. Regardless of weather. It was also the first since leaving Prague that he didn’t feel the least sick. He felt good.

He also felt enthused about working on the case and felt a lack of grief, and he felt guilty about both.

Hiding from this, he got up and washed and groomed himself in the broken bathroom mirror. This was a hopeless task. He was past the point of repair from soap and water. So he gave up and went to the front door of his apartment.

Outside it stood a burly guard. He led him to his lesser colleague sleeping in the car that had taken Hermann home the night before.

It drove them through Maisel Street. Across the Maisel Synagogue’s gate, whose door was ajar.

This perplexed Hermann. He contemplated its meaning.

The three continued through the ghost town’s maze. They made their way over the river.

Hermann could hear the melody of Smetana’s “Vltava.” He heard it as he had the day before.

The music ended upon reaching the other side of town and a pair of paramedics. They were carrying a corpse down a sidewalk.

Hermann couldn’t be sure of what had happened. But he had a strong guess.

This was a sight too common in Prague at the time. The suicide rate had skyrocketed since the occupation and was showing no signs of abating. No matter how the population was dwindling.

They passed the paramedics. Hermann locked onto the dead person.

With his envy toward the corpse increasing so that he was leaning toward it, he heard another song. Zdena Vincíková’s brittle voice echoed inside his head. She was singing the chorus of “Smutná neděle.”

This song ended too. As a tram passed them from the opposite direction.

Now locked onto it, Hermann recollected how—from the occupation’s early days—Jews had to ride in the back. That was if there were two cars. If there was one, he had to wait for the next to arrive.

It was because of experiences such as these that Jews like my grandparents so supported the American Civil Rights Movement. The prejudice and discrimination that they were reading about in newspapers wasn’t abstract. They were experiencing history repeating itself.

The tram left Hermann’s view. Replaced by the sprawling gardens below the castle. These recalled the Saturdays that he had spent in them with Ana and Klaus during the months of their friendship. It recalled his falling out with Klaus there as well. This was powerful enough that he could see the horror and betrayal on Klaus’s face. He saw it as he had on the day it had happened.

His guilt from this was unbearable. It only let up when the Mercedes parked by the palace.

Klaus disappeared, replaced by the empty parking lot.

Hermann surmised how early it was. This was reinforced on entering the building with his guards. He didn’t need to check the clocks on the wall to determine that it wasn’t seven. It was sufficient that Marta wasn’t around.

Slipping into his office, Hermann reviewed the police reports. It took a few paragraphs for his eyes to become heavy. He fell asleep.

He woke from the door whipping open. He jumped back. He came close to falling from his seat.
