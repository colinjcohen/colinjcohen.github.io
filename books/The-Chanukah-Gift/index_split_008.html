the eighth night

 

 

I HAD WEDNESDAY off. I expected an extended day of introspection and self-flagellation.

With the latter much greater.

I did get the extended day. But it was not what I had expected. Nor was it of either introspection or self-flagellation.

It began when I stumbled into the courtyard’s murk that morning. Nichelle was screaming at people in a pair of booths. Her fists were shaking. “He was in the bathroom with her!”

Mateo tilted his head in confusion. “You were there with them?”

“Wendy was taking a shower after getting back from work. And Zara was waiting for her at the table by the office. She wanted to ask her questions about working at Amazon. Josh came out. He ordered Zara to bed. He walked into the women’s room. He did this as if it were normal. He locked the door behind himself! Zara said that Wendy was crying when she returned.”

“But Zara doesn’t know what happened in the bathroom, does she?”

Nichelle didn’t answer.

I took a step toward her. “What’s going on?”

“Wendy left in the night. With her stuff. Because of Josh.”

“You can’t be sure of that,” Mateo shot back.

“I can. You don’t see how he slobbers over us from our door each night.”

“But—”

“—I’m calling the cops.”

She took out her phone.

Mateo slapped the table. “What will you do when they throw you out? Have you asked yourself that and what’ll happen to your kids?”

Nichelle hesitated.

“Tell it to Dan,” Mitchell implored. “Then it’s his responsibility. Then you can’t get into trouble.”

Instead, she dialed a number. She was fearless. It reminded me of someone other.

“I was outside the women’s bathroom last night,” echoed my voice. Or a voice that sounded like mine. Though it was more like that of someone other. My perfected me. The one whose moral code so thrived. “Josh was threatening her . . . assaulting her. I was also around when he stole the steaks and ribs.”

Mateo roared at me. With his hands and body. “Are you willing to file a police report? You won’t be able to take it back!”

I hesitated. In the same way that Nichelle had. But I wasn’t fearless. I wasn’t sure what words would put in an appearance. If any.

“I’ll file it.”

Nichelle inclined toward me. “You sure?” Her eyes were giving me an out.

“I’m sure.”

She called the police. Behind her Mateo was glaring at me.

 

 

NICHELLE AND I waited outside the shelter gates for the police. We waited and waited.

The murk had gone. But no sun was out. It was hiding behind 7th Street.

I supposed my reticence would resurface and rise when the consequences of what would happen became clearer and nearer. But I felt better. About me.

A police car cornered into the alley. Moving as if in a funeral procession, it pulled up beside us. A burly, bald cop climbed from the vehicle. He took my report.

He grunted. “I need to speak to the person in charge.”

Nichelle pointed through the gates. “Dan, he’s the assistant director. Just turn right at the entrance to the dorm.”

The policeman plodded inside. Nichelle and I went back to waiting.

We didn’t wait much.

Nichelle jumped on his approach. “Well?”

The man shook his head at her. “This Josh has every right to be in the bathroom with the girl.”

“She’s not a girl. She’s over thirty!”

“He has the right to be in there.”

“In the women’s room while she showered?”

“If that’s where he has to carry out his duties. You two should be ashamed. This Josh is a good guy. He’s got a family. He’s a vet. Dan was telling me how he volunteers in the community. He’s helping you lowlifes, and this is how you thank him.”

“He was threatening her . . . assaulting her. We have a witness.”

The policeman sneered at me. “What he heard is not enough. It’s hearsay.”

“That’s not what ‘hearsay’ means,” I said, regretting the words before they made it out of my mouth.

He sneered at me more than before. “She’ll have to file the complaint herself.”

Nichelle thrashed about. “She’s gone!”

“So am I.”

“What about the food that he stole from us?”

“Dan says that there’s no record of steaks or ribs delivered in the past week.”

“Because Josh was in charge that night!”

“I bet you’re lying about that too.”

The policeman dove into his car. He drove off. He drove a lot faster than he had driven in.

To the average person, we’re garbage. No better than cons.

Mateo’s words rumbled below my feet. They reverberated while Nichelle and I faced each other with a glumness that was trampling upon us.

We slipped through the shelter gates.

Mateo was scowling at me with his arms crossed. “Dan wants to see you both in the office. You first.”

“Don’t you have to be at work?” I said.

“You should’ve asked yourself that.”

 

 

I MEANDERED INTO the office. I did so no longer glum. I couldn’t figure out why.

Dan had stuffed himself behind the desk, next to an underling who was taking notes. He told me to take a seat.

“You have to leave,” he barked the moment I had sat. “You have to leave now. You broke so many rules that I haven’t a clue where to begin. And I warn you, repeat any of this outside the shelter, and we’ll sue.”

“Good luck serving the papers.”

“You should’ve let us handle it.”

I rose in my chair. I wasn’t the Empire State Building. But I didn’t lack elevation anymore. “You’ve turned blind eyes, all of you, to what’s going on here. If it was only thievery, some could excuse you for that. But not for the abuse of women without family or friends or protection. Worse than that, you’ve created an environment that let it happen. You’ve let a monster reign to prey.”

“That’s a lie!” he howled. He grabbed the phone on his desk. “I should file charges against you!”

“For what?”

His mind launched into overdrive. “Perjury!”

This time I kept my mouth shut. I recognized how doing otherwise would’ve accomplished less than what I already hadn’t.

I also ceased resisting Mateo’s advice. Getting to my feet, I smiled and waved.

 

 

IT DIDN’T TAKE much effort to pack what I had brought with me months earlier. Into the same grimy blue backpack that I’d brought as well.

I had accumulated other stuff since coming to the shelter. But I didn’t take any. I recalled the nifty Meister Eckhart saying that my grandpa told me when I left for my first job after college:

 

The more we have, the less we own.

 

 

I STEPPED INTO the bathroom with my backpack across my shoulders. To the mirrored wall behind the sinks.

The image reflecting off it was as gray and as gaunt as ever. But it wasn’t rotting.

I hadn’t fixed the world. Or my piece of it. But the pursuit of it had meaning. The same meaning that Rudi had gotten from her pursuit of it. What she got from it to that day.

It could’ve been what has drawn a lot of us Jews to pursue it. No matter how at times we make a mess of it.

Above this, it might’ve been why I wasn’t glum. It might’ve been why the darkness and demons didn’t scare me as much, and why I no longer felt like a ghost.

 

 

I LEFT THE dorm.

Nichelle was waiting for me at the shelter gates. She was eluding me too.

Patrick slid beside me. “Dan didn’t kick her out. He assumed that a man must’ve duped her into it.”

I started toward her. I picked up speed.

“I’m sorry,” she mumbled.

“You’re a hero,” I said. I said it with joy that wouldn’t have been possible eight days earlier. “You risked more than I ever could.”

We hugged.

“Josh might get away with this now,” she whispered. “But they won’t bury it ever. I promise you that. He will never be alone with another woman or girl here. He’ll never have the chance. Everyone’s gonna be watching him.”

Who watches the watchers?

Juvenal had to wait a pair of millennia to get an answer to his question. But he got one. The one that could stop all the Joshes.

Mateo groaned. “Congratulations.”

Nichelle and I released our arms from each other.

Mateo was masking his “I told you so” face. But he wasn’t masking it much. “You got thrown out, and for nothing.”

“It wasn’t for nothing. I’ve roused my moral code.”

“Your what?”

“I remember what Ben Gazzara told us on the set,” Patrick interjected from behind me.

“What was that?” I asked.

“He said, ‘Evil can only prevail when good men do nothing.’”

“It was Edmund Burke who said that.”

“You sure? He could’ve picked it up from Benny.”

“He could’ve.”

The conversation’s turn chafed Mateo. He was ready to bite my head. “The truth’s closer to what your Uncle Lenny sang: ‘Everybody knows the war is over. Everybody knows the good guys lost.’”

“Everybody but me.”

“These ‘good men’ must’ve been scarce during the Holocaust. And in Nanking and in the Ukraine and in Armenia and Rwanda. And after that Prague Spring of yours. And Tiananmen Square. And they must’ve gone extinct when the Khmer Rouge murdered millions of their own people in a country the size of Missouri and nobody cared.”

“The Dead Kennedys cared.”

Mateo opened his mouth. But the only words that would trickle out were “Now what will you do?”

“I’m gonna give Palm Springs a try. It’s warmer.”

“And gayer.”

“I could use some gaiety. It is the season.”

“What about Amoun?”

“He’ll have to get himself another Hebrew.”

“In Victorville?”

We both laughed. We embraced too. I thanked him for getting me a job. For being my friend.

Without looking back and with owning more than I ever had, I swaggered through the gates. I swaggered down the alley. In recent years, “being woke” had become a slur. But it wasn’t to me. I reveled in it. I was Christopher Plummer crossing the Alps.

“Enjoy the streets, loser,” Josh called out from the gates.

Like with Dan, I was going to keep my mouth shut. I was going to keep on going. Doing otherwise wouldn’t have made a difference.

But I came to a stop. “Enjoy prison, winner.”

“I’m not going to prison.”

“Not today and not tomorrow. But one day.”

“How can you be certain?”

“I have faith.”

 

 

THE SHELTER KICKING me out wasn’t a total cluster-you-know-what. I was free to spend money.

At my bank, I took a hundred dollars from the ATM. I bought a debit card at Walgreens. I used it to buy a train ticket to Palm Springs over the phone.

But the only train didn’t leave until 4:30 the next morning.

I had mucho tiempo.

With nothing better to do and no place better to do it, I made my way toward D Street and the Amtrak station on the other side of town.

This place was notorious. People had told me that those who would hang out there would roll others. Often after drugging them.

But this was at night. During the day it was in a border region of nice.

A field was beside the pretty station. Trees and canyons were behind them.

I sat on the grass in the near warmth. I took it all in.

 

 

I LEAPED TO my feet in the falling sun.

It hit me that I had forgotten Yon’s last letter.

I hurried toward the shelter.

But I stopped with the same hurry.

Fearing that the letter would “go missing” if I went back and asked Josh for it, I made a call.

“I heard you got kicked out,” Gerry said.

“Yup.”

“The tentacles of the CIA reach far and wide.”

“You can say that again.”

“The tent—”

“—How’s that kleptomania of yours?”

 

 

GERRY HANDED ME Yon’s letter by Starbucks.

I thanked him. I walked off.

“Give my best to Rudi,” he said.

I froze. I was positive that I had misheard.

Gerry spelled out to me that I hadn’t. “Just don’t tell her that it was from me. I told her that I was visiting relatives.”

I twisted myself toward him. “You know her?”

“She’s so sad and lonely on Chanukah. Despite the people in our house and at work. She misses you. And others. Last year she was desperate enough to . . .”

“The letters from Yon, they were from you?”

“I was fixing some leaky pipes in the basement when I happened upon a pair of boxes. They were wet. So I took out the contents. Among junk was Yon’s journal. Kinda strange that Rudi never came across it before. It was like someone had left it for me to give her as a gift. A Chanukah gift. I wanted to make Rudi happy this holiday. As happy as she’s made me. I could only do this through you.”

“How did you find me?”

“You put your address on the I-9 that you filled out for your job. The security on these government servers, it’s woeful. But I needn’t tell you that. Nor will I need to tell it to Josh after I ruin his life.”

“You broke into Homeland Security?”

“It was easier than breaking into the bank sites that I would pwn before my confinement.”

Gerry’s shoulders drooped. “I can’t understand why Rudi took me in after I got out. Maybe she saw a pinch of good. Maybe one day I will too.”

 

 

I WENT BACK to the train station, processing what Gerry had told me.

In such a haze was I that I didn’t grasp that night had come until I’d gotten back. Which brought a darkness that would shine in darker ways.

The temperature fell with the light. The winds soared. With the station closed, I had to sit on a bench by the tracks.

Never had I been so cold. It was colder than times that I had spent in Eastern Europe when the temperature was dozens of degrees below zero. Regardless of what measuring system you used.

With the chill came the zombies. They came from nowhere. These were homeless people. The same as me and others like me. But they weren’t the same. Lacking semblances of being, they roamed the grounds in a fog. They made utterances only they could fathom while wielding crowbars that they swung through the air against horrors only they could see.

They should’ve frightened me. But my disease had robbed me of fright from physical harm. This has gotten me through rough spots over the years. Predators, however many legs they have, feed off fright. They need it to attack.

My disease didn’t mean that I couldn’t feel sorrow. I felt it plenty. For each of us.

 

 

TIME DRAGGED ITS legs that night. Checking it on my phone dragged the legs further.

To flee the monotony, I bought hot chocolate and snacks in the 7-Eleven across the street. I tallied the freight cars that would pass at intervals close to clocklike. I listened to A Love Supreme on my phone.

But time stretched.

 

 

THE MONOTONY CRUMBLED.

In the guise of a man standing by the tracks down from me.

He was different from the others that night. He was no zombie. He had no crowbar. He was in no fog.

Turning in my direction, he had hollowness in his eyes. The same that has been in mine. But his was crueler.

This did scare me. I feared becoming him. I could tell what he wanted to do.

Another freight train approached.

The man returned forward. He counted down aloud. The entire desert could hear him. “10 . . . 9 . . . 8 . . .”

The train bore down on us.

On zero, the man ran across the tracks. He ran over the train’s frantic horn.

It called out to him. It pleaded with him. It begged.

He reached the other side. He ran harder. He ran into the nowhere. He didn’t turn back.

 

 

EARLIER, IT HAD taken the Victorville Police an hour to send a single squad car to the shelter.

But it didn’t take a minute for three to speed into the station’s lot.

I was the sole person by the tracks. So it was easy for the cops to assume that I had been running across them. They weren’t pleased.

A man proceeded toward me with wariness. It increased with each step.

It only faded after he checked my explanation and my train ticket (and my record).

He jabbed his thumb at the tracks. “Whoever it was put a real panic into the train’s driver.”

“I bet.”

“You have no idea why he was playing chicken with the train?”

“It wasn’t chicken. It was Russian roulette.”

The guy didn’t respond to this. He strode back to the others.

So their trip wouldn’t be a waste, they chased the zombies through the grounds.

This went on into the wee hours. I had a seat to my own Keystone Kops picture. A bad one. As police sought to herd and capture those on the other side of either.

 

 

THE CHASE WOUND down.

I remembered that I had forgotten about Rudi.

Whipping out Yon’s letter and my phone, I rang her. “Sorry to call you so late.”

“Are you okay?”

“Why do you ask?”

“What’s that commotion?”

“Just some kids.”

I opened the envelope. I pulled out the pages.

But I paused. “What about you? Are you okay? I mean really okay?”

“I’m really okay. I am now.”

 

 

THE NEW DAY shone into Yon’s room through the shades. They couldn’t hold it back. Nothing could.

This didn’t prevent Yon from drowsing off.

He had been drifting into and out of consciousness since Rudi had left the night before. With each that he had drifted out of, he doubted more whether he would drift back into another.

“Hey,” Rudi’s voice chimed.

This was loud enough to wake Yon. It pushed him toward Rudi. He came close to falling from the bed.

Her tired but elated face was poking through the doorway. “Shut your eyes.”

“They were.”

“Shut them more.”

“What’s going on?”

“Just do it.”

He clicked his tongue. But he obeyed.

“You can open them,” she said.

He did.

She waltzed into the room. A paper shopping bag was in her right hand.

What was in her other hand startled Yon: the train from the white wedding gown she had on.

Rudi smirked at his stupor. She dropped the bag. She showed off the gown, releasing the train.

It didn’t fit in the room.

Yon rubbed his eyes. “You’re wearing a dress.”

Rudi tossed her hands onto her hips. She snarled at him. “It’s a one-time event. Enjoy it while it lasts.”

The gown was familiar to Yon. “It’s the one my . . .”

Mrs. Levy tiptoed through the doorway. She lifted her feet over the train into the room.

The woman would later tell me and others how shocked she had been at the state of her son. But she said that the bigger shock was how happy he was. Happier than she could remember.

She concluded, at that instant, that there could be but one reason for her son’s all-too-obvious contradiction. She clutched this person’s fingers. “Hello, Yon.”

He shook his head. Many times he shook it. He was unable to believe what his eyes were telling him.

But they convinced him. He threw open his arms. “Mom?”

She ran to him. She hugged him. “I’m so sorry.”

“I’m who’s sorry.”

“I missed you.”

“I . . . how did this . . .”

The woman let go of Yon. She turned to Rudi. “You were right about her. We do have a lot in common. Hopefully, she’ll outgrow it.”

“How did this happen?”

“Shall we tell him?”

 

 

A TAXI DROVE off.

It left Rudi and the shopping bag outside the white manor in Newstead. The house that had so frightened her when she’d first been there.

It frightened her the same. But she had no hesitation in approaching it. She marched to the door.

Taking an extended breath, she rang the bell and prayed. She prayed for a single wish:

 

The courage to change the things I can.

Just for this day.

 

The door slid back.

Elizabeth took a glance at Rudi. She shifted away.

Rudi exhaled. “Hi.”

The woman shifted farther. “Hello.”

“Is Mrs. Levy home?”

“I’m sorry.”

The air rushed from Rudi.

But the woman added, “I’m afraid she won’t talk to you.”

“Please. Yon’s dying. He’s dying right now.”

“I’m sorry.”

“Please.”

Elizabeth wrestled with herself over what to do. She shuddered. “All right. I’ll try.”

“Thank you so much.”

“I’m not promising.”

Elizabeth scurried off. She left the door open.

Sounds commenced. The muffled sounds of two women conversing.

They got louder and more animated. To the point that there wasn’t the slightest muffling.

“How dare you talk to me like that!” Mrs. Levy hollered.

“Somebody has to!” Elizabeth hollered back.

“Tell her to go away!”

“You tell her!”

The conversation ended. Another sound overtook it: footsteps. They stomped toward Rudi.

Following them was Mrs. Levy. She appeared in the doorway with a scowl. “What do you want?”

Rudi exhaled another time. “Hi.”

“What-do-you-want?”

“Yon’s very sick.”

“As if I weren’t aware of this. Who do you think has been paying his medical bills? Not to mention for that nest of yours?”

The latter revelation upended Rudi. But she pretended it hadn’t. “Why won’t you visit him?”

“He’s made his bed. Literally.”

Rudi pitched her fists. “I’d tell you how he got it if it mattered. But it doesn’t. The disease doesn’t care who, why, or how. People like you care!”

“What do you want from me? Money?”

“That’s all you rich people live for: money. Making it and spending it and lording it over others. If you ever left this palace, you’d discover that a whole world is out there!”

Rudi was no longer angry alone. Mrs. Levy’s face turned a shade darker than red. She became another someone. Someone Rudi found menacing.

This someone lurched toward her with her own fists pitched. It backed Rudi up. A happening that hadn’t happened since Deke had died.

“You rich people?” the woman shrieked with a gruff accent. One that was light years removed from her usual one. “You can’t be stupid enough to believe that I was born in this, this palace. They didn’t allow Jews in this neighborhood when I was your age.”

The woman’s nostrils blazed. “You want the whole world to cry for you. Because of how rough you’ve had it. You want them to cry you a sea. Well, I’m from Flatbush. The worst part of Flatbush. Growing up, we had no heat. No hot water. Half the time we ate pickles! So don’t you lecture me, you . . .”

Mrs. Levy didn’t finish. Slinging herself around, she stormed into her house. She was about to slam the door.

Rudi lurched forward. “This is your last chance!”

The woman froze. Her hand gripped the wood. Her knuckles were whiter than snow.

Rudi didn’t stir either. “This is your last chance to say goodbye. Your last chance to make it right. There won’t be another. Please, Mrs. Levy, hate me as much as you want, but don’t hate Yon. He doesn’t deserve it.”

Mrs. Levy didn’t react. Rudi couldn’t say whether she ever would.

It took eons, but the woman’s head started around. It reached Rudi. “Let me get my coat.”

“There’s a favor I need to ask.”

 

 

MRS. LEVY ZOOMED into her house.

Her body shook in a constant motion. “You’re not wearing my dress.”

The idea of Rudi marrying her son was horrifying enough. But that she wanted to do so in her wedding gown was too much.

Rudi stopped in the doorway. “Mrs. Levy . . .”

The woman thrust herself at Rudi. Flummoxed by her desperation, she paused. For a full stop she did. “Don’t you see how outrageous this is?”

Rudi lowered her eyes. “I do. You have every reason to say no. I would.”

The woman thrust herself back around. But she didn’t go anywhere.

She puffed, and she huffed. “Okay. You can wear the dress. But I won’t be part of this, this so-called wedding. I will say goodbye to my son and leave.”

Rudi assented. Without saying a word.

Mrs. Levy didn’t dawdle. She hiked up the winding and gleaming wooden staircase. “You know the way.”

Rudi hurried after her. At the landing, she tipped over the railing.

Elizabeth’s eyes were following the two. Disbelief was flooding them.

 

 

MRS. LEVY SAT on the edge of her king-size canopy bed, her arms and legs crossed. She projected disinterest at Rudi’s putting on the wedding dress.

Her disinterest waned. How the gown fit Rudi recalled to the woman the day that she had worn it.

This got her off the bed. It got her to a dresser.

From it she grabbed a box of needles.

Kneeling beside Rudi, she shortened the sleeves.

She became lost. She was as stationary as she had been outside her house.

Rudi snuck a glimpse of her. “You okay?”

The woman returned to the dress. “On my first date with Yon’s father, he took me to The Sound of Music on Broadway. He wasn’t paying attention to it. Or to me. It wasn’t exactly love at first sight.”

“I know how that is.”

“So you can imagine my surprise, after we got engaged, when he told me that he wanted me to have the same dress that Mary Martin had worn on our first date.”

“I never miss the movie when it comes on TV. I’ve never told anyone that. Including Yon.”

Mrs. Levy stood. She led Rudi to a full-length mirror in the room’s corner.

Rudi blushed at her image.

This must’ve melted whatever coolness the woman had left. “You’re so beautiful.”

Rudi blushed further. “Nah.”

“Don’t argue with your mother-in-law.”

The woman’s last word hammered Rudi. She couldn’t suppress this while locked onto the woman in the mirror.

The woman met her stare. “Not until after the honeymoon. That’s how it was with my mother-in-law. She was peaches and cream until after the honeymoon.”

Rudi sagged. “I guess I won’t have to worry about that.”

“What do you mean?”

Rudi withdrew from the mirror. She grasped Mrs. Levy. She teared. “There’s not gonna be a honeymoon.”

The woman hesitated. But she grasped Rudi back. “Please don’t cry. You’ll ruin your makeup.”

“I can’t help it. I miss him already.”

“Oh, well. You can’t ruin your makeup more than it is.”

Both women laughed. Breaking their embrace, they took each other’s hands.

Mrs. Levy lobbed her head toward the hallway. “We better hurry. We’ve got a wedding to go to.”

 

 

RUDI TOSSED HER hands onto her hips. She stepped toward Yon with another of her snarls. “So, are you gonna marry me or what?”

He gushed. “You call that a proposal?”

“It’s the best you’re gonna get.”

“I recollect someone telling me that marriage is an outdated and sexist—”

“—You’re making me mad now.”

“We’d need a . . .”

Rabbi Orenstein sprung into the room. With both his arms in tow. He came off as if he had swallowed a flock of canaries.

He slipped a juice glass from behind his back. “I believe that’s my cue.”

Mrs. Levy put her palms on Yon’s chest. “We need to get you a tux.”

“A tux?”

“And I’ll have to get my hands on a photographer.”

Rudi jaunted to the bed. “And guests will be coming at any time.”

Yon’s jaw fell. “Guests?”

Mrs. Levy swung toward Rudi. “What about music?”

Rudi took her portable stereo from the shopping bag. Slapping it onto the bureau next to Yon, she hit play.

“Moonlight Serenade” filled the room.

“Glenn Miller?” Mrs. Levy gasped.

Rudi sat on the bed next to Yon. Snatching his arm, she told the woman that she would forever call her mother, “It’s our song.”

“Kids today.”

 

 

I HUSTLED INTO Yon’s room with a cardboard box, dressed in an itchy suit that the moths in my closet had more familiarity with than I had.

The rabbi passed me. “I’ll be back after my rounds.”

Rudi’s appearance sent me teetering. Along with the box.

She vaulted to me. She pirouetted.

I did a triple-take. “Who are you? And what have you done with my sister?”

She slapped my arm.

I headed toward the floor for a second time. Along with the box.

But she caught me. She threw her arm around me. “You’re gonna have to give me away, big brother.”

I laughed at what she had called me. She calls me that to this day. Despite us never learning who had been born first. “I’m not sure Jews give away brides.”

“We’ll make our own rules.”

I opened the box. I showed her a three-tiered wedding cake. On top of it stood painted wooden figurines of a bride and a groom. With the bride’s hair black and spiked. “Grandma was up till three making this.”

“Where are they?”

“They left me off in front. But I should tell you . . .”

“Is this what I got dressed up for?” carped Butch’s voice in the corridor.

He strutted into the room. In a Black Flag T-shirt. With a green tie around his neck.

Rudi hugged him. But she got annoyed at the record in his hand. “What’s that?”

“A gift.”

“I told you, no gifts.”

“It’s not for you.”

Butch trotted to Yon. He handed him Live Yardbirds: Featuring Jimmy Page.

Yon reeled. “I’ve never heard of this.”

“It was only out for a week. It includes some early Zeppelin songs.”

“Wow.”

“Now you have some music to listen to when you get home.”

Yon battled his emotions. He took Butch’s hand with both of his. “Now I have some music to listen to.”

 

 

GRANDMA LED A dazed Grandpa into the room. He was somewhere but not there.

Rudi and her dress sent Grandma to an abrupt halt. She cast her arms above. “My sweet little girl!”

Rudi smirked. “No one has ever called me that.”

“You better get used to it. Because I will be saying it always, and there is nothing you can do about it.”

Rudi ran into her snatches. The two kissed each other’s cheeks.

Grandma choked on her words. “You have none of my DNA . . . but all of my soul.”

Neither wanted to let go. But they did.

Rudi drifted to Grandpa and his daze.

“Už jsme na Lucerně?” he asked, giving away his location: the dance hall in Prague that he had frequented as a young man.

Grandma took his shoulder. “I am afraid that this is not one of his good days.”

Rudi didn’t mind. She took the grand old man into her arms. “Thank you, Grandpa. For giving me this day.”

Holding him tighter, she murmured, “Mám tě strašně moc ráda.” She told him in the language he loved how much she loved him.

 

 

TONY WHEELED HIMSELF into the room.

This Tony was different from earlier. Since his experience with Grandpa, never had he been without a smile. But never was it as bold as that day.

He stopped by Yon. He seized his hand. “I guess this means that you’re off the market.”

Yon laughed. “It appears so.”

“The best usually are.”

 

 

MRS. LEVY WAS able to hire a photographer. He sprinted into the room after the tailor, who fit Yon into a tuxedo under the direction of his mother.

Nurse Templeton came next. On her day off. She brought more men that Rudi had helped raise. They had come to return the favor.

The Crisses came too.

Trailing them was the rabbi. “We have another guest.”

Into the room snuck Mr. Agnellino. With a stack of pizzas.

Rudi stammered to him with alarm. “We didn’t order pizzas.”

“These are on me,” Mr. Agnellino said.

“I don’t get it.”

The man dropped the boxes onto a dresser. “For months you have come into my restaurant. You come each day and buy a pizza from me. Even though me and the rest of us are mean to you. And none of us can figure out why. Why is it that she cannot get the message? This awful person everyone speaks so terribly about?”

Rudi’s eyes darted off.

Mr. Agnellino’s followed them. “Then the other day, Rabbi Orenstein, God bless him, he tells me who the pizzas are for. He also tells me the wonderful things that you have been doing for this poor . . . and I have never been so ashamed.”

Rudi wouldn’t let him stay this way. “Forget it.”

“I will not forget it. You are a saint.”

“I’m not.”

“Listen to me. This is a subject that I have some knowledge about. You are a saint. They come in many shapes and sizes. And hairstyles.”

Rudi gave it her best not to smile. But it pushed its way out.

Mr. Agnellino raised his hand. Over both his shoulder and his head. “From this day on, for as long as I own that restaurant, for as long as my children and grandchildren own it, we will treat you like a member of the family when you walk inside. This is a promise.”

She hugged him. “You’re staying for the wedding, aren’t you?”

He hugged her back. “It would be an honor.”

The rabbi approached them. “So, are we ready?”

Rudi couldn’t answer.

Mariana had charged into the room. She charged into it wearing a blue gown. Alongside a woman in a red one, who had auburn hair and glasses and the manner of someone about to introduce herself to her in-laws.

Mariana screamed her head off. She flapped her arms and shimmied. From her toes to her fingernails.

Rudi did the same.

They flew into each other.

“I was sure that you wouldn’t make it,” Rudi cried.

Mariana’s friend crossed her arms. “She drove so fast that I don’t remember crossing Delaware.”

“It’s easy to miss,” Mariana said.

 

 

IN A PLACE and time far from that day, I would reminisce about the wedding with Mariana.

She never did become a playwright. After college, she moved to Hollywood to become a screenwriter.

All kinds of years later, years that included several all-night calls with Rudi, she achieved success. She became known for her striking female characters.

I ran into her on Sunset Boulevard. Amid the heat and smog, she said, “I write about larger-than-life people. But Rudi is the only one I’ve met. There’s probably some of her in the ones that I’ve created. More than some.”

 

 

RUDI AND MARIANA broke their hug.

Mariana beamed at the woman next to her. “This is Sandra.”

Rudi took Sandra’s hand. “It’s nice to finally meet you. Thank you for coming to my wedding. On no notice.”

“Thank you for inviting me. This is so romantic, and you look so totally awesome.”

The rabbi tiptoed behind Rudi. “I hate to be a spoiler, but I need to be leaving soon.”

“All right. Let’s get this on.”

“Wait for us!” shouted the voice of a boy down the corridor. Along with shuffling feet. Lots.

Eliot ran into the room. He was a senior and wasn’t so geeky. His suit, shirt, and tie came close to matching. He was on the way toward a brash man. One who’d go to Stanford before becoming an engineer and a high-tech entrepreneur in Silicon Valley.

Rudi clenched him. “My favorite Quincy Punk!”

Owen streaked into the room next. In a suit that made mine appear comfortable.

With the help of his friends, he had graduated from high school. He was working at a local auto repair shop. Later on, with an investment from Eliot, he would run a chain called Good Karma, whose motto was “We treat our customers like friends. Because they are.”

Rudi jumped into Owen’s arms. The arms of a man who—from somewhere past the bleacher seats—she had come to love. She kissed him on the nose.

He blushed.

Jared was the last to enter. He was premed at Seton Hall University down the road. He would become a doctor. Working for a missionary group with the woman he had run into on his ascent from the bottom, he would spend his career traveling the world, giving care and comfort to anyone in need. Making the most from the second chance he got.

Something big. Important.

Rudi held him the longest. He, beyond the rest of us, could relate to the conflicting feelings that she must’ve been having. Of both jubilation and doubt as to whether she deserved any of it.

She stopped herself from crying. She released her arms from Jared. She whipped herself to the rabbi. “We’re ready.”

The man blinked at the photographer’s flash. “Where do I start? I’ve never done a wedding like this. We have no ketubah or chuppah. We have no wine. What we do have are two young people who love each other. I can more than attest to it. So hopefully God will give us a pass on the rest.”

He put his eyes on Rudi. They were embracing her with the same ardor as they had in the restaurant. “You’re supposed to circle the groom seven times. But under the circumstances, why don’t you just join him?”

Mrs. Levy, sitting beside Yon on the bed, got up. Rudi took her place.

We crowded around them. The rabbi gave Yon a kippah. He struggled to put it on.

The rabbi jetted back to Rudi. “You are Jewish? I’ve presumed by your last name and your interest in the Talmud and Hebrew and . . .”

She didn’t respond. She couldn’t. In spite of all the reading that she had done and all the cooking and Sabbaths that she’d experienced and all that Jewish law had told her, she couldn’t say what she was. The leaf blowing in the wind had stayed that way.

The rabbi scanned the room. “I realize that this is rather un—”

“—She’s Jewish,” Mrs. Levy interrupted. She placed her hand on Rudi’s shoulder.

Rudi placed her hand on the woman’s.

The rabbi remained hesitant.

“She is our granddaughter,” Grandma called out.

The rabbi pivoted toward her and Grandpa.

“She is as Jewish as we are.”

“Thank you, Grandma,” Rudi said. She said this with a voice rooted in love. “That was better than the cake.”

“Who has the rings?” the rabbi asked next.

The question floored Rudi. Its answer came in silence.

“Someone does have the rings?”

Rudi slumped over. “We don’t have any.”

Mrs. Levy grabbed her wedding ring. “Oh, yes, you do. You have one.”

The woman took off the ring. She offered it to Yon.

He wouldn’t take it. “I can’t let you do that, Mom.”

“Your father would’ve wanted you to have it. He would’ve been so proud of you. I’m proud of you. This is our wedding gift to you.”

Yon shook his head. Countless times he did. But he took the ring.

The rabbi leaned over it. “Put it on Rudi’s right index finger and repeat after me, ‘Behold, you are consecrated to me with this ring according to the law of Moses and Israel.’”

Yon slid the ring onto Rudi. But he struggled with the words. He struggled worse than he had with the kippah. Multiple false starts kept interrupting him. Multiple pauses did the same after he got it going.

But he got through it.

It was Rudi’s turn.

Nobody needed to tell her what to do. Nor did it matter that she had no ring for Yon.

She wrapped her finger around his. She locked her eyes onto him. She took her vow: “Ani l’dodi, ve dodi li. I am my beloved’s, and my beloved is mine.”

Rudi wasn’t finished. Becoming overwhelmed in the moment, she squeezed Yon’s finger and babbled, “I didn’t know what ‘happy’ was until you.”

Yon hiked his head off the pillow. Through the pain. “I wouldn’t trade the last year with you for any amount of years without you.”

The rabbi wasn’t expecting Rudi and Yon’s afterwords. His emotions took over. It took seconds to get them under control. “The last step is to break the glass.”

He got the juice glass. Covering it in his handkerchief, he set it between Yon’s foot and the bedpost.

Yon pushed. With all he had left he pushed.

But the glass wouldn’t crack.

The rabbi peered into the hallway. “I could get a light bulb. It would be easier.”

Yon would have none of it. “I can do it.”

He gave it another try. But he got no closer.

Rudi stretched toward the rabbi. “Is it that important?”

“Yes, it’s that important!” Yon snapped, his face red and sweating. “It signifies that we must temper joy. No one gets this more than us.”

Rudi was coming undone. But she hung together. She took Yon’s free hand. “You can do it, Yoni. You can do it.”

Yon clenched his eyes closed. He clenched Rudi’s hand. He willed himself to break the glass.

Everyone was crying. Grandpa was crying. So was the photographer, who had to stop taking pictures.

Teardrops also ran over Yon’s face. As the glass splintered.

The rabbi leapt off his feet. “Mazel tov!”

We erupted in applause.

But this didn’t dampen anyone’s tears. They bathed Mrs. Levy. She ran into the hallway, calling out for her son.

The rabbi composed himself another time. He continued, “What comes now is the yichud, where we give the bride and groom twenty minutes of seclusion.”

Mr. Agnellino grabbed the pizzas. “We can eat downstairs.”

He went off with the boxes.

The rest of us trailed behind him. We congratulated the couple. We shook their hands.

Yon did this with as much affection as he could muster. He wasn’t saying goodbye. He was saying farewell.

My turn came. “Brother.”

He brought me close to him. “Brother.”

Mariana was the last to leave. She had been crying before the ceremony began and was crying while cradling her best friend. “I love you so much.”

“I love you,” Rudi screeched with one arm around her and the finger of her other hand around Yon’s.

With a final cuddle, Mariana released her arms from Rudi. She stumbled from the room. She closed the door.

Yon dug under his blanket. “Shut your eyes.”

“What’s going on?” Rudi groused.

“Just do it.”

She clicked her tongue. But she complied.

It took time, but he gave her the okay.

She opened her eyes. To him placing his chai necklace around her neck.

He couldn’t do more.

She took the chain from him. She fastened it.

“This is my wedding gift to you,” he said. “To remind you.”

She gripped the pendant between her fingers.

“But it isn’t to remind you of me. It’s to remind you of that word . . . living. The most precious there is. Don’t you forget it.”

Rudi could no longer stop what had been destined. She cried.

Yon shot her a dirty look. “Don’t get soft on me now, Rudi. That’s not what I signed up for.”

“I don’t want to live without you. It’s not a figure of speech or a hyperbole or whatever the right term is. It just is.”

He clasped her hand. “Each person you touch, I will touch. Each life you change, I will change.”

Rudi cried more.

To hide from this, she leaned over the stereo. She hit play.

“Look at us,” she blubbered. As Glenn Miller serenaded them with his trombone. “Who would have thought?”

“Me. I knew it from the beginning.”

Neither knew what to do next. This lingered.

Yon snarled. He snarled at Rudi as she had at him so many times. “So, are you gonna kiss the groom or what?”

“You call that a proposition?”

“It’s the best you’re gonna get.”

Rudi leaned over Yon. She kissed him. By less than the breadth of a particle had their lips met.

Yet he swooned.

 

 

THAT WAS HOW Yon’s journal ended.

But that wasn’t the end of his story. I was sure of this. From how it had started and from that cryptic entry he had written.

Chanukah was different this time, and I got a better gift. But Rudi’s holding back.

Desperate for the story’s end, I begged Rudi for it.

She grumbled. “I told you, I have no idea what he’s talking about.”

“Grandma was right. You lie badly. As badly as Yon did. And that’s the best compliment anyone could get.”

“I swear I don’t know.”

“You do.”

I kept pressing. But she kept denying, and I couldn’t bribe her with more pages.

“Please,” I said, “it’ll be the best Chanukah gift I’ll get.”

She laughed. Without adding to it.

But she couldn’t turn down my request on the last night of Chanukah.

This wasn’t because she was my sister. It was because she was a rabbi.

The kind others would aspire to be.

 

 

THE END OF Yon’s story began on the same night that his story had begun. That first night of Chanukah. The night Rudi had argued that nothing special had happened.

That year it coincided with Christmas Eve. Swarms of people were shopping.

With the day tumbling from the skies and snow falling around her, Rudi stood outside a mobbed Livingston Mall.

This was a place she loathed. Like any place like it.

But she wasn’t there for herself.

So, while listening on her Walkman to Lee Ving’s most unusual holiday take—the closest punk rock would get to Yuletide cheer—she stormed into the building. She passed through a corridor leading to the stores. Toward a woman leaving with two children and twice that many bags.

Petrified by Rudi’s appearance, one that must’ve been more menacing due to the snow on her, the woman pulled her kids from Rudi’s path as if she were a charging lioness.

That wasn’t nautical miles from the truth.

 

 

RUDI SHUFFLED THROUGH a row of men’s shirts in a department store called Bamberger’s.

She had been in the mall for hours. She’d come upon the ordinary when she needed what wasn’t.

What do you give someone who’s about to die?

Across the room, a heavyset security guard was prattling into a handheld radio. His bugged-out eyes had bolted onto Rudi.

She took this as a sign to go elsewhere. She left, followed by the guard.

He followed her till she departed the store and reached the mall area.

She swayed to a railing and what was below it.

An ill-stuffed Santa was going through the motions.

“Fake,” she growled. “This entire place is fake.”

She trudged toward an escalator. But paused.

A chocolate shop was behind it.

 

 

THE TEENAGE CLERK dropped white chocolate into a paper bag. With his hands and body shaking.

He offered it to Rudi. His hands and body were shaking more.

Frowning, she slapped money onto the counter. She snagged the bag and took off. She flew down the escalator and past it.

But, on reaching a corridor to the parking lot and the bus stop beyond it, a toy store window nabbed her eye. In it was a model waterfall. Like the one Yon loved but couldn’t visit anymore. Best of all, she had enough money to buy it.

“There it is, Mommy!” yapped a voice from the store’s other side. “There it is!”

Rudi wanted to rush into the store. She wanted to buy the waterfall. But she stuck her head around the corner.

A girl of around five was jumping up and down in front of a lone Cabbage Patch doll in the window. The source of that craze sweeping the nation.

Her mother crept toward the doll. She examined both it and its price.

Slinking toward her daughter, she searched for words. “I’m sure, I’m sure next year Santa . . .”

The girl hid her disappointment. By avoiding her mother’s eyes. “It’s okay, Mommy. Really.”

“How about some hot chocolate?”

The two slogged away hand in hand, their heads hanging.

Rudi wanted to ignore this. More than this, she wanted to forget the disappointing Christmases of her childhood.

They were hurling at her. The mornings without a tree, let alone presents. The mornings no different from any other. The mornings she wasn’t special. The mornings she didn’t matter. The mornings she had no love.

The ignoring and forgetting wasn’t easy. But she did both. She scurried into the store. To a counter.

Behind it kneeled a man with black-rimmed glasses and a bow tie who had outlived time.

He lifted himself. It took ages, but he got to his feet.

Unlike the others Rudi had encountered at the mall that day, the man wasn’t frightened of her. Or disgusted by her. He expressed warmth. “Happy Chanukah.”

Rudi was certain that he must’ve been talking to another person. She checked around the store.

But no one was nearby.

She spun toward the man. She didn’t get his kindness. But she didn’t get more how he could tell she was Jewish when she herself couldn’t.

He edged toward her. “What can I do for you?”

Rudi’s eyes fell upon the waterfall in the window. They stayed on it. But she asked for the doll.

The man took it from the window. “You’re lucky. This is our last. We couldn’t keep them in stock. That’s why we had to mark the price up so much.”

He brought the doll to the counter. “Do you want me to wrap it?”

Rudi squinted out the window. At the mother and daughter sipping cups at a bench in the distance. “If you can do it fast.”

“It won’t take but a minute.”

The man wrapped the doll. He wrapped it in gold paper fashioned from stars.

She tipped toward him. “Could you do me a favor?”

“What’s that?”

She pointed to the mother and daughter. “Could you give the doll to that woman?”

The man froze. In mid-wrap.

He bent in the direction of Rudi’s glossy black fingernail. “I don’t understand.”

“Tell her, tell her that she’s won a contest. Or that it’s some kind of store promotion. Tell her whatever you want as long as it’s not in front of her girl.”

The man’s sight persisted on the pair. He must’ve noted their sadness.

Returning to Rudi, he expressed further warmth. I bet he expressed it further than before.

“What about you?” he crooned. “What can I get for you to give?”

She fished through her seabag for her purse. “I’m fine.”

The man finished with the wrapping.

Rudi slapped money onto the counter. She sped off.

“Don’t you want a receipt?” he asked.

“No.”

“Or your change?”

Rudi didn’t answer. She ran from the store. She cursed her stupidity.

Beside the corridor she stopped. For a reason unknown she waited.

The old man left the store. With a plain-paper shopping bag in his hand. He inched toward the woman and her girl.

The girl who would still believe in fairy tales.

 

 

RUDI OPENED THE door of her apartment. To “Moonlight Serenade.”

Yon was staggering and limping. He was carrying a red gift-wrapped box to the simple metal menorah on their mantle. Followed each step by Flutter. She followed him like her life depended on it.

No longer was Yon the gorgeous football player Rudi had first met. But nothing was ever as beautiful. She wanted to seal this inside her.

Yon lowered himself to his knees. He lowered the gift under the menorah.

Turning toward Rudi, he said, “You’ve caught me.” He said it with a smile. A smile she loved. He was only smiling for her.

She didn’t smile back. “I didn’t get you a gift.”

“You’ve already given me it.”

“I did get you some . . .”

Rudi shut the door. She marched to Yon.

Kneeling beside him, she took the white canvas seabag from her shoulder. She reached inside it for the chocolate.

Which he enjoyed that night. In the arms of his gift.
