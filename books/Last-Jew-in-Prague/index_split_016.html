chapter sixteen: a mirror reflecting time

 

 

GERTRUD PARKED HER Horch 930V Phaeton convertible on Smetana Quay. The sun was reflecting off both the car’s polished white exterior and the river across the street from it.

From the way she described this to me, she must’ve appeared like the cover image of one of those magazines that promote an affluent lifestyle. Any objective observer, judging from the smile on her face, would’ve believed that not a single notion was going on inside her head.

The smile left her. A gunshot echoing in the distance had wiped it away.

Gertrud gripped her steering wheel. She recalled a similar sound. A sound that she didn’t want to recall.

This became intolerable. So intolerable was it that she forced her hands from the wheel. She forced the smile back on her face. In defiance of herself.

Jumping from her car, she raced to the riverbank.

By the railing, she set her sights northward. At the docks and the boats floating upstream. As well as at what she could only set her sights on inside her mind. An object that was tugging her forward.

She resisted this. She went in the opposite direction. She waltzed down the quay.

I bet she looked like she owned the whole damn city while chugging the cool air blowing off the water rumbling by her. She did so while pretending that she was the happy and carefree woman she was projecting. That she could control her emotions and the hate accelerating from them. That she could keep them from controlling her.

She couldn’t keep this pretending up. This was due to the bench that she was approaching.

On it sat a woman too familiar.

Klara was a few years older than Gertrud. She was both unkempt and filthy, wearing a yellow shawl over her head and shoulders. She was smaller than Gertrud. With black hair and the same-colored eyes. The latter were boring into the river without signs of life. They didn’t blink.

I bet she also had a look of quiet desperation. The same that I’ve witnessed in others over the years. It’s how we distinguish each other. Even when we’re not filthy or unkempt.

Despite the differences between the women, Gertrud saw in Klara someone who was much the same as her. Standing in front of her was like standing in front of a mirror that reflected time. It made her doubt whether she could control feelings that were desperate for a reason to leap out.

Gertrud had confronted the likeness between the two before that morning. She would confront it whenever she’d run into the woman. Which happened more often than what any standard deviation of coincidence could allow.

Could their fates have entwined?

Gertrud eluded both the question and the answer. She wanted to walk past Klara with the barest of acknowledgment. She had important tasks to complete that morning. She didn’t want any distractions from them.

But she couldn’t convince herself to do this. She strode to the woman.

“How are you?” she murmured. Though, from experience, she was positive that she wasn’t going to get a response.

Klara didn’t disappoint her. She didn’t speak. She acted like she hadn’t heard.

“Did you sleep well? Or at all?”

Klara neither spoke nor reacted.

“What happened to the blanket that I gave you?” was the next question Gertrud didn’t expect an answer to.

Not getting one, she took the woman’s shoulder. “What am I going to do with you, Klara?”

Klara didn’t answer. But her eyes skimmed over Gertrud’s.

Gertrud could catch a speck of hope in the woman’s eyes. Or the wanting of it. “My offer from yesterday stands. You could regard it as a favor to me. Which would be true. You have no idea how.”

Klara said nothing. But she did project acquiescence.

Gertrud snatched at this. She tossed her head toward the building across the street. “I need to speak with someone inside the café. But after that, I’m coming back for you. Will you promise me you’ll wait?”

Klara didn’t respond. But her acquiescence carried on.

Gertrud took this for a yes.

Swinging her arms, she backed her way across the street. She sprinted toward Café Slavia. She pretended that she was back in control of herself.

Passing the doors, she neared Tonda, a colossal and corpulent balding man a few years younger than her. He was sitting at a table in the middle of what was and is a narrow and lengthy hall.

Tonda stood with joy. From her strutting toward him with joy of her own.

They kissed each other’s cheeks. They sat across from one another.

“I’d order you a cup,” Tonda said with the voice of a man far smaller, after lifting his own cup. “But it took a half hour to get mine, and I’m a regular here.”

“Why do you keep coming to this place? The service is terrible. It’s been terrible forever. It’ll be terrible a hundred years from now.”

He swung his cup toward the riverbank. “I love the view. Honestly, I can’t conceive of what I would do without it. The thought of this frightens me.”

“Tonda, you’ve been a very naughty boy.”

He contrived innocence. “Whatever do you mean?”

“I haven’t heard from you in weeks, and you know how I don’t like that.”

“I couldn’t avoid it, my dear. It’s been hectic. Lots of craziness in Josefov, everywhere. My duties have me coming and going during the wee hours of each night. I’ve barely slept since the last we met.”

“Malý, you scoundrel!” boomed a voice nearby.

The two turned to it.

A gleeful man was holding out his hand to Tonda.

Tonda shook it. “Good to see you.”

“How is it that they haven’t arrested you?”

“Perhaps they haven’t gotten around to it yet.”

The three laughed. The man shuffled off.

“So,” Gertrud said after the man was out of earshot, “business is good, I presume.”

“Better than good, and it’s going to get better. Early next week.”

“Is this the deal that you’ve been alluding to?”

“It is indeed, my dear. It’s a deal on the cusp of completion. My associate and I need only to arrange a time to pack up the goods.”

“Associate? What associate?”

“Luděk.”

“You’ve never mentioned such a man. Who is he?”

“Someone I’ve worked with for years. I knew his father some too. Before they sent him away for embezzlement. A fine man otherwise, whom I’d trust with my life. Or someone else’s.”

Gertrud gave Tonda a slow shake of her head. “I sure hope that you can trust his son.”

“I’m telling you that I can, my dear. It’s downhill from here. The difficult part is over.”

“And you were worried that the deal would collapse.”

“You were right. Not the slightest hitch has happened.”

“You sound surprised.”

“Not surprised. Astonished. I was certain that this would be the death of me. I made out my will.”

“Don’t talk like that. I’ve got enough to worry about.”

“Like what?”

Gertrud checked the near-empty café. “Like Klaus. He’s been acting awfully odd of late. It’s making me awfully suspicious.”

“You don’t imagine that he’s up to something unkosher, so to speak?”

“I can’t imagine what he’s up to. The man’s impenetrable. But speaking of ‘unkosher,’ I came upon a terrible peculiarity this morning. To say the least.”

“What?”

She made another check around the café. She leaned toward Tonda. “He was with a Jew.”

“A Jew? Some Jews are left in Prague?”

“Apparently,” she said, not believing it herself. “Klaus gave me some nonsense about how they’re old friends.”

“Who is he?”

“I haven’t an inkling. Other than that he appeared as if he had come from the camps. Still, the two were congenial. They were about to take a car ride together as if this were normal. Someone might’ve gotten the impression that they were colleagues.”

“Could this Jew be a collaborator?”

“That’s what I’m going to learn.”

“I could delay my deal. In case you have a problem that you need help with. Another week probably won’t make a difference.”

“But it could. You’ve been working on this deal for so long, Tonda. I’d hate to see it fall apart at the end. It would pain me greatly. If anything . . . you should hasten the deal.”

“Hasten it?” he gasped. “Hasten it by how much?”

Gertrud inched forward. “By how much could you hasten it?”

Tonda inched forward himself. “I could get it done tomorrow. If necessary. Or the day after tomorrow. At the latest. My partners would love nothing more than to hasten it. They’re anxious to get the deal done.”

Gertrud sat back in her chair. “So you have nothing to lose by hastening it?”

“Nothing much,” he said.

Patting his jacket pocket, he added, “And I have my will with me at all times.”
