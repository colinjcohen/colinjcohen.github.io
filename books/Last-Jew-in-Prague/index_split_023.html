chapter twenty-three: the voice on the phone

 

 

THREE MEN SAT in a sparse room. It had a low, uneven ceiling. A narrow width and depth.

Earlier, when Hermann had walked through its doors, the place recalled the claustrophobic nightmares that Max Beckmann had painted. The paintings he and his Ana loved.

It recalled the room in Beckmann’s Family Picture the most.

Klaus finished reading the report that Captain Kleiner had written.

He dropped it onto the table. In front of a young man who was stages past terrified.

Hermann had never met Lieutenant Maier. But he had a sense of what he was feeling. He had observed the same fear in others over the years. Victims of circumstances beyond what they could grasp.

So he felt some sympathy toward the man. No matter how he didn’t want to.

“Who ordered you to send Major Schiller to the synagogue?” Klaus asked Maier, his voice no more than a whisper, not wanting to scare him further.

Maier gripped the table. In a way that suggested he was keeping himself from shaking. “I got a call from a Major Hoffmann. He said that he was from Legal Affairs. That it was urgent that I get Major Schiller to the Maisel Synagogue. He also told me that this order was for his ears alone.”

Hermann drifted toward the man. “You didn’t know this Major Hoffmann?”

Maier turned to Hermann. “I had never heard of him. But I don’t know anyone who works in that department.”

“Could you make out his voice?”

Maier measured his answer. “In what way?”

“Was it familiar?”

“No.”

“Was there anything unusual about his voice?”

Maier measured some more. “He might’ve had a cold.”

“But he could’ve been masking his voice.”

“I suppose.”

“Did you tell any of this to Captain Kleiner?”

“I told him about the major’s call. But he said that no Major Hoffmann is in Legal Affairs. He said that this proves my guilt.”

The man spun back to Klaus. “But I’m not guilty. He did call me. From a line within the palace. He asked for me by name. Why would I suspect that he wasn’t who he said he was?”

“What was the message you delivered?” was Hermann’s next question.

The man threw out his hands. “I can’t remember it exactly. But the gist of it was that the major needed to go to the synagogue. To look inside it for an arched door.”

Klaus shook his head at the man. “Didn’t you find this message odd?”

“I’ve received odder. It’s not unusual for people to leave cryptic or coded messages.”

“What about the belongings from the three men that they found in your flat?”

“I know nothing about them. They were not there when I left this morning. Please, Captain, they’re threatening to shoot me for this.”

“How did you know where Major Schiller was that night?” Hermann interjected.

“I got his phone number from his file. I called there. A woman told me that he was at the Theater of the Estates with his wife.”

“Didn’t Major Schiller find it odd that some man he obviously didn’t know wanted him to go to a synagogue in the middle of the night?”

“He didn’t find it odd in the least. When I gave him the order outside the theater, it was as if he’d been expecting it.”

“Did you accompany him to the synagogue that night?”

“He told me to go back to my post.”

“But you knew the next day that he’d been murdered, didn’t you?”

The lieutenant stammered. “No one said anything about a murder. I only heard a week or so later that he had been killed. Soldiers get killed all the time. Why would I imagine that it had to do with me?”

Klaus slapped the table. “Why didn’t you inform your superiors about this incident? Why didn’t you report it in your log for the night?”

“Major Hoffman ordered me not to put it in my report. And I wasn’t going to question a direct order from a superior.”

Neither Klaus nor Hermann had more questions.

They started from the room. They left Maier with harsher fright than what he had when they’d found him.

“Please,” the man begged Klaus.

But Klaus could do nothing for him.

The two left the room. They headed outside. They moved slower than how they had come in.

“He’s innocent,” Hermann said to Klaus while questioning why he had. Or why he cared what would happen to the lieutenant when he didn’t care what would happen to him.

“I knew that before we got here. But I can’t prove it.”

“They should have to prove his guilt. Not the other way around.”

Klaus didn’t bother to respond.

The men exited into the spitting rain. They got into the Mercedes.

It drove off.

“What will you do with me?” Hermann asked.

He asked this, but he wasn’t sure that he wanted an answer.

Klaus shut his eyes. He fell back in his seat. “Tomorrow, I’ll take you back to Theresienstadt.”

Hermann was skeptical. “But not tonight?”

“Not tonight. Because I’m exhausted.”

You must promise me that, no matter how impossible it may be, you’ll wait through whatever ‘now’ you have left. If it’s to last seconds more.

Ana’s words rang out in Hermann’s head. As well as the substance in them. What’s more, instead of seconds, he would have a whole night.

This he could make last a long time.
