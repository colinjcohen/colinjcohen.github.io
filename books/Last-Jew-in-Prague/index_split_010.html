chapter ten: the café across from jáchym street

 

 

HERMANN AND KLAUS returned to the Mercedes. Its driver took them through the streets of Hradčany, crossing the river over the Mánes Bridge.

Hermann would swear that he could hear the melody from Smetana’s “Vltava” rising from the water.

Was this what the composer had heard while not being able to hear?

The music faded. The car reached the other side of town. They headed north into Josefov along Maisel Street.

Hermann set his overstretched eyes on a quarter where he had lived for decades and where Jews had been living for a thousand years. For most of this, Josefov had been a walled-off ghetto of cramped streets and the lone place Jews could live in Prague. After the walls came down and Jews were free to live where they pleased, there were upward of twenty thousand packed within an area of what in the prior half-century the local government had reduced to a few dozen streets. Now Hermann was the last Jew there. Or anywhere in the city.

That didn’t mean that no one was living in Josefov. Non-Jews were living there and had been before the occupation. Yet the place was a ghost town. So Hermann could fathom why there had been no witnesses to the murders. A district that had been so alive to him had lost this.

This didn’t stop the three from getting stuck in traffic among the empty sidewalks. Crawling along, they passed building after building.

Each elicited its own memories in Hermann. One was so intense that it came to life. Upon reaching a café across from Jáchym Street.

 

 

HERMANN AND ANA were passing away a Sunday morning in the café.

Like many that they had passed there, they were reading the latest editions of Lidové noviny and the Prager Tagblatt. They were sipping cups of Turkish-style coffee. They were arguing too. Over the latest doings of President Masaryk and Prime Minister Udržal.

Ana could see no wrong in either man. The least in Masaryk, who in many ways was the father of the country. They had named a train station in Prague after him while he was living.

But Hermann was critical of both. Despite a love for Masaryk greater than Ana’s. A love that many Jews shared for the man and do to this day. He has a street in Jerusalem and a square in Tel Aviv. From his efforts to free Leopold Hilsner. A mentally handicapped and homeless Jew, convicted in a renowned case of blood libel prior to the republic’s founding.

Hermann’s problem with Masaryk was that he believed that the president and the government were alienating the country’s extensive German minority. With their policies and unfulfilled promises of autonomy. He was right that there would be consequences to this. It would be the excuse Hitler would use in 1938 to “free” the Sudetenland in the country’s border areas. Under the Munich Agreement’s infamous terms. Which would be the precursor to Hitler “freeing” the country’s rump less than a year later.

Hermann’s arguments exasperated Ana. She flung her newspaper open in front of herself. “I don’t know why I married you.”

Hermann lowered his eyes. “I don’t know either.”

“Fortunately, knowing has nothing to do with it.”

Hermann remembered these same words. Spoken in a different time and place. Reaching his hand over the table, he took Ana’s.

Her touch was comforting, and it was as aspiring as the first he had seen her face.

 

 

HERMANN RETURNED TO the present. The Mercedes had parked by the Maisel Synagogue’s closed gates.

But Ana’s touch and what it had evoked remained. He caressed his hand.

Klaus turned to Hermann. “Your old flat on Eliška Krásnohorská Street was available. I arranged for you to stay there.”

“Oh,” Hermann muttered, more than surprised. He was frightened too. Some memories were best not reexperienced.

“Is there a problem with that?”

“No,” Hermann said. Though he could hear the hesitancy in his voice.

“There isn’t much furniture, and it’s a mess. There will also be a guard at your door whenever you’re there. But it was either that or a cot in the office.”

Klaus’s words raised Hermann’s surprise. He was surprised by the kindness in them.

Unsure of how to feel about this, Hermann reminded himself that Klaus was his enemy, that he needed to hate him. He pretended the kindness away.

The two exited the car. Hermann followed Klaus to an apartment building across the street from the synagogue.

Klaus pointed at a wall. “The policeman mentioned in the report found Major Schiller over there.”

Hermann examined the area.

“Do you want to question the policeman?”

“I’ve never met this Hrubý fellow. But I’ve met plenty like him. Attention to detail is not their best quality.”

Hermann squinted at a slight discoloration on the wall. He walked over to it and the collection of black fiber bits along the wall’s coarse surface.

“What is it?” Klaus asked.

Hermann pulled a fiber from the wall. He showed it to Klaus. “It’s probably from Major Schiller’s jacket.”

“Does it have significance?”

Hermann measured the distance between the pattern and the ground with his eyes. “It would appear that the killer carried the major to the wall and slid him down it.”

“So?”

Hermann responded by asking his own question. “Why would he do this unless he murdered him elsewhere and brought him here?”

Klaus had no answer.

Hermann touched the discoloration. “From the height of this, I can say that the killer is tall. Somewhere between 185 and 190 centimeters. It would also appear that he is right-handed.”

“How did you figure that out?”

“A right-handed man would likely carry the torso in his right arm and the legs in his left. It would explain why the fibers are thickest on the pattern’s right side.”

Klaus slung up his hands. “How does this help us discover who the killer is?”

“There’s seldom a ‘eureka’ moment, Klaus, when you discover the full breadth of a mysterious crime. It’s usually a progression of minor clues that—when summed together—lead you to the perpetrator. Now we have our first ones.”

Klaus bent his head down the street. “If there are no others, the major’s widow lives on Staromák.”

Klaus’s last word humored Hermann. It was funny that he had used the Czech slang term for Staroměstské Náměstí. Old Town Square in English. While Hermann had often heard this and similar forms of slang in the years he’d lived in Prague, it was odd hearing it from an SS man.

The oddness didn’t last. Hermann’s college days with Klaus roared back. During these, all that was Czech consumed Klaus. Regardless of how he would insist otherwise. As someone who’d profess love only for what was German. The Czech language, along with its idioms and peculiarities, consumed Klaus the greatest. To the extent that Hermann had believed Klaus wanted to be Czech and that he had hidden this from everyone. Including from himself.

The two men headed back to the Mercedes. With Hermann reflecting on the times that Klaus had wrestled in college. He would recall the fabled Czech hero Bivoj. In that he would lift his opponents on his back. Like Bivoj had with a boar on Jackdaw Mountain.

The glee from these moments hadn’t left Hermann on returning to the car or on its taking off. Along with it, it struck him that hating Klaus wasn’t going to be easy. No matter how he reminded himself to do so.

They made a sudden stop. At the intersection of Široká Street.

The driver had hoped to turn right before taking another right onto Pařížská Street a block up. Streets from Old Town Square. Instead, he had to turn left onto the one-way street.

The three got tangled in a maze of roads. Roads that some say had been designed to make foreign invaders lose their way. These same streets would confuse the Soviet Army two and a half decades later when they invaded the city. Which heightened after the locals removed street signs.

After my grandpa read about this in a book by Alan Levy called Rowboat to Prague, the happiness overwhelmed him. A stranger would’ve been certain that it was Christmas at our house.

 

 

BACK IN THE Mercedes, the three men were approaching the river to the west. Rather than going south toward the square. They might’ve driven right into the water if Hermann hadn’t guided the driver to what was at the time Sanytrová Street.

This sent them eastward through the maze. They reached Pařížská and could make the right toward the square.

“How am I supposed to investigate a crime when I literally can’t drive two blocks without getting lost?” Klaus growled at no one specific.

“I’m sorry, Herr Captain,” the driver babbled, “I was sure that I could make a right onto Široká.”

Hermann leaned up front. “You can down half of it. But only west of Žatecká Street.”

The driver said nothing.

Hermann slid toward Klaus. “You knew these streets as well as I do.”

Klaus drifted from him. “That was years ago.”

“Remember the night when we got so drunk at U Hynků that we set off for that legendary street from which there was no exit?”

A hint of humor pinched Klaus’s face. “Ulice Prokletých. The Street of the Damned.”

Along with the humor came a spark. The old Klaus had revealed himself.

The spark made Hermann want more. A lot more. He didn’t try to pretend otherwise.

Klaus’s face continued to spark. “We would’ve found the street if the sun hadn’t come up. We were so close to it. I have no doubt about it.”

The man’s spark didn’t last. It extinguished as the car parked above the square.

The two started over the ancient cobblestones. Dragging their feet, they headed toward the three-story apartment building where Frau Schiller lived, alongside tracks that carried trams across the square during that period. They did so with Klaus’s head down and a scowl on his face. In the same manner, they entered the building. They went up to the top floor. At the first unit by the staircase, Klaus knocked on the door.

Answering it was a servant woman. She brought them to the lady of the house, who was preparing to move. Boxes were lying in each shred of free space.

The interruption didn’t please the woman. Hermann’s presence displeased her greater. She insisted to Klaus that she had no time to talk.

“Please, Frau Schiller. We are trying to catch your husband’s murderer. We’ll take only a few minutes. I promise you.”

The woman grunted. But she acquiesced. She asked her servant to bring them coffee, and she led the two into the living room.

They sat on a sofa by a bay window. It overlooked the square and the statue of Jan Hus.

Hermann swayed in its direction. He recollected a different and better occasion.

“When will you be moving?” Klaus asked the woman.

She glared at Hermann. “In a few days. To be honest, a few seconds more in this city is too much.”

The woman kept up her glare.

Breaking it was the servant. She left a silver service on the coffee table.

Frau Schiller grunted a second time. While pretending that Hermann wasn’t there, she served what passed as coffee during the war to both Klaus and herself.

This brought Hermann’s focus from the square to her. “I read your statement, Frau Schiller. But, in my experience, people often leave out minor but not insignificant details. So I do hope that you don’t mind going through the events another time. Can you describe for me the night your husband was killed?”

It was clear that the woman had no intention of responding to Hermann. Or recognizing that he was there. But with Klaus waiting for her to answer, she said to him, “We were at the Theater of the Estates to watch Don Giovanni.”

“What a marvelous opera,” Hermann cooed, harking back to when he and Ana had seen it at the same theater. “Was Ezio Pinza performing the title role?”

The woman ignored the question. Her sight remained on Klaus. “Right before the performance, a lieutenant hurried up to my husband. He led him off on what he said was an urgent matter.”

“He didn’t mention what this matter was?” Hermann asked.

She swung her arms across herself. “No.”

“And you don’t know this lieutenant’s name?”

“No.”

“Or what he looks like?”

“Only vaguely,” she snapped. “I wasn’t paying attention. The man was . . . average. Average height, average weight. Unimposing, I would say as well. Like you. He was young too. But they’re all young.”

To Hermann, these details were too detailed for a passing glance from someone who wasn’t paying attention. “Are you sure that you never saw him before that night?”

Unlike with the other questions, Frau Schiller flinched at this one. A silence overtook her.

This broke. “Maybe.”

“You’re not sure?”

“I may have seen him before.”

Her answer stunned Klaus. It did so more than the question had stunned the woman. It embarrassed him too. “You didn’t tell my men that.”

“Your men didn’t ask me that. They asked me if I knew who he was, and I don’t.”

Klaus was about to respond.

But Hermann interrupted him. “Where did you see him before?”

Further stillness ensued.

The woman shook her head. “It could have been anywhere.”

“I realize that you have a lot on your mind. But if you could concentrate. This information could lead us to your husband’s killer. It may be the grand total of what can.”

The woman closed her eyes. They stayed that way awhile.

With abruptness, she opened them. “It was at a party. A Christmas party. At Colonel Mueller’s home. Yes, it must’ve been there. We went by the man on the way out and . . . he tipped his head at my husband.”

Hermann inched closer to her. “Like he knew him?”

“Yes.”

“Tell me, when did your husband leave the theater?”

The woman tossed up her wrists. “I don’t know. I had no reason to look at my watch or at a clock. But as I told you, it was right before the opera started.”

“8:30,” Klaus interjected. “It started at 8:30.”

“You sure about that?” Hermann asked.

“I saw the opera a week earlier. And, no, Ezio Pinza doesn’t perform the title role. The Americans arrested him last year.”

“What a pity,” Hermann said.

He returned to the woman. “Frau Schiller, did your husband have enemies?”

For the first time since the conversation had started, the woman put her eyes on Hermann. She sneered at him as well. “You.”

“You mean that he didn’t like Jews?”

“That’s exactly what I mean.”

“What I mean is someone who had a personal reason to cause him harm.”

Frau Schiller whipped herself back to Klaus. “There were people who feared my husband. But no one would dare cause him harm. He was the strongest man alive and feared nothing. He didn’t fear going to the front. Eastern or otherwise.”

Hermann had no more questions. So Klaus said goodbye to the woman.

The two men left the apartment and the building. They headed toward the car.

“I’ll contact Colonel Mueller when we get back to the palace,” Klaus said over the two crossing the cobblestones, exhausting both men past what they were. “To find out if he can identify this average and unimposing lieutenant. At the least, he should be able to give us the names of everyone at the party.”

Hermann yawned. “It would be interesting to know if the other murdered men attended the party.”

“I’ll find out.”

Klaus yawned himself. He stretched his arms. “When we get back, I’ll arrange for some guards for you and transportation to your flat. We can visit the second murder scene in the morning.”

Hermann no longer felt tired. He was afraid.
