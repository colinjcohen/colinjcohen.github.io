chapter thirty-two: when a chicken scratches

 

 

HERMANN AND KLAUS trudged down the hallway. Hermann was gauging his former friend, deducing what could be on his mind.

He drew a blank. Klaus had put up a wall. Not only between them. But between himself and the world.

Hermann’s eyes wandered. “I hope you won’t hold what Marta said against her.”

“I can’t imagine what you mean,” was Klaus’s matter-of-fact response. “I heard nothing. But she might want to be more careful about what she says in the future. Because next time, someone may be listening.”

Hermann stepped into his office with Klaus.

Klaus closed the door. “How do you want to proceed today?”

Hermann asked his own question: “Do you believe Colonel Reidl’s story from last night?”

“Some truth was in it. But not all of it was truth. Or most. For example, he had no reason for showing up there. If Kleiner had needed assistance, he could’ve sent a whole division. Could he be the killer?”

“He would’ve had as much trouble carrying those men as Lieutenant Maier. If not greater trouble. Also, he had no mud on his uniform, and he’s not tall enough to be the killer either. What’s more, from the look on his face when we arrived, I’d say he was afraid and not of us.”

The two sat across from each other beside the desk. Hermann dipped toward Klaus. “But he’s involved in this. At least tangentially. He and Captain Kleiner must’ve framed Maier. This can’t be without a reason. As Czechs say, ‘ani kuře zadarmo nehrabe.’”

Not even a chicken scratches for nothing.

This was a proverb that a veteran detective had taught Hermann on his first case. A proverb that he applied to a slew of cases that came after it. That no act, criminal or otherwise, is random. Behind each is a motive. If you can uncover what this is, you’ll uncover other sorts of facts.

“How do we discover why this particular chicken was scratching?” Klaus asked.

“Do you have access to the murdered men’s personnel files?”

Klaus rose to his feet. He left the room.

 

 

KLAUS RETURNED.

He dropped four folders onto the desk in front of Hermann, who opened each side by side.

“What are you hunting for?” Klaus asked.

“We need to connect the men. Perhaps connect them with Colonel Reidl too. By the way, was Reidl at Colonel Mueller’s party?”

“He was. He mentioned it when I had first brought the subject up. But he insisted that he didn’t recall meeting Schiller or Fischer at the party. Or at any other time. He said that there were numerous people at the party. Mueller’s guest list backs that up.”

“But he did have the opportunity to meet and talk with both. Now what do these files tell us?”

Klaus examined them. “If there’s a connection between them, it’s not in plain sight.”

“If solving murders were as easy as looking in plain sight, no unsolved ones would persist. Let’s start with the basic facts about the men. We’ll drill down from there.”

Klaus emptied the folders’ contents onto the desk. He spread them across it. “Which basic facts do you want to start with?”

“How about where they were from?”

Klaus sifted through the material. “They, they were from all over. Schiller was from Danzig, for instance. And Fischer, he was from Königsberg. Gruber was from Essen. Kleiner grew up close to me in Tetschen.”

“Had they served together? Or trained with one another?”

Klaus scanned through the data. He shook his head.

“Did they have any common traits?”

Klaus went back to his digging. “As I told you before, they were well-trained and capable soldiers. Apart from Kleiner. They also had significant combat experience other than Kleiner, and the three received medals for valor. Though nothing extraordinary. Nothing like you.”

Hermann ignored the last remark. “Did they have any special skills?”

Klaus gleaned more facts. “Captain Gruber had some interesting skills. He was an expert in Jewish studies. He spoke Hebrew.”

“He could’ve been the one reading that Kabbalah book in the Spanish Synagogue. He could’ve brought it there, believing that a golem was after him.”

Klaus continued through the files. “One more thing. Other than Kleiner, the men had disciplinary problems. Nothing extraordinary, though.”

“So, other than Kleiner, they were competent and brave. But not too brave. And they didn’t walk what your version of the Bible calls the ‘straight and narrow.’”

“What are you getting at?”

“If you were recruiting a group of men to commit a crime . . .”

“These men would be suitable. But what crime?”

“That is for us to discern. Can we search through the men’s belongings? Just as importantly, may we?”

“We both can and may. But Schiller’s wife has probably already left town. And my understanding is that Kleiner’s mother is on her way to Prague. To pick up his belongings. I further hear that she makes her son agreeable in comparison.”

“He was the exception.”

“That leaves Gruber and Fischer.”

Hermann collected the two men’s files.

The clock on the wall caught his eyes. It was approaching nine. “But first there’s a lawyer we need to visit.”
