chapter fifteen: the stomping feet

 

 

HERMANN AND KLAUS returned to the Maisel Synagogue.

The gate was closed. It was locked too.

Klaus spied Hermann. In a manner that intimated that he was angry over what had happened outside the palace. “I could get someone to open it.”

Hermann gripped the gate’s bars. He peered into the building’s darkness. He shook his head. “I’d rather speak to Mr. Hrabal.”

The two returned to the car. It drove off.

While Hermann munched on the crescent rolls that Kamča had baked—ones so good that he had to keep himself from fainting—he guided the driver.

He had to guide him well to the east of where they were going. By driving down Dlouhá and Kozí streets. Before going back around Vězeňská.

On top of this, the two had to walk through a courtyard that led to the apartment building beyond it.

Hermann felt the shiver of Klaus’s continued hostility. “I only accepted the invite to avoid a scene. You can make an excuse for me tonight.”

Klaus scowled. “She’d reschedule it. She gets what she wants.”

“I apologize.”

“Don’t make believe that you’re sorry,” Klaus snapped. “Or that you’ve ever been sorry.”

It was clear that Klaus regretted his outburst. It was clear, too, that its source had come not from what had happened earlier. But from what had happened well before it. In the gardens below the castle.

Hermann lowered his eyes. “You’re wrong. I’m sorry for a lot of what I’ve done. I’m sorry more for what I haven’t. I’m sorry for ‘whats’ that I can never apologize for.”

The quarrel ended. The two reached Dušní Street.

They hustled into a five-story apartment building across from the synagogue. They hustled up to the fourth floor.

On a door down the corridor, Klaus knocked.

An elderly man greeted the two in Czech. With friendliness.

Klaus responded in the same language. With perfect fluency.

He told the man what they were doing there. What they wanted from him.

The man grabbed the two by the arms. He dragged them into his flat. To a window above the synagogue.

He showed them where he had discovered the body.

“Pane Hrabale,” Hermann began in Czech.

His mind fumbled for the right words to add after it. His voice pleaded for a confidence that wasn’t in it.

Hermann had lived in Prague for twenty years with a native Czech speaker. He could follow the language—both written and spoken—without much problem. But he was never able to turn himself into a fluent speaker. Despite how he loved Czech. How he enjoyed speaking it and peppering his sentences with it.

He did the latter so often when I was growing up that I would repeat the words in school and among others. Not getting that no one would get them.

To this day, I have to fight the urge to use several. They sound better than their English equivalents.

Why say “bad weather” when you can say “slota”? Why say “careful!” when you can say “pozor!”?

The words suggest their meaning.

From my grandpa, who was the least likeliest of ambassadors for the Czech language, I learned not only it but how beautiful it is.

It’s a beauty that brightens the darkest of my days. As I recall the tales that Grandpa would read to me from the threshold of my room when I was a child. The tales of Božena Němcová and the Brothers Čapek and Ota Pavel.

Before I could get a grasp of what these tales meant, their sounds would send me drifting to a blissful sleep.

They had me drifting in that rain. To a sleep that I might not have drifted out of.

Part of the language’s beauty comes from the musical quality it possesses. Which never ceased to cause wonder in Hermann and me.

Czechs sing when they talk. Not aware that they’re singing. Grandpa had told me that he’d been to operas that weren’t as melodious as an everyday talk between two random Czechs. The banal exchange between Klaus and Mr. Hrabal sounded musical to his ears.

The problem Hermann had with speaking Czech well was that it had been too easy getting by with German in Prague. With Ana it had been the easiest.

At times like the one in Hrabal’s apartment, he regretted how easy it had been. He didn’t want Klaus to have to translate for him.

“I can speak German,” Hrabal said in this language. Over a dog barking in the next room.

The man pointed to a spot through the window. “He was sitting there. The Nazi officer. Against the shrubs.”

“You discovered him while walking your dog,” Hermann noted.

“That’s right.”

“Was this when you left the building? Or when you returned?”

“It was when I came back.”

“You’re sure that he wasn’t out there when you left?”

“I’m sure of it. I would’ve noticed him from the building’s front door. Pepin would’ve too. That’s my dog.”

“You said that this happened at ten. Are you certain of this too?”

“I’m certain of it. I take Pepin out each night at ten. Or within minutes of it. We go after the nightly news. Or what passes for it.”

Hrabal must’ve remembered who was in the room with him. He spun toward Klaus. “Of course, I don’t listen to those horrible BBC broadcasts.”

Klaus smirked. While Hrabal shot Hermann a furtive wink.

Hermann had to hold back his own smirk. “Had you ever run into the dead man before?”

“Never.”

“You never saw him in passing?”

“Never in passing.”

“Do you remember where you went that night?”

Hrabal waved out the window. “Where I go each night. Around the block.”

Twirling in a circle, he showed him his path. “Down Dušní. Around U Staré Školy. Onto Vězeňská. Before coming back up Dušní.”

It wasn’t difficult for Hermann to deduce that the man’s route was a simple and compact rectangle. “You didn’t hear sounds along the way?”

“Like what?”

“A fight. Or a racket. Some disturbance.”

“I didn’t hear any sounds like that. But my hearing isn’t what it used to be. It surprises me that I can hear those stomping feet.”

“Stomping feet?”

“Each night I hear them.”

“For how long has this been going on?”

“Months now.”

“Who’s behind them?”

“That I can’t tell you. But he must be somewhere nearby.”

“Did you hear these stomping feet on the night of the murder?”

“I did.”

“On your way from the building? Or on your way back?”

“Strictly speaking, from the moment that I step outside my building, I’m on my way back.”

“Was it during the first half of your walk? Or the second?”

“I’d say that it was in the middle.”

Hermann had no more to ask. So he stepped toward the door.

But another question came to him. “Have you spotted other oddities of late?”

“I did spot this woman a few nights after I discovered the body. As Pepin and I were leaving the building, she was walking into that shuttered synagogue over there.”

“Can you describe her?”

“All I can tell you is that she was wearing a yellow shawl.”

Now Hermann had run out of questions.

The two exited the apartment, much to the chagrin of Mr. Hrabal.

They went down the staircase. They exited the front door.

Klaus turned to Hermann. “What do you make of those ‘stomping feet’?”

“I can’t say. What I can is that, like the earlier murder, this didn’t take place where you discovered the victim.”

“He could’ve not heard it. He did say that his hearing is bad.”

“He had no trouble hearing those ‘stomping feet.’”

“They could have been loud.”

“So would a murder, most likely. Besides, he had no trouble hearing me, and I wasn’t speaking loudly. The dog, also, would’ve alerted him to the unusual. He barked the moment he heard my bad Czech.”

“So where was Captain Gruber killed?”

“That I can’t answer yet.”

The two stopped by the shrubbery.

Hermann lobbed his head at it. “This was about where Gruber was found.”

“About.”

“With his back against the bushes?”

“Yes.”

Hermann inspected the area. In and around the bushes. As well as along a cobblestone path that was behind them. It ran parallel to them.

All of it was ordinary.

No footprints. No broken branches. No sign that someone had trampled through the area.

The scene was devoid of clues.

This deepened Hermann’s speculating. “Tell me . . . were you here when the body was found?”

“I got here a half hour later.”

“Had the body been moved?”

“I don’t believe so.”

“Was it sitting straight ahead? Or was it at an angle?”

Klaus groaned. “What difference does it make?”

“Humor me.”

“It, it was at an angle.”

“What kind of angle?”

Klaus groaned another time. But he motioned his right arm 45 degrees across his body. “He was facing us in this direction.”

Hermann breathed the facts in. “As we’ve established that the killer is right-handed, we can assume that he didn’t lay the body down from this side of the shrubs. Or the victim would’ve been facing the left of us. This means that he must’ve come from the path. He must’ve laid the body down from there.”

Both men inclined toward the path.

It headed to the Spanish Synagogue.

Without saying a word, they headed toward it.
