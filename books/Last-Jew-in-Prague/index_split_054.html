chapter fifty-four: limitless unknowns and no equations

 

 

IN THE DARKNESS, Hermann focused the flashlight’s beam onto the arched door’s handle.

He unlocked and opened it. This exposed Klaus.

The two hurried down the stairs. To a cellar like the one in the Pinkas Synagogue. It was packed with supplies and had the same flat, deep, and immense footprints in the dirt floor that had been at the Pinkas Synagogue. These, too, led to a stone wall upon which someone had painted a Star of David. Leading to it as well were wheel marks and two pairs of normal footprints.

“This was where our friends went,” Hermann said.

He and Klaus stopped at the wall. Klaus gave it a once-over. “Another dead end?”

“There must be something behind it. Like there must’ve been something behind the wall in the Pinkas Synagogue cellar.”

Hermann felt along the stones. He pushed and slid the wall in many places. But he couldn’t get it to stir.

Klaus pounded his fist against the wall. “It’ll take more than a battering ram to bring this down.”

Hermann’s pushing and sliding went on. “There must be an easier way.”

But he couldn’t figure one out. So he sought and sought it. Certain that the only path to solving the mystery lay through the wall. It was also the only means for him to reach wherever fate had been directing him.

The minutes passed. The hours followed. But Hermann didn’t care. There was nothing but the wall and what it represented.

Klaus didn’t have the same problem. He cared too much about time. He made Hermann alert to this.

Hermann ignored him. So obsessed was he with the puzzle in front of him. With its limitless unknowns and lack of equations.

Though this obsession had limits. He became compelled to give up.

He couldn’t voice this. Methodical and heavy footsteps descending a wall grabbed his attention.

“What’s that?” Klaus wheezed.

Hermann opened his mouth. As the sound changed. To that of someone descending a wooden ladder. “There are metal rungs on the synagogue’s back wall. They used to run up the entire wall. But now start three meters from the ground.”

“What happened to the others?”

“They removed them.”

“Why?” Klaus asked, fear scratching his voice.

Hermann gave him an accentuated grunt. “Because, because people kept climbing up them. To break into the attic. To see if there really was a golem hidden inside it.”

“And that’s where he’s coming from. The attic.”

“It’s a legend, Klaus.”

“So how come we didn’t hear any footsteps going up?”

“That’s a good question. There’s one way to answer it.”

Hermann started toward the stairs. But he stopped when Klaus didn’t follow. He shined the flashlight onto him and his fright.

Grunting a second time, Hermann went back for Klaus. He snatched his arm. He dragged him up the stairs. Reaching the ground floor, he dragged him outside into the evening’s darkness.

Stomping feet echoed in the distance. The same kind from the night before.

They thinned out.

The two ran to the building’s back and to the ladder, the metal rungs, and the closed attic door.

Hermann directed the flashlight at the door. He ascended the ladder.

But he stopped when Klaus didn’t follow. His exasperation multiplied.

“I’ll wait here,” Klaus slurred, more afraid than ever.

Hermann rolled his eyes. He climbed the remaining ladder steps as well as the metal rungs. He reached the attic door.

He gripped the handle. Unlocked, he opened the door and lurched inside.

Moving the light across the room, he discovered that it was uninteresting apart from the floor. In addition to immense footprints of dried mud in the same shape as those in the cellars were slippers.
