chapter forty-eight: the kindness of his smile

 

 

IVO LOCKED THE Pinkas Synagogue’s gate. He placed the keys in his coat pocket next to the other set. The one that he had taken from the body of Captain Kleiner the night before.

With slow and steady steps, he made his way onto Široká Street.

Klara was strolling toward him from the direction of the river. She stopped on sight of him. At the corner of Valentinská Street. She smiled.

He smiled back. Which increased with her shuffling to him.

“Good evening,” he said while placing his hands behind his back.

“Good evening to you as well.”

“Have you been visiting the synagogues?”

“I was about to. I was at the river. It’s lovely there. The moon’s reflecting off the water.”

“Which synagogues do you plan on visiting tonight?”

Klara drifted to the building in front of her. “Oh, I don’t know. This is my favorite. I come here often. I visit the High Synagogue often as well. But tonight I’ll visit the Maisel too. It’s on my way home.”

“I cleaned the walls there this morning.”

“I’ve noticed how clean the buildings are. Both inside and out. You do an impressive job. A lesser man might’ve slacked off. With no one to check your work.”

“Someone’s always checking.”

“Who?”

Ivo lifted his head. He lifted it as far as it would go.

Returning to Klara, he said, “Besides, the buildings are so lonely these days. They need love like everyone else. I try to give it to them.”

She pointed to his feet. “I like your shoes.”

“Thank you.”

“I’ve never seen a pair like them.”

“Dr. Brod made them for me.”

“You weren’t wearing them last night.”

“I had to work last night, and I don’t wear them when I work. I don’t want to get them dirty or hurt.”

“So what are you doing now?”

“I don’t know.”

Klara laughed.

Ivo believed that she was laughing at him. He lowered his eyes. “I’m afraid that I’m not a very smart man.”

“I wouldn’t say that.”

He raised himself toward her. “You wouldn’t have company. Other than that Jewish policeman.”

“Jewish policeman?”

“He’s investigating the killings at the synagogues.”

Klara staggered backward. “There have been killings at the synagogues?”

“Many.”

“How awful.”

“Don’t feel sad. The people who died in them were not nice men.”

“Still, I wouldn’t wish them harm. I wouldn’t wish harm to the men . . .”

“What men?”

“It doesn’t matter.”

“You’re a nice person if you can feel sad for bad people. You’re like the man in the book Mrs. Mlýnková is reading to me.”

“What man is that?”

“The fellow who keeps forgiving people. No matter what terrible they do.”

“That’s kind of you to say.”

“Would you like me to walk you to the Maisel?”

“Actually,” she stuttered, with a fear that showed itself from nowhere, “I, I’m very tired. I’ll probably peep at the building and go home.”

“All right. Goodnight.”

Klara started toward Maisel Street. “Goodnight.”

With suddenness, she stopped. She turned back to Ivo and the kindness of his smile. “Tomorrow night we could visit the synagogues. We could take a walk too. To the river.”

“I’d like that,” he murmured to the woman who would become his wife for the next fifty years. “I’ll be here. If not at this time, then a mite later.”
