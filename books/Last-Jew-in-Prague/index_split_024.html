chapter twenty-four: something like a drug

 

 

HERMANN TOOK A step into his old apartment.

With it came hesitancy, tinged by a fear that he was letting Ana down. By not doing enough to survive. He now supposed that this had caused the disappointment he’d felt in Colonel Reidl’s office.

Could he convince Reidl that Maier wasn’t the killer?

He couldn’t answer this. He continued into the room.

The guard closed the door.

Another sound resonated. Of an item slipping under the door.

A folded pink slip of paper.

Hermann sped to it. He grabbed and opened it.

It was the deportation order that he had received to Theresienstadt.

“What is it?” Ana asked.

He revolved toward her. She was in her rocking chair, brooding over her paintings.

This had happened during a difficult period of their marriage. With nothing to do and nowhere to go during his last years in Prague, Hermann spent most of his time in the apartment with Ana. This led to unavoidable tension as the weeks and the months of his confinement expanded. On top of this was the constant fear from the sword hanging over their heads.

A sword that was about to drop.

Hermann crumpled the page. He put it behind his back. “It’s nothing. A stupid ad.”

She whirled toward him. From her fright, Hermann could tell that she didn’t believe him.

But I bet she wanted to believe. Because, based on what Věra had told me, the alternative would’ve been unbearable.

“Hermann had become something like a drug to Ana,” the woman said in the restaurant on top of her building. “During the occupation, whenever he was away from their apartment for the briefest of periods, she’d go crazy with worry over what could happen to him. She couldn’t think straight. She couldn’t control her actions.”

Hermann couldn’t avoid the worry on Ana’s face that night in their apartment. Or how her upper lip quivered.

She sought to allay her fears. By asking him in Czech, “Přísaháš?”

Hermann couldn’t swear. He couldn’t do so in the slightest.

He scurried off. With a single notion pounding the insides of his head. He wouldn’t tell her the truth. He convinced himself that it would be better if she were ignorant of it. Though he’d admit that it was only better for him.

His shame from this sent him scurrying more. He scurried to a closet. He hid the deportation order.

Faltering into the bedroom, he found that he was back in the present tense. His featherbed was on the floor. The bare pillow was on top of it.

He hollowed out. He collapsed onto the bed. He grabbed the pillow while willing himself toward a memory that could bring him happiness.

But Hermann had no control over what he relived. He was back on the night that he had met Ana.

 

 

IT WAS LATE. Hermann was dragging a drunken Klaus home. Ana was paces behind them.

Hermann couldn’t figure out why she was hanging around with them. As he had become exhausted by them.

Klaus, foremost, had exhausted him. He’d been getting more ornery and unpleasant with each beer that passed through him. He’d been getting more obnoxious.

The latter’s climax was when he slurred the opening verse of a popular Czech folk song about a town not far away. A song called “Kolíne, Kolíne.” Which would have a lot of meaning to me. My grandpa taught it to me when I was a boy because the town’s name is close to my own.

 

Kolíne, Kolíne,

Stojíš v pěkné rovině.

Šenkuje tam má milá,

Má panenka rozmilá,

Šenkuje tam ve víně.

 

I, too, was slurring the song. On that freezing desert night. As the rain fell harder and the water began swallowing me. I wanted it to. I prayed for it. I felt my sleeping bag filling out.

Yet the water didn’t swallow me. Some inexplicable, rising with the story, was preventing it.

Klaus didn’t bother with the song’s second verse. He leaned against Hermann. He raised his fist. He screamed the final line to the entire city, “Kolínečku, Kolíne!”

Ana laughed. “You sing that better than Czechs do. Where did you pick it up?”

“I can’t say,” Klaus sputtered, losing his balance. “I’ve never been to Kolín. The song flew into my head. It started singing itself.”

Hermann struggled to haul Klaus forward. “I should’ve guessed with a name like Stamm you’d be a drunkard.”

“In the army,” Klaus said, saluting the sky, “they called me Corporal Stammgast.”

Ana laughed. Louder than before. “That word belongs to the teensy set of whatnots Czechs and Germans can agree on. We spell štamgast differently. But it means the same: a good-for-nothing drunk, wasting his life at the same pub each night like the stump of a tree.”

Klaus shook his fists. “It’s the same word because you copied it from us. You people are incapable of an original idea. Like you say ‘brýle’ and ‘makléř’ and ‘kšeft.’ All German in their origin.”

“If you hold Czechs in such contempt,” Hermann interjected, “why do you speak their language so well?”

“Know thine enemy,” Klaus screeched. “Know thine enemy!”

Ana smirked. “Is that what you think of me, Klaus?”

“I’m going to turn you into a German, my dear.”

“Ježišmarjá! As if I didn’t have enough problems.”

Klaus didn’t respond in words. He toppled onto the sidewalk. In defiance of Hermann’s efforts to keep him upright.

Ana tossed her hands onto her hips. “It’s time for me to go home.”

Hermann could read beneath her words. She didn’t want their night to end any more than he did.

“Let me walk you home,” he said while registering that one of Klaus’s shirt buttons was in his hand. “It’ll take me but a minute to get him into bed. Well, it’ll take me longer than that. But you can wait in the lobby.”

She gave him a dirty look. “I can get home on my own. I’ll have you know that I was schooled by Eliška Krásnohorská herself.”

“Who’s Eliška Krásnohorská?”

“Among this country’s greatest writers. And a feminist.”

“Whh, what’s a feminist?”

“Someone who believes women are equal to men. In every respect.”

“Then I’m a feminist too.”

“Men can’t be feminists!”

“What about President Masaryk? I remember reading an article that he wrote in Naše doba. In which he was speaking much like you. And didn’t he take his wife’s last name when he married her?”

“What I was saying is that I don’t need someone to walk me home.”

“I know that. But Klaus would be angry with me if he learned that I let you walk home alone. That’s all.”

“That’s all?” she cried with her arms crossed and her back to Hermann. “That’s the only reason you want to walk me home, Herr Weiss?”

He didn’t answer.

She edged toward him. Their eyes met. His fell upon hers with such depth that he lost himself and his train of thought. Though he was able to shake his head.

They both smiled.

A sound interrupted it. A knocking sound.

Hermann wanted to ignore it. He wanted to remain in his memory. Or whatever it was.

The sound was persistent. It returned him to his bedroom. He could tell that it was from his front door.

Louder it got and frantic.

He tumbled off his bed. He tumbled onto his feet and from the bedroom.

Rain was falling outside a window. A lot of it.

“Slota,” his voice rasped.

The knocking went on.

He stumbled toward the door. “I’m coming!”

But he didn’t get there. He stopped when what was in his hand jumped out at him.

A shirt button. Klaus’s shirt button.

Hermann reeled. “It, it can’t be.”

He reached into his jacket pocket. With slow and deliberate movements he reached. He took out the old brass compass that the bookshop owner had given him during his re-experience the night before.

The front door swung open.

In the threshold was Klaus. He was wet and more excited than he had been since the two’s reacquaintance. “Captain Kleiner has been murdered!”
