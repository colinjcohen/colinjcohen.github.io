chapter nine: dreaming of knaidels

 

 

IN THE CRAMPED and cluttered windowless room that was now his office, Hermann sat at a wobbly desk and chair across from Klaus. He ate the cold and pickled fist-sized sausages that Marta had brought him.

Utopence, as Czechs call them, were Hermann’s favorite food.

They remained so the rest of his life. Like with his love of Smetana’s “Vltava,” they were an inseparable part of how I remember him.

But they were far from kosher. So my grandma wanted nothing to do with them.

He had to make them himself in the basement of our house. He had to eat them there too.

At times, I would sit on the floor while he measured and prepared them. He had so much excitement in his eyes. The kind reserved for a child.

But it wasn’t the food that excited him. It was his ability to enjoy it.

It was because of my grandpa’s experiences in Theresienstadt that my family placed such value on food. Never did we waste it in our house.

We regarded meals as a source of celebration. They were a miracle that we never took for granted. To the extent that I can remember the most mundane of dinners.

The most memorable of these included my grandma’s knaidels.

According to the dictionary, “knaidel” is the Yiddish word for “matzo ball.” But the knaidels of my youth had no more than a passing resemblance to any matzo balls that I would later have.

The latter were never special. Bland and lifeless dumplings, whether they were in a bowl of chicken soup mattered not.

Knaidels were different. Their richness was unique. They were comforting. They warmed you everywhere at the same time.

In my house, they were the meal’s best part. Its main attraction.

It would take Grandma days to prepare them, using a recipe passed down to her through generations. She would make chicken soup from scratch. She would wait for the fat to rise and gel.

Shmalts, she called it. The key ingredient in what I could only dream about on that cold and rainy desert night and on others like it.

I would dream about them when I was awake. I’d dream about them when I was asleep. I’d dream about them till I could taste them in my mouth.

What made our meals special went beyond food.

Both my grandparents would cook. Watching them was like watching magic shows. Which I would enjoy through the increasing bitterness that I’d feel as a teenager.

My grandparents would glide through the kitchen like characters in a ballet. They would toss plates and ingredients to each other. They’d sing. I couldn’t help get excited from their excitement.

Hermann’s meal at SS headquarters wasn’t as exciting.

Having been starved for eight months, he had believed that he would devour the meal in seconds. He would be desperate for more.

This didn’t happen.

He was full after a single sausage. Stuffed after two.

Angered, he felt cheated by a stomach alien to quantities of food. Much less good food.

Despite his lack of appetite, Hermann enjoyed the meal. He enjoyed the aromas rising from the plate. They brought back his own recollections of meals past.

Similar to my grandma and her knaidels, Ana would spend days preparing utopence. She would use her own special recipe. Adapted from the years that she had spent making them for Hermann.

On countless evenings, he would catch her in her flowered apron, sneaking another item into the pickle jar by the kitchen window.

Peppers. Onions. Ingredients he couldn’t name.

This would stoke his craving. Till he had the chance to taste the food.

Over and over, he would tell me how Ana’s utopence were the best that he ever had. But it wasn’t the taste he loved the most.

He didn’t need to put this into words. I could tell that what mattered most was how he could feel Ana’s love for him in each bite.

Contrary to their coldness, the sausages generated a warmth that never ceased to marvel him.

Because of this, because of all Ana would do for him, Hermann would show his gratitude of her.

By buying her white roses on Fridays. By taking her to the Lucerna on Saturday nights, where they would dance until they couldn’t walk. He also wrote her verse in two languages. Verse that he said was bad. But would put joy on her face.

However, all this diminished as the years went on and his immersion in his police work got stronger.

It got to the point where it stopped happening.

So a question haunted him.

Had she been aware of how he had felt about her? How he had valued her and the time that they had spent together?

Hermann couldn’t answer this.

He could do no more than refill his empty glass with the Velkopopovický Kozel beer from the flower vase, a mix of dark and pale lagers. Řezané, Czechs call it. Meaning cut.

He took a slow sip. He enjoyed each moment of its journey through him. He dismissed its weakness by Czech standards.

They grade beer not by its alcohol percentage. But by its density.

This beer was 8 degrees. It made it about 3 percent alcohol. But it was enough to numb a guilt that derived from multiple sources.

“Don’t get drunk,” Klaus said to Hermann with a glare on his face. Though it was unclear whether he was glaring at him or at the beer.

Hermann scoffed at the man. “You couldn’t get drunk off this.”

Klaus was silent.

Hermann grabbed the round loaf of rye from beside the plate.

He tore off a chunk. He soaked up the pickle juice with it. As well as lots of onions. “Are you going to show me the case’s details?”

“You might want to finish eating.”

Hermann forced himself to both chew and swallow the bread. “Trust me, eight months of Theresienstadt have given me the ability to eat through it all.”

Klaus didn’t respond to this either. He picked up his attaché. He placed it on the table.

He took out a folder. He dropped it in front of Hermann, who opened it to three full-sized photographs. They displayed the dead men’s faces. Each with varying states of horror.

Well before his time at Theresienstadt—well before becoming a policeman—acts of brutality had inured Hermann to these kinds of states.

To worse ones.

With matter-of-factness, he put the images side by side on the desk. He read the police reports beneath them.

“The causes of death were different,” Klaus droned. In disregard for how Hermann could have figured it out himself. Hermann presumed that Klaus was doing so to make himself relevant. In an investigation that was heads over his own. “A broken neck. A strangulation. A blunt blow to the back of the head. And that’s only what killed them. They also had broken limbs and assorted internal injuries. What links them together, besides that they were left near synagogues, is that they were sitting up. Either against a wall or bushes. And as you can tell, they were afraid.”

Hermann tuned Klaus out. He focused on the facts. These began two weeks before. At the end of March.

A Czech policeman named Petr Hrubý, while making his rounds before dawn, blundered upon the body of Major Ernst Schiller. He was sitting against an apartment building across the street from the Maisel Synagogue.

At first, Hrubý believed that the man was drunk. But on tapping the man’s shoulder with his club, he fell to the ground. The policeman learned that he was dead.

The killer had broken Schiller’s neck. But no signs of struggle were at the scene. Nor witnesses.

According to the coroner, Schiller had died earlier than when Hrubý discovered him. Hours before midnight.

The last known person who saw the man alive was his wife Sieglinde. On the night of his death, the two were about to see an opera at the Theater of the Estates when a lieutenant came. He led him away.

Frau Schiller couldn’t identify the lieutenant. She could only provide a basic account of him.

Above that, the major’s whereabouts from the time that he had left the theater to the time that he had shown himself to the policeman were unknown.

Three nights later, the second murder occurred.

At around ten, a Josefov resident named Radoslav Hrabal discovered the body of Captain Franz Gruber against a shrub. This happened while Hrabal was walking his dog near the Moorish-looking synagogue that people had called the Temple on Geist Alley. A place some were now calling the Spanish Synagogue.

The coroner believed that Gruber’s death had occurred at about the time that Hrabal walked into him. Or within hours of it.

Unlike with Major Schiller, no one could relate any of Gruber’s whereabouts that evening.

He left duty at five. No one could say where he went afterward.

Also unlike Schiller, he had no wife. Or girlfriend. Or a lover. He was a loner without friends.

Gruber had died from a blow to the back of the head. As with Schiller, no signs of struggle were at the scene. No witnesses.

The most recent victim—the one who would have the most relevance to me—surfaced a week after Gruber’s murder.

An SS lieutenant named Greta Krieg came across Captain Max Fischer’s body at nine in the morning.

He was squatting outside a building not far from the Klausen Synagogue where the lieutenant worked. She stated that she had seen the man often in the synagogue during the past month. The last was late in the afternoon on the day that he’d died when she left work.

Captain Fischer’s cause of death was strangulation.

Like with the others, no signs of a struggle were at the scene. Or witnesses.

The coroner stated that Fischer had died during the night before. But later than the other two. Near midnight.

No one had seen the man. From the time that the lieutenant left the synagogue in the afternoon. To the time that she showed up in the morning.

Like Gruber, Fischer had no wife or girlfriend. His friends had said that he’d been distant toward them in the past weeks.

 

 

WHAT HERMANN HAD read surprised him.

He raised his head at Klaus. “I can’t imagine these reports are accurate.”

“In what way do you imagine they’re not?” Klaus said. With surprise of his own.

“Some evidence must have been left at scenes of such violent encounters. Some item that the killer left. Some disturbance of the area.”

“I’m telling you that no evidence of a crime whatsoever was at the scenes. But understand that nobody in my office, including me, is a professional detective. And we haven’t allowed the Czech police or others to investigate the scenes.”

“I also can’t believe that you have no witnesses. Three separate murders in the streets of Prague and no one saw them? I realize Josefov has fewer people than it had before the war. But it’s in the city’s center. People are living in it. They’re passing through it all the time.”

“No witnesses have come forward. Other than those mentioned in the reports.”

“May I talk to them?”

Klaus gave weight to the question. “Tactfully.”

“We should start with the first victim’s wife.”

“Frau Schiller?”

“Yes, her.”

“She lives blocks from the crime scene. I can take you to it before we speak with her.”

Hermann wiped his mouth with a napkin. He leapt to his feet. “Let’s go.”

Klaus didn’t follow. Skepticism crept over his face. “Where is this enthusiasm coming from?”

It was Hermann’s turn to give weight to a question. It had caught him off guard.

Was he enthusiastic?

He was.

This confused Hermann. In the same way that it had Klaus.

It made him feel guilty too. The source of this was that, for the first time since that August day a year earlier, grief wasn’t foremost on his mind.

Why? Why was he enthused when earlier he had planned to go through the motions? Or hamper the investigation to the amount that he could?

He answered aloud. He put forward as much honesty as possible. “It could be that I enjoy the thrill of the chase more than I had supposed. Or it could be that I want to shake the killer’s hand. Does it matter?”

Klaus rose to his feet. He started toward the door.
