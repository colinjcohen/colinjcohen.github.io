chapter three: a promise unsaid

 

 

IN A GENDARMERIE closet lit by a single dim bulb, Hermann stood in the mirror hanging on the door. He was putting on the musty, off-black suit Klaus had given him.

It was big. So big was it that it made him a clown.

Not on purpose. No suits his size were in the camp. Or anywhere.

This didn’t make Hermann happier. More than a clown was in the mirror. This had hollow cheeks. Complemented by sunken eyes and skin as coarse as sandpaper. His earlier shave and shower had heightened this. It had made him look more foolish.

He stopped. Both with the suit and the absurdity. His anger rising, he called out Klaus’s name. He believed that the shame he was feeling had made him no longer complicit in what would come next.

Klaus groaned from the door’s other side. “What is it?”

“I’ve changed my mind, I want . . .”

Hermann couldn’t finish. A vision had broken his focus. Of his Ana.

She wasn’t in the mirror. Or in the closet. Nor was she in his mind. As she had been on his way to the gendarmerie. She existed beyond it. In a memory that had come to life.

It was as vivid as the present. It returned him years earlier. To an evening in his old apartment on Eliška Krásnohorská Street in Josefov.

 

 

ANA WAS IN their living room. Along with her unsold paintings.

They had been there since the Reich had closed her art gallery, having judged her work a form of “degenerate art.” Which they judged with inconsistency and when it suited them. Such as when punishing the wife of a prominent Jew.

She was steps from the paintings. She was brooding over them from her rocking chair. She had told Hermann that her whole life’s arc was in her works. From her carefree student days. To her quest for greater meaning as she neared midlife. Right up to the occupation’s despair.

Hermann believed as well that she viewed herself as a piece of art. That would never realize itself. Or leave the slightest of footprints.

This was his fault.

Hermann studied the paintings. He sensed how their subjects had grown darker in recent years. Ana’s early works, while not joyous, had expressed the promise of joy. They’d voiced the hope that went with it. But her newer ones expressed the certainty of regret and ruin.

This was truest of her latest painting.

In it, the buildings in their neighborhood were leaning over each other in the dead of night. About to collapse. Their building was in the center of it.

Hermann shifted his eyes.

A record was playing on the phonograph behind Ana. Zdena Vincíková’s “Smutná neděle.”

Known as “The Hungarian Suicide Song,” in the years since Rezső Seress had composed it, singers had recorded it in many languages. In countries throughout the world.

Some had banned it. Its tale of mad desperation had been the alleged source of a rash of deaths. Including in the English-speaking world, where Billie Holiday had sung a haunting rendering of it called “Gloomy Sunday” that few listeners could forget.

The record hypnotized Hermann. It made him face the window and the escape it offered.

Another sound brought him out of his stupor. An envelope had slid under their front door.

He crept over to it. He picked it up. He opened it.

Inside was an official-looking letter. Along with plenty of stars made from cheap yellow cloth.

He didn’t get the chance to read the letter. Ana grabbed it. She grabbed the stars. She read the letter herself.

Clutching them both in her fist, she collected his coats from the nearby closet. She collected his suit jackets from the bedroom that was steps from it.

“I won’t do it,” he hollered from outside the bedroom.

Balling up the envelope, he flung it onto the floor. “I won’t wear them. V žádném případě!”

Ana didn’t respond to any of this. Including the avowal that he had sworn in Czech. A language that he spoke to her when he couldn’t state in German what he had wanted to say. In the way that he had wanted to say it. Ignoring it in its entirety, she returned from the bedroom with the garments and her sewing kit. Along with a look that muted his fury. The same look that she had given him on the way to the gendarmerie.

She went back to her rocking chair. She began working. She was relieved. She could take her eyes from her misery.

You couldn’t say the same of Hermann. Ana sewing the stars into his clothes brought his fury back.

This was out of character. But not without reason. It was bad enough to suffer the stars’ disgrace. Worse was making her a part of it.

His fury built. He stormed to her.

Snatching the letter from the chair’s armrest, he ripped it to shreds. He snatched his clothes from her too. “I’ll dare them to shoot me. I’ll go to the street right now. I’ll dare them!”

Ana rose from her chair. She shook her head in a slow but continuous manner. This she would do—not when she wanted to display displeasure of his actions—but when she wanted him to do so himself.

He whined. “They’re gonna shoot me anyway.”

With these words, he wanted to cry. But he couldn’t make himself do so.

Ana took back the clothes. “But not now. Until then there’s now.”

She sank back into the rocking chair. She continued her sewing. Her relief transformed into a sentiment that went beyond it.

It was happiness.

Hermann couldn’t figure out why she was happy. Or from where it had come. He would tell me that it was the single mystery of any weight that he could never solve.

 

 

I WOULD LEARN the source of Ana’s joy. It happened after the Iron Curtain fell and I could travel to Prague.

Visiting the National Gallery, among the largest art museums in Central Europe, I stumbled upon a pair of Ana’s paintings in a far-off corner.

In the first, a mass of people were smashing into each other in the streets. They were getting angry. But going nowhere.

The second painting was a self-portrait. She had broken into pieces and bits. She was frantic to arrange them in the right order. Despite failing at this, her resolve wouldn’t.

For hours, I sat by the paintings. I was seeking the cause of Ana’s joy that evening in their apartment. I was seeking, too, what would push her to the choice that she would later make. The choice that would so affect Hermann’s life and the lives of others. Including my own.

I so sat by her imagery that someone from the museum’s staff strutted to me.

He was curious as to why I was interested in such an obscure artist.

I told him.

He pointed at the canvases. “They have an interesting history. Someone smuggled them to Switzerland during the occupation. Hidden in a bridal chest. The Nazis would’ve burned them otherwise. Along with the rest of her work. Such insolence is in them. They’re spitting at what those monsters were about.”

“What can you tell me about her?”

“Nothing. Other than that her talent was in excess. But I could give you a phone number. Of who donated the paintings. Věra Davidová. She’s a benefactor of ours.”

I jumped from my seat. I got the number. I called the woman.

She agreed to meet me the following day. In a restaurant on top of her office building in the city’s New Town district.

I couldn’t wait for this to happen. But on getting to it, I had difficulty focusing. The table overlooked the Vltava River and its surroundings. It provided a view straight from a childhood fable.

“I knew Ana,” the aging woman murmured.

This shook off my daze. “You did?”

“My mother was her patron until . . . until we had to leave the city.”

My eyes swayed to the woman’s forearm. Tattooed on it was the same kind of number that had been on Hermann’s.

I leaned over the table. “Did you know my grandfather?”

“Not really. I knew who he was. Everyone at Theresienstadt did. But he kept to himself. Understandably. And we never approached him. My mother didn’t want him to relive what had happened to Ana.

“I’m trying to better understand Ana. What my grandfather meant to her.”

“She loved him very much. I can tell you that. Whenever she visited us, she talked less about herself and her work than she did of him. It was as if he were her work. And I suppose he was.”

This was why Ana was happy that evening in their apartment. She must’ve caught on that her life’s arc wasn’t in her paintings. They were nothing but figments of it. While the reality was in the man who had been beside her through it. Through him her life had realized itself. She must’ve felt that it would stay this way. If he stayed beside her.

 

 

“YOU MUST PROMISE me,” Ana told Hermann from her rocking chair. “You must promise me that, no matter how impossible it may be, you’ll wait through whatever ‘now’ you have left. If it’s to last seconds more.”

He snarled. “I won’t.”

Further happiness rose in Ana.

This time, Hermann could guess why. She had heard the promise in his voice. He had heard it as well.

 

 

“YOU’VE CHANGED YOUR mind about what?” Klaus howled from the closet door’s other side. Over musical instruments tuning in the courtyard. “I’m not enjoying this any more than you. And I can end it at any time. Say the word.”

The closet and the mirror and Hermann’s current self returned.

With them, he wanted to say that “word.” He opened his mouth to say it.

But returning to the present didn’t mean that Ana had gone. Or that his memory of her had left.

They’d stuck with him. So did the promise that he had made.

Earlier, when he had agreed to Klaus’s demands, he believed that he could change his mind. He could do so whenever he had a nominal excuse.

But this was a lie. He could have no complicity in his death. No matter what excuses he made. Ana had made sure of this. She’d made sure that he would live as much as he could. He’d go after the murderer of Nazis if it meant living for seconds more.

That didn’t mean that he had to like it. So, as he reached for the door, he made himself another promise.
