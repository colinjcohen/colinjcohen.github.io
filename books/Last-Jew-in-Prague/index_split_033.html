chapter thirty-three: a ride in a paternoster

 

 

THE MERCEDES STOPPED beside the Palác Dunaj.

Hermann leaned upfront.

The driver was smiling in the rearview mirror. He had taken the two somewhere without Hermann’s help.

Hermann and Klaus jumped from the car. Onto Národní, an avenue that connects the riverbank with the center of town. It ends a block short of Wenceslas Square. At the heart of this center.

The two marched into the building. They passed the security guard in the foyer.

In the lobby, they came upon a paternoster. A contraption that exists in a handful of places in Prague. As well as in other parts of Europe. It works like this: through a pair of narrow elevator-like shafts runs a continuous chain of narrow doorless cabins, whose light comes from the floors that they go by. Each cabin can hold two to three people. They travel up in one shaft and down in the other, moving at a speed that is slow enough for someone, in theory, to climb into or out of one without killing themselves. It’s called a paternoster because the chain of cars resembles the beads of a rosary, evoking the alternative name of the Lord’s Prayer in Latin.

Riding in a paternoster is disconcerting to some. It comes with the sense that fate is no longer under control. It’s disconcerting to me. Klaus must’ve felt this too. Hermann went to the upward shaft, but Klaus furthered on. “I’ll take the stairs. I’ll meet you there.”

“You’re not afraid of a paternoster, are you?”

It must’ve been clear to Klaus that this was a joke. But he frowned at Hermann. “It’s not fear. They’re too slow.”

“Don’t worry. I won’t tell anyone you were scared.”

Hermann leapt into a cabin.

Klaus climbed on board with him. He must’ve been recalling the Lord’s Prayer. He breathed its words with his eyes clenched closed:

 

Pater noster, qui es in caelis . . .

 

Klaus fast-forwarded to the final verse. Its asking God to “deliver us from evil.”

Hermann was enjoying the man’s fright. “I’ve heard that, if you miss the last floor going up or the first floor going down, the paternoster chops off your head as the cabin moves from one shaft to the other.”

“Nonsense,” Klaus mumbled.

But his eyes opened. They widened. They drifted toward the ceiling.

Hermann wanted to laugh.

But he couldn’t. He and what was around him changed into the paternoster that he and Ana had ridden up in at City Hall on their wedding day. He was back in the suit that Mr. Jacobs had made for him. She was back in her wedding dress, gripping her white rose bouquet.

 

 

LIKE CHESHIRES, THE two made it to the building’s top floor.

They kept going up. They passed three people in the hallway, whose knowing smiles were on the pair while they waited their turn to get into a downward car.

The couple submerged into darkness. They kissed in their own private parlor.

“You are my alpha and omega,” Hermann mumbled to Ana as the cabin shifted to the opposite shaft. “My beginning and end.”

Hermann, drinking in Ana’s favorite perfume as the cabin descended, became amused. At how a Jew had paraphrased the book of Revelation. At this time, no less. But the words felt right.

Light interrupted their kiss. It streamed into the cabin.

Hermann caught on that he was back in the Palác Dunaj with Klaus, who had fled to the third floor at the first chance he got.

Hermann followed. The two plodded to the last office down the corridor. Hermann knocked on Mr. Malý’s door.

“Dále,” crooned an effete male voice from inside the office.

Hermann opened the door. He and Klaus stepped into an office whose size recalled the closet in the Theresienstadt gendarmerie. Behind a desk crouched a colossal and corpulent balding man. He had shoehorned himself inside the room.

The man greeted the two with warmth. It was as if he had been expecting their visit.

He got to his feet. As best he could.

Hermann tipped his head. “Antonín Malý?”

“That is I.”

The man pointed to two chairs in front of his desk. “Please, gentlemen, have a seat.”

The three sat.

Hermann introduced Klaus and himself. He said to Malý, “It’s our understanding that you’re responsible for the synagogues in Josefov.”

“Your understanding is correct.”

“Can you tell us who retained your services?”

“The Jewish community, of course.”

“Is it safe to assume that the six limited-liability companies you represent each represent the six synagogues in Josefov?”

“You are free to assume whatever you’d like.”

“Do you have a long-standing relationship with the Jewish community?”

“I wouldn’t exactly say that.”

“So why would they engage you for this?”

“You’d have to ask them that.”

“That would be difficult under the circumstances.”

“Yes, it would.”

“It’s also our understanding that last night you gave the synagogue keys to a certain SS captain named Kleiner.”

Sudden defensiveness swept over Malý. “In this, your understanding is not correct. I can’t imagine what you’re talking about.”

“Where are these keys?”

“I misplaced my set a while back.”

“That’s convenient.”

“How so?”

“If you had them in your possession, it would’ve likely meant that you retrieved them from Kleiner’s body after he was murdered.”

Malý gasped. “You’re right. It certainly is convenient that I don’t have those keys.”

“May I ask where you were last night?”

“If this is an official interrogation, I probably should get a lawyer.”

“You are a lawyer.”

“But not a very good one.”

Klaus snarled. “You can either answer our questions here or in the basement of Černín Palace. But in neither case shall you have a lawyer present. Though I can assure you that the questioning would be far more pleasant here.”

“Well, if you put it that way . . . what was the question?”

“Where were you last night?” Hermann asked while struggling to prevent himself from smiling. He was also having a difficult time staying objective. He liked Mr. Malý. But he strove not to show it.

“Home. I was home the entire night. It’s on Celetná, by the way.”

“Can anyone attest to your whereabouts?”

“Not a soul.”

“We believe Captain Kleiner was involved in some conspiracy relating to the synagogues. Can you tell us why he and some other SS men have been interested in the buildings?”

Malý’s defensiveness rose. “Not only can’t I tell you about this so-called conspiracy. But I don’t know this Captain Kleiner. Or any SS captain, for that matter.”

“You deny that you had a relationship with him?”

“I do deny it. Are you sure that it was me he implicated?”

“He didn’t specify a first name, but—”

“—‘Malý’ is not such an unusual surname. I had a client with that name. I almost got him acquitted.”

“The murder occurred on the property of one of the synagogues that you’re responsible for.”

“That doesn’t mean that it was me who committed the murder. I further deny that it was.”

“You do understand that it looks bad for you right now, don’t you?”

“I do understand. I’m not that bad of a lawyer.”

“Captain Stamm has more than enough cause to drag you somewhere from which you’d unlikely return.”

“If that was your intention, gentlemen, you would have done so already.”

“Very well,” Hermann said, rising to his feet with Klaus, who did so with his head askew. “But keep in mind that you are our chief suspect. Actually, you are our only suspect.”

“Sir, under a Nazi occupation, everyone’s suspect. Whether you’re also the chief one or the only one hardly seems to matter.”

Hermann smirked. As much as he didn’t want to.

He and Klaus left the office. The two started down the corridor. They neared the lobby.

Klaus halted. “I should arrest him.”

“No evidence exists that he’s the murderer.”

“Like I told you, Hermann, we don’t need evidence. We need a plausible suspect. He matches the description of the man Kleiner was following last night.”

“If Kleiner had been following Malý, he would have said so to Colonel Reidl.”

“He also matches the killer’s height.”

“Listen, do you want to catch the killer? Or do you want to wait for him to kill a fifth time?”

Klaus didn’t answer. He glowered at Hermann, his arms crossed.

Hermann pointed in the direction of Malý’s office. “Like Ivo, he’s certainly involved in this. But while he’s capable of killing those men and carrying them, and like you said, he’s the right height, I don’t believe that it’s in his nature to commit such brutal crimes. He’s not the type of person who could be driven toward violence. It’s better to let him stay loose. Observe where he takes us. Can you put a tail on him?”

“I doubt Reidl would approve it.”

“He’s an ordinary Czech. Have the local police follow him. Tell them, tell them that he’s involved in black-market activities. Or some crime like that. Tell them whatever you want but the truth. Or they might be inclined to help him.”
