chapter twenty-one: an unlikely turn

 

 

ČERNÍN PALACE WAS about empty when Klaus’s Mercedes parked beside it. Only a few offices in the entire block-long building were lit.

Hermann and Klaus crept from the car. Into the building.

In the same manner, they made their way to the third floor.

They went down a quiet corridor that led to a lit office. With Hermann fraught with unease.

He couldn’t place the source of his unease. But he was nearing more than a room. His fate, too, lay behind the open door ahead.

He pointed at it. “You want me in there with you?”

“If Reidl doesn’t want you there, he won’t be bashful about it.”

The two reached the office. Klaus stormed into it. He saluted the colonel.

The man was at his desk across from Captain Kleiner. The two were smirking at each other. Reidl, in particular, was joyful. He was kicking his stubby feet under the desk.

Hermann turned from this. He stopped at the doorway’s edge. He sought to make himself invisible.

This was futile.

Reidl groaned at Klaus. “At ease, Captain.”

“You wanted to see me, sir.”

The man sneered at Hermann. “I have good news for you. The mystery of the murders has been solved. So you can take the Miracle Jew back to where he belongs.”

This should have brought Hermann, if not happiness, relief. It meant that he would no longer have to participate in what he believed was a farce. That it was a death sentence as well shouldn’t have mattered to him.

But there was no relief in it. It added to his unease a feeling that bordered on disappointment. He believed that it was because he could no longer work on a case that had intrigued him. Though that wasn’t the extent of it.

Klaus also had an unexpected reaction to the news. He was shaken. He gasped at the colonel. “You’ve discovered the killer?”

“I didn’t. Captain Kleiner did.”

“As I told you,” Kleiner said, “all it would take was some persuasion on my part.”

“Who is it?” Klaus yelped. “Who’s the murderer?”

“Lieutenant Maier.”

“Hans Maier?”

“Yes.”

“He confessed?”

“More or less.”

“What does that mean?”

“He confessed that he directed Major Schiller to the Maisel Synagogue on the night of the murder. So we searched his flat. We discovered belongings from the three dead men.”

Klaus shook his head. “Odd.”

Reidl cocked his eyes at the man. “Why do you say that?”

“I would’ve doubted Maier could carry his own weight. Let alone that of much larger men.”

“What makes you believe that he carried anyone?”

“We believe that the killer murdered each man inside synagogues. Before carrying them outside them. We also believe that he’s taller than 185 centimeters. Maier is nowhere near that height.”

The colonel’s face reddened. “You say that you believe this. So I gather you don’t know it as fact.”

“Yes. But the evidence suggests—”

“—‘Suggests’ is not knowing.”

“Yes, but—”

“—But nothing, Captain,” Reidl snapped, jumping in his chair. “And Maier could’ve had an accomplice. Which, if true, Captain Kleiner will learn in short order. The case is closed. And you should be happy. Now you can return to more useful work. You want to be useful to the Reich, don’t you?”

Klaus lowered his gaze. “Of course. But I’d like permission to talk with Maier.”

Reidl slapped his desk. “What for?”

“To be certain of the truth. So I can express it in my final report.”

The colonel gave weight to Klaus’s request. He was conflicted over it. “All right. You can question him. He’s in the basement. But if you cannot offer me proof of his innocence afterward, the case is closed. Is that clear?”

Klaus clicked his heels. He saluted Reidl with reverence.

Reidl waved his arms at him with dismissiveness. “At ease. At ease.”

Klaus obeyed. He started toward the door.

Reidl stopped him. “One more thing.”

Klaus inched toward the man. It took him ages to get there.

“I’ve had enough of this experiment of yours. So, regardless of what you discover downstairs, I don’t want to see that Jew anymore. Do we understand each other?”

Not only did Klaus understand. So did Hermann. He understood that for the moment he had eluded fate. But it was coming back. It was coming fast. It was looking to run him over.
