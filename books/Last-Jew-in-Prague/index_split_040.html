chapter forty: returning to the scene of the crime

 

 

HERMANN AND KLAUS exited the Mercedes outside the Klausen Synagogue. They approached the building.

Hermann’s eyes drifted from Klaus. “What will happen to Dita?”

“I told her that I wouldn’t put her name in my report, and I meant it.”

“That’s not what I mean. Did you not make out her state and how bare the cupboards were in that kitchen?”

“Do you care what happens to the widow of an SS man?” Klaus snapped.

“Maybe I do,” Hermann snapped back.

“I can’t tell you whether they were really married. But Fischer submitted no notice of such a marriage. Nor did he request its approval. Which is what he would’ve had to have done and which is probably why he never did.”

“Why is that? Because as a common Czech girl, she’s not Aryan enough for you?”

“As far as we’re concerned, it never happened.”

The conversation concluded. The men entered both the synagogue and the prayer hall.

Lieutenant Krieg let out a rumble. It echoed through the cavernous room.

“We won’t take much of your time today, Lieutenant,” Hermann said.

She let out another rumble. Though not so loud. “Very well. What is it that you want to know now?”

“Did you ever talk with Captain Fischer while he was here?” Hermann asked, eluding both her and the pictures on the walls.

“Infrequently but from time to time.”

“Did he mention fine jewelry, gold, or heirlooms?”

Lieutenant Krieg lowered her head.

With abruptness, she raised it. “I can’t recall talks of that nature.”

Frustrated, Hermann took a leap toward the door. But he stopped himself. “Did he talk with any other people?”

“He often hollered at that idiot. Not that I blame him.”

“Anyone else?” Hermann pleaded, exasperation scratching his voice.

The lieutenant pondered the question. She pondered it longer than she had the first. “There was this archivist. He was here for a few days last month. If I remember correctly, Captain Fischer spoke with him often.”

“An archivist?”

“He was helping me catalog various items. He’s an expert on Josefov and its history.”

“Do you remember his name?”

“Of course, I remember his name: Lieutenant Becker.”

“Where can we find him?”

“At Černín Palace. That’s where he works.”

“Thank you, Lieutenant. Thank you so much.”

The two men sped from the building. They pounced into the late afternoon sun.

Hermann cracked a smile at Klaus.

“What’s so amusing?” Klaus asked.

“It’s possible that the key to this whole mystery has been sitting in SS Headquarters from the beginning.”
