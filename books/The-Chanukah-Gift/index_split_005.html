the fifth night

 

 

I HAD SURPRISED Josh on Saturday night. By volunteering to do the house laundry on Sunday morning. I surprised him each Saturday night.

It was the worst chore at the shelter. I had to wash the towels and bedding from the previous day. I had to hang them on clotheslines behind the mess hall. I later had to collect and fold them when they were dry.

But it had a perk. I got to wash my own laundry afterward, before the others who had signed up for it that day.

That morning, I steered the laundry cart around the table and chairs in the dorm. Toward the light that was streaming from outside.

The cart’s wheels jangled and jingled over the floor. They fell with a high-pitched booommmm over the step onto the courtyard.

In front of me stood Patrick, a gray, balding man. He was grousing to Sharon.

Patrick was the most loved person at the shelter. A veteran who had seen action in Vietnam.

But he didn’t care to talk about that. Instead, he would regale us with stories from the set of Road House.

He had a role in it as a drunk at the bar. A role that he was too qualified to play.

His flurry of fame came during a brawl scene. When he laughed at someone’s thumping. Ahead of another someone thumping him.

It lasted less than five seconds. But these would repeat in him forever.

“I’m not one to complain,” he said to Sharon, squinting from the shine above.

“What is it?”

“I woke this morning drowning in urine.”

“You what?”

“Theo, the guy in the bunk above me, doesn’t want to get up and go to the bathroom during the night. So he uses plastic bottles. Those two-liter Coke bottles. This morning, one broke over me.”

She went off with a wince. “I’ll handle it.”

I did what I could not to smirk at Patrick. Without success.

He smirked back. “When it rains, it pours!”

 

 

I WAITED FOR two loads of wash to finish.

The shine from above was bearing down on me in the courtyard. It was bringing its full force.

Booths away sat Willy and Dzsenifer. They were drinking coffee after finishing their breakfast.

It wasn’t easy for couples at the shelter. They couldn’t hold hands. Much less have marital relations. For that, they had secluded places outside the shelter.

An unwritten list of these they passed between them. Along with a creative set of euphemisms. With new places added and others removed when events warranted it.

Willy and Dzsenifer were a few years younger than me. But they could’ve been teenagers by the way they were ogling each other in the courtyard that morning.

They stayed in love despite their difficulties. It might’ve been what had kept them facing forward.

I caught Willy’s eyes. “How’s your car?”

He crossed his fingers and thumbs. “I’m praying for another week. Till payday. So I can get a new starter.”

“They’ll let you buy one?”

“We’ll be gone. That’s where the rest of my paycheck will go. For the deposit on our new apartment.”

 

 

I BROUGHT A basket of laundry outside.

Josh was prancing from the shelter at the end of his shift.

He owned it. He owned us.

Who watches the watchers?

Juvenal crawled into my head. He wouldn’t crawl out. I couldn’t shoo him away.

He stayed there while I hung up the towels and bedding. He got me mulling.

A side effect of homelessness is the helplessness that goes with it. You feel that you can’t affect your own life and those around you.

You are but a spectator of life. A ghost.

But what if I could effect change? What if I could link Josh to the two men the cops had arrested?

The question got me to take out my plastic phone.

I trained on its screen. I debated whether to cross the digital Rubicon. To regress to a side of me that I had lost. That I wasn’t sorry about losing.

With a drawn-out breath, I opened a browser on my phone called Tor. It let me search the web in secret.

I learned that the FBI provided access to their criminal database. Through what’s called an API. It lets programmers use this data in their applications.

Josh couldn’t have gotten a job at the shelter if he had felony convictions. But the same wasn’t true for misdemeanors and arrests. These could’ve made him a person of interest in the police’s hunt for the traffickers’ accomplice.

Doing this wouldn’t be simple. The agency protected access to their database. By means of keys.

These are strings of numbers and letters and symbols that validate requests and are not breakable. Though this system is fallible.

Lazy programmers, not wanting to trouble themselves with securing keys, will leave them in their files.

I scanned public source code repositories for one.

But I came up empty.

This meant that, if I wanted to learn more about Josh, I would have to scan private repositories for a key.

That wouldn’t have been legal. Using the key would’ve been less so.

I begged myself not to do it. I screamed at it to stop. That nobody would care what a homeless man discovered. That it could screw me up worse than I already was.

My lack of a criminal record was about all I had going for me.

Grumbling, I flung my phone down the table.

It crawled to a halt. It hung over the edge.

My training on the screen and on Kitty’s fright lingered. I couldn’t shake my eyes from either. This and some force was compelling me to act.

The leading sites for private repositories were GitHub and GitLab. The latter geared itself toward software development. Government agencies and contractors favored it. It was the better target.

On their website, they listed vulnerabilities that they had discovered over the years. The nastiest allowed attackers to inject commands when importing files from GitHub.

While they’d fixed this, I came upon another instance in the same codebase that I could exploit in the same way.

I installed an app called Termius on my phone, turning it into a low-end computer terminal. I hunted down my old attack libraries. I used a couple in a GitHub repo that I created.

Fastened to the terminal’s command prompt, I paused. It was my final chance to step back from the river’s edge.

WWRD. What would Rudi do?

The answer wasn’t in doubt.

In the terminal, I injected code into GitLab through the repo. I spawned a reverse shell.

Grepping the repositories through the shell, I located a key in one.

The rest should’ve been routine.

I had to write a simple Python script that employed the key to access the FBI’s database. The script’s first part would identify Josh in the database. The second would take the result from the first to list out his record.

But I hadn’t programmed in years.

Several bugs were in my simple script. I had several problems getting them out.

 

 

A PAIR OF lungs heaved.

Gerry was at the corner booth. In his sweatshirt and cap. With a puppy-dog face and his manila folders across the table.

His belief that the CIA director was stalking him wasn’t the craziest conspiracy theory that I had heard from a homeless person.

No person that I’ve met on the streets has been without a theory. Most have lots and imaginative ones. Often involving the Bilderberg Group. Or the Trilateral Commission. Or the Rothschilds.

When people hit bottom, it’s easier for them to believe that someone had loaded the dice than for them to blame the someone who had been rolling them.

I needed a break from my script. I joined Gerry at the booth.

He gave me his standard-issue smile. “Are you having a happy Chanukah?”

“Who told you that I was Jewish?”

“I hear stuff.”

I ignored the unease he caused. I pursued his stalker.

This required a few web searches with a normal web browser. I got the man’s address and phone number and where he worked. His picture too.

I showed it all to Gerry. “Your typical middle-aged ambulance chaser.”

My words brought him cheer. He was splashing around in them. “The perfect cover.”

I opened my mouth. But Mitchell, the rangy guy who slept in the bunk below mine, drew me away. He marched to us in a washed-out Obama “Hope” T-shirt.

You couldn’t read the word anymore. Or make out the man’s face.

But that didn’t mean that no meaning was on the shirt. Mitchell walked prouder when he had it on.

He held out his hand to Gerry. He let out his peeve. “My phone.”

Gerry went back to his bashful look.

He slipped his hand into his sweatshirt pocket. He slid out a plastic device like my own. “I couldn’t help myself.”

Mitchell took it. He shook it. “I bet you’re the guy stealing everyone’s money in the shower.”

“I’m not a thief. I’m a kleptomaniac. There’s a difference.”

“And that is?”

“I may take your wallet. But I’d never steal your money.”

Mitchell stormed off.

“Besides,” Gerry called out, “they stole my money too.”

Mitchell stopped. “You can bet that if I had kleptomania, all they would call me was a thief.”

He didn’t get a response. He took his phone to a booth up the way. He played “Enter Sandman” on it.

This amused Gerry. “You guys listen to Metallica?”

Mitchell glowered at him. “Us ‘guys’ invented rock ’n’ roll. Not to mention blues, hip-hop, R&B, soul, reggae, jazz . . .”

“What about disco?”

“Don’t lay that on us. It was you ‘guys’ who were listening to it.”

“You also invented hardcore punk,” I interjected.

“We did?”

I invited Mitchell to our booth. I played him and Gerry a video of a pair of Bad Brains concerts at CBGB from the early eighties.

In it, punks of a multitude of shades and hues slammed into each other. In front of H.R. and at times on top of him. They created a thunderous mosaic, not aware that Gerry, years later, would regard this as “unusual.”

 

 

THE TAKING OF a break from my script cleared my head.

Returning to my code, I was able to traverse the JSON objects that the FBI’s API endpoints sent back. Josh’s record spewed out on my phone’s terminal.

Or the absence of it.

No Joshua Maxwells in San Bernardino County existed to the agency.

Convinced that I had made a mistake, I triple-checked my script. The results from it too.

But no mistakes were anywhere.

Undeterred, I scoured the Internet. For aliases and past addresses and rumors related to Josh. For a link between him and the two jailed men.

Not only did I come up empty, but the few articles about Josh were positive. He volunteered with teens in crisis and people with disabilities. He had a wife and two kids and was vice-chairman of his local PTA. He had a dog too. One of those fluffy ones.

He was as perfect as you could get. Way more perfect than me.

I begged myself some more. To forget about Josh and Kitty and the other women. To keep my mouth and eyes shut. To but “smile and wave,” as Mateo put it.

But I couldn’t make myself forget. The desire for it did the opposite.

The most frustrating part of this was that, a week earlier, it wouldn’t have been true.

 

 

MITCHELL, GERRY, AND I weren’t the only ones that day engaged in an eighties flashback.

I strolled into the courtyard after finishing my own laundry. I came upon Nichelle, Wendy, and others. They were playing Trivial Pursuit. A special edition of it. It focused on the decade that I had come of age.

They made me feel my age.

None had an answer to the vegetable-themed doll craze that had swept the nation. Or what had inspired the songs “Do They Know It’s Christmas?” and “We Are the World.”

The 1980s were as disconnected from them as the 1880s were from me.

But they got me recalling my own times.

I claimed a quiet booth. I called Rudi. “You wanna hear how I met Yon?”

She groaned. “I told you, I’ve heard this.”

“Not all of it.”

 

 

IN THE BLINDING-WHITE reception area of St. Barnabas, I asked for Yon’s room.

A man, also in white, checked the patient directory. “He’s in the isolation ward.”

“Isolation?”

“It’s for isolating infectious or possibly infectious patients.”

 

 

I MEANDERED THROUGH the ward. I went by signs on the doors that warned about the dangers of being there.

They didn’t scare me.

I must’ve felt the same immortality many young people feel.

Or was it a different fate I feared?

I neared Yon’s room, my sneakers squeaking on the shiny white floor.

“What is the contamination level?” a doctor whispered to another across the corridor.

“That is the question. We can’t say to what degree this has affected the blood supply. We can’t test for it.”

“So we should expect more of these cases.”

“‘Expect’ is too mild.”

Labored breathing and coughing came at me. From inside Yon’s open door.

I got to it.

He was lying in bed, displaying the effects from the beating that he had taken. He scribbled in a notebook with a glumness that pervaded each stroke. He could’ve been making out his will.

Still, I disliked Yon. He was the type of guy I disliked. At times hated.

He was “sublime” in human form. In spite of his appearance (or because of it). If this was how he was, beaten and unshowered and unshaven, I could only imagine how he was before.

To me, Yon was the type of guy who never had a bad day. A type so different from me.

But at that moment he wasn’t as different as I wanted to believe.

I tapped on his door.

It sounded like a knock. Like I had pounded my fist onto it.

This startled Yon.

I startled him more. He had the same shock Mariana had. “You, you’re her . . .”

“Can you tell me where she is?”

His answer was a series of coughs. Loud, hacking ones. He followed them by returning to his scribbling. Without another word.

I couldn’t figure out what to do.

On a bureau across the room was a pen and a yellow sticky pad.

I started toward it. “I’ll leave my name and number.”

He continued writing. “Don’t get near me.”

I jotted down my details. “Tell her, please tell her that I want to talk to her.”

He didn’t pay mind to me. Or to what I had said.

I set out.

A nurse entered the room.

Wearing a mask and rubber gloves, she was pushing a medical cart toward Yon. But what leapt out was her fright.

She was closing in on death.

 

 

I STEPPED FROM the elevator on the hospital’s ground floor. Into the whiteness and upon my sister.

She had flopped herself onto an easy chair in the distance.

Coming across as if she hadn’t slept in months, she had her arms around herself. She had the same face as in the mugshot. Apart from the black streaks that had run down her cheeks.

I realized that she hadn’t realized me. I had the urge to run. I eyed the exits. I hurried toward them.

But I didn’t get to them.

I searched for courage. To discover the truth about her. To make it through another bout of my own truth.

It took a while for this courage to show. Not much of it did.

Enough showed up to stagger toward her.

To be honest, “stagger” isn’t the right word. “Inched” wouldn’t have described it either. It must’ve taken minutes before she caught on to me and my likeness to her.

It didn’t surprise her. It made her angry.

“Hi,” I mumbled in front of her.

I had practiced what I would say to her at this moment. I did this from the moment I’d learned about her. But the best I could add was, “I think I’m . . .”

Rudi swiped at her streaks. She made them worse. “My brother.”

“I . . .”

“What do you want?”

I felt my own anger. “What do I want? I learn that I have a twin. That she might be a Nazi. Or a neo-Nazi. And . . .”

“And a murderer?”

“I . . .”

“You can see that it’s true.”

The problem was that I couldn’t do this. It was the opposite.

She stewed more. “So you can go back to your little life and forget about me. In the same way that I’m gonna forget about you. I’ll forget about you the second you slink away.”

I gasped for air. I scurried toward the exits, not saying more. I scurried through the automatic doors. I keeled over outside them.

 

 

I NEEDED A pause from my story.

Stretching my legs outside the courtyard booth, I asked Rudi over the phone what she had been doing between the night at the motel and the day that I had met her in the hospital.

She couldn’t tell me much. She couldn’t remember much.

Among her few solid memories was a dream.

 

 

RUDI WOKE IN a hospital bed.

So consumed with pain was she that she cursed. She cursed and cursed. She cursed until she couldn’t anymore.

Nobody came. Nobody was around.

She stumbled off the bed. Out of her room. She became awash in her loneliness.

Wandering down the corridor, she came to a supply closet.

Feeling greater pain, she threw open its door.

Inside it was a glass cabinet filled with pill bottles.

Locked, she smashed the glass with her fist. She snatched a bottle at random. She tore off its top.

It was empty.

She chucked it onto the floor. She snatched another bottle.

It, too, was empty.

As was the next.

The one after that.

They were all empty.

More curses flew from her. More than before.

She shook her bloody fist. Her pain and loneliness higher.

Going back to the hallway, she scrambled through it. She passed vacant rooms.

She passed hundreds.

Coming to a room that wasn’t vacant, she screeched to a halt.

It was Yon’s. He was lying in bed, his face beaten but beaming at Darlene.

She was holding his hand from a chair beside him. The two were swept up into each other.

Mrs. Levy was also beaming. She was swept up by the pair from her own chair by the door.

Rudi tipped forward. “Yon?”

He didn’t respond.

Frantic, she shouted his name. She ran to him. She punched his shoulders and his chest.

He wouldn’t react.

She wasn’t there.

 

 

RUDI WOKE.

For real.

Like in her dream, she was in pain from the beating that she had taken. She cursed as she had in it.

“Rudi?” chirped a voice to her right.

Mariana was sitting next to the bed. She had the same look she had when Rudi had met her.

For a different reason.

Rudi rolled into a ball. Away from her. “What are you doing here?”

“Next time you’re in trouble . . . you might want to tell your best friend.”

Overwhelmed, Rudi hugged Mariana. She hugged her with all she had.

Remembering Yon, she released her arms. She asked about him.

“He’s in another ward. I went to see him. But he wasn’t awake.”

“I’ve gotta go to him,” Rudi yelped, falling from the bed onto the floor.

Mariana helped her up. “You’re not going anywhere. Not for a while.”

“How is he? How’s Yon?”

Mariana shook her head.

 

 

RUDI LAY IN bed for days, strung out on the drugs the hospital had given her, her mind stuck on a septuple of words.

I want you to be my girl.

Weeks earlier, these would’ve been the most ridiculous words that she had ever heard.

Now they were the only ones.

It wasn’t only Yon’s words that had seized her. Or how he’d screamed them to her from outside the motel room. It was how she had wanted to run into his arms when she’d heard them. She’d wanted, in that briefest measure of time, to give herself to him. To never more belong to herself.

She didn’t because she wasn’t there for herself. She told me that ignoring him had been her first selfless act.

I want you to be my girl.

The words dragged Rudi out of bed. They sent her stumbling after Yon. She went through hospital corridors with them echoing over her head.

I want you to be my girl.

She got to Yon’s room. Other words replaced the seven.

“Leave me alone!” he screamed in between coughs.

He wouldn’t let her get anywhere near him. Nor would he place his eyes on her.

The next time that she came, he was gone.

For a reason no one would explain, the hospital transferred him to a different one.

Rudi registered that her dream had come true.

She left the hospital. She did so far from right.

This was when it got blurry. The pill bottles that she would encounter weren’t so empty.

In the pieces and bits she could recall, she would blunder through streets and hallways. Or crawl across floors. Both sets of Yon’s words would duke it out in her head.

With them, she fought the worthlessness that she had battled since her mother had left. Her impulse to self-destroy. Both were propelling her nowhere.

One day, she bumbled upon a library.

Recalling the safety another had provided her when she was a child, she wobbled inside.

She stole a paperback copy of The Heart Is a Lonely Hunter. She couldn’t say why she had. She had read it years earlier. In the same class that she’d read The Catcher in the Rye.

Each night she clutched the book. The words on its tattered cover would splatter in her head. They’d meld with the ones from the William Sharp poem that the book got its title from.

 

But my heart is a lonely hunter that hunts on a lonely hill

 

The words pushed Rudi toward the words she wanted to believe. The words she had no choice but to believe.

They pushed her to Yon’s new hospital.

He wouldn’t talk to her there either. Or place his eyes on her.

So she flopped herself onto an easy chair in the waiting room.

What she was waiting for, she couldn’t say. What she could was that it was the one “where” to go.

I wasn’t the first who found her there. A Dr. Kleinsten had. He’d told her what Yon had.

It had sent the streaks down her face.

 

 

IN THE HASTENING darkness, I was standing against a wall by the entrance of St. Barnabas.

I wanted to leave. But I couldn’t make myself go. Nor could I go back into the hospital.

The automatic doors opened.

Rudi popped out. Her hands on her hips.

I stammered. “How, how did you . . .”

“This is where I’d be.”

She leaned against the wall next to me.

The two of us stood straight ahead. We spoke not a word. For God knows how long.

Rudi broke this. “When I was young, I was going through pictures in a drawer. At the bottom was a photo of us from when we were babies. You couldn’t tell us apart.”

“You can now.”

We both laughed. For God knows how long.

I turned toward her. “I’ve no memories of you.”

“Me neither. They must’ve split us up soon after that picture.”

“But why?”

“I suppose my stepdad had no use for a boy.”

I shuddered. I had believed forever that I had it bad.

But I’d been wrong.

I shook off my horror. I asked another question. “What about our dad? Grandpa could only tell me that his last name was Cohen.”

Rudi squirmed. “That’s more than Mom told me. It’s more than I would’ve presumed she could’ve.”

“And where is she?”

“That I can’t tell you either. It’s the sole ‘that’ I’m grateful for.”

“I guess she was pretty messed up.”

“You can say that.”

“She’d been through a lot.”

“She wasn’t the only one.”

“She was born in Prague,” I said, holding back a heave. It wasn’t from defending a woman that nobody could. But from reentering my nightmare.

I struggled to get the rest out. I had to shove the syllables over my tongue. “After the war, they deported her and her Czech mother to Germany.”

Rudi dipped toward me. “Why?”

“Her dead father, he, he had been a Nazi. An SS officer.”

When my grandpa had told me this, when he told me that he wasn’t my grandpa, when he told me about the man who was, it tossed me into a tailspin that I would never exit.

Rudi was as bowled over as I had been.

She feigned otherwise. “The apple doesn’t rot far from the tree.”

“Don’t say that.”

“I’m just like him.”

“You’re not. Neither of us.”

“I thought I was Jewish. I wanted to believe that I was.”

“You are Jewish. We both are. It’s complicated. My grandparents . . . I mean our grandparents, they adopted our mother.”

“What happened to her mom?”

“The two were staying at a resettlement camp in Nordhausen. While the Czechs had hated them because they considered them German, the Germans hated them because they didn’t consider them this. Some people in the camp, they killed her mother. They killed her in front of her. They came close to killing her. Grandma says that she couldn’t make it through a night without pills.”

I sucked in air. Heaps of it. “Try to remember that, try to remember it the next time you hate her. That’s what I do.”

Daze rained on Rudi. “I don’t get it. If her father had been a Nazi, why would Jews adopt her?”

“It’s hard to explain. Grandpa had met her mom before he left Prague. She reminded him of his late first wife. But there was more to it. Grandma couldn’t have children. Grandpa didn’t have any either. Then this chance surfaced for them both. They also wanted to see our mother as some kind of Ruth.”

“Ruth?”

“From the Bible. She wasn’t Jewish. But was married to one. When he died, rather than returning to her people, she joined her mother-in-law. She became a Jew. She would later become the great-grandmother of King David.”

“But our mom was the child of a Nazi. How could they adopt her?”

“I guess they wanted to fix the world. Or their piece of it. It sounds corny, but it’s part of who we are as a people.”

“It’s not corny to me. But they failed. Badly they failed.”

“Could be. But I wouldn’t be here if they hadn’t tried. Nor would you. So we’ll have to wait on that.”

Rudi didn’t respond.

I changed the subject. “I saw Yon earlier.”

“He won’t see me.”

“Why?”

“This is my fault.”

“What do you mean?”

“None of this would’ve happened if I hadn’t shown up here . . . if he hadn’t lov . . .”

“So you two . . .”

Rudi didn’t respond to this either.

“Is he gonna be okay?”

“He, he’s got this strain of pneumonia that’s been going around. They’re having a tough time treating it.”

“Was that why he was in isolation? Why his nurse was wearing a mask and gloves? Why she was so terrified?”

“She’s an idiot, that woman. His doctor doesn’t wear that crap around him. He’s not terrified.”

“But it is a contagious form of pneumonia?”

She glowered at me. “It’s not contagious.”

I changed this subject too. “I learned about what had happened. From the newspaper and TV. But they didn’t say much.”

“This guy Deke, he was beating me. He was killing me. He only didn’t because Yon broke into the motel room.”

Rudi slumped over. “I don’t get why he did. He had no chance against Deke. He had to know that. Why would he do it?”

I pushed out an answer. “The alternative, it could’ve been worse.”

“Yon surprised Deke. He picked him up. He threw him into a wall. Right through the plaster. He was like some hero from a picture book. I couldn’t believe it. I couldn’t believe that anyone would do that for me.”

Rudi slumped further. “My legs wouldn’t move off the floor. So Yon kneeled over me. He was crying harder than I was. That’s when Deke jumped him. He was beating him harder than he’d beaten me. And I could only watch.”

It was Rudi’s turn to suck in air. She did this more than I had. “I saw Deke’s gun on the floor. I stretched for it. But it was out of my . . . and Yon, he wasn’t gonna last. I lunged. I grabbed the gun. I shot Deke.”

I bent my head toward her. “Are you in trouble for that?”

“Are you kidding? If I’d been anyone but me, they would’ve given me a medal.”

“Are you okay?”

“I’m not the one in a hospital bed.”

“What about, what about the other boy?”

“What other boy?”

“The one who got killed months back.”

“Are you asking me if I killed him?”

“I can’t say what I’m asking.”

Rudi’s stewing returned. Worse than before. “And if I told you I killed him?”

I sank myself into her eyes. “I wouldn’t believe it.”

She shifted from me.

“Where you staying?”

“Around.”

“Grandpa and Grandma would—”

“—I want zip from them.”

“They had no idea you existed. They would’ve never left you like that.”

Rudi didn’t react. Not at all.

I had many more questions. But I couldn’t form any. I could only push myself off the wall. “Can I come back?”

“I’m here all the time.”

 

 

I VISITED RUDI at the hospital each afternoon.

It was slow at first. Some days less than a pocketful of nattering passed between us.

But we loosened up after a while.

I told her about my “little life” and our grandparents. I told her about Grandpa and me the most. I told her how he would crank Smetana’s “Vltava” on our stereo when I was sad as a kid. I told her how he’d perform scenes from it to pick me up. I told her how he’d come up with nifty Meister Eckhart sayings for the important moments in my life. I told her as well how he’d come up with a way to forgive me for what I’d put him through. I told her how no one could’ve been more my grandpa.

Rudi told me about her life too. I learned how alike we were. Despite how unalike we had grown up.

I expected us to have the same birthday and similar aptitudes—and I loved punk rock as much as she did—but we had startling parallels.

Like the books we had read. This included unusual ones. Such as Miguel de Unamuno’s Mist and Kōbō Abe’s The Woman in the Dunes.

Those two novels shouldn’t have surprised me. Both were about people who learned that they didn’t control their fate. This must’ve allured both of us.

Both of us were also different. In ways greater than how we dressed. Or how we wore our hair.

Rudi had a strength that I didn’t.

It wasn’t her physical prowess. That’s not strength. Any coward can hit someone.

Her strength stemmed from her ability to charge ahead. No matter what was in her path. As she was doing with Yon.

At the same time, there was warmth under her strength. Warmth outside what I could give.

Her smile would forever come from out of nowhere. But it would come. It would light up all that was around her.

So, regardless of her flaws, I would view Rudi as a perfected me.

The me I wanted to be. But never could or would.

 

 

ONE SHIVERING AFTERNOON, I was scampering to the hospital from the parking lot.

Rudi wasn’t in her chair. She was by the doors. She was panicking.

I hustled to her. I asked what was wrong.

“Yon’s gone.”

I assumed the worst. “Gone?”

“He left the hospital.”

“And went where?”

“Nobody knows. Or cares. A nurse was smirking about it. I almost broke her teeth.”

“You said that he was too sick to go anywhere.”

“He is. I’ve gotta get to him.”

“All right. I’ll be back in an hour.”

I sprinted off.

“Where you going?” she yelled.

“To get help.”

 

 

GRANDPA TURNED OFF the TV with the remote.

He frowned at me from the living room couch. He shook his head. “I cannot do it.”

I threw out my hands at him. “She’s not a Nazi. Or a murderer. She’s my sister. Your granddaughter.”

He went back to his headshaking. “Your grandmother, she would never forgive me. You have no idea what she went through with your mother. She cannot go through that another time.”

I joined him on the couch. I pleaded. “Please, Grandpa, only you can find him.”

“You did a pretty good job of finding her.”

“Because you gave me the most important clue. Without trying to. I also had a sense of where she was. Yon could be anywhere.”

“I cannot help anyone anymore. I am a useless old man who spends his few good days with idiot boxes.”

“You can do it. We can do it together.”

Grandpa lowered his head. The choice before him had weighed it.

He shook it.

“Talk to her for a few minutes,” I cried. “How can that hurt?”

 

 

GRANDPA AND I crept to the entrance of St. Barnabas.

Rudi was panicking more. She was about to shatter.

Grandpa’s hesitance had grown the entire way there. But on sight of his granddaughter for the first time beyond her mugshot, his hesitancy heightened. I imagined that I would have to tow him to her.

The sight of Grandpa didn’t please Rudi either. Her fists quaked. Her nostrils widened.

“Sorry we’re late,” I said as Grandpa and I stopped in front of her.

Grandpa tipped his hat to Rudi. His eyes were extra overextended. “Servus.”

She whipped a finger his way. She snarled at me. “Who’s that?”

“Your grandfather.”

Rudi twisted herself from us. She crossed her arms. “I told you, I want nothing from him.”

“Remember when I told you that he had been a police detective? What I didn’t tell you was that he’d been a really good police detective.”

“In what century?”

“This one,” Grandpa deadpanned.

I had to bite my tongue. “He can get to Yon. He might be the only person who can.”

Grandpa shook his head. For the umpteenth time that day. “I did not say that I would.”

I faced him. I didn’t speak. But I was begging.

He took the exchange elsewhere. “Did I ever tell you how I sought Franz Kafka when he went missing?”

“Yes, Grandpa. Too many times.”

“Ah, but your sister, she has not heard this story. You see, Kafka was not yet a famous writer. So when he went missing, no one acted. But it terrified his family. His sister . . .”

Grandpa couldn’t finish. Rudi sighed at a volume that drowned him out.

Uncrossing her arms, she twisted herself back to us. “All right.”

Grandpa raised a finger. “First, have you certainty that this Yon of yours is missing? Kids run away and go home. Your mother did.”

“You don’t get it. He needs to get back to this hospital right now. His life depends on it.”

Now Grandpa sighed. “Have you checked his home?”

“I called. But he hasn’t been there. He and his mother have been fighting. They’ve been fighting over me. I doubt she’s been to the hospital.”

“Where could he be?”

“He goes to this waterfall. In the forest. But you’d never find it. I can’t. My friend Mariana believed that it was a myth.”

Grandpa pointed at the trees behind us. “The waterfall in the reservation? Behind Grunnings?”

“Yeah,” Rudi gasped. Her mouth was wide enough for wasps to nest in it.

“What about friends? Or relatives?”

She couldn’t say.

“Do you have a picture of him?”

Rudi yanked a page from a phone book out of her bag. “I don’t have a picture, but his mother must. I can give you her address.”

Grandpa took a pronounced breath. He took a second. “I will seek your Yon. But please get that it does not work like in these TV shows you Americans watch, where they solve the mystery in exactly an hour. It takes time.”

“He doesn’t have any.”

“Meanwhile, I have a friend who works at your school. You can stay with him.”

Rudi’s mouth widened. Wider than it had before. “What?”

“And you will return to school.”

“The hell I will!”

“I was too lenient with your mother. Because of what she had been through, I let her get away with her behavior. On this occasion, I will practice that . . .”

Grandpa couldn’t latch onto the words he was fumbling for. He became lost.

“Jak se řekne?” he whispered to himself.

He snapped his fingers. “‘Tough love.’ I will practice ‘tough love.’”

“Listen—” Rudi began.

“—If I had done this with your mother, she might have been here to tell you what to do. It is possible that she would not have to do this. But I am smart enough to learn from my mistakes. So these terms are, how do you say, nonnegotiable.”

Rudi lowered her head. She did so like Grandpa had in the living room. She did so like she had grown up seeing him do this. “I want updates every day.”

“Give me until Monday. From that point, we can meet by your school daily when it lets out. Do we have a deal?”

 

 

“YOU WILL GET along well with Stephen,” Grandpa said to Rudi, his hand clutching the Matador’s window. “You have commonalities.”

He shuffled off. He headed toward a house by the reservation. The endless trees were silhouetting the kids playing beneath them in the yard.

Grandpa rang the bell.

The door opened.

A man strode onto the terrace.

He leaned down and hugged Grandpa. He hugged him as if he were hugging his father.

Rudi fell forward in her seat.

“Who is he?” I asked.

“Mr. Criss. He’s a guidance counselor at Columbia. What ‘commonalities’ could we have?”

I didn’t answer. I couldn’t tell whether the question was rhetorical.

 

 

I PLUGGED IN my phone at a wall not far from the booth. I asked Rudi about life with the Crisses.

“Pink. It was pink.”

 

 

PAM CRISS LED Rudi up a flight of stairs. “We’ve been foster parents for years.”

A woman in her late thirties, she had stepped out of an Ivory commercial. The wholesomeness was loud in her.

Two preteen boys ran down the stairs toward them. They came close to running them over.

“We haven’t had a girl in ages,” Mrs. Criss went on while the two women furthered up the staircase. “It’s going to be so nice.”

Rudi didn’t speak. She was doing her best not to scoff at what was around her.

The two made it to the second floor. They strolled down the hallway.

“If you ever need advice,” the woman cooed, “just ask.”

“Advice?”

“Girl stuff.”

“I’ll keep that in mind.”

They got to a door. The woman opened it. To a frilly and pink girl’s bedroom.

In addition to the dolls and dollhouses and doll accessories were four pink walls. A pink carpet. A pink bed that Goldilocks could’ve slept in. A pink dresser too.

A mirror was on the dresser. Framed in pink.

The single oasis was the unpink portable stereo next to it and a stack of cassettes.

Pam raised her arms. “This was my bedroom when I was growing up. Isn’t it special?”

“It’s not ordinary.”

 

 

RUDI WAS LAYING on her way-too-soft bed.

Her eyes were stuck on the empty Saturday afternoon outside her windows. Janis Ian’s Between the Lines was playing on the portable stereo.

Mr. Criss knocked on her open door. A folder was in his hand.

Rudi kept quiet.

He wielded the folder. “I got your file from Mrs. Cavender. Can you tell me why you haven’t applied to college?”

Her quiet continued.

The man let out a wheeze. “It’s a bit twilighty in the day, but I’ve got a friend in the admissions office at Princeton. I could call in a—”

“—I wish you two were more like my parents and didn’t care.”

“I’ll make that call anyway.”

He started off.

But he backtracked. “I noticed that you have to attend meetings. I’m gonna hold you to them. And I’ll know if you don’t go.”

 

 

RUDI WAS SITTING on the terrace steps.

Sounds and smells were coming from the barbecue in the backyard. But her focus was on the trees and the waterfall behind them.

The front door opened.

Pam hopped out. “The steaks are about ready.”

Rudi remained still. “Terrific. Tripleplusgood.”

“But we have baked potatoes. And corn on the cob. You can eat that, can’t you?”

“I’m not hungry.”

“You haven’t been hungry since you got here. I can relate to that.”

Rudi eyeballed the woman, who sat next to her and embraced her.

This uneased Rudi. But not as much as she would’ve expected.

“I met Stephen on my first day of South Mountain Elementary,” the woman said. “We were the couple. I had this fantasy about the perfect life we would have together.”

The woman drifted. “But it didn’t turn out that way. And I’d spend my days worrying about him. And I got really, really thin.”

“And?” Rudi asked.

“I didn’t give up on my fantasy. I didn’t when everyone was telling me to. I was telling me to.”

“So did you . . .”

“I never did get the fantasy. I guess it doesn’t work that way. But without holding on to it, without refusing to let it fall, I wouldn’t’ve gotten my reality. And it’s pretty darn good.”

Rudi, against her will, rested her head on the woman’s shoulder. “You know, you’re okay.”

“So are you. You have to remind yourself of that.”

 

 

MONDAY AFTERNOON, GRANDPA and I parked outside Columbia. On Parker Avenue.

We waited for Rudi in the sun-soaked front seat. Over a haunting rendition of Pergolesi’s Stabat Mater, coming from a tape in the car’s stereo.

The two women singing it were emptying themselves onto us.

In my younger days, Grandpa never played this music. But in recent years he often had. Most often the last movement, “Quando corpus morietur.”

Pergolesi wrote the piece during the last days of his life when he wasn’t that older than Yon. The movement touches upon this.

 

While my form here fades,

May my soul Thy goodness praise.

 

Grandpa had been playing the music so much of late that I had asked him why. I’d asked him, too, why he was listening to a Catholic hymn.

He insisted that he had no special reason for it. But he added that music had no religion. He told me that a Jewish choir, several times, had performed Verdi’s Requiem at Theresienstadt. Performances that he said were indelible upon their listeners. This counted the Nazis who would show up for them. He said that the choir would sing the Mass like the words were their own. Like they had flowed from their mouths since birth. “Oh, if you could have heard the soprano sing ‘Libera me’ at the end of it,” he murmured with a slight bow of his head. ‘Libera me, Domine, de morte aeterna in die illa tremenda.’ It wasn’t only her voice begging for deliverance. It was each of ours. You could hear them everywhere. I tell you that God Himself must have been in tears.”

 

 

REFLECTING ON THIS, I believe Grandpa did have a reason for playing Stabat Mater so much.

Similar to what Mist and The Woman in the Dunes had meant for Rudi and me, the music was his means of accepting fates he didn’t control.

 

 

THE SCHOOL LET out.

A thousand people pushed through the doors as one.

Rudi exited. She strayed through those hanging out.

Everyone was avoiding her. They were frightened of her.

I figured this was normal.

Till Mariana avoided her. The girl who was her “best friend,” dragged by the lanky redhead that she had been with on the day I’d met her.

Not only was fear of Rudi on display.

A girl, with venom in her eyes, hurled a gay slur at her while she rambled off.

Rudi ignored it all. But all of it was bothering her. She was the loneliest person in the universe.

She came upon someone sitting on the stoop by the school.

He was a hulking boy. He had a receding hairline and eyes sadder than her own. I got the feeling that he could relate to whatever she was going through.

Conflict was on his face too. Enough to sink ten Hamlets.

I opened my window. I poked my head through it. “Rudi!”

She ran to our car. She flew inside the back door. She tilted toward us. “Well?”

Grandpa lowered the music. “Mrs. Levy was kind enough to give me a photograph of Yon. She also gave me the names and phone numbers of his relatives and friends in the area.”

“What makes you believe that he’s in the area?”

“White chocolate.”

“What?”

“A bowl of white chocolate truffles was at Yon’s house. A wrapper from one was at those falls of yours. It was no more than a day old.”

Grandpa reached inside his jacket. He pulled out a Polaroid of a tire track. “Fresh tracks from that Harley Sturgis of his were also there. I must say, these Harleys—while they cannot compare to the Jawas that I rode in my Prague days—they are very nice bicycles.”

Rudi’s shoulders drooped. “How will any of this help us discover where Yon is right now?”

“I told you that it would take time.”

Rudi fell back in her seat. She grunted.

I leaned toward her. “What’s wrong?”

“They know.”

“What?”

“That Yon’s sick. They’re talking about him like he’s got the plague.”

“Is that why everyone was avoiding you? They think you have it too?”

Grandpa spun toward me with concern. “You did not tell me that he has a contagious disease.”

Rudi scowled. “It’s not contagious. He’s got pneumonia.”

“If it’s just pneumonia and it’s not contagious,” I countered, “why was he in the isolation ward? And why is everyone so afraid of him? What are you not telling us?”

Rudi wouldn’t tell us. My worry ticked up notches.

“Are you positive that you are not sick?” Grandpa asked.

She kicked her door open. “They checked me out. I’m fine.”

“There was another tidbit.”

Rudi froze.

Grandpa weaved toward her. “Mrs. Levy was also kind enough to check Yon’s accounts. He has not used his credit cards. But he has taken cash with his ATM card.”

“So?”

“The amount he took, along with that he is not staying with friends or family, lead me to conclude that he is staying in a hotel. A cheap one. You would not have an idea of which?”

“I only have an idea of one. But he’d never go there.”

 

 

WE DROVE THE Matador to the motel in Newark anyway. But floundered in a river of traffic on Route 22.

Grandpa couldn’t evade Rudi’s despair in the rearview mirror. “While we are waiting, I can tell you my Kafka story.”

I waved him off. “This isn’t a good time, Grandpa.”

“The story will have meaning for your sister.”

I kept talking him out of it. But it would’ve been easier making the traffic disappear.

 

 

IT WAS THE day after New Year’s in 1923.

The twenty-five-year-old man who would become my grandpa was shuffling down Eliška Krásnohorská Street in Josefov, the Jewish quarter of Prague.

He was carrying a bouquet of white roses. As he did each Friday night. They covered his chest in their brilliance.

His trot quickened. With each step closer to his goal.

Voices hindered his zeal.

They came from a block away. In the Temple on Geist Alley. Today named the Spanish Synagogue. They were chanting “Shalom Aleichem.” Like others had in the shul of his youth 800 kilometers to the east.

 

Bless me with peace,

Angels of peace,

Angels of the Most High.

 

Beneath the voices was what was calling out to him.

Come celebrate with us. Come celebrate life.

This was also beneath the voices coming from the front of a pub on Pařížská Street, a block in the other way. They called out to him in the same manner.

He ignored them both.

Through the kaleidoscope of light cascading over the cobblestones, he entered his apartment building.

In the lobby was a woman around thirty. She was shivering on the stairway in her overcoat.

She leapt to her feet. “Hermann Weiss?”

He tipped his hat to her. “Servus.”

“My name is Ottilie Davidová. My sister Elli lives in this building. She says that you’re a policeman.”

He advanced toward her. “What can I do for you?”

“Our brother Franz has been missing for days. Franz Kafka is his name.”

“Franz Kafka?”

The name sounded familiar to Hermann. He had to comb through his memory to get why it had. “The writer?”

“You, you’ve heard of him?”

“I read a story of his at the university. But I haven’t heard of him since. Does he continue to write?”

“Not successfully, I’m afraid. He worked in insurance. Until tuberculosis forced him into early retirement.”

“Did you file a missing persons report with the police?”

“Yes. But they won’t help us. They told us this.”

“I can’t imagine what you expect from me. I’m a beat cop and not a good one. Twice they’ve threatened to fire me. The third time may be the charm.”

“Elli told me that she had coffee with you and your wife. She said that you were smart and driven.”

“Let’s suppose that’s true. What could I do? He could be anywhere in Europe by now.”

“Please, Franz may be a nobody to the world, but to us he is special. We must get him back.”

“When did you see him last?”

“Tuesday evening. He had been staying with me at my house on Golden Lane. The next morning he was gone. With his suitcase and belongings.”

“He could’ve gone to a sanitarium.”

“He wouldn’t have gone without telling us. He didn’t tell his girlfriend either. She’s as worried as we are.”

Hermann yearned to decline the woman’s request. The words took shape in his mouth.

Not only was it an impossible task. But he was tired. He wanted to relax over the weekend.

Above that, he wanted to spend time with his wife. She hadn’t gotten over a tragedy from months before.

But despair was in Ottilie’s eyes. He couldn’t evade it any more than he could Rudi’s. “Do you have a picture of him?”

The woman took a photo from her purse. She handed it to Hermann. “It’s old. He’ll be forty this year.”

“I could check the train stations.”

“Oh, thank you, Herr Weiss.”

“Let me drop off these flowers. And I’ll have a look. But I don’t want you to get your hopes up.”

 

 

HERMANN WENT TO Wilson Station, the main terminal in Prague.

He showed the picture of Kafka to everyone who worked there.

None recognized him.

Next, he traveled to Masaryk Station.

He had the same lack of success.

So he gave up. He convinced himself that he had done what he could.

He started home.

But he hadn’t taken a pair of steps when he recalled a commuter station in Dejvice. Close to where Kafka had been living. The station had been what the new country’s president traveled from when visiting his summer home in Lány.

 

 

NO TRAINS WERE stopping in Dejvice until morning. The new tram lines to the expanding outskirts of Prague had stopped running for the night.

Hermann had to hitch rides on cars and buggies. He had to do plenty of walking.

It was after midnight when he got to the aging building.

From a distance, it was dark and empty.

But by its doors a dimness projected from inside. It lit the dozen people waiting on benches. As well as a manned ticket booth.

Hermann hiked into the building. To a clerk wearing a suit a hundred years old.

He withdrew Kafka’s photo from his coat. “Have you seen this man?”

The clerk didn’t stir from his paperwork. “He was here. A few nights ago.”

“Do you remember where he went?”

“Ústí nad Zapomněním.”

Hermann skewed his head. “I’ve lived in this country for years, but I’ve never heard of Ústí nad Zapomněním. I’ve been to Ústí nad Labem and have read about Ústí nad Orlicí. But no . . .”

“Do you want a ticket or not?”

“How much?”

“One crown, ninety-nine hellers.”

Hermann rummaged through his pants pocket.

He dug out two crown coins. He placed them under the ticket window.

The clerk took them. He wrote out a train ticket.

He sited it below the window. With a heller that he patted. “Don’t lose this. You’ll need it to pay the ferryman.”

“The ferryman?”

Hermann waited for an answer. But none was coming.

He took the ticket and the coin. He sat on a bench across from a man who was reading the latest edition of a Czech daily called Lidové noviny.

Hermann dipped toward the man. “Excuse me, sir. My Czech is a work in progress. Could you tell me what zapomnění means?”

“Oblivion.”

“So ‘Ústí nad Zapomněním’ would mean ‘The Mouth . . .’”

“The Mouth at the River of Oblivion.”

Hermann had high confidence that no such river was anywhere in what historians today refer to as the First Czechoslovak Republic. He wanted to give up another time.

Go home. He told himself this over and over. Go home.

But Ottilie’s eyes remained in his own. They wouldn’t go anywhere. Nor would he.

Exhaling, he set his mind on the man’s newspaper and its headline: “Jaroslav Hašek Hospitalized in Grave Condition.”

Hermann wrestled through the opening paragraph. He learned that they didn’t expect the famed author of The Good Soldier Švejk to survive.

He lowered his eyes, having loved what had been a three-volume collection of books, what many consider the first anti-war novel. He loved the books despite needing his wife to translate parts. Like many World War I veterans, he read his own life in its pages. It made him less alone.

A sound broke Hermann’s focus. A train with a single passenger car was sputtering into the station.

 

 

THE RAILROAD CAR Hermann slunk aboard was like the clerk’s suit. It was a hundred years old.

He took a seat on a wooden bench. He waited with the others. He waited for so much time that he was ready to give up some more.

A stout middle-aged conductor set foot in the car. “I do hope everyone has their heller with them. The ferryman will not provide passage without proper fare.”

He lumbered through the car. He punched the tickets of those he passed. He came to Hermann.

Hermann gave him his ticket. He showed him Kafka’s picture. “I’m searching for this man.”

The conductor’s eyes didn’t leave the ticket. “He was on the train the other night.”

“The one to Ústí nad Zapomněním?”

“What other?”

“When will we get there?”

“You have a journey ahead of you, young man. Sit back and relax. I’ll tell you when we get there.”

Hermann took the ticket. He reclined against his seat. He shut his eyes.

The train sputtered from the station. It had such scarcity of motion that someone could’ve overtaken it on foot.

Hermann bellyached. “It’ll take us days to get out of Prague.”

The Mouth at the River of Oblivion.

The words returned to Hermann’s head. Why they had, he couldn’t say. But they stayed there.

It occurred to him that the river was familiar.

But where had he heard of it?

The ferryman will not provide passage without proper fare.

“Charon,” Hermann muttered.

He opened his eyes. He recollected how, in Greek mythology, Charon ferried the dead across the River of Oblivion to the afterworld. After they paid him a coin. “It’s absurd. This must be a joke.”

Hermann vaulted to his feet. He pursued the conductor.

The man marched into the car. “Ústí nad Zapomněním. Last stop, Ústí nad Zapomněním.”

Hermann tossed his arms up. “But we just left Prague!”

“We’ve been traveling through the night, sir. Check your window if you don’t believe me.”

Hermann did.

It was morning outside. It was covered in fog.

 

 

HERMANN TEETERED FROM the train.

He followed the others. With a dogged reluctance.

They made their way up a hill. Toward what, he couldn’t tell.

All he could make out was a river to his left, whose flow was a trickle, and the cliff that he was ambling alongside. It overlooked the water.

He proceeded upward. He passed a stairway that led down the cliff. To a dock where stood four people.

Spinning wheels sent his head in the opposite way.

A grimacing woman in a dirty blue uniform was pushing an empty garbage bin up the hill.

He flashed her the picture of Kafka. He asked about him.

She pointed in front of her. “If he’s here, he’s there.”

Hermann followed her finger. Through the fog was a castle-like structure on the hill’s peak.

He veered toward it. “What is it?”

“A place of death it is. And plenty of it.”

Hermann froze. He wanted to go back to the station and to Prague. He had been around too much death in his young life.

He turned around.

A boatman in a hooded cloak was rowing a skiff down the river toward the dock.

Hermann’s fear rocketed. “Charon.”

He took a stumbling step closer to the cliff’s edge. He took another. Another after that.

Slipping, he tumbled down the hill. He tumbled to the base of it.

 

 

HERMANN WOKE ON a stretcher. In a dark and dank hospital corridor. Wearing only a nightshirt.

He sat up.

Dizzy, he felt his forehead.

A bandage was on it. He was sore everywhere.

Yet he got off the stretcher. Onto the floor.

Steadying himself, he lurched forward. He hauled himself down a winding hallway.

At a set of doors, he pushed his arms and body through it.

Men on beds had packed a hall-like room. No fewer than a hundred were in an area that stretched beyond Hermann’s view.

He took a stride inside. “Franz Kafka?”

No one responded.

He continued down the corridor. He went by rooms packed with men. Into each he called out Kafka’s name.

From none did he get an answer. He couldn’t tell if anyone was breathing.

The first departure from this was in a room by a stairway.

A group of five men had encircled the bed of an obese man around forty. He had a coif of curly brown hair and a filthy nightshirt.

The man was the focal point due to the notebook he was reading aloud. “Lieutenant Dub, who thought the terrible liquor was going to his head, tapped his finger on the table and lucidly explained to Captain Ságner: ‘The district commissioner and I have always said, “Patriotism, loyalty to duty, self-achievement, these are the true weapons in war.” I’m reminded of this especially today when our troops are on the cusp of crossing the border.’”

The obese man fell silent. He dropped his notebook. Himself with it.

Those who were surrounding him, who you would’ve sworn had been listening to the voice of God, eyed each other with dismay.

“Well?” one said.

The obese man took offense. “Well, what?”

“Aren’t you going to finish?”

“There is no more, gentlemen. It’s probable that there will be no more.”

“You can’t end it without finishing the story,” pleaded another man.

“I can if I must.”

“Would you,” babbled a feeble man, wobbling a leather-bound book in his hand, “would you sign my copy?”

“What for?” howled the obese man. “What good will it do you now?”

The feeble man drooped.

This chafed the obese man. He shoved his hand out to him. “Give it here.”

He didn’t have to wait.

The feeble man, without taking a breath, handed him his book. He handed him a pen from his shirt pocket too.

The obese man signed and returned it.

“Thank you, Mr. Hašek,” the feeble man sang, clutching the book like a newborn. “Thank you so much.”

“Jarda. My friends call me Jarda. Each of you, call me Jarda.”

The men walked off. They revealed a broken-down wheelchair by Hašek’s bed.

Hermann gawked. At who was paces away.

He took some of these paces into the room. He came upon Hašek and his hate.

This was a hate that Hermann had familiarity with. He would continue to have it throughout his life. It came from the all-too-Semitic features of his face.

Hermann eluded the man. He laid his eyes on the men to his left. As well as on the expanse past them.

He cupped his mouth. “Franz Kafka? Is there a Franz Kafka here?”

“I am Franz Kafka,” came a timidness from the nothingness.

 

 

GRANDPA CUT OFF from his story.

We had gotten to a motel on the edge of Newark with no name.

The car screeched to a halt by the office.

Above it on the roof was a single-word sign. The single clue to the place’s purpose.

The red glass covering the “M” had broken apart in many sections. It left the yellow light behind it bare and harsh.

Grandpa exited the car.

With one of his famous drawn-out moans, he shuffled to and through the building’s faltering door.

“So that’s a Matador,” the unseen clerk droned, repeating a joke from a TV commercial years earlier that wasn’t funny when it came out.

The door closed. Grandpa spoke to the man.

Fluster and handwringing ensued. It mounted.

He left with his head sagging. He shuffled back into the car. He thumbed behind himself. “That vůl in there . . .”

Rudi exploded. “That what?”

“That ass,” I translated.

“Thank you,” Grandpa said to me. “That ass would not ‘spill the beans,’ as you people say. Nor could I bribe him.”

Rudi slammed her forearm against the door. “I told you that Yon wouldn’t come here.”

“But that does not mean that he will not come. I will add this place to my daily checklist on that . . .”

Grandpa sought the right words. Much like he had by the hospital.

This time “Jak se řekne?” couldn’t help him.

He had to rely on me. “What do you call that contraption in the living room?”

“A PC, Grandpa.”

 

 

GRANDPA AND I waited outside Columbia.

Kids that day weren’t wearing jackets. In disregard to the season. So many were hanging outside that the front doors weren’t in sight.

In a wink, everyone scattered.

Rudi swept through them.

That afternoon, she wasn’t alone. She was holding the hands of two boys.

The first was the hulking kid from the other day. The other was gripping a Bible.

The three headed toward us with defiance. So did the geeky boy behind them, striving to keep up.

They were flipping off the entire school. Not with their fingers. But with their faces.

Following them from the building was a man. He had on a brown suit and a brown tie. He came off as the typical no-nonsense principal, in how he was folding his arms.

But his smile gave him away. He was smiling at the four in the way that a parent does when their children please them beyond what they could’ve expected.

 

 

THE AFTERNOON WAS collapsing beneath the courtyard.

I stretched out by the booth with my phone. “You wanna tell me the story behind this?”

“No.”

But Rudi did.

 

 

RUDI WAS SLOUCHING at the cafeteria’s back table by herself.

The tables around hers were empty. As they had been since she’d returned to school.

Silence overtook the room. It elevated Rudi from her picking at her food.

Owen was standing in front of her with his tray.

Everyone’s shock pulled Owen. “I got curious about that disease. The one you’re so sure she’s got. So I went to the library last night. For the first time ever. And you know what? You people, you’re so sure I’m dumb. But you’re way dumber than me.”

The silence and shock continued.

Owen sat across from Rudi.

She was as silent and shocked as the others. If not more so.

He inched over the table. “While I was at the library, I asked about that word . . . karma. This is the good kind, right?”

Rudi couldn’t answer. She was too busy keeping herself from crying.

Owen put out his paw to her.

She grabbed his fingers. She lowered her head. She shook it.

“Come on, lift that chin.”

She did. But with a guilty smirk. “I don’t deserve you . . . not when I . . .”

“Nobody thought I deserved anything. Till I met you.”

Jared and Eliot joined them with their lunches. They masked their own guilt.

Rudi made it easy for them. She pretended that the past weeks hadn’t happened.

But she couldn’t pretend the same with Mariana.

She was off next to Terry. She was doing her own pretending.

 

 

GRANDPA DIDN’T HAVE much to report that day.

The growing doubt Rudi was having was hurling at us. Fatalism was setting in.

It must have been why Grandpa stayed out late that evening.

He hadn’t come home when I fell asleep.

 

 

I RETURNED FROM school the following afternoon.

Grandpa was tossing in his sleep on the living room couch. Columbo was playing at full volume on the TV.

I strode beside him. I could tell that this wasn’t one of his good days. “Grandpa?”

He wouldn’t wake.

I shut off the television with the remote. I stirred him awake.

He rose to his waist. “Ana?”

I averted my eyes. “No, Grandpa, Ana’s not here.”

“I have to buy her roses before she comes home,” he went on, not aware that decades had passed since he had given them to her last. “White roses.”

“Let’s go get some.”

I stood. I was about to help Grandpa up.

But the computer running in the room’s other side tugged my eyes from him.

It aggravated me. I had told Grandpa that the CRT monitor could burn in if left on when not in use. I’d told him and told him.

I went to shut it off.

What was on it altered my mood to a trance.

I got out of this. I typed some commands on the keyboard.

The dot matrix printer next to the monitor churned.

 

 

RUDI SNUCK THROUGH the Matador’s back door with her shoulders down.

She raised them toward me. Her brows with them.

I was in the driver’s seat. Grandpa was asleep next to me. He was babbling in Czech.

She pressed herself between the front seats. “What’s wrong with him?”

“He overdid it last night.”

“You certain that he’s up to this?”

I wasn’t certain. But I didn’t say it. Grandpa needed this as much as Rudi did. He needed a reason forward, and I needed him to have it. I needed him.

Grandpa roused. He jolted. He banged into the door with his elbows.

Tears formed in his eyes.

They ran down his cheeks.

“Drž se dál od vlaku!” he shrieked, repeating his final words to Ana. When he begged her to stay away from the train taking him to Theresienstadt. “Prosím tě se drž.”

“What’s he saying?” Rudi asked.

“Remember when I told you that our mother had been through a lot? So has he.”

“That music he’s been playing . . . the Pergolesi.”

“You’ve heard of Pergolesi?”

She snarled at me. I had asked the stupidest question ever. “Put it on.”

I did.

What she did sent me reeling. More than what her knowledge of Late Baroque music had.

She laid out her arms. She took Grandpa’s hand with both of hers. She caressed it. “Sshhh.”

This was the first I viewed the nurturing side of her. A side that contradicted what she was about. But would be fundamental to what she would become.

Grandpa calmed. He fell asleep.

Rudi sat back.

I pulled out the printout that I had made. I showed it to her.

“What is it?”

“He mapped all lodgings in Yon’s price range that are within thirty kilometers of here. He’s been visiting them. He visited them all yesterday. He must’ve been up all night.”

Rudi examined the printout. “Did you program that for him?”

“Don’t let that Luddite spiel of his fool you. He was programming the computer the day we got it. I noticed this afternoon that he wrote a serial communications library in C. So he could scrape data from CompuServe.”

 

 

THE DAY DISAPPEARED behind the shelter.

Rudi told me that she had to go.

The sun’s shine and its warmth left with the dusk. It left a chill and a gust that whisked through the courtyard and me.

But the story wouldn’t go with the light.

It came alive later on my bunk. Below the cacophony rumbling through the tomb. Snores from one side of it got answers from those on the other.

 

 

WITH GRANDPA OUT of service, after checking on him when I got back from school, I would drive to Columbia myself.

Rudi would be sitting outside on the stoop with her friends. They’d be helping Owen with his homework, reveling in a bond that was blooming with each day.

On an overcast afternoon, one riddled with frost, a girl joined them.

She had blonde hair and a ponytail. She was as different from them as they were from each other.

Lis sat next to Jared. She took his hand.

But what I recall more was her face. She was in another place. A good place.

I honked my horn. I upended her peacefulness.

Rudi and I took off. We went to the South Mountain Reservation.

I roamed through a dusting of snow. Rudi hung over a rock by the waterfall.

She had her arms around herself. Her eyes were closed. She was straining to hum an old song that I recognized but couldn’t name. She got stuck in multiple places. Multiple times through it. But she wouldn’t give up on it.

The tune came. She wouldn’t stop humming it. She had the same bliss that Lis had.

Only now can I imagine how she had crossed her arms not against the weather. But in remembrance of a time that she had wanted to return.

 

 

LIKE THE PREVIOUS afternoons Rudi and I had spent in the reservation, we got nowhere. Everywhere we did.

Despite being the grandson of a larger-than-life detective, despite hearing his stories to the point that I could reenact the pauses he would make between the words, I lacked the most important skill he had. The ability to see what was hiding in front of me.

Rudi and I slithered to the car on Crest Drive. A street that cut through the forest’s width.

I got to my door and stiffened. Some unknown had caught my eye.

It led me to it. Along with Rudi.

In a clearing were motorcycle tracks. The same that had been in Grandpa’s Polaroid.

They headed for a tree. They swerved from it at the last moment.

I opened my mouth. “He was trying to . . .”

Rudi rushed off.

I followed her.

She called out, “I need to go somewhere.”

 

 

I PARKED BY the South Orange Recreation Center.

The snow was falling harder. It was a sheet of white.

I turned to Rudi. “Why did you want to come here?”

“There, there’s a meeting. Attending is actually a condition of my probation. But I’ve never been to this one.”

“Is it open?”

“You wanna come?”

I didn’t answer. I didn’t have any.

The hour crawled toward six.

People streamed into the building. They were a blur.

Rudi kept waiting.

Only well after the hour did she leave the car.

I dithered. But I went with her, no stranger to these types of meetings. Or the need to go to them.

We dawdled into the building.

Climbing to the second floor, we sidled to a room and the chanting inside it that was far from in unison.

It was of a prayer that I also was no stranger to.

 

God, grant me the serenity to accept the things I cannot change,

The courage to change the things I can,

And the wisdom to know the difference.

Just for this day.

 

The voices fell.

We snuck through the doorway.

Beside it was Jared on a metal chair. A kind ever-present through the room.

He sat with his Bible on his lap. Away from the others. They had scattered and were wistful.

I sensed that no one wanted to be there. At the same time, they did.

“Jared?” Rudi whispered.

He playacted surprise.

His presence didn’t knock her over either.

We joined him.

In the room’s front, a man stood. He was behind a table covered in keychains of different colors.

He had a weathered face. It reflected each of us. Each line on it told a tale. “We have some new people. My name is Frank. Feel free to introduce yourself.”

Nobody spoke.

“Does anyone want to share?”

He got the same response.

“Anyone?”

Frank wouldn’t relent. He swayed toward the room’s right corner in back of us. “Stephen?”

A man stepped from the shadows. It was Mr. Criss.

He marched to the front with his head down, with his steps steady. He was marching to a beat only he could hear.

“My name is Stephen,” he said, “and I’m an addict.”

“Hello, Stephen,” was our reply. In the robotic fashion common at meetings like these.

“I’ve been clean for thirteen years. Thirteen. To some of you, that may seem lifetimes. But not to me. To me, it was yesterday. It was yesterday that I was gone. I was gone before I came back from the war. Man, was I gone. I was some piece of work. You wouldn’t have wanted to have been anywhere in my crosshairs.”

Mr. Criss averted his eyes. “Thirteen years ago, I was robbing a man. A little old man with a funny accent. I had a gun right to his head. But he just smiled. He smiled like this had happened to him every night. He smiled not at me. But on me.”

Stephen’s eyes returned to us. “I was born that day. That day and each one since. Each one I’m sober. Each one I’m loved. Each one I’m back holding a gun on that little old man with the funny accent. The one who smiled on me.”

He returned to his corner. He went with his head down. But he wasn’t marching with steady steps. The beat had left him.

“Anyone?” Frank said.

Jared raised his hand. He did so as if it and his arm were of iron.

Frank had not anticipated this. It jarred him.

But he waved Jared onward.

Jared trudged to the table. He dragged his feet. He dragged them in a haphazard manner while clutching his Bible. He was clutching it with all he had.

Next to Frank, he grappled for words. “My name is Jared, and I’m an addict.”

“Hello, Jared,” we said. With greater energy than before. Rudi was the loudest.

“I’ve been clean, for sixteen months I’ve been clean. That was when I came to. In a house I didn’t know. With people I had never met. In a town I hadn’t been. Right now, right at this second, I’m no more than a day from going back. The truth of this never leaves me. It’s there when I’m at school and when I’m at home with my mom and when I sit and pray.”

Jared lifted his Bible in his shaking hand. He sank his head with it. “Don’t let this fool you. I’m worse than the lot of you. You, too, Mr. Criss. I can’t wear short-sleeved shirts in the summer. So messed up are my arms. My legs too. I’ve lied and stolen . . . I, I’ve sold myself. Worse than all that, I’ve hurt people. I’ve really hurt them. I’ve hurt my own mother. Nothing will make up for it. No Bible verses. No good deeds. Nothing.”

He lowered his Bible, his hand shaking more. His head stayed down with it. “The difference between now and sixteen months ago is that I’m not alone. Not only has my mom been great—when she has every reason not to be—but I have friends. Real friends. Friends who care about me. And it’s for this I pray. I pray for it all the time. I pray not only that I’m not alone. But that I know I’m not alone.”

Jared trudged back to us. He did so slower than he had from us. But in the same manner.

He got to his chair. His eyes dropped our way.

Rudi climbed to her feet. She clutched Jared. She clutched him like he was clutching that Bible.

He cried. He couldn’t stop. He was crying after the two sat and another man began to share.

A few others shared after him.

“Anyone?” Frank asked.

No one spoke.

Though Jared encouraged Rudi. By placing his hand on hers.

She didn’t budge.

Frank moved on.

Rudi hopped up. She stormed to the table. She was racing against herself. “My name’s Rudi. I’ve been clean for weeks. I can’t say how many.”

“Hello, Rudi,” was the response. I shouted it.

She spied Frank. “I forgot to say that I’m an addict.”

“It’s okay.”

“I probably didn’t because I like to make believe that I’m not. I like to make believe that I’m tough. That I’m in control. That I’ve licked my problems. But I’m an addict. I will always be an addict. I’m not tough and I’m not in control and I’m about to fall apart.”

Frank took her hand.

She squeezed his. But to steady herself and get more out, she had to close and open her eyes. Three stints of this she did. “I must’ve been to hundreds of these. I’ve never shared. I’ve mocked the whole idea. But I’m not the same person. For the first time, I’ve let someone close.”

Rudi’s eyes drifted upward. To the ceiling and what was above it. “I can’t remember when I wasn’t alone. I was alone regardless of how messed up I got. I would never let anyone within fist’s length. But I’ve met someone. Someone who makes me so mad. Because he makes me want to believe that I matter. That I have meaning. That all around us is meaning and that it all matters and that there’s actually a reason to be alive. He might not be alive right now. I can’t make it if he’s not.”

 

 

I GOT HOME late that night.

Grandma was tucking a blanket over Grandpa. He was lying on the living room couch in a stupor.

She turned to me. “Where have you been?”

I stood beside them. I stuttered. “I, I was at one of my meetings.”

“I was under the belief that you no longer went to them.”

“But I should.”

Grandma picked up a spoon from the coffee table. She placed it in the bowl beside it. She brought some of her famous chicken soup to Grandpa’s mouth.

He would have none of it. He shook his head. Away from the spoon. “Srnčí na pepři si dám. S kroketama.”

Grandma poured ridicule onto him. A whole hilltop of it. “You know well, old man, that no venison is in this house. Peppered or otherwise. Or potato croquettes. You will have to make do with my soup.”

Neither would give in.

But she won out. He let her feed him. With her wiping his chin after each spoonful with her handkerchief.

“I love this man,” she said. She said this as much to him as to me. “He is not my bashert. Nor am I his. But I love him. He . . . he is moje všechno.”

Her all.

 

 

GRANDPA BECAME LUCID enough to resume his tracking of Yon.

But he wasn’t hopeful while the two of us waited in his car outside Columbia in our usual spot on Parker Avenue.

The school let out.

Rudi wasn’t anywhere. Or her friends.

Two stragglers left. They were snickering about Rudi and Yon.

It alarmed me. I opened my door.

Rudi exited the school.

She was holding the hand of another of her friends.

It was Mariana.

The two had been through the inferno together. Both their faces showed tears.

I captured Rudi’s eyes.

She returned to Mariana. She hugged her. She said goodbye. She sprinted toward the car.

Mariana didn’t move. But she was breaking apart. “I’ll talk to you tonight!”

 

 

I WAS RECALLING this moment from my bunk. I recalled with it that I had never learned what had happened between Rudi and Mariana. Or how they had made up.

I had to learn it. At that instant I had to learn it.

So I bungled to the floor. I came close to smashing onto it.

Snagging my phone from the bed, I jostled past the bunks in the tomb. I scrambled into the courtyard. Without my coat or my shoes (or my pants).

I called Rudi.

The wind was whizzing about. To the degree that I had to shout to get through it.

“What’s wrong?” she said, forcing herself awake.

“I need to ask you a question.” I told her what.

“Can’t it wait?”

“I’ll never get to sleep.”

We compromised. She gave me the quick version. “It was me who let out what Yon had. Or confirmed it.”

“Mariana. You told her.”

“The night she told me about meeting you, she told me about the rumors some were spreading about Yon. She didn’t believe them, she said. Yon didn’t fit the profile of those with the disease at the time.

“So I told her. At first, she couldn’t speak. Then she went nuts. The world was going nuts. Remember how people believed that you could get the disease from a toilet seat? She believed this. She hadn’t a doubt that I had it. That she’d get it too. ‘You used my bathroom!’ she screamed. ‘How am I gonna explain this to my dad?’”

“I tried to calm her. But she hung up. And never did I feel so afraid. That’s why I lied to you guys. I was afraid that you’d go nuts too.”

“We might’ve,” I said.

“Mariana told Terry what I had told her. It blew up from there.”

“You weren’t mad at Mariana?”

“Are you kidding? I wanted to kill her. But . . .”

“But what?”

“The day we were hugging, much had happened earlier. A scene at lunch that shook everyone. I was processing it as I was leaving school.

“Mariana was waiting for me in the corridor by the entrance. But I wouldn’t let on that she was there.

“Terry made up for it. She was by the exits. She was madder than I was. She slammed open a door.

“Mariana spun to her. But she wouldn’t go with her. And Terry ran out, screaming, ‘It’s over!’

“That’s when Mariana returned to me. ‘I’m sorry.’

“I ignored her. I started to go. But I couldn’t keep myself going. I guess, I guess it was because she had given up a lot to make us right. Whatever my reason was, I just stood there.

“‘You were the best friend that I ever had,’ she whimpered.

“My body slid toward her. My arms opened. ‘I still am.’”

 

 

I SAID GOODNIGHT to Rudi. I went back to bed.

But the story wouldn’t go to sleep. It unraveled before me like waves on a shore.

It crashed onto me with each snore.

 

 

RUDI GOT TO the Matador. That Grandpa was in the driver’s seat knocked her over.

She stormed into the back. She shut the door. “He was here!”

I shot toward her. “What?”

“Yon came to the cafeteria at lunch. He was drunk, his head shaved.”

“And?”

“He was standing in front. Nobody noticed him at first. But when they did, they fled. Including his so-called friends.”

Grandpa’s head fell askew. “Because he has pneumonia?”

I put my eyes on Rudi. “He’s dying. Isn’t he?”

Rudi didn’t answer.

Grandpa’s eyes broadened. “Is this, is this the disease that I have been reading about? The one that has been afflicting homosexuals?”

“Obviously, it’s afflicting more than them,” Rudi barked with defensiveness. She must have believed that someone Grandpa’s age had to have been homophobic.

He wagged both his finger and his head at her. “You must stay away from this boy.”

“I’m not.”

“This is a deadly and contagious disease. They are not aware of what is causing it. Or how it spreads. They are only aware that it kills.”

“It’s not contagious. Not in the way everyone imagines.”

“How can you have faith in that?”

“Because otherwise everybody would have it!”

“They could. I read that it can take years before it manifests.”

“If we all have it, what difference does it make?”

“You are not to go near him.”

“Listen—”

“—I will find him. I promise you that. It does not matter if I get sick.”

“You won’t get sick!”

“Stay away from him!”

“I won’t!”

“Stay away! Or I will!”

“Do what you want!”

Rudi kicked her door open. She heaved herself through it.

Grandpa’s face lost its color. He must’ve feared that he would lose his granddaughter. Much like he had lost his daughter. “Please, please do not go.”

Rudi paused. For a while she did.

She went back to her seat. She closed the door.

Grandpa hung over the front seats. “What happened after the kids fled?”

“He went crazy. He tossed over tables. He chased people. I ran after him. But he ran from me. He drove off on his bike when I got outside.”

“Did he give you a notion of where he might be going?”

“No. But there’s more. He had a Walkman. He was screaming this Black Flag song.”

“Is there some significance to that?”

“He doesn’t listen to that type of music. I wouldn’t have supposed that he had heard their name.”

“He could have heard them on the radio.”

“They don’t play that type of music on the radio, Grandpa,” I interjected.

“So he might have bought one of their recordings recently. But many stores here must sell recordings.”

“Not these.”

Grandpa returned to Rudi. “Where could he have bought these?”

“I couldn’t tell you,” she snapped. “I haven’t been buying records since I got here.”

“We could go to a library,” I said. “We could get a phone book. We could call each store.”

Rudi shook her fists. “That would take hours.”

“Do you have a better idea?”

She pounded her knees. “They have a library and a pay phone in the school.”

Her door swung open. She hurtled from the car.

But she didn’t take more than a single strut.

Owen was passing through the parking lot. To the bus stop on Valley Road.

“He’d know where to buy them,” Rudi murmured.

She yelled his name. Over and over she yelled it. Each louder.

But he was too far in the distance. He joined the others waiting for the bus that was crossing Parker.

Rudi dove into the car. She karate-chopped down the road. “Stop that bus!”

Grandpa sped off. He didn’t wait for Rudi to close her door.

She could only do so during the squealing right that we made into the intersection.

Here Grandpa’s instincts took over. As they would at moments of mania. Or when his mind was off somewhere. Or both.

He swerved into the left lane. He put us in the path of a truck and its horn.

I flailed my arms to the right. “Do práva, Dědo!” I flailed them twice. “Do práva!”

Grandpa calmed. “I know. I know.”

With the same calm, he wrenched us from the truck’s wrath. He cut off the bus turning from the curb.

Now the bus honked its horn. It did so like the truck had.

Rudi shrank in her seat. “Where did you learn to drive?”

“Uzhhorod,” Grandpa said with matter-of-factness. “If the Habsburgs had won the First World War, everyone would be driving on the proper side of the road.”

The bus went on with its honking.

Grandpa went to Rudi. He pointed out the car. “The bus.”

Rudi didn’t leave.

Grandpa’s forearm and the number tattooed on it had transfixed her.

I descended toward her. “Rudi?”

This got her out of her fog. Enough to get from the car. To the bus.

 

 

I FELL ASLEEP in the tomb.

It all disappeared.

Rudi and the bus. The story and the snoring.

Darkness settled over each.
