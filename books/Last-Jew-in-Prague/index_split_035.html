chapter thirty-five: a small amount of happiness

 

 

CAPTAIN FISCHER’S ONE-BEDROOM apartment was larger than Captain Gruber’s studio.

It took Hermann longer to discover the same nothing. This included in Fischer’s bedroom bureau.

Hermann exhaled. “Where else?”

Klaus checked around the room.

He shook his head.

Hermann heaved a sigh. He started to leave.

Klaus grumbled. “Now where?”

Hermann masked his own frustration. “Back to the living room.”

Klaus followed. But he stopped. “Wait.”

Hermann spun toward Klaus. “What is it?”

Klaus nudged his foot. He nudged it up and down over a loose floorboard.

Hermann kneeled beside it.

Klaus removed his foot.

Hermann pried open the board. He reached into the crevice. He felt around.

Klaus got impatient. “Well?”

Hermann removed his empty hand. “Sometimes a loose floorboard is only a loose floorboard.”

“How about some lunch before we go about in the same circle?”

“It couldn’t hurt.”

The two left the apartment and the building. With their heads hanging. Hermann noted that they were blocks from U Pinkasů.

They walked to the busy restaurant.

Both ate bowls of a hearty garlic soup called česnečka. They did so without saying a word. Though this didn’t mean that Hermann’s mind was on nothing. He was musing whether the case had reached a dead end. Moreover, he was questioning it in its entirety.

Had it been leading him somewhere like he had told Marta? Or had he been making himself believe that it had so he could justify his survival?

From Klaus’s expression, it was clear that his mind was also on the case. Hermann could sense as well that the man was feeling doubt. But he was at least eating. Which I believe was due to the last few days and the small amount of happiness they had brought him.

Klaus interrupted the silence. “Tell me, what do you expect to find in Fischer’s apartment that you didn’t before?”

“What I missed.”

The two finished their meals. They returned to Captain Fischer’s apartment.

Hermann rifled through the same living room that he had rifled through earlier. He found the same nothing.

Klaus searched too.

Coming to a mirror hanging on a wall that was crooked, he removed it.

Behind it was nothing but the wall.

“It happens to the best of us,” Hermann said while feeling between the cushions of a sofa. “You’re sure that you’ve stumbled upon the solution to the mystery. Only to discover that you’ve simply stumbled.”

Klaus grunted. He returned the mirror.

He opened a closet by the door. Despite Hermann having searched it when they had gotten there earlier.

Like what Hermann had done, Klaus scoured the man’s jackets and coats. Like Hermann, he uncovered nothing unusual or interesting. He was ready to close the door.

But he stopped himself. His eyes had locked onto Fischer’s SS cap. On the shelf above the coats.

You must separate what your eyes take interest in from what your mind tells you makes sense.

I bet Klaus was remembering Hermann’s words from the day before.

He pulled the cap off the shelf. He inspected it.

Under the strap, below the skull-and-bones insignia, was a folded scrap of paper. One that would impact both this case and my life.

Klaus unfolded the paper. It was a coat check from a restaurant in the city’s Malá Strana section called U Malířů.

Hermann slid beside him. “You have the makings of an excellent detective. It’s just a shame that we’ve already eaten.”
