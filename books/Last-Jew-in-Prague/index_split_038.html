chapter thirty-eight: the man who refused to collaborate

 

 

THE PANKRÁC ADDRESS of Jitka Prejslerová was a ten-minute drive from where Dita lived. But the neighborhood was the same. Working-class and dreary.

The Mercedes turned a corner. It halted in traffic. Beside an idled ambulance, halfway on the sidewalk.

Nothing moved. Or made a sound.

Hermann fixed on the ambulance. It brought back the tragedy of Jiří Orten, a poet who had died a few years earlier. An ambulance had hit him by accident, and the hospital refused to treat him because he was a Jew.

The meaninglessness of his death and of so many like him lowered Hermann’s eyes. Him with them.

Would he ever rise?

A noise brought him out of his funk. It lifted his head.

Paramedics were carrying the body of a mangled man toward the ambulance.

“Another suicide,” Hermann said.

He rolled down his window. He felt more envy toward the corpse than he had toward the one the day before.

“What happened?” someone asked a paramedic.

“God only knows. He was driving on the left-hand side of the road. He drove straight into a truck.”

The presumptive corpse smiled.

Hermann smiled too. At the man who had refused to collaborate, who had refused to conform to the will of another. He gave him the courage to do the same. He also gave him back the breath that he had lost when they’d passed the train station.

The traffic cleared. The Mercedes parked outside Jitka’s building.

Hermann, with a renewed sense of purpose, stormed inside along with Klaus. They stormed to the woman’s apartment on the third floor.

Hermann knocked on the door. Over and over he knocked.

It cracked open.

An eye was glaring at him. “What do you want?” growled the voice of a middle-aged woman.

“We’re looking for Dita Prejslerová,” Hermann said.

“She’s not here. She hasn’t lived here for years.”

“Are you her mother?”

“She has her own apartment.”

“She doesn’t live there anymore.”

“What do you want from me?”

“Any information that could help us locate her.”

“All I can say is that she’s taken up with a Nazi.”

“He’s dead.”

The woman said nothing. But that eye of hers widened.

“Do you know where she could be?” Hermann asked.

“Not only don’t I know,” the woman screeched, “but I don’t care. She has shamed the entire family!”

The woman heaved the door closed. She locked it too.

Hermann and Klaus started down the hallway. They moved in slow motion.

“Tell me,” Hermann whispered as he halted by the stairwell. “If a young lady were to call the palace and ask for Captain Fischer, what would they tell her?”

“That he’s unavailable.”

“They’d tell her that even if she insisted that they were close?”

“Even then.”

“And they’d keep telling her this, no matter how often she called?”

“Yes.”

“So it’s unlikely she knows he’s dead.”

“I’d say it’s next to impossible. Fischer had no next of kin. We notified no one of his death.”

Hermann made no response. He willed himself still.

Klaus groaned. “Is there a reason we’re standing here?”

Hermann rose his left index finger. He placed it over his closed lips.

The two waited.

A young woman cried out from inside the apartment. It reminded Hermann of his wife at the train station.

Skirting this, he returned to the woman’s door with Klaus. He knocked on it. “You’ll have to let us inside, Mrs. Prejslerová.”
