the first night

 

 

HOW DO I begin the story of a snarling, quick-fisted girl named Rudi?

That destiny fated her to Yon, the Polo-wearing boy she otherwise would’ve hated? That she fought dragons for him and defied death? That she was my sister and my twin and my salvation?

No, this flipside-up fairy tale, this punk rock Cinderella, must start thirty-five years later. On a day that started like others.

 

 

WITH THE SUN slinking above I-15 blocks away and my jacket zipped beyond my chin, I rattled a garbage can forward. I passed Mateo and his fixing of a heater behind the motel where we worked. I approached a duo of dumpsters and the scruffy man sprawled against them.

The barrel and I halted. By a dirty needle lying among the sand blowing across the cracked asphalt.

It was begging for a reaction from me. Disgust. Or shock. Or surprise. But my only response was to grab the needle with my gloved hand.

“Hey,” a voice sprang from nearby.

Amoun was whooping it up, skipping and snapping his fingers. He whooped it up a lot. He got a kick from an Egyptian bossing a Jew (or a “Hebrew,” as he called me). He was reenacting Exodus.

In a sense, I was his slave. He had me do eight hours’ worth of work in a four-hour shift. For less than minimum wage. He’d discovered a loophole around it.

But I didn’t care about the money. I couldn’t spend it. I needed the job, the more of it and the more demanding it was. So I indulged his Ramessian fantasies. I let him revel in them.

“I want you to shampoo the hallway carpets,” he said to me, ours being the one Motel 6 with interior corridors. “And power-wash the walkways. Oh, the people in 122 last night left blood everywhere. Clean that too.”

He swaggered off.

I flung the needle into the can. While the scruffy man got to his feet. He was a regular guest who paid his seventy-dollar bill in nickels.

He straightened his clothes and his hair. His finger quivered in the needle’s direction. “That wasn’t mine. I would never leave them on the ground.”

“I appreciate that.”

 

 

THE MORNING WASN’T alone in its likeness to others.

At the day’s end, Mateo and I took a pair of buses from Hesperia. We slogged our way along 7th Street in Victorville. A city at the center of an oasis of concrete and glass struggling to rise from the common source of both, churning beneath it and around it.

The two of us approached a sun eager to sink below the interstate. I was sinking with it.

Mateo snorted at me. He was the closest that I had to a friend and the reason that I had a job. He’d managed to convince Amoun that someone with a computer science degree could make a good houseman.

“Get off the road, you idiot,” he directed at my veering and quickening toward the traffic racing past us. Or was it at my chasing of its impetus? “You’re gonna get killed.”

Slowing, I shook my head. I tossed up my arms. “If I am to wither in the desert, I cannot get killed by a car.”

Mateo yanked me back. From the path of a blazing red Charger. “Not today, you can’t.”

 

 

MATEO AND I reached the gates of a dusty complex.

In the alley alongside it, Willy was brooding over the open hood of a Civic. Its fender was threatening the ground.

Mateo dragged a foot toward him. “What’s wrong?”

Willy cringed. “It won’t start. If I can’t get to work tomorrow, they’ll fire me for sure.”

Mateo’s face conveyed how tired he was. How he didn’t want to do more fixing. But he lumbered to the car.

“Mateo?” Nichelle called out from inside the gates. “The washing machines are broken.”

“I’ll take a look at them in a while.”

Mateo bent over the engine. He tinkered with it.

Willy lowered his eyes. “Thanks.”

“We’re in this together.”

 

 

THE EVENING ALSO began like others. I was eating dinner with dozens of men, women, and children—from toddlers to teens—at communal tables grouped in pairs.

Across from me was Gerry. A grizzled man my age who had come days earlier. He was slipping silverware into his pockets.

I pretended to ignore this. “How’s it going?”

He hid his hands behind his back. “The usual.”

I smiled. I didn’t want to. But I did.

Finishing my plate of sauerkraut and mashed potatoes and corn, I stood.

He edged toward me, his eyes widening. He forced his fears out. “Were there any suspicious characters outside?”

“That’s all there are. Outside and in.”

 

 

I TRUDGED TO a table by the kitchen. I dropped off my tray.

On the wall hung a calendar and the event that made this evening different. It was the first night of Chanukah. A night, like any other, I was spending in a homeless shelter.

That it was Chanukah shouldn’t have mattered. I had lost faith years before. Religion years before that. But the holiday invoked memories. Good ones.

They played through my cleaning the mess hall after dinner. They ran on during my waiting at the head of a winding line of people. In front of a glass-walled office where we would check in for the night. They only faded with Mateo’s exiting the open door with a towel in his hand.

It was my turn. I stepped into the cramped room.

Josh sat crouched over a desk. He was in his thirties. With a graying beard, bulging belly, and red cheeks. He reminded me of Santa Claus. Without the jolly or good cheer.

I couldn’t tell whether he had caught on that I was there. An oversized schedule had spellbound him. It held the galaxy’s mysteries.

He wrote my name in a slot. “You’ve got laundry tomorrow morning.”

I held back a grunt. From explaining to him five nights a week why I couldn’t do this. “I work in the morning.”

He didn’t hold back his own grunt. He crossed out my name. “Kitchen, tomorrow night.”

Check-in was complete. I grabbed a towel off the stack on the chair next to the desk. I set out.

“This came for you,” he let out.

In his hand was a bulging white envelope.

I didn’t give it much regard. I snatched it and left the office. I walked a few paces to the cubbies.

Tossing the towel into mine, I exited the dorm.

Under the bright lights, I entered a courtyard that was passing for a diner. In its dozen weathered booths, twice as many people were passing time in them.

One man was rubbing out a stain from his coat. Over Beethoven’s Grosse Fuge screeching from his phone. Groaning, he doused the stain with what was in his cup. He rubbed harder.

The stain wouldn’t let up. Neither would he. Nor would the four in the booth ahead, swatting flies.

Two women were behind him. They were telling tales. Of hunting for jobs that they didn’t want and of plans for leaving the shelter that they wanted to believe.

Mann tracht, un Gott lacht, my grandma would say in Yiddish. Man plans, and God laughs.

He must laugh at women too.

I continued listening to the two. I listened to the normality that they projected. I listened to the vanity that they applied to it.

This didn’t deter their aspiration for it.

I recalled the envelope. Having nothing better to do, I opened it.

With less enthusiasm, I wrenched out dozens of pages, handwritten in ink on both sides of lined white paper with ruffled edges. The kind that I had last come across in school.

I leafed through the crinkled and aged sheets. My disinterest decreased with each.

The person who had penned them rushed at me. My eyes locked onto the envelope and its return address. Of a Yon Levy in Livingston, New Jersey.

More shocking than this was the postmark, dated thirty-five years earlier.

 

 

THE WIND WAS bucking. A chill scorched the air.

It was a Southern California night so unlike those that had existed in my childhood from the opposite coast. It had been sunny and room temperature in Cali year-round on TV. Nobody wore jackets or sweaters.

I was alone at a booth in the courtyard’s corner under a full moon. It silhouetted the palm trees and canyons to my right and my fathoming the pages on the table.

Failing at this, I took out a plastic phone. The sort that they gave out to folks like me. “Obamaphones,” we called them, despite him not having been president in years. He wasn’t the guy who came up with them either. But we had to give them some name. To differentiate them from those that people wanted.

The phone was low on battery. It was low when charged. So I plugged it in at an exterior wall outlet.

I took an extended breath. I dialed a number that I was sure I had forgotten.

It rang.

A sudden and unknown fear overcame me. It pushed my finger over the disconnect button. I willed myself to tap it.

“Hello?” she said.

George W. Bush had been president when I’d heard this voice last. But I would’ve recognized it after a thousand years. It was the same as it had been. Confident and unafraid. It was soaring above me.

I reached out to touch it. To be part of it. But I didn’t speak.

She repeated herself.

Words tumbled from my mouth. “It’s me.”

Rudi had to wait for words too. Longer than I had. “Where are you?”

“It doesn’t matter. I don’t want to take up your time. You must be busy tonight.”

“Service ended a while ago.”

I faltered. I couldn’t get to the point. I couldn’t drag myself from the pages and the impossibility that they conveyed.

Convinced that they were real, I babbled, “It’s crazy. But, but I got a letter today . . . from Yon.”

“Yon?”

Rudi gasped for air. I could sense she was falling. I was falling with her.

I stopped myself. For her sake. “It’s right here.”

“What is?”

“A journal he wrote.”

“He never wrote a journal.”

“You certain about that?”

She paused. For a while she did. “I’m never certain. The lack of it comes with my job.”

“You want me to mail it to you? Or I could scan it with my phone and email it. There’s this new app that uses a machine-learning technique I’ve been reading about to do character recognition. It might untangle Yon’s scribble.”

“You can’t read it?”

I huffed. “I can. But I skimmed through it. It’s real personal.”

“You were important to him. He must’ve wanted you to have it.”

“But . . . how could he have sent it to me?”

“I can’t remember when I stopped questioning how. Please read it to me. It’ll be the best Chanukah gift I’ll get.”

I hesitated. I didn’t want to revisit the past. Or the nightmare buried inside it. Nor did I want Rudi to rehash her own. All this made me tremble.

Yet I ordered the pages by the numbers on the bottom. I read Rudi a story that I’ve narrated with my own perspectives of it.

The first entry was from December 24, 1982.

Its date stuck out. It was in a different color than the rest. Yon had written it at a different time.

 

 

WHEN YOU READ someone’s journal, the expectation is that it will start at their story’s beginning.

Yon’s started at the end.

Through the corner of his eyes, the reflection from the room’s full-length mirror was overwhelming him. Of a tall but peaked and skeletal eighteen-year-old figure in a white terrycloth robe. Whose flat chestnut hair was so bare that it didn’t cover his scalp. The image so troubled him that he concentrated on the yellow ribbon that he was tying over a red gift-wrapped box.

Snow framed the afternoon. It fell between the windows and the hospital across the street. The off-whiteness staggered from the skies. In time with a Glenn Miller song from a half-century earlier that was playing from a portable stereo by the bed.

A chain jingled.

Yon swayed toward a diminutive golden-haired mutt. With a spiked collar. She was guarding him by the door with the urgency of her life.

He returned to the ribbon. He was mindful that he had to get it right. He tied and untied it. He rested between the two.

 

 

THE AFTERNOON WAS gone. Sweat poured down Yon’s face.

He wiped it away with his arm. He tugged at the ribbon’s ends.

Two perfect loops spread across the box. He gushed at them, raising himself and the box off the bed.

Lightheaded and wobbly—with his legs half-asleep—Yon couldn’t keep his socked feet from slipping. He collapsed onto his knees on the wood paneling with a boom and a holler and a set of hacking coughs.

Both he and the dog fell into a panic.

Would he get up?

The finality of his disease never ceased looming.

His panic didn’t last. It subsided with the pain.

Yon stood. He staggered and limped out the door with the box. Into the living room.

The dog followed him. She matched him step for step.

Yon neared a mantle and the simple metal menorah on top of it.

Under it, he lowered himself to his knees. This took longer than it had taken him to lift himself. But he got the gift on the floor.

The front door creaked.

In the apartment’s threshold was a woman Yon’s age. She was glaring at him with her hands on her hips.

The woman couldn’t have been more different than Yon. She contrasted with him and herself. Her powder-white face contrasted with her spiked black hair and with her heavy black eyeshadow and with a glossy black lipstick that matched her nails. Contrasting with all this was her oversized blue-and-white football jacket. “Yon” and a “C” had been stitched into it.

Her presence spread a blanket of warmth over Yon. He forgot his doom.

The feeling wouldn’t wane. It was so overpowering that he wrestled with what to say. “You’ve caught me.”

Her expression didn’t change. “I didn’t get you a gift.”

“You’ve already given me it.”

“I did get you some . . .”

The woman shut the door. She marched to Yon.

Kneeling beside him, she took a white canvas seabag from her shoulder. She reached inside it.

Pulling out an envelope wrapped in gold paper fashioned from stars, she flinched. Her eyes stretched. Her brows rose.

He leaned toward her. “What’s the matter?”

Her startle got starker. She shook. “He must’ve slipped it into my bag.”

“Who?”

“The old man at the store.”

“Can I open it?”

The woman didn’t answer. She kept shaking.

Yon drew the envelope from her. He slid his finger along the seal.

He withdrew a white card. It could’ve come from a board game.

Now she was leaning toward him. She was toppling over. “What is it?”

“It says, ‘One Replay’ . . . and in fine print . . . ‘Caveat Amor.’”

 

 

THE SECOND ENTRY in Yon’s journal was from December 11, 1981. A year before the first. The remaining entries followed the second.

“Did you hear me?” spoke a familiar female voice from Yon’s right.

He didn’t answer. He couldn’t. His attention was on the image reflecting off a light-soaked window. Of a confused but striking and broad-shouldered seventeen-year-old boy.

The boy had a shock of wavy hair. An olive complexion. He was wearing the blue-and-white football jacket with his name and the “C” stitched into it.

“Did you hear me?” the question repeated. This time with a touch of a growl.

Yon’s focus shifted to two other items in the reflection: the turquoise school backpack across his shoulder and who was hanging over his other arm.

Her name was Darlene. The prettiest girl at Columbia High School. With her strawberry-blonde permed hair and legs without end and a billion-dollar smile, she could’ve become a model. “The Jewish Brooke Shields,” people called her. But her ambitions aimed higher. “Yon?”

He spun toward her. “What?”

“I said, ‘Happy Chanukah.’”

She roused Yon to his new reality. He was standing by a cathedral-like school, half old and half new. He was by its front doors. He and his friends and dozens of other kids. They were talking and joking around, their breaths tinting the morning air.

Yon strained to reconcile his present situation with his previous. The minutes that he had spent with the crazy-looking girl he couldn’t recall beyond this.

He grasped at the most logical conclusion. That he had been dreaming. The girl. The apartment the two shared. The disease that had been killing him. It had to have been a dream.

But there was a problem with this. A dream may appear real while it’s happening. But when you wake, it becomes obvious what it was.

This wasn’t. It had been as real as what he was experiencing now. The two traits that it shared with a dream were that—as when waking from one—he had a limited memory of it, and he was forgetting it.

He yanked his arm from Darlene. He swung his backpack off his shoulder. From it he whipped out a spiral notebook and a pen. He used the latter to scribble on the double-sided lined white paper.

Darlene retook his arm. She gripped it. “What are you doing?”

He shook her off. “I’ve gotta write this down.” He wrote what he could remember. From tying the yellow ribbon to reading the card. “One replay? A replay of what?”

Darlene gripped his arm harder. “What are you talking about?”

Yon didn’t answer. His mind was on a more troubling riddle.

Shouldn’t he have been happy? Whatever he had experienced wasn’t real. He was healthy. Death was in some far-off reality.

But he wasn’t happy. He felt loathing. The same that he had felt for whatever had been in his horizon since the summer. Including himself.

Before that, you could say that he had a semblance of happiness. From that his heaviest contemplations were over “suede vs. leather.” Now the weight of his weightlessness could’ve brought Atlas to his knees. The single time that this wasn’t true, the single time he was happy, was the time with the girl in his dream.

“Did you guys catch the ‘Pretty Woman’ video on MTV?” asked Joey. A boy in the same blue-and-white football jacket as Yon and who was much the same as him. All his friends were. They all had pretty boy haircuts and designer clothes and were all as good-looking. Judging by the pictures Yon would show me in his yearbook, they could’ve started a boy band if such had existed.

Nobody answered Joey’s question. The chatter tailed off. The silence got so loud that Yon gravitated toward it.

He shuddered at what was approaching.

Two individuals were making their way to the school from the parking lot. The first was a middle-aged man with a crew cut and a pockmarked face, sporting an off-the-rack blue suit. But what the crowd was eyeing was the girl steps behind him. A girl incapable of a quiet entrance.

It was the same girl from Yon’s dream. The same wild hair and makeup. Carrying the same white canvas seabag over her shoulder. The differences were the Walkman she was clutching and what she was wearing: a navy overcoat, faded and torn jeans, and a pair of black Chuck Taylors.

The last two she had on before either was in fashion. Or was close to coming into it. Yon wrote that he couldn’t remember anyone wearing Converse since he had been a kid. What was more bizarre was that there were no laces on them.

She must’ve been some sight that morning. The way that she clashed with the suburbia around her. Punks weren’t a novelty in early 1980s America. Or a lifestyle choice. They were an aberration of humankind, decried by a popular TV show called Quincy as a murderous menace.

Today, no one would give the girl a second gawk in the Bible Belt. But it must’ve been as if she had fallen from the stars. The creature from Alien would’ve been less horrifying to everyone. They had seen that before.

Yet the girl’s appearance wasn’t the characteristic that stood out to Yon. It was her apathy.

This she was feigning. You could say a lot about punks. An awful lot. But what you couldn’t say was that they didn’t care. It was the whole point. I’m positive, too, that the indifference the girl was projecting was hiding. The cowering child underneath it desperate for a way out.

It was Yon who was apathetic. He told me that, before this happened, he had struggled to wake in the morning.

Closer the girl got to Yon.

His knees buckled. A sensation that he had experienced playing football. But at no time did he experience it like this or this much. “Who is she?”

No answer came.

The girl acknowledged the eyes on her. Her apathy turned into a scowl. She stared down the entire school. She was ready to fight them all.

Cranking the volume of her Walkman, she sang with a song that Yon had never heard. About someone who belonged to a “Blank Generation.”

Yon didn’t care for punk rock. He didn’t get it. But this song was different. It was different from any other. The lyrics were about him. Or someone who could’ve been him. Someone who had it all and nothing. Someone surrounded by others but alone. Someone frantic to get away. Whether or not this meant never getting back anywhere.

The girl stopped singing. Her glare fixed on Yon. Along with her snarl. He was the only one there. The only person in the world.

Yon grimaced. But not at her. It was at himself and his attraction to a girl vile in many ways but fascinating in many more.

It wasn’t because of his dream that she fascinated him. She was unlike any girl around. She stood so uprightly and cared not how others regarded her. His heart pounded with each stomp she took toward him. His body tilted forward. He was tipping over.

“Isn’t Halloween in October?” Joey joked within her earshot. To a gust of laughter that compelled Yon to join in.

The girl trained her spite onto Joey. “How would you like to go next year as a rug?”

Joey didn’t respond. He couldn’t.

“Them’s fighting words, Joey,” a fellow boy in blue chimed in.

“I don’t fight girls. Hell, I wouldn’t fight her.”

The girl opened her mouth. But the man ahead of her interrupted. “That’s enough.”

She sneered at him. She returned her glare to Yon. Her snarl with it.

Yon was only steps from her. Her and her bloodshot eyes and the sweating that she was doing despite the weather. Beyond that was her obliviousness of a girl and the doors that the two were heading toward.

Mariana was mousy with no makeup. Under her plain black wool coat, she wore a white blouse and a plaid skirt that flowed below her knees. She could’ve been going to Catholic school.

She hurried to the entrance without heeding who was hurrying with her. She and the new girl were about to slam into each other. They avoided it at the last instant.

Wide-eyed and wider-mouthed, Mariana took the girl in. She was witnessing a new form of life. “Hey.”

The girl took Mariana in. Without the wide eyes or the wider mouth. She raised her fist at her, insulted by the recognition. “Hey, how about some dentures?”

Mariana didn’t answer. But she did take a leap back. As far as she could.

This didn’t placate the girl. She went after her.

She didn’t go far.

Her blue escort grabbed her by the arm. “What did I tell you, Rudi?”

The man didn’t wait for a reply. He threw his shoulder into the heavy wooden doors. He tossed Rudi into the building. He followed her inside.

“Who are you?” Yon whispered. At a volume that only he could hear and only a sliver. With his fear accelerating past the red line.

 

 

I BROKE OFF from the story. One too bizarre to believe.

But was it crazier than the pages in my hand? Or the postmark on the envelope?

I had no answer. I went in a different direction.

Yon wasn’t the only person curious about Rudi. This particular Rudi was a stranger to me.

I lowered the page that I had been reading. “Who were you?”

She didn’t want to answer. She wasn’t bashful about it. “This isn’t my story.”

“It belongs to the three of us.”

Rudi was silent.

But I was in control. I wouldn’t read any further till she relented.

She did so with a grumble. “What I’ll say is that I had bigger worries than some preppy jock.”

“Such as?”

“Such as I was jonesing like hell. I would’ve raced up Everest for a handful of pills. It wouldn’t’ve mattered what. A sword was also hanging over me. It would’ve made Damocles piss in his pants. Or whatever he wore.”

“But Yon got to you.”

“That doesn’t mean it was love. I had no interest in him at all. However gorgeous he was. Or because of it. He was the best-looking guy ever. Better looking than any celebrity. He could’ve posed for Michelangelo. And that shot up my fury. I was sure that he was stuck-up and boring. Most guys like that are, in my experience. But . . .”

“But what?”

Rudi grumbled more. More than before. “He had this inexplicable. In how those amber eyes of his were cutting through me. It was like he knew me better than I did. That nothing could I hide from him. This threw me.

“But I brushed it off as biological. I can tell you that, when I stepped through those doors (or flew through them), I had completely forgotten about him.”

I doubted that. But I continued reading.

 

 

YON AND DARLENE entered the school.

He was there in body. But his spirit was jumping between his reality and his dream.

Down the corridor, Rudi and her escort stood by the school offices and the principal, a man wound tighter than the most inoperable knot. Garbed in a tight brown suit and a tighter brown tie.

The principal jabbed his finger at Rudi while baring his teeth at the man next to her. “I want to reiterate what I told your superior. I don’t care about the importance of your case. We have a sufficient criminal element here.”

Rudi angled away from the man. She slung her arms across herself. She crooked her lips. “The real Nazis run your schools!”

The principal thrust himself toward her. “What did you say?”

“Don’t you worry, Mr. Gonzales,” the pockmarked man hooted, seizing Rudi by the neck. “She won’t be giving you trouble for more than a month or so. With any luck, less.”

The man hauled Rudi away. He dragged her past Mr. Criss, a guidance counselor.

He was the lone person that morning that Rudi didn’t put off. Or horrify. It was all over his face.

 

 

YON WANDERED THE third-floor hallway. Among those shuttling to class and yakking about nothing.

He was clueless of where he was wandering through or to. He had taken spins in both a washer and a dryer. He couldn’t walk straight.

Coming to his first-period computer class, he stopped.

His mind wasn’t on it. But he poked his head inside the door.

A half-dozen kids were entering punch cards for use in the old computer on the room’s other side. That was how you programmed it. You would type lines of code on the cards and wait for an operator to feed them into the machine. You could wait a week to compile the simplest of programs.

Yon’s heart didn’t pound in anticipation of this. His knees didn’t buckle. He wasn’t tipping over.

The bell rang.

He walked off. Instead of spending an hour punching the code that would’ve spewed a bunch of zeros across a page, he continued down the hall. He poked his head into each classroom he passed.

At the corridor’s end, he entered a stairwell. He climbed down to the second floor. He poked his head into the rooms down that hall. He did so with a doubt hanging over him. It was about to collapse onto him.

Had his time in the apartment with Rudi, including the disease that had been killing him, not been a dream?

If it hadn’t, it meant the incredible. Someone had gifted him the chance to replay a part of his life and elude his fate.

Caveat Amor.

Yon had never taken Latin. But he was sure that he had gotten the gist from the fine print on the card.

Beware Love.

Was that why his replay began right before meeting Rudi? Did he need to beware of her? Would falling in love with her trigger the events that lead to his disease and death?

He stopped. With haste.

Why was he chasing after her? Regardless of how she had capsized him, she couldn’t be worth dying over.

But this could have been what made her so exciting: the risk that came with her. She was a one-woman rollercoaster, and Yon was in need of a thrill.

“What are you doing?” somebody squeaked.

Behind Yon was a teacher. A man whose name he had never bothered to learn, who didn’t come up to his neck and had shoe-polish black hair like President Reagan’s, as well as an argyle sweater over a yellow button-down Oxford and a paisley tie.

“What?” Yon muttered.

“Why aren’t you in class?”

“I have a free period, and I was, I was going to Mrs. Elkind’s . . . she promised to help me with calculus.”

“Isn’t her classroom on the first floor?”

“Yeah. I guess I’m out of it today.”

Yon scurried down the hall. He closed in on another stairwell.

He checked behind himself.

The teacher was gone.

“If you’re expecting to sleepwalk through this class, Ms. Weiss,” a woman’s voice blared from the last room on the floor, “you might as well drop it now.”

Yon strode to the open door. With an anxiousness that heightened and widened with each step.

He came upon a setup common at the school. Three tables stretched along three walls. It formed a square with the teacher’s desk in front.

Slouching at the back table by herself was Rudi. She was wearing spiked bracelets and a tattered white T-shirt held together by safety pins. Scrawled on it was “Black Flag” in marker, above four black bars drawn in the shape of a flag.

Like earlier, Yon grimaced.

How could he end up with such a girl? She couldn’t care about anyone.

He persuaded himself that his dream had to have been one. Yet he couldn’t budge. Neither himself nor his eyes. From Rudi and what she was about.

Darlene broke his trance. She was sitting in the center of a gaggle of girls. They were congregating by the table alongside the wall that separated the class from the hallway.

Back and forth Yon went. Between Darlene and Rudi. He compared the two.

Darlene was better. In each tangible way. Looks, in particular.

But it was in the other ways, the ways far from tangible, that she didn’t compare to Rudi.

Were these what mattered? Did they matter more than the danger she posed?

“Modern Poetry is among the most challenging courses in the school,” said Ms. Krasner. In black-rimmed glasses and a black turtleneck sweater.

The woman was both minuscule and towering. She towered bending over a table while rummaging through a stack of textbooks in the room’s far front corner. “You’ll write about poetry. You’ll write some yourself and do it well. Shelley was publishing verse at your age. So I’ll make no excuse for your youth.”

Darlene smirked. “She had Frankenstein for inspiration.”

The teacher sighed. It seethed from her. “Wrong Shelley. But it was a nice try, Ms. Cantor. Almost.”

Another sigh arose from Krasner. Though not as loud.

She picked out a book. She marched it to the back. “This is an elective. That means no one is forcing you to take it.”

The woman marched the book to Rudi. As Yon slanted from her line of vision.

Krasner sneered. At Rudi’s stab at apathy. She dropped the book onto the table in front of her.

It bounced.

Rudi yawned. With deliberateness.

Scowling, the teacher clenched her hands behind her back. “Have you ever read poetry?”

“Can she read?” Darlene asked.

Laughter cascaded through the classroom. Krasner had to cover her mouth.

“Can you?” Rudi shot back. “I mean other than Vogue.”

Everyone laughed louder. Krasner had to return her hand over her mouth. She had to leave it there longer. “Touché, Ms. Cantor.”

The woman galloped to the center blackboard. To the poem “since feeling is first.” In perfect script.

She immersed herself in its beauty. “What can anyone tell me about E. E. Cummings?”

Darlene humphed. “He wrote his name in lowercase.”

Rudi slammed her forearms onto the table. “That’s a myth.”

Krasner whipped herself around. “That’s right, Ms. Weiss. Can you tell me more about him? As in ‘more interesting’?”

“He did use unorthodox typography and grammar. It was a product of his hatred of convention and constraints. Of whatever got in his way. Including language.”

Rudi propped her feet on the table. She hung her hands behind her head. She lifted her chair back. “Basically, he was a punk.”

The teacher laughed. Without her hand over her mouth and less at Rudi’s assertion than at her. “Is that so? He didn’t dress the part.”

“Punk has nothing to do with dress. Or hair. Or music. It’s about attitude. He-was-a-punk.”

“You need to dig deeper, Ms. Weiss. Your argument is interesting but lazy. We’ll have none of that here. Way more is to Cummings than his attitude.”

Rudi and her chair smashed to the floor. She knotted her face. “Too bad that the same can’t be said of you.”

The woman blasted daggers at Rudi. Lots.

She returned to the poem. She read it aloud.

Poetry wasn’t Yon’s taste. It was less so than punk rock. So, with Krasner’s voice echoing through the classroom into the hallway, he went off.

 

kisses are a better

fate than wisdom

 

Yon froze. It wasn’t the words that froze him. But his reaction to them. Despite being unable to say what this was. His only certainty was that he did react. He was slipping toward the class and Krasner’s reciting.

 

the best gestures of my brain

is less than

your eyelids’ flutter which says

we are for each other

 

Yon was back in the room’s doorway.

Rudi had removed her feet from the desk. She was tilting forward. She was pushing the table over.

Krasner’s voice faded.

She whirled toward the class. With as much excitement as Rudi’s. It swept through the room. “His words mix splendor and dissonance, and he riddles them with the passion that he’s unleashed. Cummings was a Romantic. Or a ‘neo-Romantic,’ as some call him.”

The woman paused. For a fraction of a moment. “‘Shelley in a blender’ is what I call him. That’s Percy Shelley, Ms. Cantor.”

Krasner took another pause. She sat on the edge of her desk. “I want to see this passion unleashed in your next assignment. Due a week from today. You’ll craft me a poem worthy of Cummings and his magic. A poem that he might’ve crafted if he were you. And I’ll flunk anyone who does no more than play with their typography or grammar. I want to see passion. I want to see it all over the page!”

At that second, Rudi began to drift. Toward the door. She had registered that someone was there.

Her jaw sank on sight of Yon.

I picture that her insides had flipped turvy-topsy in less than a flicker. Nothing was conforming to what she had expected.

Yon’s jaw sank too. From her eyelids and how they had fluttered.

He stumbled away, his mind drowning in an unbearable uncertainty.

Had his eyelids been doing the same?

 

 

IN THE SCHOOL cafeteria at lunch, Rudi paraded through the center aisle with a tray laden with food. An eruption of it had taken place. With fruit on top.

Table after table she passed. Waves of heads crashed onto her from each direction.

She ignored them. Or she acted like she did.

I can say that she did care how others regarded her. This would anger her. It must’ve been why her ignoring amplified.

It didn’t last. She came upon someone that she couldn’t ignore. From a table of football players and beautiful girls. A someone with amber eyes.

Yon spiraled from Rudi.

He convinced himself that her eyelids hadn’t fluttered. That he had imagined it.

For how could some poet’s overromanticized notion of life be true?

Eyelids don’t flutter. Nor do people blush or swoon. We live in a rational world. 2 + 2 can’t equal 5.

So why was he shivering all over?

Yon snapped out of this. He became aware that Darlene was sitting next to him. That her arm was around his.

He wasn’t the only one aware of it.

Rudi twisted herself around. She stormed toward the back wall. No longer was she coming across apathetic or uprightly. She was about to smash through the bricks.

At the empty table by them she plopped down. She picked at her pile.

Dawdling in her trajectory was a boy with a more modest lunch. In another place, as he often was.

Eliot was bones and skin. With a pimply face and greasy brown hair, wearing a stained purple button-down polyester shirt, tan Haggar slacks whose cuffs dragged across the floor, and dirty white Keds.

He wasn’t cool. He was the kid bullies dream of. To the extent that some felt sympathy for him. Including those who would bully him anyway.

Eliot got to the back table and who was there. He stiffened mid-step.

Rudi couldn’t take much of this. Her snarl returned. “Can I help you?”

“I . . . I usually eat here.”

She clenched her fists. “So?”

He didn’t answer. He backed up. In slow motion he backed up. He backed up as if retreating from a roaring lioness, and that wasn’t nautical miles from the truth.

Rudi gave him no further notice. She took a notepad and a pen from her seabag.

Laying an eye on Yon, she doodled.

 

 

DARLENE BRUSHED HER hair back. She stewed at what Yon was unable to evade. “Can you believe that she’s in my poetry class?”

Yon, of course, could believe this. But he stayed quiet.

“She’s also in my chemistry class. She probably should be teaching it.”

“What do you mean?”

“She’s a big-time druggie.”

“How did you figure that out?”

“Lis has gym class with her. Her NA keychain fell to the floor when she was changing.”

“NA?”

“Narcotics Anonymous.”

Yon wanted this to make a difference. He wanted it to dissuade him from his snowballing obsession of Rudi. He wanted and wanted and wanted.

But it didn’t make a difference.

What could?

 

 

“I WAS DISAPPOINTED with the quiz’s results,” said Mrs. Elkind, a graying woman in a graying dress handing papers to her students in class.

Counted in this was Yon, horror-struck by the D on top of his from his regular front-row seat.

“You should’ve mastered conic sections by now. Few of you, I fear, have a chance of passing the AP exam.”

The woman was about to add further invective. But a presence had disturbed her.

Rudi sauntered into the classroom. She was clutching a crumpled printout of her class schedule. It could’ve been a fast-food wrapper that she was about to discard.

Elkind probed Rudi. She probed her down and up and several times over. With incredulity. At the girl’s discordance with the order of her classroom. “Yes?”

Rudi waved her schedule at the woman. She waved it with irreverence. “This is where I’m supposed to be.”

The teacher stormed to Rudi. Her incredulity elevated.

She grabbed the printout. She uncrumpled it. Her eyes lasered onto it. “We’ll see about that, Miss Weiss.”

Pain shot across Rudi. Mountains of it. “Ms. Weiss.”

“Excuse me?”

“Do I look like a ‘miss’?”

The woman pointed behind Rudi. “Solve the problem.”

Rudi skimmed over an equation. That was the length of a blackboard:

The teacher lowered her arm. “Use partial fraction decomposition to integrate that function.”

Rudi went back to the woman. “You’re joking, right?”

“I rarely joke about partial fraction decomposition.”

Rudi sighed. But she plowed to the board.

Picking up a piece of chalk, she weighed the problem.

In less time than that, she answered it. She filled the board’s remaining space with numbers and formulae. Yon gave up following it when she started taking arctangents of square roots.

The teacher stepped back. “That took me fifteen minutes to solve this morning.”

Rudi finished. She wrote “Q.E.D.” next to the solution. In letters that extended across another board.

Raising the chalk over her head, she dropped it onto the easel. She dropped it much as Ms. Krasner had dropped the textbook.

It bounced as the book had.

Elkind’s face lit up. “Quite easily done.”

Rudi let out another sigh. She frowned. “It means quod erat demonstrandum. That which required proving.”

“A girl after my own mind. You’ve earned your seat here, Ms. Weiss. Maybe you can help others keep theirs.”

Rudi snagged the schedule from the woman. She tramped to a seat by the back wall.

The teacher shuffled to the blackboard. “You can start by explaining to us how you completed the problem. As we’re mostly adults here, you can skip the polynomial division. As well as factoring the denominator. Get right to the decomposition.”

Rudi sighed a third time and louder. “I can’t see over that wavy hair.”

It struck Yon that she meant his hair. He fell back in his chair. He faced her. “Better?”

“Not really.”

Yon laughed.

It knocked Rudi sideways. I bet she couldn’t pretend anymore that he was stuck-up. Or boring.

 

 

YON FLOATED FROM a first-floor stairwell. Into the hallway. He and hundreds of others were leaving school.

He was numb and befuddled from his whirlwind day.

Or had it been longer?

He came to a restroom and the familiar female voice inside it. He halted.

“He doesn’t care about you!” Rudi howled, her voice unsteady.

Who could she be howling at?

“Nobody does! I don’t care about you!”

The door flew at Yon. It smacked him in the face.

Rudi and her discombobulation dashed from the bathroom in her coat.

The door returned to its frame. It swung like a pendulum.

Yon begged himself not to follow Rudi.

It was his choice in what he did with his life and his so-called replay. It was no one other’s. It sure wasn’t hers. He didn’t have to end up that peaked and skeletal and doomed figure that had been in the mirror. He could change his fate. It was so easy.

Still, he couldn’t not follow Rudi. He followed her down the hallway. He followed her when she went left toward the front doors. He followed her through them. He followed her outside.

She was trotting toward a crowd on the edge of Parker Avenue and a happening that had enraptured them. She approached Darlene, who was standing alongside Lis, a platinum blonde with a ponytail and girl-next-door looks. The two were whispering among themselves and snickering at Rudi.

Joey rambled behind them. He tapped Lis’s shoulder.

She twirled toward him.

He pitched his thumb behind himself. He started off. “Let’s go.”

Lis didn’t linger. She trailed behind Joey. As if tethered to him. She left Darlene to snicker at Rudi alone.

Rudi wouldn’t snatch the girl’s bait. She continued to the throng. She tiptoed over a shoulder.

Swelling with fury, she pushed her way through everyone. Yon wrote in his journal that she was Moses parting the sea. She was an irresistible force.

She got to the street. She stood above a diminutive golden-haired mutt, whimpering and bleeding in the road by the curb. The same mutt from Yon’s dream.

Rudi kneeled beside the animal. She swooped it into her arms. She rose with it and cradled it. She comforted it and calmed it.

Never had Yon beheld such gentleness. It belied what Rudi was about.

But what didn’t?

“There’s an animal hospital at the end of Valley,” a girl yelled.

Mariana was jumping up and down and about. She was flailing her arm down the road. “I volunteer there on weekends. The vet, Dr. Winston, is really awesome.”

Rudi examined the dog’s state. She kissed its head. “We wouldn’t get there in time.”

Yon vaulted into her view. In spite of himself. “But we can.”

 

 

RUDI CLUTCHED THE dog in her arms as she raced through the school parking lot with Yon.

They raced to a Harley Sturgis.

Her eyes spread wide while she caught her breath. “You drive a motorcycle?”

“I live dangerously.”

“I bet.”

Yon hopped onto the bike.

Rudi didn’t join him.

He sighed. “Come on.”

It took eons, but she crept onboard. She did so as if onto a hot stove.

Though she did no more.

He wheezed. “I suggest you put your arm around me. Unless you want to fly there.”

Rudi’s hesitation heightened. But she hoisted one arm around him while continuing to grasp the whining dog in the other.

They bolted from the lot. Onto Parker.

Picking up speed, they got to the corner of Valley. They zoomed left. They zipped through traffic. They weaved around cars and trucks. They ran both lights and stop signs.

Driving with recklessness Yon was good at. He repeated it and repeated it, wanting to make himself feel alive and wanting this to have meaning. But never had it brought him any. Coming close to death didn’t bring it.

Only now had meaning come.

 

 

IN THE PACKED and ultra-bright animal hospital waiting area, Yon and Rudi were scrunching next to each other, their eyes straight ahead.

Yon was doing what he could to not admit to himself how good he felt pressed against her. Both there and on the bike. “The dog’s gonna be okay.”

Rudi gave him another of her snarls. “You can’t be sure of that.”

“Actually, I can.”

“What?”

“You’ve got some blood on your coat.”

She inspected it. “It’s not the first. You’ve got some too. On the back of your jacket.”

“It’s okay.”

“Thanks. I mean, thanks for doing this.”

A veterinarian in a white lab coat verged upon them. She was reading from a chart.

The two stood. They waited for the woman.

Dr. Winston stopped by them. She went on with her reading. “She’s in bad shape.”

Rudi slumped over.

The vet lowered the chart. Her demeanor picked up. “She’s got a lengthy recovery. But she’ll be fine.”

Rudi lifted herself up. “Yeah?”

“It’s fortunate that you got her here so fast.”

Neither Rudi nor Yon spoke.

Dr. Winston eyeballed them. “I assume that you two can’t pay for this.”

Yon pulled out his wallet. “I can.”

He withdrew a credit card. He handed it to her.

The woman took it and took off. She left the two to search for words.

They were well out of their reach.

Rudi backed up. With abrupt unease she did. She dragged her feet from Yon. “I should be going.”

“You want a lift?”

“I’m fine.”

She moved with greater hurry. She bumped into a wall.

Veiling her embarrassment, she fled from the office.

Yon’s eyes didn’t stir from her. They hadn’t stirred after she was gone.

 

 

YON LAY ON his bed. He was scribbling in his notebook.

Crumpled pages were around him on the floor. “Over Under Sideways Down” was playing on his stereo.

He scribbled what had happened that day. Many times over. He also sketched scenes on the papers’ edges.

Getting the words close to right, he dropped his pen.

The music changed. Into the same Glenn Miller song from his dream. Or whatever it had been. But now it was playing in his head.

He was there too. He was dancing with someone in his arms. Someone he couldn’t see. They were cheek to cheek. With his heartbeat matching not only hers but the beat bound by the trombone.

Never had Yon been so perfect. So himself. He prayed for the rising melody to never fall.

“Yon, you up?” his mother said from outside his door.

The music changed back into the Yardbirds. Yon opened his eyes.

He was clutching a pillow.

“Yon?”

He heaved the pillow onto the floor. “Yeah?”

“How about inviting Darlene over for dinner tomorrow? I’d love to see her.”

“Some other time.”

The sole response was the steps of his mother sputtering off.

What Yon couldn’t hear was Glenn Miller. No matter what he did or how much he did it, the song wouldn’t return. Not a fragment.

His mind wandered. To a girl and what she was doing. It did so no matter how assured he was that it shouldn’t have wandered there. That his life depended on it not getting within a light year of there.

 

 

MY MIND WANDERED to that girl too. “What were you doing?”

She didn’t want to tell me. But she didn’t have a choice if she wanted me to resume reading her the story.

“I was banging against the walls in the dump I was staying at,” she said through her moaning.

“You were what?”

“I was listening to Bad Brains’ Yellow Tape, which a friend had given me after I got out of jail, and I was doing what I did whenever I listened to it. H.R., his voice would reach out and grasp me and throw me into wall after wall. It does this today. I bang into walls whenever I listen to them, and I can tell you that it raises eyebrows at work.

“I listen to Richard Hell when I’m afraid, but I listen to Bad Brains when I want to forget. That night, ‘Big Take Over’ was blasting into my head. It was a jackhammer into concrete. I listened to it six thousand times. Nothing but the music existed as I hurled into walls, breaking plaster everywhere. I was in such a lather that my body stopped tormenting me over drugs. Or I could pretend that it did. But . . .”

“But what?” I said.

“A fist hit a wall from the other side. It came close to punching through it. A man hollered, ‘Stop that racket!’

“I didn’t holler back. I fell to the floor. I gasped for breath. Sweat was running down my face. My arms were around my legs. I was willing myself to a different place. Any but that one.

“But this didn’t work. It never did.

“‘There’s some fries out here if you want,’ he said next.

“But I didn’t want them. I especially didn’t want them from him.”

My tone turned playful. “Yon wasn’t on your mind?”

She growled. “Why do you think I was listening to Bad Brains? I had been listening to them since I’d left the animal hospital. They had me running over parked cars.”

“But now you couldn’t listen to them.”

“The drawing did have Ving’s eyes. They were piercing into me when rendered in two dimensions. But the hair was too wavy. The nose and chin were too chiseled.

“Then it hit me who was staring at me from the drawing.

“Furious at myself, I ripped out the page and crumpled it. I threw it across the floor. But . . .”

I tipped forward in my seat. “But what?”

“This accomplished nothing. So I slunk over to the paper. I uncrumpled it. I forced myself to accept that my attraction was hopeless. ‘You’re so not good enough,’ my voice rasped. It was unsteadier than it had been in the washroom.

“I shook my head. I put it everywhere but on the drawing and the mirror across the room and what it would’ve reflected back to me. ‘You’re so not him. And this, this is so not a fairy tale.’”

I exhaled. “I have one last question.”

“What’s that?”

“The person that was facing you from the drawing, had his eyelids fluttered?”

“Some of this I’ll keep to myself.”

“That was a yes,” I blurted out.

I turned the page. I was back at the beginning. “That, that’s it.”

Fumbling about, I shuffled through the entries. I was anxious for more. More anxious than I had been in years.

But no more entries were anywhere.

“What do you mean?” she said.

“That was the final page he sent me.”

“But that’s not the story’s end.”

“But that’s all he sent.”

“Today.”

“What?”

“That’s all he sent you today.”

“What do you mean?”

“There are seven more nights of Chanukah.”

“He’s gonna send me more? I mean, he sent me more?”

“Call me tomorrow night.”

Not a doubt was in her voice that I would have a reason to.

 

 

I SHOVELED YON’S pages back into the envelope.

It was past midnight. The moon had buried itself behind clouds. It had taken the palm trees and the canyons with it. The one gleam shone from the courtyard. It burned into the night. It would throughout it.

I had to work the next morning. But I couldn’t go to bed. I had to get a bus pass. So I could get to work. Getting it in the morning had been iffy.

This getting was a ritual. Regardless of who was working the night shift. But with Josh it was the worst. Some nights he would make me stand outside the office till my feet ached, enjoying my reliance on him.

He didn’t make me wait much that night. He got up and tugged the door back. “Yes?”

I told him.

He grumbled. But he led me to a room beside the mess hall.

In the corner of it, he unlocked a vault that was taller than him. He snatched from it a stack of day passes that the shelter got for free.

He offered me a pass.

But he wouldn’t let it go. “This is a pretty sweet deal you get here. Free transportation each day.”

“I’d buy my own pass if you let me spend the money I earn.”

“That’s right. We’d throw you out if we caught you spending money.”

He released the pass. But not without adding, “We could throw you out for just about any reason. Any at all.”

I didn’t respond. Not in any way. I put aside how I wished I could and went off. I stuffed the pass into my wallet. I returned to the dorm.

Slithering down the hall, I passed an open door to sleeping quarters on my left. Here not only the women slept but the children. Teenage boys had to sleep there.

This must’ve been uncomfortable for the women. But leaving the shelter would’ve been worse. While nobody wanted to be on the streets of Victorville in the winter, the cold was the least bad awaiting women there. It made them more dependent on the place than the men.

Our quarters were past them across the hall. I pushed myself through the doorway. To snoring so loud that it could’ve woken a cemetery. Including the one in Arlington.

I made my way through the dark. In a tomb filled with dozens of bunk beds and the weary men on top of them. I stumbled to the back. To my bed.

With a heave, I climbed to the top. I kicked off my shoes. I took off my coat and jeans. I slid them under the pillow with Yon’s letter.

Stretching out on the slight and rotting mattress, I struggled to fall asleep. This struggling was another ritual. On this night, it was the same and not.

The difference was that I was excited about a next day.
