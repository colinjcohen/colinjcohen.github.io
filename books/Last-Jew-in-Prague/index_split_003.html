chapter three: a promise unsaid

 

 

IN A GENDARMERIE closet lit by a single dim bulb, Hermann stood in the mirror hanging on the door. He was putting on the musty off-black suit that Klaus had given him.

The suit was too big. It was so big that it made him a clown. But not by intention. There were no suits in his size. In the camp or in any place.

This didn’t make Hermann happier. There wasn’t only a clown in the mirror. He had hollow cheeks, complemented by sunken eyes and a complexion as coarse as sandpaper. His earlier shave and shower had accentuated this and made him look more ridiculous.

He stopped. With both the suit and the absurdity. His anger rising, he called out Klaus’s name, believing that the humiliation he was feeling had made him no longer complicit in what would come next.

Klaus groaned from the door’s other side. “What is it?”

“I’ve changed my mind, I want . . .”

Hermann couldn’t finish. A vision had broken his concentration. Of his Ana.

She wasn’t in the mirror or in the closet. Nor was she in his imagination. Like she had been on his way to the gendarmerie. She existed beyond it. In a memory that had come to life. It was as vivid as the present, returning him years earlier to an evening in his old apartment on Eliška Krásnohorská Street in Josefov.

 

 

ANA WAS IN their living room. Along with her unsold paintings.

They had been there since the Reich had closed her art gallery, after determining her work a form of “degenerate art.” Which they determined with inconsistency and when purposes suited them. Such as when punishing the wife of a prominent Jew.

She was steps from the paintings. She was brooding over them from her rocking chair. She had told Hermann that her whole life’s arc was in her works. From her carefree student days to her search for greater purpose as she approached midlife. Right up to the occupation’s despair.

But Hermann believed that she viewed herself as a piece of art as well. Art that would never realize itself or leave the slightest of footprints.

This was his fault.

Hermann studied the paintings. He sensed how their subjects had grown darker in recent years. Ana’s early works, while not joyous, had expressed the promise of joy and the hope that accompanied it. But her newer ones expressed the inevitability of disappointment and ruin. This was truest of her latest painting. It depicted the buildings in their neighborhood leaning over each other in the dead of night. About to collapse. Their building was in the center of it.

Hermann shoved his eyes from the painting. Toward the record playing on the phonograph behind Ana: Zdena Vincíková’s “Smutná neděle.”

Known as “The Hungarian Suicide Song,” in the years since Rezső Seress had composed it, singers had recorded it in many languages. In countries throughout the world.

Some of these had banned it. Its tale of mad desperation had been the alleged source of a rash of deaths. Including in the English-speaking world, where Billie Holiday had sung a haunting rendition of it called “Gloomy Sunday” that few listeners could forget.

The record hypnotized Hermann. It made him face the window and the escape it offered.

Another sound brought him out of his stupor: an envelope had slid under their front door.

He crept over to it. With slow and deliberate movements. He picked it up and opened it.

Inside was an official-looking letter. Along with an abundance of stars made from cheap yellow cloth.

He didn’t get the chance to read the letter.

Ana grabbed it and the stars. She read the letter herself.

Clutching them both in her fist, she collected his coats from the nearby closet and his suit jackets from the bedroom that was steps from it.

“I won’t do it,” he called out from outside the bedroom.

Balling up the envelope, he flung it onto the floor. “I won’t wear them. V žádném případě!”

Ana didn’t respond to any of this. Including his affirmation of his intentions in Czech, a language he spoke to her when he couldn’t express in German what he had wanted to say in the way he had wanted to say it. Ignoring it in its entirety, she returned from the bedroom with the garments and her sewing kit. Along with a look that muted his fury. The same look that she had given him on the way to the gendarmerie.

She went back to her rocking chair and began working, appearing relieved. She could take her eyes from her misery.

You couldn’t say the same of Hermann. His wife sewing the stars into his clothes brought his fury roaring back. This was out of character for him but not without reason. It was bad enough to suffer the stars’ humiliation. Worse was making her a part of it.

His fury built. Exploding, he stormed to her. He grabbed the letter from the chair’s armrest, ripping it into shreds. He grabbed his clothes from her too. “I’ll dare them to shoot me. I’ll go down to the street right now and dare them!”

Ana rose from her chair. She shook her head in a slow but continuous manner. This she would do—not when she wanted to express disapproval of his actions—but when she wanted him to do so himself.

“They’re gonna shoot me anyway,” he said, wanting to cry but unable to do so.

She grabbed the clothes from him. “But not now. Until then there’s now.”

Sinking back into the rocking chair, she continued her sewing. She conveyed an emotion that went beyond relief. It was happiness.

Hermann couldn’t figure out why she had. He would tell me that it was the single mystery of any significance that he could never solve.

 

 

I WOULD LEARN the source of Ana’s joy. It happened after the Iron Curtain fell and I could travel to Prague.

Visiting the National Gallery, among the largest art museums in Central Europe, I stumbled upon a pair of Ana’s paintings in a far-off corner.

The first depicted a mass of people smashing into each other in the streets, getting angry but going nowhere. The second was a self-portrait. She had broken into pieces and bits and was desperate to arrange them in the right order. Despite failing at this, her determination wouldn’t.

For hours, I sat by the paintings. I sought the cause of Ana’s happiness that evening in their apartment. Beyond that, I sought what would push her to the choice she would later make. The choice that would so affect Hermann’s life and the lives of many others. Including my own. I so sat by her paintings that someone from the museum’s staff strutted up to me. He was curious as to why I was interested in such an obscure artist.

I told him.

He pointed at the paintings. “They have an interesting history. Someone smuggled them to Switzerland during the occupation, hidden in a bridal chest. The authorities would’ve burned them otherwise. Along with the rest of her work. There’s such insolence in them. They’re spitting at what the Nazis were about.”

“What can you tell me about her?”

“Nothing. Other than that her talent was in excess. But I could give you the number of the woman who donated the paintings, Věra Davidová. She’s a benefactor of ours.”

I jumped from my seat. I got the number and called the woman. She agreed to meet me the following day in a restaurant on top of her office building in the city’s New Town district.

I couldn’t wait for this to happen. But on arriving, I had difficulty concentrating. The table overlooked the Vltava River and its surroundings. It provided a view straight from a childhood fable.

“I knew Ana,” the aging woman murmured.

This shook off my daze. “You did?”

“My mother was her patron until . . . until we had to leave the city.”

My eyes shifted to the woman’s forearm. Tattooed on it was the same kind of number that had been on Hermann’s.

I leaned over the table. “Did you know my grandfather?”

“Not really. I knew who he was. Everyone at Theresienstadt did. But he kept to himself, understandably, and we never approached him. My mother didn’t want him to relive what had happened to Ana.

“I’m trying to better understand Ana and what my grandfather meant to her.”

“She loved him very much. I can tell you that. Whenever she visited us, she talked less about herself and her work than she did of him. It was as if he were her work, and I suppose he was.”

This was why Ana was happy that evening in their apartment. She must’ve grasped that her life’s arc wasn’t in her paintings. They were nothing but representations of it while the actuality was in the man who had been beside her throughout it. Through him her life had realized itself. She must’ve felt that it would stay this way if he stayed beside her.

 

 

“YOU MUST PROMISE me,” Ana told Hermann from her rocking chair. “You must promise me that, no matter how impossible it may be, you’ll wait through whatever ‘now’ you have left. If it’s to last seconds more.”

He snarled. “I won’t.”

Further happiness fell over Ana.

This time, Hermann could guess why. She had heard the promise in his voice. He had heard it as well.

 

 

“YOU’VE CHANGED YOUR mind about what?” Klaus howled from the closet door’s other side, over musical instruments tuning in the courtyard. “I’m not enjoying this anymore than you. And I can end it at any time. Say the word.”

The closet and the mirror and Hermann’s current reality returned. With it, he wanted to say that “word.” He opened his mouth to say it.

But returning to the present didn’t mean that Ana had gone or that his memory of her had left. They’d stuck with him. Along with the promise he had made.

Earlier, when he had agreed to Klaus’s demands, he believed that he could change his mind. He could do so whenever he had a nominal excuse.

But this was a lie. He could have no complicity in his death. No matter what excuses he made. Ana had made sure of this. She’d made sure that he would live as much as he could. He’d pursue the murderer of Nazis if it meant living for seconds more.

Though that didn’t mean that he had to like it. So, as he reached for the door, he made himself another promise.
