chapter twelve: it happened on kant street

 

 

HERMANN WOULD SAY that the mystery of Klaus was vaster than that of who murdered the men.

In some respects he knew Klaus well. In others he knew him not whatsoever. In so many ways, he would remain a mystery.

It was during my trip to Prague that I would uncover some of this mystery. This began when I visited Klaus’s hometown in the country’s north. In what had been part of the Sudetenland.

I made out right away that there wasn’t much to indicate that the city and its surrounding towns had had German names and German-majority populations. After World War II, under the Beneš Decrees, Czechoslovakia expelled most of its German citizens from the area.

These people had been living there as far back as Jews had been living in Prague. But they had also been among the Nazis’ most ardent supporters. They would count among the fifteen million ethnic Germans compelled to flee from their homes across Europe in that period.

I wouldn’t discover a lot about Klaus in the city where he was born, raised, and buried. Apart from his gravestone, I located his birth and death certificates and various unimportant documents from his early life. But I could learn nothing about what had happened to him from the time of his falling out with Hermann in college to his reuniting with him in Theresienstadt.

For that, I had to return to Prague and visit the Military History Institute and its archives on U Památníku Street. Archives that I would discover I couldn’t access. They were open to accredited historians alone, and I was not.

But the chase was not lost. My grandpa had taught me how to become fast friends with the locals.

This entailed taking a trip to Karlovy Vary, a city a few hours to the capital’s west that is famous for its hot springs and Becherovka, an herbal liqueur produced there that in my grandpa’s day had been called Karlsbader Becherbitter.

From the Jan Becher company store, I bought bottles of a special edition of Becherovka that was infused with honey and only sold there. Which Grandpa had told me could put a smile on the face of the Devil.

These bottles would open doors for me. Such as the institute’s.

I spent many weeks in its stuffy rooms, digging through stacks of mundane war documents with a German dictionary and my rudimentary knowledge of a language that I had never learned. Despite my grandparents being native speakers and my own special connection to it. I dug until I discovered that, in the aftermath of this case, the Nazis had conducted a thorough inquest into it.

This wouldn’t provide insight into Klaus’s thoughts and feelings at the time the murders had happened. In this respect, he is as much of a mystery to me as he was to my grandpa. But I was able to cobble together facts that are relevant to the story and speak to Klaus’s motivations. They started with the events that coincided with Hermann’s return to his old apartment.

 

 

KLAUS LEFT ČERNÍN Palace with the sun setting over the fairy tale.

He came upon his Mercedes and the driver inside it. But he also came upon U Černého Vola across the square.

Klaus’s driver would tell the inquest that Klaus was standing there, glaring at the pub. I suppose much as he had been glaring while Hermann sipped his beer.

He took steps toward it. But he stopped. He rushed into the back seat of his car.

Slamming the door, he ordered the driver to leave.

The Mercedes sped off. It made its way through Hradčany and over the river. Passing through the maze, it returned Klaus to Old Town Square. This was a short distance from his destination that evening: Železná Street, an ancient and crooked cobblestone road leading from the square’s southern end.

Klaus slithered from the car. He crossed the square. At the same time as a tram.

Heedless of it speeding in his path, he didn’t stop or speed up. With indifference, he went over the tracks seconds before the tram passed.

Reaching Železná, he made a half revolution. Toward a sun taking its last breaths over the horizon.

According to the driver, Klaus’s face had lost color. He appeared elsewhere.

Where he was, I can’t say. But the inquest had brought up Klaus’s troubled past. An incident that sticks out took place on Berlin’s Kant Street in 1928. It does because, during this case, Klaus would have an adverse reaction whenever anyone brought up the philosopher’s name.

 

 

KLAUS STAGGERED FROM The Eldorado club late one September evening.

It was so late that the streets were empty.

But they didn’t stay this way.

Two men ran from their parked Dixi 3/15, a forerunner of today’s BMWs. They accosted Klaus. They beat him against a club wall.

According to a witness inside the club, who had viewed this through a window, Klaus could’ve handled the men. His state wasn’t what had prevented him.

The witness said that Klaus had egged the two on. He was sure that Klaus had wanted them to hurt him.

He laughed at their blows. In defiance of their savagery. He laughed till he could do so no more.

 

 

BACK ON ŽELEZNÁ Street, Klaus shifted his feet.

He was facing U Hynků. The pub Hermann had mentioned. Their favorite drinking spot in college.

As he had by U Černého Vola, Klaus took steps toward the pub.

But he halted. He started down Železná.

He made his way through the arched entranceway of number 18, a sixteenth-century building that today looks older than that. Inside it is a courtyard and old stone steps. Up the latter he climbed to the second floor. He rang the bell.

The door opened. A servant around thirty with bleached-blonde hair and blue eyes greeted him in a black uniform.

Klaus forced a smile at her. “Hello, Kamča.”

Kamča responded in kind. She let him into the foyer. She took his overcoat. “The lady is getting dressed.”

Klaus shuffled down the corridor toward a door, eyed by Kamča. She was hanging his coat.

“Is that you, Klaus?” chirped a woman’s voice from behind the door.

“It’s me,” Klaus said, standing in a mirror at the hallway’s back. Kamča would tell the inquest that he was doing so touching the lines and wrinkles around his eyes. Their existence had surprised him.

“I’ll be ready in a few minutes.”

Klaus looked like he wanted to cry. “All right.”

“Will you be staying overnight?”

“If you don’t mind.”

“You know I don’t. You’re welcome anytime. Where would you like to go tonight?”

Klaus wanted to answer. He was desperate for one. “Could we, could we stay in tonight?”

“Are you not feeling well?”

“I’m feeling tired. But if you want to go out . . .”

“Staying home would be a wonderful idea. I’ll have Kamča cook us up some dish. What would you like?”

“It doesn’t matter.”

“You sure you’re just tired? You sound off.”

“I am off.”

“What’s wrong, dear? Does it have to do with that nasty investigation you’ve been involved with?”

“I guess so.”

“I get that you can’t discuss the specifics of it with me. But can’t you tell me what’s bothering you?”

“Nothing’s bothering me.”

“Did you have a setback today?”

“It was the opposite. I have confidence that we will resolve the case soon.”

“Shouldn’t that make you happy?”

Klaus strove to convince himself of this. “It should.”

The door to the bedroom swept open.

Klaus swayed toward it and a woman wearing a lavish white evening gown.

Years later, the woman would insist to me that Klaus, in some ways, had adored her. She said that she was a wall that he could lean on. She also provided him a safety that he couldn’t get otherwise.

But she’d believed as well that she drained him.

Like with Kamča, Klaus forced a smile upon the woman. Based on what I know about him, I bet he also forced the belief upon himself that her appearance had brought him some joy.
