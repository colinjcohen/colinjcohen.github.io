chapter thirty-nine: what lurks in the shadows

 

 

HERMANN AND KLAUS sat at a kitchen table. Beside cups of weak tea. With Dita and her mother.

“We’re trying to discover who murdered your boyfriend,” Hermann said to Dita, whose dirty-blonde hair had been tied behind her head and whose tears had smeared her mascara.

She wiped her eyes. “Husband. We were married right before he disappeared. Oftentimes, he told me that he feared for his life. But I was sure that it was nerves. I was sure that the responsibility of marriage had been too great for him. That he’d walked out on me. I had no . . .”

“You can help us find his killer. You’re the only one who can.”

“I can’t see how.”

“Did he tell you why he had been at the Klausen Synagogue so much?”

Dita edged toward her mother.

Klaus snorted. “We are going to get answers.”

Hermann was certain that this tactic wouldn’t be helpful. He interrupted Klaus. By putting up his hand. “In no way will you or your family get into trouble for what your husband was involved in. Isn’t that right, Captain?”

Klaus sat back in his chair. “Yes. I won’t mention your name in my report. My sole responsibility is to discover and apprehend those responsible for your husband’s murder.”

Hermann added, “And we can only do this if we can grasp the nature of the scheme your husband was involved in. It’s likely your husband’s killer was involved in this scheme too.”

Dita exhaled. “What do you want me to tell you?”

“You met Captain Fischer while working at U Malířů?”

“He flirted with me when I took his coat. Lots of men flirted with me when I took their coats. But he was different. He didn’t treat me like some dumb blondýnka because of how I look. And I’m not dumb. I went to Minerva.”

“A good school. My wife went there.”

“I would’ve graduated if it weren’t for the war.”

“My husband died,” Dita’s mother interjected. “He left us with no income. I couldn’t afford the tuition.”

Dita sat up straight. “Max saw right away that I was smart. On our first date, we discussed Hegel and Schopenhauer. Kant too. He was impressed that I had written a paper about Kant’s Critique of Pure Reason.”

This tidbit pricked up Hermann’s ears. “He and Kant were both from Königsberg.”

Dita’s face lit up. “They were baptized in the same church.”

The reference to Kant must’ve caused Klaus to relive memories that he didn’t want to relive. He flinched and shied away.

“If we could get back to the night at U Malířů,” Hermann said to Dita. “It’s our understanding that Captain Fischer was there with two men.”

“Colonel Reidl and his aide.”

“Do you know Colonel Reidl?”

“Max told me about him later. After we got serious. He didn’t like the man. He especially didn’t like the man’s aide. He told me that he didn’t trust either. That he was only involved with them because he had no choice.”

“Did he ever bring up the dinner that he had with Colonel Reidl at U Malířů?”

“Please understand that Max was not a perfect man. He told me this on our first date. He told me that he had some . . . indiscretions with women in the past. That he’d gotten into trouble at work because of it. He also owed a lot of money. Anyway, he met Colonel Reidl at a party before we met, and he promised to make Max’s problems go away. His money problems the most. That’s why they were meeting for dinner that night.”

“How was Reidl going to make your husband’s money problems go away?”

“He didn’t tell me. He said that he couldn’t. He said that he had promised to keep it a secret. But he said that we’d soon never have to worry about money.”

“Did he often worry about money? Or talk about it?”

“Not really. I . . .”

“He asked me,” Dita’s mother interrupted, “he asked me if I knew someone who could buy valuables. I thought he meant a pawn shop. But he wanted someone who could buy very expensive items. Such as fine jewelry and gold bullion and priceless heirlooms and a substantial quantity.”

“What did you tell him?” Hermann said.

“That I didn’t know anyone like that. And this is the truth. He never brought it up again.”

Hermann returned to Dita. “Did your husband mention any of his activities at the Klausen? A detail that may seem insignificant could be an important clue.”

“I don’t recall him mentioning anything specific about what he was doing there. Like I said, he couldn’t tell me what he was doing. But . . .”

Hermann inched forward. “But what?”

“He would often tell me how frustrated he was with the whole ‘project,’ as he called it. Each night this got worse, and he came home later and later. Till he didn’t come home at all.”

“You said that he feared for his life.”

“He believed, he believed that something was watching him in the synagogue.”

“‘Something’? Don’t you mean ‘someone’?”

“It’s so, so bláznivé. Just crazy. But he was certain that there was a golem in the synagogue.”

Hermann and Klaus reacted in the same way. They swung toward the other with their brows raised.

Shaking this off, Hermann went back to Dita. “Why would he believe that?”

“He would hear footsteps at night. Footsteps that he insisted couldn’t be human. He was sure that this golem was pursuing him in the building. He told me that it got closer to him each night. Then one evening there it was, lurking in the shadows: a big giant monster. But like I told you, I was positive that this was Max’s nerves. That he was anxious about being married so unexpectedly. That this golem was a manifestation of this.”

Hermann had one last question: “Do you have any of your husband’s belongings?”

“I have a few of his clothes.”

“And?”

“I might have a paper or two.”

“May we see them?”

Dita climbed from the table. With deliberateness and care.

It struck Hermann that she was pregnant.

 

 

DITA CAME BACK with a slim stack of papers. She and her mother left the kitchen.

One paper stood out to Hermann. A blueprint.

He opened it. He identified that it was of the Klausen Synagogue.

Klaus identified this too. He jabbed his finger at the page. “A cellar.”

“Now we know why the captain was so insistent about its existence.”

“Where could he have gotten this?”

“It doesn’t matter. What does is that it must’ve led to his obsession with getting into the cellar.”

Hermann collected the papers. He gave them to Dita. He said goodbye to her and her mother.

He and Klaus exited the apartment. They went down the hallway. They descended the stairs.

Klaus’s face turned white. This increased with each step he took.

Hermann bent in his direction. “What’s wrong?”

“You still believe that a golem is preposterous?”

“I still believe it. This is the twentieth century, Klaus. I reside firmly within it. You as well. Besides, if there really were a golem roaming around town, I can tell you that I wouldn’t be the last Jew in Prague. The killer was trying to scare Fischer away. From whatever he was searching for. That’s all there is to it.”

Hermann said all this. But he didn’t believe it all. The existence of a golem wasn’t any more preposterous than the button that had been in his hand the night before. Or the old brass compass and rose petal that were right now in his pockets. A golem was less preposterous. Which made him feel a fear deeper than what Klaus was expressing.

Klaus flailed his arms. “How do we discover what Fischer was searching for?”

“That, of course, is the klíčová záležitost, as Czechs would say. The key to this whole mystery. But I bet it has to do with that ducat in Kleiner’s pocket. I want to go back to the Klausen. So I can ask Lieutenant Krieg some more questions.”

“She’ll be overjoyed.”
