chapter fifty-seven: a stunning realization

 

 

THE TUNNEL LEADING from the Pinkas Synagogue to whereabouts unknown was longer than what connected the synagogue with the High. Hermann and Klaus followed Gertrud and the men for a half hour without getting to an end.

“Where could they be going?” Klaus whispered to Hermann. While more overwhelmed with each step that he took and recognizing many signs that they passed.

“I can’t say. But we’re heading toward the river. Last night, Reidl stopped by the Čech Bridge. Or whatever you people are calling it. He gazed out over the water. Several docks are in the area. That could be how they’re planning to move the Torahs. By ship.”

“It would be faster than getting them through the checkpoints on the roads. It’d be safer too.”

“And after they reach the Elbe, they could take the Torahs to the North Sea and beyond. They could take them anywhere in the world. Assuming no one captured them. Or torpedoed them. Or bombed them from the air. Which is a pretty big assumption. It’d be an incredible risk. But they could believe that some entity will not only guide them but protect them as well.”

Klaus made no response.

Hermann turned toward him.

He was shaking in fright. “Didn’t you hear that?”

“Hear what?”

“Something is following us.”

“I don’t hear it.”

“It’s the golem, I tell you. It’s following us.”

“Stay in this century, Klaus. I beg you.”

Klaus wanted to do this. But he kept lurching back. He kept telling Hermann that, whatever it was behind them, it was getting closer.

Gertrud and her companions got to the tunnel’s end. They stopped in front of a stone wall.

Tonda withdrew his shofar from the cart. He blew into it.

It roared.

A wall’s section separated. Tonda and Luděk opened it.

Behind it was the river and a boat by the docks. Named the Phoenix.

I shall multiply my days like the phoenix.

Resolve spread across Klaus’s face. Without saying a word, he raced toward Gertrud and the men. With increasing speed and desperation he moved. He reached them at the same time that they wheeled their cart onto the dock.

“You can stop now,” he called out, panting for breath.

He whisked out his pistol. He pointed it at them.

The three obeyed. They turned to Klaus.

Hermann joined him. He was also out of breath from keeping up with Klaus.

“Hello, darling,” Gertrud said. By the looks of her, seeing Klaus there was normal and expected.

He glared at her. With as much contempt as he could muster. “Darling? I don’t know you.”

“Don’t be upset. Sometimes I’m not sure if I know me.”

“You used me.”

“We used each other, dear. You needed someone to call your girlfriend, and I needed a connection into your office. I call that quid pro quo.”

More puzzle parts became clear to Hermann. The missing elements from the mystery and Gertrud had shown themselves. Or most had.

He recalled the conversation at dinner the night earlier. He leaned toward Gertrud. “Was it you who told the Allies that Heydrich would be driving to Berlin on the day he was ambushed?”

She shifted away. From both men. She crossed her arms. “I simply confirmed what they had suspected.”

“This country owes you a debt it can never repay.”

“The debt lies with me alone. I will live with the faces of Lidice etched in my head. Till my last gasp.”

“If Heydrich were alive, he’d be doing worse. Much worse.”

“You do realize that you won’t live through the night?” Klaus hollered at Gertrud, his anger increasing with each word. “Worse, you’ll wish that it would finish sooner than that.”

Gertrud snarled at Klaus. “Spare me your threats. I watched my husband murdered in my own home.”

“What are you talking about? He committed suicide. You told me so yourself.”

“I told you what I was told to tell everyone.”

“I don’t understand.”

Gertrud directed her response to Hermann. “My husband was the deputy minister of defense under General Syrový when the Germans invaded. Because he was German, they expected he’d take over for the general. They all came to our house one morning. Reich Minister Frank and his henchmen. I wanted to send them away. But my husband was more diplomatic. He decided to hear Frank out in his study. That’s when I heard the gunshot.”

“So how do you know that he didn’t shoot himself?” Klaus yelped.

“Because on hearing the gunshot,” Gertrud barked, “I ran into the study. Frank was hovering over my husband’s body with his Luger in his hand. Smoke was pouring out of it. My husband, he would’ve never worked for you. He despised you. He was ashamed to call himself German.”

“I still don’t get it. How were you able to ingratiate yourself among us? It wasn’t my doing.”

“It was Frank’s. He was supposed to recruit my husband. Not kill him. But my husband got him so mad that he lost his head. Calling it a suicide was the sole way he could save face and his career.”

“But why did you play along?”

“Because he made me a deal. Tell people that it was a suicide and I could be the ‘belle of the ball,’ as he put it.”

Hermann lowered his eyes. “All three of us made deals.”

“But I made mine for this moment alone.”

Klaus had one question left for Gertrud: “How were you able to hide that you’re a Jew?”

“I never hid it. Everyone assumed what I was because of my husband and because of the men I’ve dated and because of how I look and act. They could’ve come upon the truth if they had wanted to come upon it. But that would’ve run counter to the filth you spew about how different we are.”

“I don’t know what to say.”

“Nothing, Klaus. You can say nothing. And you can do nothing further to me than what’s already been done. Shall we go?”

But Hermann hadn’t asked Gertrud the most important question. So he asked it now: “Did you murder those men or not?”

“Murder what men?” she howled.

Klaus lifted his gun at her. “We’ll be asking the questions. Let’s go.”

“Let’s not,” bellowed a baritone voice to Klaus’s left.

It drew everyone.

Colonel Reidl was pointing a pistol at Klaus. With a gleam on his face. “Put the gun down, Captain.”

Klaus didn’t.

“I will shoot you,” Reidl said with matter-of-factness. “I could’ve had you shot anytime. I’ve known about your proclivities from the start of your assignment. You’ve just been more useful to me alive. But not anymore. So drop the gun.”

Klaus stayed still. But there wasn’t much he could do.

He placed the pistol on the ground.

“Good girl,” Reidl cooed.

Luděk rushed to him.

The colonel thumbed at the man. While smirking at Tonda. “You made a single mistake, my friend. You didn’t know that your man was working for me.”

Tonda’s shoulders fell. “Nobody’s perfect.”

“I do hope you didn’t think I believed that ‘honest broker’ nonsense you were spewing. Or the lies you were telling me about delivering the crate.”

“About that crate—” Luděk interjected.

“—Put it on the boat.”

“They’ve double-crossed us, Colonel. Only junk is in the crate. A hunk of Jewish junk.”

Taken aback, Reidl swung his gun toward Gertrud. “Where is it? Where’s the treasure?”

Gertrud didn’t say a word.

Reidl aimed his gun at her head. “I suggest you come up with an answer. Quickly.”

“Silence can be an answer,” Hermann said.

Reidl whipped himself toward Hermann. He trained his gun on him. He sneered. “Speaking of silence . . .”

Hermann didn’t flinch. He had been ready for fate to catch up with him.

But Klaus wasn’t so ready.

He jumped in front of Hermann. “No!”

Reidl fired.

The bullet pierced the right side of Klaus’s body below his shoulder, sending him into the arms of Hermann.

Hermann struggled to hold on. He would say that what held Klaus up was the stunning realization that came with his actions.

Reidl shook his fist. “I should’ve guessed that he was your lover.”

He backed up. Toward the tunnel entrance. He put his gun on the others.

Luděk followed. The colonel said to him, “We’ll find the treasure ourselves.”

“There is no treasure, you idiot!” Gertrud shouted, halting both men. “At least none you’d value. You’ve been conned. By a bunch of Jews. Please never forget that.”

Reidl returned his gun to Gertrud’s head. “We’ll see who gets the last guffaw.”

He pulled the trigger. But a rock landed near his feet, making him misfire.

They all wheeled toward where the rock had come.

A lone woman was standing above them on the embankment. She was wearing a yellow shawl.

“Klara,” Gertrud murmured, surprised by the woman. What surprised her the most was how the reciprocation for her kindness had come much sooner than she had expected.

Reidl fired his gun at Klara.

It missed her.

She ran down the sidewalk. Her face was white with horror.

This wasn’t good enough for the colonel. He was about to fire at her another time.

Gertrud rushed to the rock. She picked it up.

Reidl spun his head toward her.

She smashed the rock into his head, releasing her pent-up hate.

It knocked the man onto the ground unconscious. Amid a growing pool of blood.

Bug-eyed, Luděk grabbed the gun from Reidl. He raised it at Gertrud. “Put it down,” he babbled, shaking the weapon toward the rock. “Put it down!”

Gertrud dropped the rock.

Luděk couldn’t decide what to do next. So he stood there. His fright increased with each second that passed.

This got worse. Stomping feet rose from inside the tunnel.

Luděk dropped the gun. He ran down the riverbank.

The others didn’t move. Including Hermann, who was continuing to hold on to Klaus. They were more curious as to who was behind the stomping feet than they were frightened of it.

Their curiosity rose.

A figure stood in the tunnel entrance. It was Ivo.

It wasn’t the Ivo Hermann and Klaus had come to know. Or the one Tonda knew. This Ivo wasn’t limping. He was standing tall in immense and shapeless shoes. Which Hermann would suppose had been constructed with specialized cushions and foot orthoses.

Ivo marched to Colonel Reidl’s body. He lifted it into his arms. “You needn’t worry. They won’t find the tunnel. I’ll do with him what I did with the dead men in the synagogues. So they wouldn’t soil your holy places. So they wouldn’t bring you shame.”

No one said a word.

Ivo carried the colonel to the tunnel entrance. He turned back. “You people, you’re my friends. You’ve helped me since I was a boy. You gave me a job and respect. Love too. You gave me all that I have. Like these shoes Dr. Brod made for me. He finished them before he left the city. He worked late at night. So I could walk right. Without pain. I wish I could wear them more. They’re too nice for work. I’m afraid that I’ll hurt them. And Mrs. Mlýnková, she don’t like no shoes in her house. I have to keep them in the attic of the Old-New Synagogue. I can only enjoy them at night, remembering everyone I love. If you happen to see Dr. Brod, please tell him that I enjoy his gift. I will never forget his kindness.”

The man entered the tunnel. He closed the wall behind himself with his shoulder. “I will care for your buildings till you return. I’ll care for them like they are my own.”

The last vestiges of Ivo were his stomping feet. They faded in the distance.

“It seems,” mumbled Klaus, who was slipping from Hermann’s grasp, “it seems there was a golem.”

Hermann fell to one knee. “Why? Why did you jump in front of me?”

“You don’t imagine that I had a choice, do you?”

Hermann had no answer. He changed the subject. “Was it you who killed those men?”

“Why would you say that?”

“I’ve run out of suspects. I might’ve started suspecting myself if I had had the opportunity. But you had the opportunity, the capability, and I see now that you had the motive as well. To get me out of Theresienstadt.”

“I did it for myself.”

“What do you mean?”

“It was the only way I could make up for what had happened to us. To relieve my guilt.”

Hermann strove to hold back his tears. “Make up? But, but it was me who betrayed you.”

“No, it wasn’t. It wasn’t your fault that you couldn’t love me.”

Hermann could no longer look Klaus in the eyes. “I . . . I don’t know what to say.”

“I’ve been haunted by a dream. I’ve been haunted by it since I got stationed in Prague and learned what had happened to you and Ana.”

Klaus turned away. “In the dream, I was running through the darkened streets of Josefov. Chased by some unseen man. I could hear his breathing and his footsteps and could tell that he was close by and getting closer. That’s when I’d wake.”

He went back to Hermann. “This morning, though, I saw streets and street names that I didn’t know. Like you were saying earlier, more streets were in the district than normal. They were crowded together. With the buildings on top of each other. And many shop signs were in Yiddish. Like in the tunnels.”

“Then what happened?” Hermann asked.

“I ran till I reached a road from which there was no exit. It was a dead end in each direction. So I looked up at the street sign. I was on Ulice Prokletých. The Street of the Damned.”

Klaus gulped. “I could still hear the footsteps. They were right behind me. I spun toward them and who’d been chasing me. It was me. A gruesome caricature of me. With a distorted face and skin rotting so badly that it was dripping over his clean black uniform. ‘What do you want from me?’ I screamed, backing up. I fell to the ground. ‘I haven’t been coming for you,’ he shrieked. ‘I’ve been running from you.’”

Confusion blanketed Hermann. “What do these dreams have to do with the murders?”

“The nightmares got so bad that I couldn’t sleep. I’d walk through the night. On one, I found myself in Josefov. Despite this being the last place I wanted to be. That’s when I came across them. The SS men lurking in the synagogues.”

Klaus raised his head. “It hit me at once. By killing them I could save you from Theresienstadt and myself from the nightmares. I only needed you to turn up a plausible suspect to blame. One who wasn’t exactly guilty. But wasn’t exactly innocent either.”

“How could you have been certain I would?”

“Those men had no good reason to be in the synagogues. From what I had read about your career, I was certain that you would discover why they were there. That this would lead to a suspect. And I was right. You did turn up one. More than one. My plan almost worked. Actually, it did. No more nightmares will be coming.”

Hermann couldn’t keep back his tears.

Klaus waved them off. “Don’t. My . . . my pocket.”

“What?”

“Reach into my jacket pocket.”

Hermann did. He pulled out the papers that Klaus had earlier written the equations on. The ones that had been lying on the car’s floor that morning.

Klaus grasped them with his shaking hand. “Among them is the visa. It’ll get you to Switzerland, where you’ll discover a gift waiting for you. Just bring the bill of lading that’s also there to the man that it’s addressed to.”

Hermann gasped. “There was a visa?”

“I was hoping to leave with you. But now, now maybe you can choose a better traveling companion.”

Hermann shook his head. Over and over he did. As Klaus, with the gentlest of smiles, closed his eyes. He died.

Hermann cried more. He embraced the man. “Goodnight, my friend. And . . . sbohem.”

Gertrud touched Hermann’s shoulder, making him jump. “I’m sorry, Hermann. But we have to leave. You as well.”

Hermann lifted his eyes onto her and Tonda, who lobbed his head at the cart. “I need you to help me get the Torahs on board.”

Gertrud took Tonda’s arm. “This is my brother Tonda.”

“Shalom,” he said to them both.
