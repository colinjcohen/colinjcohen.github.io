chapter fifty-eight: the road from home

 

 

THE BOAT DISEMBARKED from the dock. Hermann and Gertrud were standing on the deck’s center. By the railing.

Hermann spun to her. “Who will pay Ivo?”

“The caretaker? My brother and I have been planning this for a while. Ivo will be well taken care of. I promise you. All our friends will be well taken care of.”

Hermann reached into his jacket. For a folded piece of paper. He handed it to Gertrud. “Could you take care of her too?”

“What’s this?” she said.

She unfolded the paper. She read it. Her eyes widened. “A pension request for the widow of a Nazi?”

“She didn’t deserve the fate she got.”

Gertrud placed the paper in her purse. “We’ll take care of her too.”

Hermann thanked her. Both their eyes lingered.

His went astray.

He shifted his feet. Toward the body of his friend on the dock. He spotted that it was the most peaceful he had seen Klaus. It reminded him of when he had met him. When he was so desperate for a rest.

Hermann hoped that Klaus would get this rest in the place of truth he was traveling to. A place Hermann hoped would be kinder to him than this one had been.

The boat continued down the river.

Hermann drifted toward its stern. He wanted to keep his eyes on Klaus. He was also eyeing Josefov and the city that he loved for the last time.

The city where his Ana lay.

With her in mind, he clutched the rose petal in his pocket.

Prague had about disappeared when the river’s waters slapped against the ship. Hermann heard the song that only he could hear. Smetana’s “Vltava.”

It was clearer than the night before. It was as clear as it had been on the morning that he’d left Theresienstadt. He could feel the song’s beats and rhythms rising from the waves. He could feel Karel Ančerl directing them.

He hummed along. While hailing the pearl that had picked him up from the bottom. Like the river, we are born time after time to experience the magic of beginnings like these.

“That song,” Gertrud called out. She stepped to Hermann.

The music ended. Inside Hermann’s head and out.

“What about it?” he asked.

“Do you know that its melody comes from an older piece? And that this piece is the basis of ‘Hatikvah’?”

“‘Hatikvah’? My mother would sing that around the house. I hadn’t thought about it in years till yesterday. But you’re right. The melodies are indeed the same. I knew that there was something off about that Smetana song.”

Hermann marveled at this. “Isn’t it odd?”

“What is?” Gertrud said.

“How two very different people found their dreams of self-determination in the very same melody.”

“‘Hatikvah’ will be the national anthem of the state that we’re building in Palestine.”

“Is that where you’re taking the Torahs?”

“And myself. I hope never to return to Europe. What about you?”

“Me?”

“Where will you go?”

“I really can’t say. Prague is the only home that I’ve ever wanted. I will miss her dearly.”

“I’m an officer in the Haganah. That’s the army we’ve been forming over there. We could use a man like you in my division.”

“Is that so?”

“Assuming you can take orders from a woman.”

“I was married for twenty years.”

They both laughed.

Hermann recalled his dream from earlier and what his mother had said in it. As well as what had been her dream. He whispered, “Next year in Jerusalem.”

“What was that?”

“I guess, I guess I wouldn’t be getting utopence over there.”

“Or venison. But wait till you try my chicken soup and knaidels.”

“You can cook?”

“I can do some of everything.”

The two smiled.

This was when my grandpa and my grandma first leaned against each other. I can tell you that it wouldn’t be the last.

In these opening moments of her life with a man with whom she would try to repair a piece of this world—by raising the daughter and the grandson of their enemy as their own—Gertrud took out her mother’s jewelry box. She opened it.

This exposed a gold Star of David pin.

She put it on her coat. In the same motion, she tore off the National Socialist Women’s League pin. She tossed it into the river.

It sank into the abyss.

Hermann could hear the music yet another time. But it was changing. Into the other song. The one that was both different and the same. The one that his mother had sung to him. The one that was leading him toward what, days before, would’ve been unimaginable. The same “what” that was holding me above water on that freezing desert night.

The possibility that comes with each tomorrow.
