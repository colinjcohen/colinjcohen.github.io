chapter forty-one: a large number of valuables

 

 

HERMANN AND KLAUS returned to the palace.

Lieutenant Becker was in a cramped and windowless office like Hermann’s.

A man in his mid-twenties, Becker was brawny with blond hair and blue eyes. Hermann would say that he was a young Klaus. I bet Klaus recognized this as well.

Klaus introduced himself and Hermann, who showed the lieutenant a picture of Captain Fischer while asking him the same question that he had asked Lieutenant Krieg: if the captain had asked him about jewels or gold or heirlooms when he was working at the Klausen Synagogue.

The lieutenant didn’t need time to answer. “Captain Fischer did ask me about that. He asked me about a Jewish treasure.”

Hermann took a step back. “A Jewish treasure?”

“He said that he had heard a rumor about a group of wealthy Jewish families in Josefov who’d pooled their resources. According to him, they had collected a large number of valuables that they hoped to use to facilitate their escape. But they never got the chance to use it. He said that this ‘treasure,’ as he called it, consisted of fine jewelry, gold bullion, and antiques. That some items were priceless heirlooms. He asked me if I had heard of such a treasure through my research.”

“Had you?”

“Never. I told him so. I also told him that, if there had been such a treasure, he could feel assured that the Reich would’ve discovered it by now. That they would’ve properly taken care of it.”

“Thank you, Lieutenant. You’ve been most helpful.”

Hermann and Klaus rushed from the office. Klaus closed the door. The two stopped by the corner of a corridor.

Klaus couldn’t hide his excitement. “You suppose Captain Fischer was looking for this treasure?”

“It all fits, doesn’t it?”

“Could this treasure be real?”

“I can’t say for sure. But from the occupation’s beginnings, I’ve heard rumors.”

“I’ve heard them as well. I’m sure Colonel Reidl has too.”

“A slew of wealthy people were in Josefov. Some very wealthy. A treasure of a meaningful magnitude is plausible.”

“We were positive that people were hiding their wealth.”

“I at least can believe in a set of murders driven by greed. And now we have a good notion as to why you discovered the men near synagogues. Including Captain Kleiner. They must’ve believed that this treasure was hidden in or near one. It wouldn’t surprise me if that jeweler on Pařížská Street was part of this pool and that’s how Kleiner wound up with the ducat. We also now have a good notion as to why Reidl and Kleiner framed Maier. They must’ve been worried that we were getting close to the truth. He was their escape hatch. Their means for getting us off their backs. It’s also probably why Reidl sent for your replacement early.”

“What do you suppose is Mr. Malý’s involvement in this?”

“Rumors are rumors, but someone had to give Reidl and Kleiner specific details about the treasure. To get them to act the way they did. Likely it was Malý. He might’ve learned about the treasure through his responsibilities with the synagogues and needed Reidl to ‘properly take care of it,’ as Lieutenant Becker would say.”

“So why would Reidl be searching for it?”

“It might be that they don’t know where it is. Or it might be that Reidl wants to steal it from Malý. It might be both.”

“That would give Malý a motive for killing those men.”

“Your hunch may have been right. But the murderer could be someone else. Only one way for us to find out. If we find the treasure, my hunch is that the murderer will not be far away.”

“And how do you propose that we find the treasure?”

“We have but one possibility: follow the known suspects and hope that they lead us to it. Fortunately, our list is short. Are you going to have someone follow Malý?”

“I’ll call the Czech police when I get back to my office.”

“What about Reidl? Are you willing to follow him?”

Klaus paused. For a while he did. “I’m willing. But it would have to be us who do the following. I couldn’t have anyone do it. For lots of reasons.”

“It would mean following him day and night. Are you willing to do that?”

“Yes.”

“We’ll need another car. One that’s inconspicuous. So that we can follow him. And let’s have a flashlight with us this time. With a fresh battery.”

Klaus checked his watch. It was minutes past 4:30. “We can start after the dinner party.”

Hermann couldn’t hide his shock. Or his unease. “You’re not planning on bringing me to that party, are you?”

“Reidl will be there. Gertrud invites him to all her parties.”
