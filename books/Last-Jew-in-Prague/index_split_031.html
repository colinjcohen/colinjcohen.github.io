chapter thirty-one: collaborating with the enemy

 

 

HERMANN ARRIVED EARLY at the palace and its barren lot.

He entered the building. His two guards were trailing behind him.

On this morning, Marta was waiting. She was standing by her desk with another piping hot cup of faux coffee.

She gave it him when he stopped beside her.

“Thank you, Marta. Just what I needed as usual.”

She watched him gulp down the cup. She blanched. “I had a busy day yesterday.”

“Tell me about it.”

She picked up a notepad off her desk. She read it. “I checked with city records. To discover who’s responsible for those six synagogues in Josefov. The responsibility for each belongs to a joint-stock company called Golem a.s.”

“An interesting choice of name. Were you able to discover any tidbits about the company? Such as its owners?”

“For that I had to check state records. The company’s stockholders are six limited-liability companies. When researching each, I learned that the same person was the responsible party for each.”

“Let me guess: his name is Malý.”

“How did you guess?”

“He came up multiple times yesterday. But we know nothing about him. Other than his first initial and last name. Can you fill in the details?”

She returned to her notes. “I can. But it wasn’t easy. He was listed with that initial and last name alone. So, armed with one of those special bottles of Becherbitter you left me, I visited Jindřich Vidlička. He’s a deputy at the Finance Ministry.”

Marta shook her notes. “He could only give me the companies’ financial statements. But I went over these. I came upon an odd line item in the balance sheets: peníze na cestě.”

Hermann translated this in his head to money on the way. It confused him. This must’ve been all over his face.

Marta read this face. “Actually, money in transit is a better translation.”

Hermann smiled. All over he smiled.

“It turns out that, when someone makes a bank transfer, the money doesn’t go directly from one account to another. The request goes to the National Bank. They must process the transaction, and this can take weeks. Hence the need for ‘peníze na cestě.’”

She shook her notes for a second time. “I got to supposing that these companies could’ve made transfers to Malý. I had Vidlička see if he could unearth some.”

“And he did,” Hermann blurted out while continuing to smile at Marta. At a brilliance that he was positive exceeded his own.

“He did. He got hold of a bunch of transactions that Golem a.s. had made. A number were in the same amounts. To the same recipient. From the bank account of this person, Vidlička was able to tell me that Malý’s first name is Antonín.”

“Were you able to discover more about him?”

“His name sounded familiar. So I checked if we had arrested him.”

“Had we?”

She shook her head. “But I was confident that we had run across him. So I went through our old case files. About six years ago, he represented Milan Žabka. Whom you arrested for murder.”

“So Malý’s a lawyer?”

“He’s a lawyer.”

“I remember Žabka. But I don’t remember Malý.”

“Žabka fired him after ten minutes and before attempting his second murder. This must’ve happened before you got in that morning. Anyway, I did some additional research. Malý has got an office at the Palác Dunaj. That’s that new building on Národní. The modern-looking one with the paternoster.”

“And?”

Marta conferred with her notes. “I contacted the bar association about him. They don’t have a fond opinion of him. To put it mildly. He’s come close to disbarment on multiple occasions, and his former clients have filed a slew of complaints against him. And that doesn’t include Žabka.”

“That makes him an interesting choice to be named responsible for six irreplaceable synagogues.”

“The bar association also told me that some of its members had wanted to throw Malý out a few years ago. Rumors were that he was a Jew. But they investigated these rumors. They found them unsubstantiated.”

Hermann couldn’t conclude a lot from this. He let Marta go on.

“I tried calling this Malý. To get more information about the arrangement that he has with the synagogues. I called him well into the afternoon. But no one picked up the phone. So I went there myself.”

“So that’s where you were yesterday afternoon.”

“That’s where I was. I even took the paternoster to his office.”

“And you lived to talk about it.”

“It appears so.”

“Ana and I rode up and down in one of those on our wedding day,” Hermann said while becoming lost in the moment. “I can’t tell you how many times we did.”

Marta beamed at him. For a while she beamed. “This Malý has a spec of an office on the third floor. Way in the back. But he wasn’t there when I arrived. I had to wait till the day’s end for him to show up.”

“Did he tell you much when he did?”

“He would only tell me that he’s not the owner of Golem a.s. Or the limited-liability companies that control it. He’s simply representing the actual owners. But he wouldn’t tell me who these people are.”

“Excellent work as usual, Marta. If it were up to me, I’d make you a detective.”

“I’m glad it’s not up to you. My husband complains enough about the hours I work.”

“I bet he never complains about your pay.”

“Not once.”

Hermann figured that Klaus would be arriving soon. He leaned toward Marta. “I have but a single task for you today. I want you to dig up as much as you can about a certain Gertrud Gross. She’s a well-to-do socialite whose husband died recently. Whatever you collect, put it on a piece of paper. Place this folded over in the top drawer of your desk. Then take some time off. You’ve earned it.”

Hermann started off. But he stopped. He sought a place for his cup.

Marta grabbed it. As usual.

He headed toward his office.

“Can I ask you a question, Herr Weiss?” she whispered.

He returned to her. “You can ask me any you want.”

She eyed Hermann’s guards. They were well behind them, engaged in their own conversation. “What are you doing with these Nazis?”

“That’s a good question. I’ve had to ask it myself. In the beginning, they didn’t give me a choice. If you know what I mean. But I wanted to say no. I didn’t because I had promised Ana not to give up. Though I expected to do the job by rote.”

“And that hasn’t happened?”

Hermann averted his gaze from her. “I can’t explain it. Not to myself. Part of it is that this case has taken my mind off Ana. But it’s also leading me somewhere. I can’t say where. Or where this feeling is coming from. But it’s taking me somewhere I need to go.”

“People are talking.”

She stretched herself toward two uniformed Czech policemen down the hall. They were by a door and scowling at Hermann. “They think that you’ve become a collaborator.”

Hermann hung his head. “I’ve questioned that myself. While I’m not helping them catch Jews or partisans—while I’m not helping them advance their agenda in any way—I’ve questioned if helping them investigate a normal crime constituted collaboration. I’ve questioned if help was fungible. That by providing it, I was freeing another man who’d be able to commit atrocities.”

“If that were true, we’d all be collaborators.”

“We could be. The simple act of driving on the right-hand side of the road could be collaboration. Imagine if everyone went back to driving on the left-hand side. The whole country would grind to a halt. And what would the Nazis do? Would they shoot seven million people?”

“It wouldn’t be the fear of seven million shot that would scare people from this. It’d be the fear that they’d be the first shot.”

The conversation fell silent.

Marta broke it. “Can I ask what you’re helping them investigate?”

“Just some dead Nazis.”

“Well,” she said under her breath, “I hope the investigation leads to making some more.”

Hermann’s face lit up.

But it didn’t stay that way. Someone nearby cleared his throat.

It was Klaus.
