chapter forty-seven: the last goodbye

 

 

HERMANN BELIEVED THAT he was dreaming.

Not only because he was walking into the setting sun. He was walking along a frozen path toward his old family home in the shtetl where he had lived as a boy.

But was it a dream?

The cold was pulsating in his bones.

Could he dream that?

Hermann continued on.

His breath was shining through the air. Smoke was churning from his house’s chimney. Mr. Jacobs from next door was waving at him. From his shoveled walkway. “Thank you, Hermann!”

He didn’t respond. He went into his home.

“Is that you, Hermann?” chirped a familiar female voice from the kitchen.

The voice floored Hermann. He followed it. He followed as well the scents and the crackling sounds of chicken and potatoes roasting in the oven. He followed them to his mother.

She was standing over the Shabbat candles, guiding the light into their home with her hands.

This was a ritual Hermann loved. It was among the few he did. He loved it from the time he was a boy through its performance each Friday night at Theresienstadt. He enjoyed it until my grandma died.

The waves of his mother’s hands at this moment, in particular, fascinated him. He was sure that she was bringing God upon them as she sang the prayer:

 

Barukh ata Adonai

Eloheinu Melekh ha-olam,

Asher kid’shanu b’mitzvotav

V’tzivanu l’hadlik ner shel Shabbat.

 

“Amen,” Hermann said.

His mother tossed herself toward him. She flung up her arms. “Next year in Jerusalem! Next year we will welcome the Sabbath in Jerusalem!”

Hermann said nothing, while remembering how his mother had said this each Sabbath night.

Why would he be dreaming of it now when he couldn’t remember dreaming of it before? Or remembering it?

He had no answer. He went farther into the house. Over his mother singing “Hatikvah.” A song that perplexed him for a reason that he couldn’t fathom.

Hermann got to the door of his old room. He opened it. He lurched inside.

But he didn’t enter his bedroom. He was back in his Prague apartment. As it had been a few years before the occupation. He was also wearing Mr. Jacobs’s suit. A suit that didn’t fit him so well due to the beginnings of a belly that he was developing.

Hermann became more convinced that he was dreaming. But he questioned more whether it was a dream. He had never experienced one in which he believed that he was dreaming.

He strode into the kitchen.

In the bright midday sun was Ana in her flowered apron. She was preparing another jar of utopence by chopping onions on a wooden cutting board.

Ana cut each onion with precision. She cut them as fine as she could. How they were cut mattered to her. They mattered to her.

She emptied the onions into the jar. It had room for nothing further.

It was so real to Hermann. He could smell both the sausages and Ana’s favorite perfume. The kind that she wore on special occasions. Such as on their wedding day and when she tried to join him in Theresienstadt. He could feel, too, the warm summer sun on his cheeks and forehead. Beyond that, he could feel the warmth of Ana’s presence. He marveled at how she could aspire him after so many years of marriage.

He rushed to her. Afraid that she would disappear otherwise. He took her hands. They were warmer than the feelings that she had evoked.

Ana got annoyed. She broke away from Hermann. So she could close the jar’s lid. “One more day. You can hold out till then.”

He retook her hands. He pulled her away. “I can’t hold out for another few seconds.”

She smirked. She dragged her feet. “Are you being naughty, Herr Weiss?”

He answered her by sweeping her into his arms. Like he had done on their wedding day when he first led her into their home as husband and wife. He spun her in circles.

Both giggling, they spun into the bedroom.

Instead of there, they were on Bled Island. By the 99 steps that had so captured Hermann’s imagination as a young man when he’d read about them in a book that he’d otherwise forgotten.

Hermann let out a drawn-out moan. With his heart beating as it had on their wedding day, he carried Ana up the staircase. She was wearing her white gown and gripping her white rose bouquet.

She whined. “What are you doing?”

“Sshhh!”

He rushed down the stairs. To the bottom. “Don’t you remember what I told you? To have a successful marriage, the Slovenians believe that the groom must carry the bride up the 99 steps without her saying a word.”

“We’ve already had a successful marriage.”

“This will prove it!”

Hermann hiked back up the steps with Ana. For the first half, this was easy. The two rose above Lake Bled. Toward the towering mountains surrounding it. Bled Castle was in the distance, hovering over them.

The next half wasn’t so easy. Hermann tired.

This made him question for another time whether he was experiencing a dream. He couldn’t recall feeling tired in any.

Upward he scaled the staircase. With sweat pouring down his face and stinging his eyes. He had no doubt that he would collapse. Neither did Ana. She braced herself for a fall.

But he didn’t fall. He ran up the final steps to the top.

Conveying his bride through a set of doors, he and Ana were in their bedroom.

He lowered her upon their well-made featherbed. “This is real, isn’t it?” he whispered, kissing and touching her everywhere.

She broke their embrace. She gave him the look that she would give him when she was about to disappoint him.

“It’s real,” he said. “It is.”

To prove this, he reached into his pants pocket. He pulled out the flower petal and showed it to her. “I know it’s real.”

She took his hand. “Knowing has nothing to do with it. You must let me go. You must let you go. This, this is sbohem.”

What she was telling him was that there would be no more na shledanou. No more until we meet again. This was farewell and forever.

Hermann shook. His whole self shook. “No.”

He said this and said this, hoping to make it so.

Ana gushed. Her face lit up. She caressed Hermann’s cheeks with the radiance of her palms. “You must live, miláčku můj. I’ll be with you wherever you’ll be. But you must live. Not in what was. But in ‘now.’ You must keep making me proud.”

He held back his tears. “You, you’re proud of me?”

She answered him with a kiss.

It made him swoon more than the venison had earlier.
