chapter twenty-two: the blessing after the meal

 

 

BY THE TIME of my visit to Prague, I had spoken to several participants in this story. But I wanted to talk to those who had knowledge of missing elements of it.

I spent days searching for them. Only to discover that—apart from Ivo—they were either dead or, for various reasons, I couldn’t track them down.

Tracking down Ivo wasn’t easy either. The first place I went was the Maisel Synagogue. I walked through its doors. With voices echoing off the prayer hall walls.

 

Yit’gadal v’yit’kadash shmei raba.

 

They were reciting the Mourner’s Kaddish. A prayer said in memory of the dead that I believe is the most beautiful in Judaism. This despite it having led me down a hole as a young man.

Spoken not in Hebrew but in Aramaic, it’s a haunting prayer. Listeners can hear the cries for comfort and relief of those who say it. Without understanding what they’re saying.

When I was a child, each Yom Kippur I would listen to the Mourner’s Kaddish from outside our synagogue’s prayer hall. I’d listen to my grandparents and others recite it. I was certain that they were speaking to God.

Later, I would sight the tears on my grandparents’ faces as they left the hall arm in arm. Each year the tears were the same. Their mourning for those they had lost never ended.

I can see my grandma walking past me in the temple lobby. I see her and her gentle but firm steps. I see also the gold Star of David pin on her dress. The one that she wore on Yom Kippur. The one that got her through that prayer.

This was a prayer that I, too, was reciting while joining the mourners in the Maisel Synagogue. I mourned for both my grandparents, who had given me much more than a home. I cried for them as well and for all that was missing in me because of their passing.

People in the Maisel, hearing an unusual voice, turned toward me. So did the rabbi.

After the service, I asked the man about Ivo.

He didn’t know him. Neither he nor the other rabbis in Josefov were around at the time of this story. Or during the years following the war. But he was able to guide me to a handful of elderly congregants.

Most recalled Ivo. They did so with fondness.

While none could say where he was or was sure he was alive, a woman gave me an important clue. Where he went to work after the communists closed the country’s synagogues in the early 1950s. During a wave of antisemitism that coincided with the Slánský Show Trial in Czechoslovakia and the Doctors’ Plot in the Soviet Union.

The following morning, I took a drive to a town outside of Prague called Pečky. I walked inside a hand tool factory called Tona.

Passing some workers drinking beer outside the canteen, some with missing fingers, I entered the personnel office. I asked them about Ivo.

They insisted that he didn’t work for the company. They also refused to give me access to their old files. One of my special bottles of Becherovka couldn’t change their minds.

I left their office and the building, my head down.

A grizzled man in blue dungarees yelled out to me. He approached.

Taking the bottle from me, he told me that he was a friend of Ivo’s. He told me that Ivo had returned to Prague after he retired. He told me where I could find him.

Months earlier, Ivo’s wife had died. He was staying at a nursing home in Háje, the city’s southernmost district.

I went to the home at the opening of visiting hours the next day. I stopped at Ivo’s open door.

Knocking on it, I introduced myself. “Promiňte, dovolte, abych se vám představil . . .”

I couldn’t finish. Ivo jumped off his bed and greeted me. He greeted me like he had greeted my grandpa on the day he’d met him. Recognizing how Grandpa and I mangled the Czech language in a similar manner, he had no doubt who I was. He would provide me with valuable insights into this story.

Chief among these unfolded about when Hermann and Klaus were having dinner with Gertrud.

 

 

IN WHAT PASSED for Ivo’s kitchen, he was enjoying the enormous serving of potato dumplings that he himself had prepared.

He did so shaking off the difficult day that he had had. Not only had he endured the odd incident with the two odd men that morning in the Spanish Synagogue. Lieutenant Krieg in the afternoon had been crueler to him than usual at the Klausen.

Yet he took solace. In that the other German was no longer haunting the place. Or him. A man who was crueler than the lieutenant and who would haunt more than him.

Ivo finished his dinner, noting how good it had been. As it had been for years. But he could remember the times prior to this. The times he seldom had a dinner. Let alone a good one. This made him wish that the blessing were said, not before the meal, but after it. When he felt the most thankful. He wished and wished for this to come true.

It didn’t. But he expressed his thanks anyway in silence.

Ivo didn’t dawdle. He brought his dish, cup, and utensils to the cracked and stained but tidy ceramic sink by the lone windows in his room.

He washed and dried them. He placed them on a towel beside the sink. Next to the bowl and pot that he had used to make the dumplings. Which he’d washed earlier so they would be ready for the following night’s supper.

Ivo took a deep breath. A long night was ahead of him.

He removed his coat and hat from the closet. He put them on. He staggered from his room.

Mrs. Mlýnková was sweeping the hallway. Much as she did at this time.

Much as he did at this time, Ivo wished the middle-aged woman a good evening. He asked her how she was doing.

“What a mess this place is,” was her response. Much as it was at this time. “I keep telling people to take off their shoes when they come into my house. But do they listen? Do they?”

“I listen.”

“Only you listen, Ivo. I swear, the next pair of shoes I find, I’ll throw right out the window!”

This was a threat also made each night. It would make Ivo shudder on each one.

It would become moot a few years later. The onset of communist rule and its banning of servants led to everyone taking off their shoes when entering a home. A custom that outlived communism.

Mrs. Mlýnková smirked at Ivo. “Out chasing the ladies again, Mr. Pokorný?”

Ivo shook his head. Over and over he shook it. “Oh, no, Mrs. Mlýnková. It’s nothing like that. Honestly.”

“Just be quiet when you return.”

Ivo nodded. Thrice he did.

Wobbling down the stairs to the building’s foyer, he stepped into his slippers. He opened the door.

On the front stoop, burning coal wafted in the cool night air. An odor that had been getting fainter as the days had been getting warmer.

Ivo could sense that it was about to rain. He hoped that it wouldn’t do so too hard.

With increasing pain, he limped his way north up Maisel Street.

But he stopped when he came near the Maisel Synagogue. A sight had frozen him under an awning.

A woman in a yellow shawl was by the synagogue’s gate. Her eyes were boring into the building.

She was the same woman who would often lurk by the synagogues at night. She often enchanted Ivo. But on this night, his enchantment rose.

Unlike before, Klara wasn’t filthy. She wasn’t disheveled. She looked beautiful. She so enchanted Ivo that, with his hands behind his back, his eyes—on their own—followed her across the street.

Well after Klara had left his view, Ivo’s eyes were on the spot where she had last been. She was as enchanting when she was gone as when she was there.

He only came out of his stupor when he remembered what he had to do that night. He continued his limping up the street.

To reach his destination, he only had to go a smattering of blocks. But it took him twenty minutes. His feet hurt so much. He had to keep halting.

He got to the Old-New Synagogue, a thirteenth-century building. He made his way to the rear of it.

Pulling out an old wooden ladder that he had hidden behind a tree, he placed it under metal rungs in the synagogue’s back wall. They rose from halfway up the building. To an attic door at the top.

Ivo climbed. Both the ladder and the metal rungs. With agony he climbed. One slow step after another. He climbed slower than he had walked up Maisel Street. But his movements were steady. He had a resolve that never wavered in the face of his misery. It stemmed from that, what he would be doing later, was of greater meaning than any discomfort now.

He made it to the wall’s top rung and the door to the attic. A place that Rabbi Isaacs had told him before he left town was for all that was unholy.

Ivo withdrew a set of rusty iron keys from his coat pocket. He opened the door with the rustiest. He lurched into the room.

Joy overwhelmed him. It got stronger upon flipping on the light and coming upon what was waiting for him.
