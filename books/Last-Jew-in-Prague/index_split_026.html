chapter twenty-six: one mystery leads to another

 

 

THE SYNAGOGUE GATE was unlocked.

But the building itself wasn’t, and the keys to it weren’t on Captain Kleiner. Nor did Reidl’s men have them. They insisted Kleiner had them when they entered the synagogue.

So, they waited for the men to fetch a flashlight. They used it to search the synagogue’s grounds.

In the graveyard, within the mass of crooked tombstones going back centuries, they came upon Kleiner’s Luger.

Klaus picked it up. He showed it to Hermann. “Could this have been where he was killed?”

Hermann focused the flashlight’s beam onto the trampled mud where someone had fallen. “No question about it. But why would the killer move his body from here to the gate? For what purpose?”

No one had an answer. Nor could they locate the keys.

“Look,” Reidl growled at Klaus, “if you want to go into the building, break into it. It’s not like you have to worry about the law.”

But Hermann wasn’t keen on damaging the synagogue. He convinced both men that he could get inside the building without disturbing evidence. This entailed Klaus and him driving a few blocks to Ivo’s home. Which didn’t take them much time. Even through the maze.

The two parked outside the building. They galloped to the entrance.

It was locked.

Hermann banged on it.

This accomplished nothing. He got the same result when he pounded his fist on it.

Klaus pointed at the door. “Surely, there’s no evidence here I can disturb.”

He slammed his shoulder into the door. It swung open.

Hermann took a step back. “Bivoj.”

“What’s that?”

“Remember when you wrestled in college? You’d lift your opponents on your back. Like what Bivoj did with the boar. I was sure that you were the strongest man in the world.”

“That was many years ago.”

“Apparently not that many.”

The two entered the building and the lobby. Hermann fumbled for the light switch.

Flipping it on, he and Klaus climbed to Ivo’s floor. Hermann knocked on his door.

No response came. So he kept knocking.

Ivo whipped open the door. He raised his hands, wearing only a shirt. “Have I done wrong?”

“Not at all,” Hermann said. “I’m sorry to wake you, but we need you to open the Pinkas Synagogue.”

“Right now?”

“I’m afraid so.”

“Do you need to pray?”

“We need to investigate a crime that happened there.”

“Oh.”

Ivo swung his head back into his room. “Should I put on my overalls?”

“We don’t need you to do work. We need you to open the door. We need you to lock it after we leave. Just put on an overcoat.”

Ivo grabbed his coat from the closet. He slipped it on.

Hermann felt it. “Were you out this evening?”

“What?” Ivo mumbled, his lips shaking.

“Your coat is wet. You must’ve been outside tonight.”

“Yyyyes. I, I went for a walk. You can ask Mrs. Mlýnková. She saw me leave.”

“I believe you.”

Hermann did believe him. But he couldn’t avoid how guilty the man appeared. He had to pretend this away.

He and Klaus started down the stairs. Ivo followed them as fast as he could to the building’s foyer. He stepped into his slippers.

The three went outside and into the Mercedes. Which took them to the synagogue at the same time an ambulance drove off with Kleiner’s body.

In the light rain, the three exited the car. Hermann, Klaus, and Reidl followed Ivo, who hobbled to the building.

He unlocked and opened the creaky front door.

Switching on the lights, he let the three inside. Along with a group of six soldiers who had arrived during the time Hermann and Klaus had been gone.

The soldiers fanned out across the vestibule. While Ivo took two kippahs from his coat pocket. He placed one on his head. He gave the other to Hermann, who put it on as well.

Reidl poked his head into the deep and narrow prayer hall. “I don’t get what you expect to find. The killer must be long gone.”

Hermann poked his own head into the room. “It’s what he may have left behind that interests me.”

The ark in the room’s front was open. It was missing its Torah.

Hermann went back to Ivo. “What happened to the Torahs?”

“Somebody took them.”

“Who?”

“One morning, I came to work and they were gone. All six. I went to the rabbi that day and told him. But he told me not to worry about it.”

“When was this?”

“After they closed the synagogues. But before everyone left the city.”

Reidl sneered. “What does this have to do with Kleiner’s murder?”

“I’m not sure,” Hermann said. “Ivo, is there an arched door here like those in the Spanish and Klausen Synagogues?”

“Why?” Reidl roared. “Why would you be looking for such?”

Klaus answered for Hermann. “For the same reason we’re here, Colonel.”

Hermann tilted toward Ivo. “Is there a door?”

Ivo pointed down the hall. To a pair of arched windows. “Over there.”

Everyone marched to the windows. The door was across from them. It was no surprise that it was locked.

They waited for Ivo. This took time.

Hermann jiggled the door’s handle on the man’s approach. “You don’t have the key to this one either, I suppose.”

Ivo shook his head. He came off as guilty as he had in the Spanish Synagogue.

Klaus bent toward Reidl. “We need to get through this door.”

The colonel stomped his foot. “So shoot off the lock.”

“Captain Fischer tried that at the Klausen Synagogue. It didn’t work.”

Reidl reached for his gun. “Let me try.”

“We could damage evidence,” Hermann interjected. Like when Reidl had wanted to break into the building, this had more to do with him not wanting to destroy the synagogue.

Klaus passed his hand over the door. “I have a better way of opening it.”
