chapter five: the envy of all women

 

 

A MERCEDES-BENZ 770 sedan sped from Theresienstadt on Klaus’s command. Which he issued while swallowing the stale air swirling around the car’s back seat.

Next to him in the gray daylight was Hermann.

He noted how relieved Klaus was. He was more relieved than Hermann. He was more tired than before too.

His eyes fluttered.

The camp’s front gate couldn’t disturb this when it swung open. When it screeched across the pavement.

Hermann turned from Klaus. As the car slowed.

It reached an impasse.

A mass of men, women, and children were trudging in the opposite way. Flanked by soldiers.

The vehicle passed through them.

They struggled to the camp. With the remains of their goods.

These they had taken from the Bohušovice train station, a pair of kilometers away. Toward what for many would be their final destination.

A thousand people were on the road. But one stuck out to Hermann. A girl carrying a broken doll that echoed the fears her face couldn’t.

Hermann kept eyeing the crowd.

He came upon himself. He was trudging to the place the summer before. Among those both different and the same as those that day.

This was happening in another memory-like re-experience.

He was holding a suitcase in one hand. A crumpled pink slip of paper in the other. He was also turning back to Prague.

It didn’t matter that no more was left to turn back to.

Hermann returned to the Mercedes.

It stopped in front of a graying woman. She was on her knees with eyes that were without hope, grasping her suitcase with both hands.

“Move!” roared a soldier from nearby.

She didn’t. Either she couldn’t or wouldn’t.

The soldier repeated his command. Firmer. His rifle drawn.

But the woman didn’t budge.

The man placed his finger on the trigger.

This dredged up a similar incident from Hermann’s not-so-distant past.

He grabbed the door handle. He was about to push it.

Someone in the crowd took the woman. He took her and her suitcase away.

The Mercedes went on.

Hermann returned to Klaus, who had fallen asleep.

He glared at him. He sensed how different he had become from the man who’d been his friend. He sensed how this man could never return.

His glare didn’t last.

They reached the train station. Along with a train and its empty cattle cars.

It was waiting to collect more cattle.

This brought Hermann to a car like them at Prague’s Hybernské Station.

He was sitting on the floor, pressed against a splintery gate made from planks of wood. He was taking in his last glimpses of Ana. Apart from those inside his head.

This re-experience was quick too. He went back to the Mercedes.

It got to the end of both the street and the town. It cornered right onto an empty country road. In route to Prague.

Klaus was writhing in his sleep. He was flopping his arms. He was tossing himself around. Amid both a nightmare and a conflict.

This uneased Hermann.

Despite his anger toward the man, he turned away another time.

The car and what was inside it changed. Into the railroad car that had taken him to Prague two decades earlier. A few years after the close of World War I.

 

 

HERMANN REACHED THE city around midday.

This was months after it had become the capital of what people would later call the First Czechoslovak Republic. It was three hundred years after the Battle of White Mountain and the previous time that it had been the capital of an independent country.

Hermann sat against the car’s window.

His widened eyes were straining to stay open. His stomach was grumbling too, having survived the three-day journey on a sack of deep-fried bread called langoš.

The journey had led from his childhood home in the new country’s eastern frontier. It also had led from the arms of his mother, who had cried during their extended goodbye. She cried more than she had when he left for the army years earlier.

They were parting for good.

He recounted this years later at Newark Airport when sending me off to my first job after graduating college.

With my grandma passing that summer, it would be the first that he’d be alone since Theresienstadt.

Like his mother had, he had tears in his eyes. For the same reason.

It would be the last I’d see him. Apart from my memories.

 

 

“YOU’LL COME VISIT us in Jerusalem, won’t you?” Hermann’s mother said to him in the forest outside their home. She said this well aware that the only Jerusalem she would ever experience would be in her dreams.

Hermann started down the road with his heavy trunk. “Yes, Mama.”

He was excited to leave. But he was afraid to go. So he tried not to look back.

But he couldn’t help himself.

Now his father was crying.

Hermann believed that he was crying because he was happy. Hermann had realized a dream that he never could for himself.

 

 

BACK ON THE train in Prague, Hermann jumped off with his heavy trunk before it stopped at Wilson Station. Renamed that after the American president who had helped Czechoslovakia come to be.

This Hermann was different from the one leaving Theresienstadt.

It wasn’t his age. The years that he had spent in the army had thickened him.

From his reflection off a mirror-like wall, he could tell how sharp he was in the hand-tailored suit that his next-door neighbor had made for him. A suit that took three months of savings to pay for.

That was only for the materials.

He could only afford it in total because Mr. Jacobs had provided his labor for free. As a thank you for the snow that Hermann had cleared from his home’s walkway since he’d been a boy.

Twenty-two-year-old Hermann came across as a boy in that crowded station. His fancy suit and thickness aside.

Part of this was from his youthful face. But complementing it were his lush brown curls. Those that he would say were the envy of all women and needed trimming twice a week.

He was a boy on the inside as well. It had spited the horrors that he had witnessed during a war that people had said was to end them.

Hermann exited the station.

Breathing in the hot and dense late-summer air, he wanted to splurge for a cab.

But figuring that he needed to conserve his funds, he hurried his trunk to Wenceslas Square, blocks away.

He skipped down the rectangular, one-kilometer square. He skipped in the path of Charles University, where the law faculty had accepted him.

Decades earlier, the 500-year-old school had split into separate Czech and German colleges. Reflecting how the country’s population was split into Czech and German speakers. The latter was what he would attend.

The jaunt to school was brief for Hermann. The heaviness of his trunk couldn’t alter this. Nor could his fatigue.

He forgot about his hunger and his increasing thirst. He did so while ignoring the smells of sizzling kielbasas wafting from the vendors lining the streets. As well as the aroma of fresh pilsner drifting from the pubs he passed. He could do this because the trip to and into Old Town was like making his way across the fantastical tales that his father had told him. Tales filled with ghosts and golems and impossibly beautiful women. In ways, the trip was like passing through the man’s dreams. Those that had become his own.

The trip awed Hermann. But it wasn’t the size of Prague that had. Or its age.

By this time he was no stranger to cities. In the army, he had been through many.

Some took up greater space than Prague. Others had histories that went beyond it.

But none were like it.

The center of Prague was a place where time had stopped. Each building was more ancient than the next.

With each step Hermann was taking, he was stepping into a past that was expanding before him. He couldn’t keep his mouth shut.

He got to the Karolinum, the seat of Charles’s German college. A place not much newer than the university itself.

This collection of structures mesmerized him. His future entwined with them.

They so mesmerized him that he couldn’t remember entering the lobby of his residency hall. One moment he was facing the Aula Magna’s façade. The next he was in front of his room.

Inside it was the oddest of sights.

A mountain of a man his age, with a shock of blond hair and the brightest blue eyes, was doing handstand push-ups against a wall. He was doing them with beads of wetness glistening off his chest, wearing only the type of buttoned undershorts that soldiers had worn during the war.

Hermann had no idea what to make of this.

So, while checking out the books on math and physics and geology in a case by the wall, he dropped his trunk by the unmade bed in the room’s corner.

He spied his new roommate with rising curiosity. “You must be Klaus.”

 

 

HERMANN RETURNED TO the Mercedes.

It was closing in on Prague and the other wonderful memories waiting for him to re-experience.

But there would be some not-so-wonderful memories lying in wait with them.

They overwhelmed the rest.
