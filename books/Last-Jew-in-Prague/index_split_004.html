chapter four: smetana in theresienstadt

 

 

HERMANN SQUEAKED OPEN the closet door. He took a step into the gendarmerie office.

Klaus was glaring at him with his arms crossed. He was also struggling to stay awake. He could’ve fallen asleep on his feet.

Hermann pointed to his jacket. “No star.”

“It’d be gratuitous on you,” Klaus said.

He marched toward his black attaché on the floor. He picked it up on his way out the door.

Hermann followed. The two SS troopers followed him. They marched into a cloudy and damp morning. It was the type of day Hermann would say that “you feel like you’re swimming through the air.”

This brightened and warmed. They entered the camp’s courtyard. The town’s marketplace back when it was a town. In a music pavilion, Karel Ančerl and his orchestra of fellow inmates were preparing to play.

Live music, theater, and art were among the features that distinguished Theresienstadt from concentration camps farther east. Along with better living conditions and a fleeting notion that you might survive the war. But Hermann would question whether this made the place better. He was inclined to believe that it made it worse. By creating a false sense of tolerableness.

He described Ančerl to me as a serious man in his mid-thirties. Nearing him, Hermann recalled how he and Ana had watched him conduct the orchestra at the Prague Free Theater on Vodička Street years earlier.

With astonishment, Hermann discerned that the man was the same as he had remembered him. The Nazis could starve him. They could dehumanize him. But they couldn’t alter him. Hermann discerned with this that, while Klaus could make him appear as a clown, it was up to him whether to play the part.

Closer the four got to the orchestra. It played Bedřich Smetana’s “Vltava.” A song known in the English-speaking world as “The Moldau,” the river’s German name.

Hermann had heard the song many times in the twenty years that he’d lived in the country. Several of these were in the camp. It was and is popular because it’s more than some classical song from a forgotten era. It’s a national song. In some ways, it’s more of a national song than “Where My Home Is,” the country’s anthem. During my trip to Prague, I heard “Vltava” often. I heard it on the plane when we landed.

The most famous of a collection of symphonic poems called My Country—composed by Smetana after he had become deaf—“Vltava” symbolizes the Czech nation’s drive toward its destiny. It follows a pair of springs in the Bohemian countryside, a cold and a warm, that join and grow into the roaring river that splits Prague in two before careening its way toward the Elbe.

Hermann, listening to the orchestra perform that morning, recollected those the song had enraptured. Including his Ana. It did so without respect to how many times that they’d heard it.

It had never meant much to him. He’d made himself believe that this was because he wasn’t Czech. But it wasn’t only that. The song, while beautiful and moving, was, for some reason, off to him. A wrongness was to it that he couldn’t place.

This performance would change that. To the extent that I would know this song more than any other. I hum it when I’m in need of comfort that nothing other can bring. I was humming it on that rainy desert night. As the water rose and encircled me. It was taking away escapes.

The song helps me remember my grandpa and the afternoons he would listen to it in our living room with the volume cranked up. The time I remember above the rest was when I was around five, after I had asked him and my grandma what had happened to my parents.

They were hesitant to say a word. But Grandpa told me that they had died in a car crash.

I was too young to fathom this and had no memories of either parent. But I hung my head that day. I slouched on the sofa.

Grandpa wanted to pick me up. He stormed into the room. He whipped on the music. He conducted it, describing each aspect of it.

“Can you hear the horns?” he shouted. With his hand cupped around his ear and the river sweeping us through a forest. “We are coming upon the huntsmen!”

Later in the song, with the introduction of a gentle polka, he cried, “We are at the wedding!” He danced with the peasants along the riverbank.

At the song’s conclusion, during its thunderous climax—as the harmonies clashed to capture the waves smashing into each other—Grandpa raised his fists over his head. “The rapids! We are reaching St. John’s rapids!”

Despite this exuberance, the performance at the camp would be Hermann’s favorite. He would swear that he could feel the Vltava’s freezing waters flowing into him and around him, smothering him in its tears. More than that, he could feel the currents pushing him somewhere he couldn’t see. But was driven to go.

The four men got to the orchestra.

Ančerl caught sight of Hermann and those accompanying him. Like the man across from Hermann in the barracks the night before, he smiled. With the same mild and forced smile. Hermann believed that it was for the same reason: the men were to shoot him.

But this wouldn’t happen here, Hermann assured himself. Nor would it happen in Auschwitz, a place they sent people from Theresienstadt without end. It’d be in his hometown. Where his Ana was. He would have no complicity in it.

Hermann smiled back at Ančerl. He could only gain from what would happen next. He was smiling while approaching the camp gate.

On the other side was a black-and-white sign on the archway. The sign that had greeted him on entering the camp those months earlier. It was greeting him now. From the gate’s opposite side. It read: Arbeit Macht Frei.

Work Sets You Free.

At last, Hermann seized its truth. He raced toward it.

But he slowed. He doubted the promise that he had made. Not the promise that he’d made to Ana not to give up. The other promise. The one that he’d made to himself upon leaving the gendarmerie closet. He had promised that he wouldn’t put any effort into investigating the murders. That he would švejkovat his way through it.

Švejkovat is an informal Czech verb. It’s difficult to translate. It derives from the antihero in the novel Hermann had mentioned to Klaus, who resisted authority through willful incompetence.

It’s a word that I learned at an early age. I would experience it a lot during my adolescence. Whenever I’d avoid some chore or would make a token effort to complete it—and this happened a lot in the temperament I was in after learning the details of this story—my grandpa would say, “Stop with all your švejking, young man” or “There will be no švejkování in this house!”

The word would make me laugh. It made me laugh when this was the least I wanted to do. It made me laugh on that freezing desert night.

It wasn’t because the word sounds funny. It was because of a man who never stopped caring about me. Disregarding how I gave him plenty of reasons to.

In the gendarmerie closet that morning, the word had a different connotation. Hermann was saying that he would be just like the good soldier Švejk. In that he’d undermine any help that he’d give the Nazis.

But he was now unsure whether this was a promise as hollow as the suit he was wearing.
