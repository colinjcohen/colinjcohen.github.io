chapter thirty-seven: the swatting of a fly

 

 

WHEN THE MAÎTRE D’ told Hermann that Dita lived on Dlážděná Street in the city’s New Town district, it held no meaning to him.

It continued having none after he and Klaus made their way there in the Mercedes.

He couldn’t have conceived that this trip would forever bind my fate with his.

Only when the Mercedes crossed the river did it strike him that they would be driving past a critical landmark of his recent past.

It wasn’t a positive one. It had been Masaryk Station and was now Hybernské Station. It was where a train on an August afternoon took Hermann to Theresienstadt.

The Mercedes closed in on the station.

Hermann remembered the day.

He returned to the onset of it.

 

 

HERMANN WAS STANDING in his bedroom late at night next to Ana. She was sleeping on their featherbed.

Dressed in Mr. Jacobs’s suit, he was ready to go.

A suitcase was at his feet. A letter was trembling in his hand. His way of making it easier on them both.

But on him the most.

With his hand trembling more, he placed the letter on the nightstand. It read:

 

My dearest Ana,

 

No words can express what I want to say to you. Nor can they make up for the hurt they will cause. For hours, I’ve written and rewritten this letter. But it never improves. It never could.

 

So I will just say it. The day that we’ve known would come has come. I must leave you.

 

But you will not leave me. You’ll be with me wherever I’ll be. The memories of you will hold me up.

 

I will never let them go.

 

Hermann had agonized over the letter’s body. But he agonized greater over how to finish it. He had to do so in Czech, and he couldn’t say “farewell.” He couldn’t use sbohem. A word that translates to “go with God.”

The last he wrote to her was “Na shledanou!”

Until we meet again!

Hermann scrambled.

He grabbed his suitcase. He left the apartment.

On tumbling out the front door of his building—rather than finding himself on Eliška Krásnohorská Street in the dead of night—he was back in Klaus’s Mercedes.

He was facing the train station down the street.

His eyes avoided the building. They avoided its surroundings. They avoided the train tracks. They were set in the opposite way of all this.

Yet he came upon the station. He came upon the cattle car that he had come upon eight months earlier.

 

 

THE HEAT AND humidity was suffocating. Hermann was gagging for breath.

Making matters worse was the proximity of others like him.

Among dozens, he climbed onto the train car. He was carrying his suitcase in his left hand. The crumpled pink deportation slip was in his other.

The car reminded Hermann of similar ones from his army days.

“Forty-and-eights,” people had called them. They could hold forty soldiers or eight horses.

But an excess of forty people were in the car. This was growing.

The crowd pushed Hermann to the back. Guided by the Nazi soldier outside, his weapon over his shoulder. He wasn’t expecting the slightest problem.

Hermann resisted the pushing. He pushed forward.

He wanted to put his eyes on his home and where his Ana was. He wanted to leave them there for as much as possible.

But whenever he got close to the opening, someone would drive him back.

With frenzy, he told himself that nothing more would stop him from getting to the front. He pushed harder.

Reaching some light, he rushed to the edge.

Along with the Prague skyline was a sight unexpected.

Ana was staggering toward him in the distance with her suitcase. Their meeting again was to happen a lot sooner than he had expected.

 

 

ANA’S DEATH WAS the last part of this story that my grandpa would tell me.

For years, much as he had refused to tell me about his military service, he refused to tell me how she died. He wouldn’t tell Grandma.

He only relented when I stammered into the house at the climax of all those times that I had disappointed him.

The memory of this flashed back to me on that desert night. As the rain petered out. As the water receded. Enough to hold my head over the water.

With the memory flashed what Jorge Luis Borges had written. That no matter how long or complex your life may be, it comes down to the single moment in which you learn who you are.

My moment came on the first Yom Kippur after my bar mitzvah.

Only at this time was I allowed to say Kaddish. I had wanted to say it for my parents. Only at this time did I learn from my grandpa that they hadn’t died.

I had understood why Grandpa lied to me when I was a child.

It wasn’t because it would’ve been awful learning that my parents had abandoned me. Or because it would’ve been worse not knowing where my decades-troubled mother was. Or who my father had been.

None of this would’ve compared to learning that my grandpa wasn’t really my grandpa. That my real one had been a Nazi.

It’s difficult describing my state after hearing this.

I had become two separate people: the quiet Jewish boy that I had been and the monster now inside him. A monster full of hate.

Some of this I directed at my grandpa. But the majority of it was at myself.

My guilt and shame were so strong that I wanted everyone to hate me. To the same degree that I did.

I did what I could to make this happen.

When I got home that night, stumbling drunk, brought home by cops who hadn’t arrested me out of respect for my grandpa, he was waiting for me on the staircase’s foot in his robe.

I was sure that he was throwing me out. Much as Grandma had threatened. Much as I deserved.

As I swayed toward him, ready to tell him off for good, he said, “I watched her die.”

I reeled. “What?”

He nodded toward the empty spot on the step where he had been slouching.

I sat beside him.

He told me what had happened to Ana.

 

 

WHEN HERMANN SAW Ana rushing toward the train, he couldn’t speak. Or react. She was impossible to believe.

She only became real when she yelled his name.

Hermann would say that he couldn’t imagine how Ana ended up at the train station.

But I can.

Hermann had become something like a drug to Ana.

From my talk with Věra, I believe that Ana, from before finishing Hermann’s letter, was searching for him. Her life had become too entwined with his. She couldn’t untwine it.

I can see her fighting to control the basic functions of her body. I see her straining for air. She had to change this. Regardless of how.

She packed a suitcase. She threw on her coat and shoes.

Throwing on a certain other item, she sped from the apartment with the suitcase. Toward a fate that she was steps from.

It was at this second that Hermann could speak.

“No,” he said. At a volume she couldn’t hear. “Stay back,” he called out seconds later. A fraction louder. “Stay away from the train!” he screamed.

Her head spun toward him. With mad desperation in her eyes. Desperation that meant that she was too far gone.

She was no longer rational. She hurled herself into the mass of people pushed onto the train.

The soldier pushed her forward.

But spotting neither a yellow star on her coat nor a pink slip of paper in her hand, he grabbed her arm.

She cried out. Like a wounded animal.

“You don’t belong here!” he howled.

“I do. This is the only ‘where’ I belong!”

He dragged her away.

To stop her from screaming and grappling, he slapped her across the face with the back of his hand.

Her screaming and grappling intensified. She clawed at his eyes. “You won’t take my ‘now’ from me! You won’t!”

With one hand, she shoved the soldier to the ground.

She ran through a tight opening in the crowd. She was steps from Hermann’s car.

“What are you doing?” he yelped, not able to follow what was happening.

He was frozen as well. He couldn’t make his body move.

Ana reached the car. She struggled to climb onto it with her suitcase. “I’ll be with you . . .”

She pulled herself on board.

Amid the stunned silence around her, she smiled at Hermann. Tears ran down her cheeks. “I’ll be with you wherever you’ll be.”

But the soldier had risen. He was ramming his way past the people outside the car.

Furious at what Ana had done and the humiliation that it had caused, he removed his weapon from around his shoulder. He jumped onto the car.

With the same casualness that he would’ve swatted a fly, he slammed the butt of his rifle into the back of her head. He knocked her lifeless body into Hermann’s arms.

“Ana!” he whimpered, drawing in the scent of her perfume. What she wore on special occasions. Such as on their wedding day.

The soldier tore her away.

He shoved Hermann back into the masses. Onto the car’s floor.

The last Hermann would see after they thrust the splintery wooden-planked gate in his face were the trail of blood and the soldier dragging his wife by the collar of her coat.

He dragged her as if she were garbage left in the road.

For Hermann, one horror was worse than witnessing this. His inability to prevent it.

He had saved a dozen strangers at Verdun. But he couldn’t save his own wife.

All he could do was watch her die.

 

 

I HELD MY crying grandpa on the staircase. I couldn’t grasp why he had told me about Ana.

For years I couldn’t.

But I do now. I realize now that he was telling me that you can’t carry the weight of what others have done.

Even when this weight falls into your arms.

 

 

“ARE YOU OKAY?” Klaus asked Hermann when the Mercedes parked beside Dita’s apartment building. This in reaction to the terror on his former friend.

Like by the Maisel Synagogue days earlier, Hermann could hear the kindness in Klaus’s voice.

But he wanted nothing to do with it.

“No,” he barked with his grief rushing back. As well as a squall of hatred toward Klaus that he couldn’t control. “I’m not okay.”

“Would you like a drink? A beer? Or stronger?”

“Let’s get this over with.”

Hermann meant this. He wanted to finish the job as soon as he could. He wanted to meet the same fate Ana had. He hoped that he could do so with the same lack of fear she had.

He and Klaus exploded through the car doors.

In the same manner, they entered the apartment building and a rundown lobby.

They reached a staircase. They went up it.

On the second floor, they found Dita’s apartment halfway down the corridor from the stairwell.

Hermann sucked in as much air as his lungs would allow. He knocked on the door.

The lone response was from down the hall. “Nobody’s there.”

Hermann and Klaus wheeled behind themselves.

A graying woman was pulling an empty shopping cart toward them. “The girl moved out weeks ago.”

Hermann took a step toward her. “You don’t happen to know where she moved to, do you?”

“I don’t happen to know.”

“What about the landlord?”

“He couldn’t help you either. She left like a burglar. She didn’t bother to close the door.”

Hermann sighed.

The woman passed them on the way to the staircase. They followed her.

“Where are we going?” Klaus asked.

“I have an idea,” Hermann said.

The two shot past the woman. They returned to the building’s lobby.

Hermann stopped by Dita’s mailbox. “As Colonel Reidl told you last night, you don’t have to worry about the law.”

Klaus joined Hermann.

He fished out the Ehrendolch dagger that SS men carried with them. He used it to break open Dita’s mailbox.

Mail poured out onto the floor.

Hermann picked it up. He pulled out what remained in the mailbox.

In the latter was a handwritten envelope. With a return address from a Jitka Prejslerová. She lived in the city’s Pankrác section, a few kilometers south of where they were.

Klaus tapped the woman’s name. “Could that be her mother?”

“We’ll find out soon enough.”
