chapter forty-four: dinner with hegel and heisenberg and a man named saul

 

 

NIGHT HAD DESCENDED upon Old Town Square when Klaus’s Mercedes parked on the north side of it.

With a lengthy evening awaiting them, Hermann and Klaus took their time climbing from the car.

Klaus stretched out beside it. He told the driver that he could leave.

Hermann surveyed the square. “Where did your aide park that car?”

Klaus surveyed the square too. He pointed at a late-model Škoda Popular up the road. “That’s the one.”

“The Škodovka?”

“Will it do?”

“It’ll more than do. What about Reidl’s car?”

Klaus stepped into the square. He checked around it. Including down Železná Street across from it. “It’s not here. He probably hasn’t come yet.”

“We better wait. If he parks on the square’s other side, we couldn’t follow him.”

“Why couldn’t we cut across the square?”

“I’m assuming that you’ve never followed someone. The whole point is not to be noticed.”

Klaus clicked his tongue. He followed Hermann to the car. He followed him into it.

The two waited. Hermann did so with his wits far from the task ahead of him. He was wishing that he were anywhere but where he was and was about to go.

Klaus was elsewhere too. He had moved farther from Hermann and himself.

This changed. Three Nazi officers strutted across the square toward Gertrud’s building.

The sight sank Klaus in his seat. “It might be better if we sit here till the party ends.”

“You were cognizant that Nazis would be here?”

Klaus shoved his head toward the officers. “I wasn’t cognizant that they’d be here. Usually, Colonel Reidl is the highest-ranking officer there.”

“We could sit here. I wouldn’t mind. But Gertrud could make note of our absence. This could make Reidl suspicious. And cautious. That could limit the impact of following him.”

The two returned to their waiting.

 

 

COLONEL REIDL ARRIVED. His Mercedes parked a short distance away on Pařížská Street.

He lumbered across the square. He lumbered up Železná.

Hermann and Klaus shadowed him. They crossed the ancient cobblestones in the cooling air.

Klaus’s eyes were far from Hermann. “You’ll be uncomfortable tonight.” He said this looking more uncomfortable than Hermann felt.

“This isn’t breaking news.”

“Stay as invisible as you can and as quiet as you can. We’ll leave at the earliest possible moment.”

The two reached Gertrud’s home. Outside it was a security detail. It took them more than ten minutes to get through it and enter the building.

Kamča answered the front door. She smiled at Klaus.

The smile vanished on shifting to Hermann. She stood in silence. A condition uncommon for her.

Some words stumbled out. “Who, who’s this?”

Klaus told her. He told her, too, that Gertrud had invited him.

Kamča let the two inside. She brought them into the crowded living room.

A half-dozen well-dressed women were cavorting with twice that many Nazi officers. A few were more senior than the two that had been crossing the square. One sent Klaus teetering. He was desperate for an excuse to leave.

Hermann also had a reaction from viewing the guests. It was rage. He felt it with intensity. At first, he couldn’t say what had brought it on. He had seen plenty of Nazis at the palace and at other places around town since his return to Prague without feeling this.

Could it have been related to the anger that he had felt earlier when re-experiencing Ana’s death?

No. It was the occasion’s gaiety and how everyone was drinking in excess. Hermann was aware that men could perform atrocious acts under order and during battle. But here were people under no duress enjoying themselves despite the misery that they were causing. This infuriated him.

Hermann wasn’t the only person upset. The Nazis and their female companions became aghast. At him. They fell quiet and glared at him.

“You people act as if you had never seen a Jew,” Gertrud called out, sweeping into the room in an elegant black gown that she had bought earlier that day. “It’s all right. He’s working with Captain Stamm on an important case for the benefit of the Reich. I invited them both.”

This placated most guests. But their conversation and frivolity were muted. Even if their drinking was not.

One guest, though, wasn’t placated. Or muted.

Colonel Reidl marched to Klaus. He howled under his breath, “What are you doing? Have you left your mind somewhere?”

“Gertrud invited him,” Klaus said, restating what Reidl had already learned.

“Have you not heard of the word ‘no’?”

“It’s she who has a difficult time with it.”

“How do you plan on explaining his presence here?”

“As she said, he’s helping us with an important case for the benefit of the Reich.”

The colonel pitched his fists. He set his focus on the man who had become the head of what during the war the Nazis called the Protectorate of Bohemia and Moravia. “Do you not see General Daluege? Do you believe that he’ll believe such nonsense?”

“That nonsense happens to be the truth. If anyone presses me on it, I’ll explain that Hermann’s a collaborator. That I can’t leave his side until he completes the services that he’s providing us.”

“And if they ask you what services he’s providing, what will you say?”

“I could, I could say that he’s helping us track down some missing Jewish property. A treasure, perhaps. One that would greatly enrich the Reich if found.”

Reidl quieted. He sputtered off, his face flushed.

Klaus covered his mouth with the back of his hand. “I shouldn’t have said that.”

Hermann covered his own mouth. “It’s okay. ‘Fright’ is a whole lot better than ‘suspicious.’ Or ‘cautious.’ It could cause him to be rash tonight. This could help us.”

Gertrud wedged herself between the men. She took each of their arms. “What are you two murmuring about?”

Klaus locked onto General Daluege. “I was asking Hermann a question.”

“And that was?”

“How could someone be stupid enough to invite him and me to a dinner party attended by a man who was effectively the absolute ruler of both the country and our fates?”

“And what did Hermann say?”

“He had no more of an idea than I had. I’m only surprised that Reich Minister Frank is not here.”

The mention of this man’s name made Gertrud wince. But she overcame it. She swaggered toward General Daluege. “I’m going to advance your career tonight, dear. Just you watch.”

Klaus did. He watched Gertrud stride to the man like she was approaching her best friend since childhood. “What am I going to do with her?”

Hermann didn’t answer. But like when he had met Gertrud, he felt a visceral dislike toward her.

That didn’t prevent his fascination of her. It was higher than before. He couldn’t keep his eyes off her. Nor could he avoid listening to what she and the general had to say.

A sly smirk fell over the man’s horse-like face. “You certainly have a flair for making a scene.”

“It’s early. The real scene will come with dessert.”

“I really should have you arrested for pulling this stunt. Or shot.”

“Then you’d miss dinner. And trust me, you’d regret it.”

They both laughed.

Gertrud continued through the crowd. The party continued with it. So did a drinking that got heavier. Everyone forgot about Hermann. They got as giddy as before.

However, the arrival of dinner reminded them of Hermann. They were reluctant to sit near him. Gertrud had to place him at the dining table’s left end next to Klaus. She had to place herself across from him.

Hermann felt discomfort. The discomfort that Klaus had warned him about. The discomfort that he’d expected before this.

He felt more rage with the discomfort. The prospect of eating with these people made him sick. He hadn’t a doubt that he wouldn’t touch a drop.

This went away. When Kamča served him a plate of her venison in a red wine sauce. It intoxicated him. On both sight and smell.

Gertrud floated toward Hermann and his ogling of his food. “The sauce, it’s made from the same wine that you enjoyed last night.”

Hermann tried a bite. It was better than what he had had at U Bílého Lva. Never had he tasted venison so tender and ungamelike. It was so good that he swooned. Also, for the first time since returning to Prague, he had an appetite. He devoured all that was in front of him. Down to the crumbs. Like many, he had a second serving.

He forgot about his company. What mattered was the food. How it made him enjoy being alive.

The feeling didn’t last. Hermann’s discomfort returned after eating. The silence among the guests returned with it. Everybody was uncomfortable. Apart from Colonel Reidl. He was elsewhere.

The middle-aged woman next to Gertrud tired of her malaise. She sneered at the cause of it. “Isn’t it amazing how distinguishable Jews are? I swear I could spot one from blocks away. As easily as I could spot a rat.”

Gertrud plastered a smile in the woman’s direction. “That wasn’t very nice, Lina. Hermann’s a guest here. And he’s obviously on our side. Or he wouldn’t be working with Klaus. Is that not true, darling?”

“I wouldn’t say that he’s on our side.”

Hermann dipped his head forward. “I can attest to that.”

“So what are you doing with him?” Gertrud asked Klaus while lifting a glass of wine that the entire party was enjoying other than Klaus.

“He’s helping us sort out an investigation,” Klaus said. He said this not only to Gertrud. But to the guests. “An investigation that only he could sort out. As a man who was among the best police detectives in the city. If not the best.”

“Even Protector Heydrich,” General Daluege interjected, “who was no lover of Jews, had personal relationships with a few. Which he maintained while orchestrating their race’s destruction.”

“Is that so?” Gertrud blurted out.

“Twice I had dinner with him and Paul Sommer, the champion fencer. Heydrich believed that you could isolate certain exceptional Jews from the race as a whole.”

The general paused. He scoffed at Hermann. “An individual rat can be exceptional.”

Daluege went back to Gertrud. “So it doesn’t surprise me that Captain Stamm would want to make use of an exceptional Jewish police detective if it would further the ends of the Reich. I commend him. For his ingenuity and boldness.”

Gertrud switched her attention to Klaus. “And how’s that case coming, dear?”

“It’s fast approaching its conclusion. I’m hopeful that we’ll resolve it presently.”

Gertrud moved on to Hermann. “What about you?”

“Me?”

“Why are you helping us?”

“Let’s just say that they made me a compelling offer.”

“Don’t you hate us?”

“What do you mean by ‘us’?”

“Germans, of course.”

“I don’t hate all of you. I don’t hate Hegel. Or Schopenhauer. Or Kant. I especially don’t hate Eckhart. I admire them all.”

“Ah, Hegel. I’ve admired him too. Him and his dialectics. What a delightful concept.”

Lina rolled her eyes. “Isn’t that a Marxist concept?”

Gertrud placed her hand on the woman’s. “That’s dialectical materialism, my dear.”

“Oh.”

“Hegelian dialectics describe the development of things. You start with a thesis: an instance. This party, for example. Opposing this is an antithesis. Hermann here stands (or sits) as the antithesis to what this party represents. And the synthesis is the outcome of the thesis and the antithesis reacting to each other. In our case, we are experiencing an actual synthesis in progress.”

“How interesting,” Lina droned. With an expression that suggested it wasn’t interesting to her in the least.

“But tell me, Hermann,” Gertrud said, “are there living Germans you don’t hate?”

“Some.”

Hermann meant someone in particular. Someone who was close to him. In many ways. Someone who could infuriate and enrage him. But only for a moment. “I can’t hate them regardless of how much I want to hate them. To be honest, I don’t believe that the average German is bad. The loudest voices are those we hear.”

“I bet if we turned off the lights, we wouldn’t guess you were Jewish.”

This amused Hermann.

Gertrud eyed him sideways. “Was that funny?”

“It’s just that I used to wish the same. I wished that people would not see my face and its Semitic features. That they would see only the man underneath it. I did what I could to make this happen. Each choice I made in life was to facilitate this. To fit in with you. If not become one of you. You see, I didn’t choose to be Jewish. I didn’t want to be Jewish. Any more than I chose or wanted to be left-handed.”

“But Jewish you are.”

“Jewish I am. And do you want to know what? I’ve stopped being ashamed of it. And of how I look. I stopped the second that I stepped into Theresienstadt. The very second itself. It was then that I had to accept what I am. And I’ve since discovered a more extraordinary revelation. I like what I am. I’m proud of it.”

“‘Pride goeth before a fall,’” yapped a Nazi from down the table.

Laughter erupted across the table. A wave of it.

Hermann, facing straight ahead, countered with his own quote from the Bible: “‘But by the grace of God I am what I am, and His grace wasn’t given to me in vain.’”

Fury shot over the Nazi’s face. “That line is from our Bible.”

Hermann ignored the anger. “But the man who wrote it, a man you know as Paul but whose name was Saul, was a Jew like me.”

The conversation fell silent. The discomfort level rose. Some people made gestures that intimated that they were getting ready to leave. Including General Daluege.

Gertrud became desperate to breathe life back into her party. She tilted toward Lina. “Klaus and Hermann are old friends.”

Lina took in Klaus. Her head tilted to the right. A hint of suspicion was in her eyes. “Now, that’s interesting.”

Klaus sipped his milk. “We were roommates in college. For a few months.”

Gertrud nodded at him. “And then it hit you that he was Jewish?”

“No.”

“So what happened between you two?”

“It’s an old story. Best forgotten.”

“Last night, Hermann, you mentioned a wife. Where is she?”

“She passed.”

“I’m sorry. Did it happen at Theresienstadt?”

Hermann lowered his head. “She wasn’t Jewish. She could’ve lived a life mostly unaffected by you and others like you. She suffered what she did, and she suffered a lot, for my sins alone.”

“Can we please change the subject?” Klaus begged.

“To what?” Gertrud asked.

“To you,” Hermann said. “Tonight you’ve accumulated much data about me. But I’ve collected none about you. Apart from that you are utterly charming.”

“You know what Heisenberg says? The more you know about one aspect of an object, the less you can know about its other aspects. The same applies to people, I’m afraid. Besides, I’m quite uninteresting.”

“I doubt that.”

“I’m nothing but your average brainless socialite living off my family’s money.”

“I’m not familiar with many brainless people who can not only articulate Hegelian dialectics and Heisenberg’s uncertainty principle but make them understandable for the average person.”

“I picked them up from a former lover. He was a rather boring man. But he did have his usefulnesses.”

Klaus scanned the table. “She’s being modest. Beyond it. I have colleagues who are less knowledgeable about the war. This didn’t happen through osmosis.”

General Daluege added, “Her role in presiding over the protectorate’s National Socialist Women’s League provides her with significant access. I’ve been surprised by what she has picked up.”

Gertrud clasped her hands on the table. “You also pick up a good deal when you travel as much as I do.”

“You travel with a war going on?” Hermann asked.

“I’m not easily frightened,” she said. She said it with conviction that coerced Hermann to admire it.

Lina took Gertrud’s arm. “And she has friends in high places. She was on a first-name basis with Protector Heydrich. Didn’t you have dinner with him right before that ill-fated trip of his?”

Hermann gawked at Gertrud. The veil had slipped from her face. Enough to make him believe that her presence in this case had been more than circumstantial. “Hmmm. With connections like that, I bet you could open all sorts of doors.”

Klaus spun toward Hermann. He glared at him.

Hermann caught wind of it. But he chose to let it pass over him. He continued gawking at Gertrud while Klaus seethed beside him. He was seething as he had on the day of their falling out.

Confusion struck General Daluege. “What dinner was this?”

Gertrud became defensive. “The dinner was well before his, his mishap. And it wasn’t a private dinner. Or anything of the kind. It was a state dinner. In celebration of Grand Mufti Husseini’s visit. We were barely in the same room. You were there, in fact.”

“Ah, yes, I remember.”

The conversation’s twist roused Colonel Reidl. He sneered at Hermann. “Being that you were in Theresienstadt, I guess you didn’t hear what happened to our dear beloved protector, who governed your nation with such a gentle hand.”

“It happened right before I left for Theresienstadt,” Hermann said with what he would later recognize as a startling lack of fear. Which sprang from his not having much left to fear. “I heard about how he was assassinated. In broad daylight, no less. On the road to Berlin, wasn’t it? The whole of Josefov heard about it. And if I can be honest another time, we felt joy for days. Until, until we heard what you did to Lidice. In response to his killing.”

“A town that nobody will miss,” the colonel cooed, “along with its people. I can barely recall watching it burn.”

Gertrud squirmed in her seat. She grimaced. “They certainly learned their lesson, didn’t they?”

She was now more uncomfortable than Klaus.

He must’ve taken this as a sign to leave. He left with Hermann in tow.

Gertrud led the two through the front door.

“Goodnight, darling,” she said to Klaus in the doorway.

She kissed him.

Hermann turned from them. He crossed his arms.

“Goodnight,” Klaus said to her. “Please don’t let Colonel Reidl stay up too late. He’s got a lot to do.”

“He’s not alone.”

Gertrud shut the door.

Klaus thundered down the stairs. With his fists clenched.

Hermann followed him.

“Don’t fool yourself into believing that I didn’t catch the insinuation that you made,” Klaus barked, fighting the urge to shoot Hermann. He was shaking with fury.

“It wasn’t an insinuation. It was an observation.”

“It was nonsense.”

“You must investigate a crime objectively. I’m not accusing her. Or calling her a suspect. I was making an observation.”

“I’d suggest that you leave her out of your observations. If you want to keep making them.”
