all the beautiful words

 

 

I WAS HAVING a day that nightmares seek refuge from.

It started before dawn. My phone’s alarm woke me.

I was simmering on my sleeping bag. It was cooking me raw.

The morning spiraled downward from there.

Ants had discovered my water bottle, hidden in the canyon facing me. They were inside and out of it, the single oasis in the sands surrounding me.

A likewise descent followed in the swelling sun.

The 111 bus to Cat City was forty minutes late. The 30 bus to Palm Springs and EōS Fitness was later than that.

It didn’t matter.

No water was in the gym. None would come.

I had to wash myself in a sink at the Rancho Mirage Public Library.

Slouching in its Quiet Room, I willed myself the wanting to want ahead. I battled to stay above the floor all afternoon.

The temperature had risen when I left the building at night. A furnace swept across me.

But it wasn’t the surge of heat that would bring me to my knees. Nor was it the scarcity of hope encroaching on me. Or my inability to escape either. Or my self-pity.

A strap of my backpack broke.

Cursing, I dropped to a ground that was burning through my jeans. I tied the strap with safety pins.

They wouldn’t hold for two hours.

A drunk bumped into me on the 111 bus back to nowhere. He knocked the strap off before I could get a seat.

“Look where you’re going!” he howled, staggering off the bus.

I started to howl back. But I didn’t finish.

The bus kicked.

A tattered book slid down the aisle. To my toes.

Jiří Orten’s Jeremiah’s Tears.

What would obscure Czech poetry be doing on a bus in the middle of Coachella Valley?

I didn’t care. I leaped at the volume. At the light shining from it. As it had long ago. In a place far away.

 

 

THE SUN LABORED above Prague in August 1942.

My grandpa Hermann woke. Ana was in his arms.

This made him happy. The same happy that got him through each morning since his life had fallen apart.

It wasn’t enough to keep him that way.

So, careful not to wake his wife, he eased himself from their featherbed. He dressed. He did so with a sense of purpose, having tired of his moping.

In the full-length mirror on the closet door, he put on his favorite suit. This he had made after the previous war. It no longer fit him because of a belly that he’d been developing from before the occupation.

Getting into the suit wasn’t Hermann’s toughest challenge that morning. It was pretending to ignore the yellow star stitched into the jacket.

He overcame both. He left the bedroom. He grabbed his fedora on the way out of his apartment on Eliška Krásnohorská Street.

Hermann was slight. Belly and all. With graying curly brown hair and an unimposing gait. You would never guess that he would’ve evoked fear in those who would induce it.

On this day, he wasn’t evoking. It was so early that he was the lone person on the streets in the city’s dwindling Jewish quarter of Josefov.

He made his way to Old Town Square. He followed a path that he could’ve walked blindfolded.

On its ancient cobblestones was a single person other than him. A svelte young man nearing from the opposite side.

Wearing a checkered cap, he was carrying a stack of typed pink papers into the district that Hermann had left.

Like with the yellow star on his jacket, Hermann pretended to ignore the man’s pink papers. To help with this, he tipped his hat to the statue of Jan Hus as the man passed.

Hermann reached Celetná Street. He strode to the local police station.

He halted outside it. He remembered that this was where he no longer worked.

Turning around, he crept back to his moping.

The station door swung open behind him. A voice called out, “Herr Weiss!”

Hermann inched toward Marta, a young woman with light-brown hair and the same-colored eyes.

She rushed to him. She hugged him. “How are you?” she whispered in German.

“I’m fine,” he lied in the same language, breaking their embrace.

“We miss you.”

He masked his disbelief. “Yeah?”

“Many of us. It’s not the same without you. The criminals are on the loose.”

A truck interrupted them. It drove by packed with armed Nazi soldiers.

Hermann craned his head at it. “They are indeed on the loose.”

The door to the police station creaked.

Detective Klima, an unkempt, heavyset man a decade younger than Hermann, slithered from it. He evaded Hermann. “Hey.”

Hermann answered in kind.

Klima swung himself toward Marta. “Let’s go.”

Marta veered to Hermann. “Another suicide. On Široká. A leaper.”

Hermann shifted in the street’s course.

“I went by there fifteen minutes ago,” he said with no surprise in his voice.

“The call just came in.”

Klima crossed his arms. He scowled at Marta. “Let’s go.”

The two headed off.

Hermann lurched at them. “Mind if I tag along?”

He regretted the words. They stank of desperation and pitifulness. These were the last he wanted to express. To Klima the most.

Klima groaned. “Go home, Hermann.”

Marta tilted to the detective. “It’s on his way home.”

“Fine. But don’t get in the way.”

Hermann followed them.

The three passed over the cobblestones to Široká Street. They made no sounds but their footsteps.

Down the block, a uniformed policeman waited. Along with a pair of paramedics. An ambulance. A dead woman.

She was on a stretcher beneath a sheet. Only her shoes stuck out.

The three reached the scene, drenched from the morning’s rising humidity and heat. Klima sneered at the paramedics. “You boys got here quick.”

One man, who had a faint black mustache, scratched his head. “We were in the neighborhood when we got the call.”

Klima lifted the sheet.

He didn’t react to the woman’s state. A state Hermann couldn’t avoid reacting to.

“Where did you find her?” Klima snapped at the policeman. It was as if he blamed him for her death.

The man pointed at the ambulance. “They parked in front of her.”

He whisked his arm toward the building beside it. “Her name was Berta Ohrensteinová. She lived up there.”

Klima pivoted to Marta. He glared. “Write this up for me.”

He didn’t say another word. He lumbered across the street.

On the other side was an unnamed pub. It was not only open at this hour but hadn’t closed the night before.

Hermann took more interest in the scene than Klima had.

His eyes were on the five-story roof from which the woman had fallen. It was a good distance from the street.

Marta skewed her head at him. “What’s wrong?”

“Wrong?”

“You doubt it was a suicide, don’t you?”

“I still can’t hide myself from you.”

“Why don’t you believe it was a suicide?”

“I didn’t say that. Or think it. But in my experience, when people leap off buildings, they don’t leap far from them. They typically land face-first.”

“She was pushed?”

“Either that or she had a running start and somersaulted in the air.”

Marta eyed the woman’s heels. “Not in those shoes.”

“You have an excellent mind for this work, Marta. You should be the detective. Klima should be your assistant.”

“I’d rather be your assistant again.”

“Oh, well. It was nice seeing you.”

Hermann dithered.

But he tipped his hat to Marta. He shuffled off.

The man in the checkered cap cornered onto the following street. The man with the pink papers under his arm. The papers Hermann wished he hadn’t spotted. That he didn’t want to approach.

“We could peek inside the apartment,” Marta said.

Hermann spun toward her. “What?”

She got the policeman’s notice. “We could, couldn’t we, Pepa?”

“Sure. It’s number 16. On the third floor. Jirka’s going through it.”

Hermann shook his head. “I’m not sure.”

Marta gripped her hips. “You’re sure.”

Hermann’s hesitance lingered. But he trailed Marta into the building. He trailed her up the stairs.

A policeman exited the woman’s apartment.

On Hermann’s ascent, he stood up straight. He buttoned his jacket. “Herr Weiss.”

Hermann’s face gleamed. This was a gleam that would welcome me my whole childhood. I saw it on the bus driving across the void. It was sitting me up. It was pushing me beyond my day. “Hello, Jirka. How are you?”

“Just fine, sir.”

Marta swayed toward the door. “Do you mind if we have a look?”

“Klidně,” the man babbled, not able to convey other than in Czech a word that conveyed his assent.

He opened the door. He let the two inside.

Hermann inspected a closet by the foyer. He spied Marta. “You’ll have to notify her husband.”

“She had a husband?”

Hermann opened the door in full.

On a rod hung coats belonging to a man and a woman. Their hats were on the shelf above them.

Hermann moved on. To a set of drawers. Marta went into the living room.

He tugged out a passport. He flipped its pages. “His name is Eduard. Ana and I have chatted with him at that café on Maisel. A nice guy.”

“Take a gander at this,” Marta shouted.

She hustled to Hermann. With a typed pink slip of paper that made him cringe. “The Nazis were sending her to Theresienstadt. She was supposed to leave from Hybernské tomorrow morning.”

“Hybernské?”

“That’s what they’ve renamed Masaryk Station.”

“Where did you get that paper?”

“On a bureau.”

“Was there one for Eduard?”

“Nothing but this.”

“Odd. They usually send families to the camps together.”

“He could have his slip with him. He could be at work. Or running an errand.”

“He could.”

Hermann set foot into the living room.

A book was lying flat on a writing desk steps away.

Jiří Orten’s Jeremiah’s Tears.

He picked it up. He fixed on its pages. “Ana loves his poems,” he murmured. He read a couplet aloud.

 

if there is light in me

it is also in pain, it’s in you, in you as well!

 

Hermann felt happy. As happy as he had been in Ana’s arms earlier. The same happy.

Marta scanned the room. “Odd.”

He placed the book on the desk. “What is?”

“It’s odd that a German-speaking, middle-aged Jew would take an interest in Czech modernist poetry.”

“It may surprise you at what a German-speaking, middle-aged Jew would take interest in.”

“No offense.”

“None taken.”

“It does appear that she killed herself. First, they were sending her to that camp. Then she was reading that book. Leave it to you to select the sole hopeful lines in it. The rest could bring down a city block. Or two.”

“You’re right. It does appear a suicide.”

“But you believe someone killed her?”

“The extent of my certainty is that she didn’t jump.”

Hermann continued through the apartment. He paused at a window facing Široká Street.

Klima was stammering from the pub. Down the road.

Hermann edged toward Marta. “How are you for time today?”

“I can take as much as I want. Klima must be drunk by now. If I type that report before I go home, it’ll be fine. I’d like to type the truth. It’d be a nice change.”

“Let’s see what we can uncover.”

The two walked across the street.

They walked into the pub.

The stench of stale beer was palpable. Wallowing in it were a half-dozen groggy patrons and an aging bartender.

Hermann explained to them what had happened to Mrs. Ohrensteinová. “Did any of you notice her fall?”

A man raised his hand. “I did. I told Honza.”

He lobbed himself toward the bartender. “He called it in.”

“Did you know her?” Hermann asked.

He shook his head.

Another person, tables over, slumped in his seat. “I did. In passing. She was the mother of that poet who got killed.”

Hermann lowered his gaze. “Jiří Orten?”

“That’s him.”

A guy in the room’s far-right corner raised his brows. “He got killed?”

Hermann sank his gaze farther. Much farther. “An ambulance hit him. The hospital wouldn’t treat him because he was a Jew.”

“That’s a lot of tragedy for one family.”

Hermann lifted himself. “Are any of you familiar with her husband? His name is Eduard.”

The bartender drifted to Hermann. “He comes in here. A nice guy.”

“When was he here last?”

“A few days ago.”

“Did he ever mention where he works?”

“He lost his job some time ago.”

“When a man loses his job,” interjected a patron, his weariness drilling into his beer, “he loses his purpose.”

Hermann exhaled. “I have one final question. Did anyone exit the building after the woman fell?”

The men swiveled to each other. The bartender nodded at the front window. “A back door is in that building. It leads to an alley.”

“Thank you. You’ve been most helpful.”

Hermann and Marta left the pub. He did with a drawn-out moan.

She sighed. “What we learned in there corroborates that she killed herself.”

Hermann couldn’t prevent a bout of self-doubt. No matter how alien it was to him. “You’re right, Marta. I could be wrong about this.”

“It’d be the first.”

“I’ve lost my touch. Along with my purpose.”

“I don’t believe it. Let’s check that back door.”

“We should check the roof beforehand. We should’ve checked it before the apartment. I have lost my touch.”

“You’re just rusty. It’ll wear off.”

The two returned inside the apartment building. They climbed to the roof.

Marta followed the echo of a door opening and closing in the alley. Hermann faltered toward the spot where the woman had fallen.

No sign of foul play was about.

But he felt the same pull many have from the brink. Including me. Though mine was fleeting when I got off the bus across the street from the canyons where I camped. I let the story take me onward.

Hermann dipped his head over the precipice. Above the chasm below. A force was compelling him. It wouldn’t relent. “One step.”

He recalled another of Orten’s verses. One far from hopeful.

 

I’ve found my fall. And on what? Tears!

 

Hermann dragged out his left foot. He would swear that he should’ve plunged.

A smile prevented it.

Ana’s.

It was everywhere.

The smile that he had run into twenty years earlier when he stumbled past the doors of U Zlatého Tygra. The smile that had greeted him each morning. The smile that sent him to sleep each night. The smile that said he mattered. The smile that was holding him up.

“Herr Weiss!” Marta wheezed from the roof’s other side. “Quick!”

The shock of her voice sent Hermann teetering.

But he regained his balance. He scurried to the ledge facing the alley.

The same ambulance from earlier was in the road. As well as a suited man speaking to the driver. The paramedic with the faint black mustache.

Hermann pointed at the man in the suit. “That’s Eduard.”

Eduard doddered. Toward the vehicle’s back door. He doddered inside it.

Hermann jabbed his finger at the fender. “Do you have a pen to write down that license plate?”

Marta yanked a notepad and a pen from her handbag. She jotted down the number.

The two ran down the stairs. They burst into the alley. They wheeled toward the ambulance.

It sped from them.

Marta caught her breath. She wiped the dampness from her face. “Now what?”

Hermann needed moments to catch his own breath. He needed further ones to wipe his own face and for his own words to form. “When we got here, the ambulance surprised Klima. Why?”

“Much has changed since you left the force. Because getting spare parts is so difficult, fewer ambulances are on the road. But more people are needing them. They must give priority to the living ones. It takes hours before they collect a corpse. Sometimes longer.”

Hermann twisted toward the building. “A phone was in the apartment. Let’s call the dispatcher. Let’s learn who was driving that ambulance.”

The two went back to the Ohrensteins’ flat.

Marta made the call. She spoke with someone.

She covered the receiver with her hand. “The woman insists that that ambulance is out of service. She insists that they haven’t sent one here yet.”

“Give her the paramedics’ descriptions. Ask her if she can identify them.”

Marta did.

The dispatcher told her that she worked in a different place than the paramedics. She couldn’t put a face to a name.

Hermann heaved a breath. “Get the names and addresses of paramedics who aren’t working. And the reasons they’re not working.”

The dispatcher collected the files of paramedics who worked on different shifts. Or were ill. Or on vacation. They were so plentiful that Hermann wanted to give up. He and Marta wouldn’t have time to call on many.

Instead, he asked for what he hoped would be the smallest and best subset of suspects. Those who had called in sick.

Four people were in this group. But their homes were outside the city center and spread out.

Making the task harder for Hermann and Marta was that they had no car. They had to take the city’s rickety and furnace-like network of trams.

Adding to their challenge was the law that had forbidden Jews from riding in those with a single car.

It took two hours to get to the first man.

He lived in a southeastern section called Hostivař. He was so racked from flu that it took him minutes to crawl to the door.

To reach the next man in Podolí, the two had to go west to the river.

He was in worse shape than the previous man. He couldn’t get out of bed.

“Mám takovou kocovinu!” he yelled, letting them aware of his hangover.

The third man lived in the city’s northern outskirts. In an area called Libeň.

Hermann took off his jacket as they got to his apartment. He used it to dry his head.

Laying it and the yellow star across his arm, he knocked on the door.

A woman in her thirties answered it.

He showed her his old police ID. “We’re looking for Petr Dobrý.”

“That’s my husband. But he’s not here.”

“We were told that he called in sick today.”

“It’s not that kind of sickness.”

“I don’t understand.”

The woman peered into Hermann. She peered into his soul. “Why don’t you come in?”

They did.

Mrs. Dobrá served them lukewarm tea in her living room. Or what passed for tea during the war. “My husband hasn’t been right since the accident.”

Hermann leaned toward the woman. “What kind of accident?”

“He killed someone in his ambulance. By accident.”

“Did he tell you the name of this someone?”

“It, it was Jiří Orten. It wasn’t Peťa’s fault. They’ve got the drivers running around like mad these days. It was bound to happen. It’s just a shame that it happened to Jiří. Peťa did what he could to help him. He begged the people at the hospital to take him. And he didn’t know who Jiří was at the time. It was only after he died that Peťa read some of his books. He’s been inconsolable.”

“Does he know Jiří’s parents?”

“He does. They don’t blame him a bit. They hugged Peťa when we went to Jiří’s funeral. But my husband feels guilty. He does what he can to help those people. He’s with them today.”

Hermann slid toward Marta. “Perhaps you could help her clean up.”

Mrs. Dobrá shook her head. “That isn’t necessary.”

Marta rose with the tea service. She grasped why Hermann wanted her to do this. “It is necessary.”

She and Mrs. Dobrá stepped into the kitchen.

Hermann jumped to his feet. He hurried to a trio of drawers.

On top of it was a photograph. Of a man with a faint black mustache.

Hermann turned over the drawers’ contents.

He got nowhere.

So he opened the closet by the front door. He scoured Peťa’s coats and jackets.

In one that was damp from the past evening’s thunderstorm, he came upon a receipt for a shovel.

Marta and Mrs. Dobrá came back from the kitchen. Marta joined Hermann.

He forced a smile at Mrs. Dobrá. “Thank you for your time. You’ve been most helpful.”

Hermann and Marta left the apartment. They stuttered down the corridor.

Marta snuck to Hermann. “What did you discover?”

“Where we can discover Mr. Dobrý.”

Hermann said this with heaviness in his voice. The case wouldn’t resolve itself in a neat manner.

The two revisited the trams.

They trekked to the city’s Žižkov section and the New Jewish Cemetery. In the setting sun was the ambulance parked by the entrance.

Inside the gates, a caretaker greeted the two. He told them where Jiří Orten’s grave was.

Peťa was by it. He was swimming in his undershirt, digging a grave. A dug one was beside it. Alongside a cart carrying twin pine coffins.

On Hermann and Marta’s approach, he halted. He had been expecting them.

The two stopped beside him.

Peťa swept the wetness. From both his forehead and his faint black mustache. He flicked his wrist toward Jiří’s tombstone. “If Prague has ten thousand more poets, it won’t have another Jiří Orten. And I killed him.”

Hermann held down his feelings. “It was an accident, Peťa.”

Peťa resumed his shoveling. He snarled. “Tell that to the beautiful words. Tell it to all the beautiful words he’ll never write.”

“What happened today?”

“I made the Ohrensteins two promises this morning. The first was that I’d give them morphine. To make it easier for them to jump. The problem was that it didn’t help. They couldn’t do it.”

“So how did she die?”

“He wouldn’t tell me. But they were arguing before she fell. Maybe she goaded him into pushing her. Maybe it was an accident. It doesn’t seem to matter.”

“How did he die?”

“When I found him in the ambulance after I parked here, empty bottles were on the floor. But if you ask me, he died of a broken heart.”

“What was the second promise you made?”

“To bury them by their boy. And that’s what does matter.”

Hermann had no more to say. He couldn’t have said more if he had.

All he could do was put a pebble on Jiří’s tombstone. What we Jews do to show the dead we remember them.

He and Marta walked off. She pressed herself against his arm. “Aren’t we having him arrested?”

“The only charge would be for befriending Jews.”

“What am I supposed to put in my report?”

“That you were right. It was a suicide.”

A glumness hung over the two while they made their way to Old Town. Only quiet passed between them.

They didn’t speak till they said their goodbyes. They said them at the same door that they had earlier said their hellos. Like earlier, they hugged.

Hermann went home.

He crossed the ancient cobblestones of Old Town Square. He tipped his hat to Jan Hus.

But he had to flail himself forward from that point.

The beautiful words and their silence were screaming in his head. They got so loud that he expected they would bring him to his knees.

Keeping him going was a shimmer.

It came from the kindness and selflessness of those that day who should’ve had neither. It was a beacon the Nazis couldn’t douse.

No one could.

Hermann made it to his apartment.

The shimmer blazed into the light in his pain.

Ana was waiting for him in her rocking chair. She was waiting with a smile.

The smile that had refused to let him fall.

This wiped away Hermann’s gloom. Nothing could bring it back.

Even when a slip of pink paper slid under their door.

 

 

the end
