chapter seventeen: the book of creation

 

 

THE DOORS TO the Spanish Synagogue were unlocked.

Hermann opened them. He and Klaus stepped into the vestibule of Josefov’s newest synagogue. The community had constructed it less than a century earlier on the grounds of what had been the oldest.

The steps represented the first in years that Hermann had been in a temple. Or in any house of worship. Apart from makeshift ones in recent times.

He sought a reaction.

But it was only a beautiful but ordinary building. Like others he had been inside.

This was deflating.

The synagogue appeared empty. But was well lit. The lights surprised Hermann more than the open doors. But what surprised him more was the friendly voice that called out, “Who’s there?”

“Hello,” was Klaus’s response. “We . . .”

Klaus couldn’t finish. A man interrupted him by limping into his view.

He was in his forties. He was larger than average. He was cherubic. He was holding clock hands and mechanical parts.

But what stood out to Hermann was how he was wearing a black cloth kippah. He was wearing it on the front of his head instead of on the back. He was also wearing grimy blue work dungarees and padded slippers.

Despite the latter, each stride brought him pain. It blanketed his face. He winced whenever his feet hit the floor. Worse, the imbalance sparked by his movements could’ve led him to fall for the slightest reason.

But the man’s feet were the least of his problems. Hermann identified this in his blank eyes.

Happiness overtook these eyes. The man was beholding a long-lost brother.

He put the clock hands and the parts in the pockets of his dungarees. He staggered to Hermann.

Giving him a kippah like his from one of his pockets, he shook his hand. He shook it hard. “You’ve come back!”

“Back?”

The man released Hermann’s hand. “You Jews, you’ve come back to Prague.”

Hermann laughed. The man’s mental limitations hadn’t limited him from spotting Hermann’s ethnicity. “I’m afraid that it’s only me who has returned.”

Disappointment rained on the man. It increased when he tweaked himself toward Klaus and his uniform.

“Do you work here?” Hermann asked while putting on the kippah.

The man’s disappointment vanished. His happiness returned. “I’m Ivo. The caretaker of the synagogues.”

“Ivo what?”

“Ivo Pokorný. Do you know that ‘pokorný’ means ‘humble’ in Czech? Rabbi Popper gave me the name. He said that it was the most important quality a man can have.”

“I gather that you take care of more than this synagogue.”

Ivo counted with his fingers. He counted twice. “Six of them.”

“All six by yourself?”

The man nodded his head. Multiple times. “Before, I worked only at the Maisel. For Rabbi Popper and his wife. That’s the pretty white building on Maisel Street. But now six is not enough work for me.”

“Until when do you work?”

Ivo pondered the answer.

Unable to come up with one, he took the clock hands from his pocket. He expressed an answer with them.

“Until five?”

Like before, Ivo nodded his head multiple times. “I get confused. Over when to use ‘five o’clock’ and when to use ‘seventeen hundred.’ But clocks don’t care.”

“Does anyone work at the synagogues at night? Like a watchman?”

“No one. I lock each synagogue after I clean them. That’s what the rabbi told me to do when he and his wife left the city. Have, have I done some wrong?”

Hermann tilted his head. “Why would you say that?”

Ivo jabbed his thumb at Klaus. “You’ve brought a policeman.”

“You’ve done no wrong. Do you mind if we look around?”

Ivo started off. With pain. “This is your house. I’m taking care of it till you all return. Call out if you need me.”

“What’s wrong with your feet?”

Ivo turned back to Hermann. “My feet are bad. And Dr. Brod is no longer here to help me.”

“What’s wrong with them?”

“Dr. Brod tried to explain it to me. But there was too many fancy words. It has to do with tall doodads in my feet.”

“High arches?”

“Could be. What I can say is that my feet have always been this bad. Since I was born, I think. But I can’t remember that far back. Anyway, that’s why I couldn’t fight in the big war. I wanted to.”

Ivo pretended to fire a machine gun. He was careful not to fire it at either Hermann or Klaus.

Hermann glowed at the man. I bet it was the kind of glow that I would appreciate throughout my childhood. It made you believe what he said. So much truth was in it. “We could’ve used a man like you.”

The compliment made Ivo happy. It shone through the pain that he conveyed from limping off.

Hermann inched toward the man. “I have one final question. Who pays you?”

“Pays me?”

“You do get a salary, don’t you?”

“Oh, yes. Mr. Malý comes to the Maisel on the last workday of the month, and he pays me. Then I pay my landlady, Mrs. Mlýnková, right away. Like the rabbi told me to do.”

“What is Mr. Malý’s first name?”

“He’s never told me. But he’s a nice man. He smiles more than the rabbi, and he smiled a lot.”

“How long have you known Mr. Malý?”

“Since the rabbi left town. He came to the Maisel days later. He told me that he’d take care of me until the rabbi returned. That he’d get me whatever I needed.”

“Did he give you his phone number?”

“I have it in the Maisel. He pinned his card on the board in the office. So I wouldn’t lose it.”

“You can read?”

“The rabbi taught me. It wasn’t easy.”

“Mr. Malý’s card doesn’t have his name on it?”

Ivo raised his head. He did so as if he were reading. “It says, ‘A. Malý.’ It doesn’t have an address either. Do you want me to get it?”

“When do you clean the Maisel?”

“First thing in the morning.”

“At nine?” Hermann asked. He took the clock hands from Ivo. He used them to make this clear.

Ivo nodded. He took back the hands at the same time.

“We’ll come by if we need it.”

Ivo limped off.

Hermann took another inch toward him. “Were you at the synagogue this morning?”

“I was.”

“Did you notice that the door to the gate was ajar?”

“I did notice it.”

“It wasn’t like that yesterday afternoon.”

“I closed and locked it yesterday morning. After I cleaned the building. Like how I closed and locked it today.”

“Who could’ve left it open?”

Ivo shook his head.

Hermann had no more questions. He let Ivo go on with his work.

The two men searched the building.

Hermann dipped his head into each room that they passed. No matter how insignificant it was. Many held nothing but furniture collected from the other synagogues.

Klaus poked his head over Hermann’s shoulder. “You believe that Gruber was killed here?”

“I’d say that it’s likely. What if the murderer killed the three men in the synagogues near where you discovered them? What if he carried them outside and set them down?”

“What would SS officers be doing in synagogues? Especially long-closed ones?”

Hermann went to another room. “That’s a good question. If we can come up with an answer to that, we might have a promising conjecture as to what happened.”

“What are you looking for?”

“If I knew, I wouldn’t have to look for it.”

Hermann didn’t locate it in this room.

He took a step into the prayer hall with Klaus. He admired the arabesque ceiling. As well as the ornate balconies on the hall’s sides. “Stunning.”

Klaus had to agree. “It is beautiful.”

“It’s difficult to believe that humans are capable of this beauty.”

The two started through the hall.

Hermann regarded the silence as eerie, having never been in such a place so quiet. Including when it was empty. The prayer halls of his youth and the makeshift ones that he had known in recent times were boisterous places full of life.

This was neither. It reflected the district’s ghost-town-like quality.

The men reached the altar. They climbed its four steps. They closed in on the ark at its end, whose door was unlatched.

Hermann checked inside it. It was empty. “What did you do with the Torah?”

“The what?”

“A Hebrew scroll bearing the Five Books of Moses.”

“You mean the Pentateuch?”

“We Jews call them by the name of the man who wrote them. Actually, in the cheder that I attended as a boy, they taught us that God spoke the words and that Moses wrote them down with his tears. You know, I always believed that was pretty while never believing it. Anyway, the Torah is the preeminent item in a synagogue. They store it in this ark. Did your people confiscate it when they closed the synagogue?”

“I couldn’t tell you. Nor could I imagine who to ask about it. Do you think it’s related to Gruber’s death?”

“I was just curious as to what had happened to it.”

The two left the hall.

Next to it was a room that Hermann had already scanned. A library.

Unlike earlier, the room interested Hermann. He shuffled inside it, followed by Klaus. They paused at a bookshelf of dry Hebrew-language religious texts.

Hermann rummaged through them.

As he had when entering the synagogue, he sought a reaction. He wasn’t expecting faith. Or any miracle like it. Only the feeling that the books had some meaning to him. Or that he had some connection to them.

But they were only books. As they had been before.

Klaus whined. “I’m sorry, but what could these books have to do with Captain Gruber’s murder?”

“In my experience, often an item that appears unrelated to a crime leads to its exposure. To do this job effectively, Klaus, you must separate what your eyes take interest in from what your mind tells you makes sense.”

Hermann kept rummaging through the books.

At the next bookshelf, he ran into a title that his eyes took greater interest in than the rest.

Sefer Yetzirah.

The book was interesting to Hermann for several reasons. The first was its content, far different from the traditional rabbinical texts surrounding it. It was a book that hadn’t crossed his mind since childhood. It led him to recall how his father had mentioned it amid his fantastical tales. But the second reason the book was interesting had nothing to do with its content.

He pulled it off the shelf. He inspected its edges.

Klaus huffed. “What are you doing?”

“How long has this synagogue been closed?”

“More than a year.”

“So why does this book have clean edges?”

“The caretaker must’ve cleaned it.”

Hermann moved his finger along the neighboring book’s top edge. He showed Klaus the dust. “So why do the other books here and those on the other shelves in this room have dust along their edges?”

Klaus had no answer.

Hermann opened the book. He perused its pages, ignoring how his Hebrew was worse than his Czech. “I’ve read that the Jews of Palestine have been reviving Hebrew as a spoken language. Like what the Czechs did with their language last century.”

Klaus bent over the book. “What’s it about?”

“The title translates to The Book of Creation. It’s Kabbalah. Jewish mysticism.”

“And what could it have to do with Gruber’s murder?”

“That I can’t say. What I can is that this book doesn’t belong here. Not in this library. Nor anywhere in this synagogue. And that’s what makes it so interesting.”

The book offered no further clues.

Hermann closed it. He returned it to its shelf.

He gave the room one final pass. He and Klaus left. They continued through the building.

Reaching the back, they stopped by an old, arched door. It was locked. So they got Ivo.

He told them that he didn’t have the key for it. But he was so nervous that Hermann became suspicious of why.

Hermann exhaled. “Who has the key?”

Ivo crept his eyes away. “Rabbi Schuler. He was the rabbi of this synagogue before. Before he left.”

“What’s behind the door?”

Ivo didn’t answer. He shrugged. He made himself more suspicious.

Klaus expressed exasperation. “I’ll get someone to open it. In the meantime, we can go to the third crime scene.”

Hermann started off. But he turned back to Ivo. “How can we reach you if we need to speak to you?”

“Just call out.”

“I mean, how do we contact you if you are not at work?”

“Oh, I live on Maisel Street. In one of Mrs. Mlýnková’s rooms. Above the café across from Jáchym Street. I live in the room by the top of the first staircase.”

Memories flooded Hermann’s mind. Of wonderful, normal times. Those he had spent in the café with Ana.

He and Klaus left the synagogue. They returned to the courtyard.

Hermann began unwrapping the mystery of Ivo. He became so puzzled that Klaus asked him what was wrong.

Hermann pointed behind himself. “He’s hiding something.”

“The caretaker? Surely, you don’t believe that he’s involved in this.”

“Why couldn’t he be?”

“First, he’s an idiot. He’s barely conscious of the basest of realities. Second, he’s lame. Frau Schiller was not exaggerating about her husband. He was indeed a strong man and well-versed in hand-to-hand combat techniques. He would’ve flattened Ivo. Even if he had caught him by surprise. And that’s unlikely. The other men were capable and well-trained soldiers too. I doubt Ivo could’ve carried any.”

“You asked me whether I believed that he’s involved in this. Not whether I believed that he was the one who committed the murders. The former is possible. If not likely. The bigger question is whether he knows he’s involved.”
