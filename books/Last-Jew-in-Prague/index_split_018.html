chapter eighteen: the exhibition of extinct peoples

 

 

THE DISTANCE BETWEEN the Spanish and Klausen synagogues is less than a kilometer.

But getting from the former to the latter wasn’t easy. It required Hermann and Klaus taking a more tortuous route through the maze than they had on their prior journeys. They first had to drive north to the river and go westward along Dvořák Quay. Next, they took a seashell-like path around Maisel Street. They parked on U Starého Hřbitova.

The driver shook his head. “How do you figure this out?”

Hermann leaned up front. “When I first got to the city, I had an old brass compass. I can’t tell you how helpful it was. But I lost it long ago. I’ve learned since that—to make my way through Josefov—I needed to solve a puzzle that consists of finding a path between two points. And this isn’t such a difficult task. The number of puzzles isn’t as infinite as you might expect. It’s limited to the number of streets in the district squared minus this number of streets, and there aren’t that many of these, even when you consider the ones that change direction. Like Široká yesterday. In the end, you realize that making your way from any point in Josefov to another is a matter of recollecting a puzzle that you’ve already solved.”

The driver said nothing.

Klaus and Hermann left the car.

Hermann stretched his arm toward a concession stand. It was selling kielbasas and beer among the vendors lining a cemetery wall.

Klaus gave his grudging assent. He bought Hermann a burnt sausage. Of dubious origin. It came on a piece of newspaper with mustard and a few slices of stale rye bread. Klaus bought him a beer too. Weaker than what he had the day earlier.

The two sat on a bench under grayish clouds. Hermann took a bite while wishing that he would eat better than he had the day before.

Klaus grimaced at him. “Your diet hasn’t gotten better since college. Nor has it gotten more Jewish.”

“But it has gotten better since Theresienstadt,” Hermann countered, angered that his stomach was continuing to prevent him from enjoying what he was eating. “I thank you for that much.”

Klaus didn’t respond.

“You’re not eating?”

Klaus’s grimace got stronger. “I had a big breakfast.”

Hermann was sure this was a lie. Not only because Gertrud had brought him the rolls earlier. From the man’s sallow skin and gaunt cheeks, he suspected that Klaus hadn’t been eating much better than he had.

Did this have to do with the nightmare Klaus was having in the car the day before?

Hermann told himself that he didn’t care.

This didn’t work. So he pretended it. By lifting the sausage half that he couldn’t finish. “Each morning I would have one of these as svačina,” he said, referring to the snack many Czechs eat between breakfast and lunch. Along with the peculiar New Jersey family that I grew up in. “They’re awful. But they’re a special kind of awful.”

Hermann sensed Klaus was anxious to get moving. He wiped his face with the newspaper, tossing it and the leftovers into a garbage pail.

The two set off. They entered the synagogue as well as a prayer hall that had a high vaulted ceiling, where hung a chandelier that extended to the level of a balcony that itself extended halfway around the room. Beneath the chandelier stood a heavyset SS officer of around forty. She was directing three men in placing oversized photographs on walls labeled “Jews,” “Gypsies,” “Homosexuals,” and “The Enfeebled.”

She swung her fists at a man. “Don’t put it on that wall, you fool! Do you not see the man’s hooked nose? Whether he was also a homosexual is of secondary concern.”

“That must be Lieutenant Krieg,” Klaus whispered to Hermann, masking his disdain for her. They both masked it as they stepped toward the woman.

Klaus introduced himself and Hermann. With an excess of brevity. He told the lieutenant that they were interested in the circumstances of Captain Fischer’s death.

Hermann gestured toward the photographs with his hands. “What’s going on here?”

The woman didn’t respond.

Klaus groaned. “The man asked you a question.”

She snarled. “I don’t answer the questions of Jews.”

Klaus snarled back at her. “You will answer this one’s, Lieutenant.”

The lieutenant groaned herself. She shifted her eyes from both men and crossed her arms. “We’re turning this building and the other synagogues in the district into museums. This will be one of its main exhibitions.”

Hermann tilted his head. “An exhibition of what?”

“Extinct peoples.”

“None of these people are extinct.”

“The exhibition hasn’t opened yet. It’ll take years before it’s completed. But hopefully not many.”

“And what will be the point of this exhibition?”

Her answer was matter-of-fact: “To chronicle your race and similar forms of life. Much like other museums chronicle Neanderthals and dinosaurs and other creatures that weren’t fit to survive. You should be thankful that the Reich is taking such trouble and expense to keep the memory of your people alive.”

Hermann wasn’t thankful. But he wasn’t angered either. He wanted to feel anger. At what she had said and at how she’d defiled a holy place. But he could feel no more anger toward her than he could feel joy in a synagogue. He could only feel unease. At himself.

He ignored this. “Are you developing these museums in cooperation with Mr. Malý?”

“Who’s Mr. Malý?” the woman snapped.

“The person responsible for the synagogues.”

“What do you mean by ‘responsible’?”

“He’s responsible for their upkeep.”

The lieutenant shook her head. “That is none of my concern. Colonel Reidl’s office deals with issues related to the buildings themselves. My responsibilities involve curation.”

Klaus took his turn with the woman. “You’re the person who discovered the body of Captain Fischer.”

She pointed out the door. “He was sitting out there when I got to work that morning.”

“It’s our understanding that you knew him,” Hermann said, evading his eyes from both the pictures and the woman.

“He had been observing the exhibition’s progress.”

Klaus’s head flew sideways. “He said that was his purpose?”

“Not exactly. But why would he be here so much? There is great interest in this exhibition. I hear Eichmann is interested in our work.”

Hermann interrupted them. “Was Captain Fischer ever here at night?”

“I don’t know how late he stayed. But he was here a number of times when I left for the day. Such as on the day he died.”

“When did you leave that day?”

“The same time I leave every day: 4:30.”

Hermann paused. For several seconds. “Was anyone here with him when you left?”

“Just the idiot.”

“The idiot?”

“The crippled idiot who works here,” she growled, her voice soaked with contempt.

“You mean Ivo.”

“I wasn’t aware that he had a name. We call him ‘idiot.’”

The woman raised her arm at the wall to her left. “One day his picture will be here. Along with others of his kind.”

Hermann strove not to wince. But he couldn’t stop himself. “I have one more question: what was Captain Fischer doing when you left that day?”

She thrust her head toward the hall’s far back corner. “He was interested in that door over there.”

This was an old, arched door. Like the one in the Spanish Synagogue.

Hermann couldn’t control his excitement. “What’s behind it?”

“It leads to a cellar.”

“Can we go down there?”

“The door’s locked, and no one has a key. Captain Fischer had a locksmith come. But he couldn’t open it. Nobody can. The captain couldn’t shoot the lock off.”

“So what makes you sure that it leads to a cellar?”

“Captain Fischer insisted it did.”

“How did he know this?”

The woman gave Hermann a dismissive wave. “That I can’t answer. But he became insistent on it after spending a few days here, despite my showing him the building’s blueprint. Which has no mention of a cellar.”

“Where does the door lead to on the blueprint?”

“There is no door on the blueprint.”

The men had nothing left to ask. They started to leave.

But Hermann froze. The ark by the altar was open and empty. “One last question.”

Krieg clenched both her fists and her jaw. “You said that before, and you asked another. More than one.”

“This time I mean it. What have you done with the Torah that was in that ark?”

“I haven’t a hint as to what you’re talking about.”

“The Hebrew scroll that was in that compartment.”

“That compartment has been empty since I began working here.”

“When was that?”

“Half a year ago.”

The men left the synagogue. Without another word.

Hermann felt greater thankfulness than he had before entering the synagogue. He was thankful for fresh air.

“I guess you were right about Ivo,” Klaus said on the way to the car. “I’ll have someone pick him up.”

“We have no evidence against him,” Hermann shot back, fathoming too well what would happen to Ivo if someone did “pick him up.”

“We don’t need evidence, Hermann. It’s sufficient that he was with the dead man the night he died. He was also in the other dead men’s vicinities, and he was acting suspiciously about the door. We’ll discover his connection to this in short order.”

“If you don’t kill him beforehand. Besides, you yourself said that he’s conscious of only the basest of realities.”

“Do you have a better suggestion?”

“We can get into those cellars. If the evidence leads to Ivo, we’ll discover it. In short order.”

Klaus stopped by the Mercedes. He flung up his arms. “Fine, we’ll get into the cellars.”

“And how do you plan on doing that?”

“Trust me, Hermann, if the Reich can get into this country, it can get into two lousy cellars.”

Klaus checked his watch. “I have a meeting that I must attend. I’ll take you back to the palace and get you some new guards. They’ll take you to U Bílého Lva when it’s time for dinner.”

Hermann stayed quiet.

“One more thing. I care deeply for Frau Gross. But she is the biggest gossip in town. She is not to learn what we’re doing. If asked, you will tell her that you’re assisting me in a mundane and uninteresting crime investigation.”

“Will she believe that?”

“Convince her,” Klaus barked. “We both know that you have a way with women.”
