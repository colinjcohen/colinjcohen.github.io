chapter one: a pair of nights

 

 

GRANDPA WAS CERTAIN they would shoot him.

I was recalling this and him through the gloom in the Rancho Mirage Public Library’s windows.

But why?

I turned back to the scratched screen of my ThinkPad X220 and its battered keys. In my mailbox was an email from the freelancing site that had been keeping me afloat. “Update regarding AB5 and Textbroker.”

They had suspended me. Me and all writers from California.

AB5 had banned freelancing. Its target was Uber and Lyft. But it snared me in its net. One overreaching law would have me reaching for a cardboard sign. It was hurtling me below the bottom that I had hit.

Seething, I slung my laptop above my head. I was about to smash it to the floor.

“Did you get sandbags?” A woman whispered behind me.

“They were all sold out,” another woman whispered. “We boarded our windows. We did plenty of praying. We’re leaving at two.”

I returned to the gloom. It had started raining.

It was raining harder in the afternoon. It was raining harder than that when I left the library with my backpack and my sleeping bag and my belongings over my shoulders.

I walked to the post office. A pocketful of blocks down the road.

Nothing had been in my box for months. But I checked it.

Why? Why was I searching for a reason forward?

The box was empty.

I didn’t bother going to the Aldi at the hill’s bottom. Where I’d buy dinner each night. A $1.99-pound bag of granola with almonds. I went to the Palm Desert Library. It stayed open later than the one in Rancho Mirage. Till eight.

Worse gloom was in their windows.

Grandpa was certain they would shoot him.

A woman stopped by my cubicle. “We’re closing an hour early.”

I didn’t wait for this. I packed up. I left in the freezing downpour. I tramped across the swamp in the parking lot and across the one next to it by the McCallum Theatre. I was the only person at the stop on the corner of Fred Waring and Monterey.

A bus lumbered left through the slush. It staggered toward me. It splattered water onto the sidewalk as it slowed along the curb.

I didn’t step from it. I couldn’t get wetter.

The bus halted. I climbed its ramp. I ran my damp monthly pass over the slot in front. I took a seat in the back row.

We sloshed right onto Highway 111. We plowed up it. Into the nowhere.

“Where you camping tonight?” the guy in the seat ahead of me asked someone on the other side.

“They’re calling this an ‘atmospheric river.’ A Category 5. The Well is setting up a shelter for us. The valley is gonna flood.”

I pulled the yellow cord. I jumped up doing so.

The bus shrieked to a stop. I skipped out into the torrent.

With the howls of coyotes calling me, luring me onward, I crossed the road. I marched toward the moonless skies and the boundless desert.

Through muddy sand that was above my ankles, I trudged to what I called my home. In a ravine filling with water.

Grandpa was certain they would shoot him.

The words echoed. They rose higher than the canyon soaring over me. They hung in the air.

I rolled myself into a ball, inside my tattered and soaked sleeping bag. Clutching an old brass compass, I grasped how my struggles had entwined with my grandpa’s greater ones.

He first told me about his on the night of my bar mitzvah. Days after I had turned thirteen and after years of prodding him to tell me about his time during the war without having had expectations that he would. On that day I had come of age, he and his smile led me through our home into the living room. I sat on the sofa under the glow of a fire he was starting.

Loosening my tie, I drifted into the world that he was letting me into. Unaware of how the story that was unraveling itself would upend me.

 

 

“WEISS!” A VOICE called out to the forty-five-year-old man who would become my grandpa. “Hermann Weiss!”

Hermann woke. With grogginess.

He squinted into a beam of brightness, lifting his head off a straw-filled sheet that was making believe it was a mattress. Through the white was the silhouette of two soldiers in the doorway. One was waving his flashlight toward himself.

Hermann crawled down. From the top of a triple-decker bunk bed.

A man was facing him from the bunk next to his. A man he hadn’t met but wasn’t a stranger. He had cropped and graying brown hair like his. The man’s dirty and torn jacket and the tattered shirt underneath it mimicked his own. He was in front of a reflection in a dirty mirror. Of a scarecrow missing its stuffing.

The last Hermann took in of him was his smile. It was mild and forced. He must’ve believed that the soldiers would shoot Hermann.

Hermann believed this too. He moved faster. He jumped to the tight opening on the floor between the beds. Hurrying toward the light in a closet that was making believe it was a room, he reached a pair of towering SS troopers.

The three started forward in the dimness. They moved through the heaps of flesh sleeping around them in the Hanover Barracks. Two centuries earlier, the building had quartered a few hundred Austrian soldiers serving Emperor Joseph II. But now, in a garrison town that had become both a ghetto and a concentration camp, it housed thousands of cattle like Hermann. All waiting their turn to die.

It didn’t take the three much time to leave the building. Which left them along the eastern edge of Theresienstadt. About halfway across its length.

They marched south and west. Into a dark and wet and wintry morning in April 1943. Passing barracks after barracks and the corpses that people had left outside them that night were two Aryan giants and a speck of a man who had lost will. The same man who would be so towering to me. In the face of his diminutive size.

The man striding through the streets of Theresienstadt had lost much in the years before this. What he hadn’t was the skill that had served him well in his past profession: the power of observation. He made out how similar the night was to the first that he had spent at the camp months earlier. That had been a muggy August evening. But it was the same night. With the same paucity of aspiration.

Hermann also made out dogs barking in the distance.

Would these be the last sounds of life he’d experience?

He expected that the troopers would place him against a wall. They would release him from the flames to which he had been consigned.

Despite his eagerness for this, Hermann was scared. His lone comfort was that he wasn’t alone. A woman was beside him.

She had her arm wrapped around his. She was smiling at him. He was all that mattered. She gave him the courage to continue on. The same courage that she had given him during the months that he’d spent at the camp.

She only faded when the men, instead of shooting Hermann, marched him to the Czech gendarmerie near the southwestern edge of Theresienstadt. The building where those responsible for guarding the camp worked.

Taken aback, Hermann became further so. The men marched him into the building. To the commander’s door.

From the other side were voices. Of Theodor Janeček and another man.

Hermann couldn’t place the other voice. But it was more familiar than Janeček’s.

His curiosity rose. It pushed him forward.

“I don’t like this idea,” Janeček cried in accented German. “You can’t—”

“—If I want your opinion,” growled the second voice, one so identifiable to Hermann that he wanted to scream, “you can be certain that I will ask for it.”

A pause in the conversation ensued. A trooper took this opportunity to knock on the door.

“Come in!” shouted Janeček.

The trooper flung open the door. He flung Hermann by the arm into the room.

A pale Janeček was perching behind his desk. A blond SS officer the size of a mountain was sitting across from him.

The latter had his back in part to Hermann. But regardless of this, regardless of all the years that had passed since he had been in front of him last, Hermann recognized Klaus Stamm.

His jaw opened. It opened wide.

The two troopers saluted Klaus. The man who had been doing the knocking said, “Herr Captain, we—”

“—That’s it for now,” Klaus interrupted, his sight remaining on Janeček.

The troopers backed out. The talking one slammed the door.

Klaus nodded at Janeček. “You can leave as well.”

“It’s against protocols.”

Klaus didn’t reply. He glared at Janeček. His eyes were burning with contempt.

Janeček recoiled from this. He hopped up. Like a dog before its enraged master he hopped. He saluted Klaus, shouting, “Heil Hitler!”

Klaus’s glare got stronger. It pushed Janeček from his desk.

He rushed from his office. He shut the door behind him.

Hermann wasn’t sure what to do. Other than to gaze at the man who had been his friend.

“Sit down,” Klaus ordered. He did so as if Hermann were a child he was about to punish.

“Where?”

“Where is there a free seat?”

“Janeček’s?”

“Is something wrong with it?”

Hermann didn’t answer. He stepped past Klaus and the black attaché at his feet. To the commander’s desk.

Sitting across from Klaus, he got his first good look at a man that he hadn’t seen in twenty years.

Two particulars jumped out. The first was how Klaus had aged beyond what he had. The second was how tired he was.

A sudden anger burned inside Hermann. But it wouldn’t stay there. “I’m not surprised that you’ve become a Nazi. Nor am I surprised that you’re an SS man.”

But both facts surprised Hermann. He was surprised that the man was so different from how he had remembered him. The two couldn’t have been the same.

He sought some shimmer of his former friend in what was left. He sought and sought it.

“How are you?” Klaus asked. With his eyes far from Hermann. Though this couldn’t prevent his own surprise. From how terrible Hermann must’ve appeared. Klaus was compelling his focus from it.

The casualness of Klaus’s question shook Hermann. “How am I?”

“As you can imagine, this isn’t a social call.”

“That I can imagine. What I can’t is its purpose.”

“I’m provisionally the head of the Prague office of the Kripo. Unlike in Germany, we cannot staff career police detectives. This means that, while we’re capable of solving simple and ordinary crimes . . .”

“I still don’t get why you’re here.”

“My office has spent days sifting through the Prague Police archives. It’s come to my attention that you were among the best detectives in Josefov. If not the best. It’s also come to my attention that you were someone who embraced challenges. And, unlike many of your colleagues, pursued them.”

Hermann was more surprised. The revelations surprised him as much as the man who had made them. “I’m not the same person who’d been that.”

“We have a situation. In Josefov. In the past weeks, someone has murdered three SS officers in the district.”

Hermann understood the words Klaus was telling him. But not their context.

This rendered them nonsensical.

He shook his head. “Why are you telling me this? You can’t possibly think I care.”

“We’d involve the local police if they weren’t so useless,” Klaus went on, as if he hadn’t heard what Hermann had said. But I suppose he didn’t care what Hermann had to say. “We can’t tell if they’re incompetent. Or acting that way.”

Hermann smirked. At the quagmire of feckless acquiescence his former colleagues were creating for people like Klaus. “You’ve read The Good Soldier Švejk. Passive resistance has ingrained itself in the Czech character.”

“But not in the Jewish one.”

Hermann could tell Klaus was coming to the point. But he had no idea what it was. So he asked him.

“As I’ve alluded to, my office doesn’t have the capabilities to solve this crime. To be honest, the SS is not adept at tracking down murderers. Including in Germany.”

“That’s because you give them medals.”

“And Josefov is alien to us. It has become alien to me. We get lost driving through it.”

“What is it you want from me?”

“I’m authorized to offer you your old job back. For this case.”

Hermann couldn’t decide how to react. Whether to be aghast or humored.

He chose apathetic. “Why would you imagine I’d take it?”

Klaus touched his jacket pocket. “I have a visa that’ll provide you with passage to Switzerland. It’s yours if you can reveal those responsible for the murders before my replacement arrives in two weeks.”

Hermann didn’t believe that there was a visa. In Klaus’s pocket or anywhere. He didn’t believe that they would give it to him if there was. But if he had believed the totality of what Klaus had said, his answer would’ve been the same as it was: “I won’t do it.”

For the first time that night, Klaus’s expression shifted from its matter-of-factness. To a shock that draped over his face. “You, you’ve misunderstood me, Hermann. I wasn’t asking you. Either you come back with me to Prague and help me catch the killer or those men outside the door will shoot you right now.”

Hermann didn’t respond in words. He glared at Klaus, I’m sure like how Klaus had glared at Janeček. But with more contempt. He was angry at him. More than before. For making him complicit in his death when he had expected and needed the opposite. He wanted to scream at him. To demand that they shoot him without conditions. But this in itself would’ve been complicity. The single sin that he couldn’t have.

Klaus showed greater shock. At Hermann’s response to his threat. “Did you not get what I said?”

“I’m thinking about it,” Hermann snapped.

He was. He was searching for a way to extricate himself from the snare that they had trapped him in. He searched and searched for it.
