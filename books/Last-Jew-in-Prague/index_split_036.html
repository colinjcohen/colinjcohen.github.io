chapter thirty-six: the skirt-chaser

 

 

THE MERCEDES PARKED on Maltézské Square. Beside U Malířů in the late midday sun.

Klaus shook his head at the restaurant. “This is a wild goose chase.”

“It’s what I’d call a refined wild goose chase,” Hermann said. “I can tell you that, from experience, if you approach them in the right way, you often catch yourself a goose.”

“What makes it refined?”

“You saw Captain Fischer’s apartment. Did it look like it had belonged to someone who frequents the most expensive restaurant in the city?”

“No specifics in his file would indicate it either. His tastes ran to the contrary.”

“But he was here.”

“So he was here. What do you expect to discover?”

“We might discover his dinner companions. We might discover what they were discussing.”

“What makes you believe that you’d discover that?”

“Before they drafted me into the army, I was working at a restaurant in Uzhhorod. It wasn’t much different from this one. We even served the emperor.”

“The one who pinned the Order of the Iron Crown on you?”

“The one prior. Anyway, I was amazed at how freely the patrons talked among themselves. It was as if they were eating at home and the staff weren’t there. The head waiter, a Mr. Szilágyi, retired handsomely from the investment tips that he gleaned from working there.”

Klaus slumped over. “We’re talking now about well-trained SS men. Not drunken bankers.”

Hermann opened his door with Fischer’s file in his hand. “It’s our only lead.”

Klaus had to admit this. He left with Hermann. But he wasn’t too excited.

On the restaurant wall was a sign that said that the place didn’t open until seventeen hundred. But the two stepped inside.

They passed through the lavish furnishings. They passed under the fine art painted on the ceiling. They neared the maître d’, sitting at a table in the room’s far left corner.

The man didn’t stir from a stack of receipts. “We’re not open, gentlemen. Not till five.”

Hermann and Klaus ignored this. They continued to the man. Hermann showed him Fischer’s photograph. “Do you recall serving this gentleman?”

The maître d’ skimmed over the picture. “I can’t say that I do.”

He returned to his paperwork. He also pointed at a set of swinging doors. “Some of the staff are in the kitchen having lunch. You can ask them if you’d like.”

Hermann and Klaus went to the doors. Hermann slowed. “You should wait outside.”

Klaus slowed himself. “Why?”

“If you were them, would you openly talk about an SS officer in front of another?”

Klaus heaved a sigh. But he stepped to the side by the doors.

Hermann tapped the doors’ bottoms with his foot. “You can listen from here. Just keep it open a morsel.”

Hermann shuffled into the kitchen.

“You can’t come in here,” squawked a chef, eating with a half-dozen other men at a wooden table.

“It’s okay,” Hermann said. “The maître d’ told me that I could.”

The man viewed Hermann and his suit askance. “He did?”

“It’s police business.”

“You’re a policeman?”

“I’m helping the authorities in an unofficial capacity. You can ask your boss if you’d like.”

The man didn’t respond.

Hermann stopped at the table. He showed everyone a picture of Captain Fischer. “Have any of you seen him? We believe that he ate here in the recent past.”

A man lifted his head. “I remember him. I served him.”

“Was he here with someone?”

“Two other Nazis.”

“Can you describe them?”

“One was big. Big and fat and older than the other two. The other was wispy. Kind of weasel-like.”

“When was this?”

“I don’t remember. But Dita was working here. When did she quit?”

“In January,” the chef said.

“Who’s Dita?” Hermann asked the man.

The waiter answered for him. “Dita Prejslerová. She was a coat-check girl here. And the guy in the picture, he was flirting with her a lot. A real sukničkář, that guy. If you know what I mean. He noticed each woman who came into the restaurant. He especially noticed Dita. He was flirting with her each chance he got.”

“And she quit soon afterward?”

“Yeah.”

“Do any of you know where she lives?”

Everyone shook their heads. But the waiter said, “You can ask Tomáš for her address.”

“Who’s Tomáš?”

“The maître d’. He manages the place.”

“Do you remember anything else about the men? Like what they were talking about?”

“I remember that they’d clam up anytime I was in sight. Like they were discussing some big secret. The guy in the picture, he was also real swept up in what the fat guy had to say. The whole night he was hanging over the table. His life depended on what this guy was telling him.”

“Thank you,” Hermann said to them. “You’ve been most helpful.”

“Say,” the waiter let out, “what did he do?”

“That’s what I’m trying to establish.”

Hermann bowed at them. He left the kitchen.

At the doors, he whispered to Klaus, “Did you get it all?”

“I got it.”

“Now we know why the captain never returned his coat check that night.”

The two marched to the maître d’.

Hermann asked him about Dita.

The maître d’s eyes remained on the receipts. “Who?”

“Dita Prejslerová. She was a coat-check girl here. She quit at the beginning of the year.”

“She didn’t quit. She stopped showing up. She didn’t collect her last pay.”

“Can you give us her address?”

For the first time, the man gave Hermann and Klaus his full focus. He gave this, in particular, to Klaus and his uniform. “I guess I don’t have a choice.”

Klaus didn’t bother to respond.
